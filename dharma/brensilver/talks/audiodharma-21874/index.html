Dharmette: The Divine Abodes (4 of 5) Sympathetic Joy

AudioDharma 16:34 2024-12-12

Mudita, or sympathetic joy, is approached here as something you can’t force and don’t need to fake. The talk moves from the difficulty of delighting in another’s good fortune to a practical account of what gets in the way: ownership, comparison, envy, and the ego’s habit of taking in every bit of goodness as its own. Along the way, the speaker uses a sutta from the Anguttara Nikaya and brief references to Joseph Epstein and Matthieu Ricard to sharpen the point that other people’s happiness does not deprive us of anything; it can actually free the heart.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Arriving at Mudita - Brensilver introduces sympathetic joy and notes that the morning practice had already moved through other territory before settling here.
  2. 00:57 Joy Cannot Be Forced - He defines mudita, distinguishes it from forced smiling, and emphasizes taking time with the physiology rather than pretending.
  3. 03:24 Resentment and the Sutta - He turns to the Anguttara Nikaya and a personal example to show mudita as an antidote to resentment.
  4. 05:41 Joy Beyond Scarcity - Brensilver contrasts worldly happiness with the deeper availability of joy through others' happiness and shared laughter.
  5. 08:00 Letting Down the Burden - He explores how joy can feel irresponsible or disloyal to grief, and argues for an emotional life that can hold several states without strain.
  6. 10:26 What Blocks Joy - The talk shifts to the obstacles to mudita—ownership, possessiveness, comparison, and envy—and how ego tries to absorb all goodness.
  7. 12:58 Jealousy and Envy - He distinguishes jealousy from envy, briefly cites evolutionary psychology, and then uses Matthieu Ricard to frame envy as wounded self-importance.
  8. 14:34 Sufficient and Unthreatened - He closes by describing the freedom that comes with sufficiency: other people's success no longer threatens the self and becomes a source of delight.
