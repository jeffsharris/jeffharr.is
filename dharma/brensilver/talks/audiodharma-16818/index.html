Dharmette: Dharma as Cognitive Therapy

AudioDharma 15:38 2022-08-17

Matthew Brensilver frames Dharma practice as a form of cognitive therapy: not just training attention, but changing the views and assumptions that organize experience. He moves from Aaron Beck’s model of schemas to Buddhist wise view, the three characteristics, and a fresh way of seeing the self. The talk ends with a practical note that attentional steadiness helps these new views take deeper root.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Opening the frame - Matthew begins by situating the talk and naming that he wants to expand the earlier discussion beyond attention alone.
  2. 00:59 More than attention - He argues that mindfulness works in part because it changes more than attention, and that religion can be understood as a kind of cognitive therapy.
  3. 03:00 Beck’s schemas - Using Aaron Beck’s language, he explains how schemas, distorted appraisal, and new information can shift symptoms and behavior.
  4. 05:02 Wise view - He turns to Buddhist practice as cognitive therapy, emphasizing that wise view comes first and shapes the rest of the path.
  5. 07:09 A fresh self - Matthew introduces a different view of self, including Gilbert Ryle’s category mistake example, to loosen fixed identity.
  6. 08:39 Seeing our stories - He describes practice as noticing the stories and hidden beliefs we live by, including moralistic assumptions about happiness and worth.
  7. 11:33 Three characteristics - The talk gathers around dukkha, anicca, and anatta as corrective lenses for common fantasies about pleasure, control, and completion.
  8. 14:01 Attention and carryover - He closes by linking cognitive change with attentional steadiness, suggesting that concentration helps new views settle more deeply.
