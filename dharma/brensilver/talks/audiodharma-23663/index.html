Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Dharmette: Wholesome Qualities of Mind (3of5); Parami of Wisdom

AudioDharma 15:13 2025-08-20

What if Dharma practice begins not with wisdom, but with raw data: the felt texture of breathing, clinging, change, and the mind’s surprising habits? The talk moves from Toni Morrison’s progression of data, information, knowledge, and wisdom to a close look at how experience becomes pattern, story, and finally a more useful understanding of suffering and its causes. Along the way, it brings in Thomas Nagel, Ajahn Sumedho, Jennifer Egan, the Four Noble Truths, and research on how people mispredict happiness, all in service of one question: can we learn to predict what actually leads to flourishing?

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Data to wisdom - Matthew opens with Toni Morrison’s progression from data to wisdom and warns against mistaking one level for another.
  2. 01:38 Direct experience - He describes practice as gathering data through immediate experience, using Thomas Nagel and the phrase 'Right now, it's like this' to point back to lived reality.
  3. 03:35 Seeing the self clearly - The talk turns to beginner’s mind, habits of perception, and the way practice exposes how quickly we impose a familiar self on experience.
  4. 05:00 Shocking mind-data - Matthew recalls his own meditation practice and the surprising facts that emerge when attention stays with breathing, clinging, peace, and change.
  5. 06:10 Patterns and story - He explains how data becomes pattern, using Jennifer Egan to show that information without story remains hard to know, and that Dharma stories are less brittle.
  6. 08:03 Fluid dharma stories - The talk develops the idea of flexible inner narratives that can include others, remain open to correction, and move toward silence.
  7. 09:49 Wisdom and causality - Matthew shifts from ordinary self-centered stories to the Buddha’s emphasis on suffering, causality, and wisdom as realization of the Four Noble Truths.
  8. 12:22 Predicting happiness - He closes with Timothy Wilson’s work on affective forecasting and asks whether wisdom can predict what truly benefits our hearts and helps others thrive.
