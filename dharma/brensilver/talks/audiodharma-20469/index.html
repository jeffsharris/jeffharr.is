Dharmette: Cruising Altitude and Upheaval

AudioDharma 15:41 2024-05-22

Matthew Brensilver reflects on the changing rhythms of practice, especially the difference between quieter stretches of stability and the more disruptive periods when a familiar view no longer fits. He begins with grief and the limits of trying to analyze feeling too quickly, then expands into a longer analogy with Thomas Kuhn’s idea of normal science and scientific revolution. The talk settles into a careful invitation to notice when life or practice has outgrown an old story, and to treat that rupture as part of the ongoing path.

Chapters

  1. 00:10 Practice in phases - Matthew opens by describing practice as something with rhythms that are often only visible in retrospect.
  2. 02:34 Grief and spaciousness - He turns to a conversation about grief, questioning the urge to reduce it too quickly and pointing instead toward something larger than the feeling itself.
  3. 04:22 Coming home, leaving home - He widens the frame to talk about how Dharma can renew old loves while also drawing us into new ones, and how some phases simply feel like cruising.
  4. 06:54 Kuhn’s normal science - Using Thomas Kuhn, he compares stable practice to normal science and suggests that growth often includes orderly periods before any break arrives.
  5. 09:01 When the story breaks - He describes the onset of disruption as anomalous data that old frameworks can no longer contain, bringing disorientation and a shaken sense of self.
  6. 11:27 Self-view under pressure - The talk moves into how feedback, conflict, and experience can rattle our identity and reveal how much of ourselves rests on familiar reference points.
  7. 13:26 Revolution and rest - He returns to the image of revolution and notes that practice seems to cycle between upheaval and rest, each round making the heart a little less naive.
