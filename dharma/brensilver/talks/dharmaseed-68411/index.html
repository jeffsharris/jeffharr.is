Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Anatta and its Blessings

Dharma Seed 50:07 2021-05-21

Matthew Brensilver explores anatta not as a new philosophy to adopt, but as a way of loosening the habits that turn experience into a fixed self. He moves from grief and confusion around self-view to the relief, tenderness, and connection that can follow when clinging softens. Along the way he uses examples from close relationships, self-esteem, envy, and identity to show how selfing shapes suffering and how its fading can open intimacy.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Setting the frame - Matthew introduces the talk through the three characteristics and explains why the teaching often begins with dukkha and letting go.
  2. 03:07 Grief before relief - He describes the initial grief of facing impermanence, unreliability, and centerlessness, and the mind's habit of resisting those truths.
  3. 09:06 What not-self means - The talk turns to anatta itself, including the confusion it can cause and the caution not to turn it into another view or position.
  4. 14:18 Looking for the self - Using the Tibetan instruction to look directly, Matthew explores how we narrate and represent ourselves and how that habit generates dukkha.
  5. 20:58 Self-love and defensiveness - He clarifies that self-love is not self-cherishing, then shows how hatred, shame, and fragile self-esteem keep the self contracted and defended.
  6. 28:00 Narrative gravity - With a reference to Paul Fulton and then Dennett, he describes the self as a fragile narrative center that is continually being rebuilt and protected.
  7. 35:49 Freedom from comparison - He shows how letting go can make experience feel less dense and how that loosening reduces envy, comparison, and the need to perform an identity.
  8. 42:39 Open air - The closing movement expands anatta into compassion, less hatred, and a wider sense of closeness to other people, ending with Parfit's image of leaving the glass tunnel for open air.
