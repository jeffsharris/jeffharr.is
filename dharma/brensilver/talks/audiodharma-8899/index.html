Dharmette: Worrying and Tranquility

AudioDharma 14:56 2018-05-16

Worry is treated here as a strategy for managing the insecurity of being human: the mind keeps leaning forward into imagined threats, hoping that vigilance will prevent pain. From there, the talk turns toward tranquility as something shaped by conditions rather than willpower alone, with clinging and letting go presented as the larger frame. It then gets concrete, considering overcommitment, time in nature, and the ways technology can train attention toward restlessness instead of ease.

Chapters

  1. 00:26 Human intensity - He begins with the sense that being human is inherently intense, with the mind constantly touched by sensory and mental input.
  2. 02:18 Worry as strategy - Using Borkovec’s framing, he describes worry as a future-oriented attempt to prepare for danger and gain control over what cannot really be controlled.
  3. 05:05 Longing for peace - He contrasts restlessness with the deep wish for peace, suggesting that even brief moments of ease can feel like a homecoming.
  4. 07:14 Causality and letting go - He argues that tranquility depends on conditions, not just effort, and links anxiety to clinging while pointing toward letting go.
  5. 09:31 Simpler commitments - With Thomas Merton’s warning about too many demands, he offers clearer boundaries and better use of energy as one support for peace.
  6. 11:18 Restorative nature - He briefly notes research and experience suggesting that time in nature can restore attention and reduce the pull of constant threat.
  7. 11:35 Technology and compulsion - Drawing on Tristan Harris, he reflects on attention-economy incentives, his own phone habits, and the relief of less compulsive access to entertainment.
  8. 14:32 Quiet conclusion - He closes by returning to the theme of supportive conditions for non-worry and settling.
