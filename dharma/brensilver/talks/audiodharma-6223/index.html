Dharmette: Practicing for oneself is complicated.

AudioDharma 11:04 2015-12-09

Practicing for one’s own benefit, the talk suggests, can quickly turn into a tense self-improvement project. By contrast, when practice is oriented toward the welfare of all beings, it can feel simpler, less self-conscious, and less trapped in the question of how one is doing. The speaker uses Shantideva’s bodhisattva ideal—and a brief reference to Joseph Goldstein’s emphasis on compassion—to show how a vast intention can loosen the tightness of self-focus and make the heart more useful.

Chapters

  1. 00:41 Complicated Self-Benefit - Matthew introduces the central claim that practice for one’s own benefit tends to become complicated, while practice for the benefit of all beings can be simpler.
  2. 01:44 The Bodhisattva Turn - He places this shift inside the bodhisattva vow, using Shantideva to frame the mind as dedicated to relieving suffering rather than serving the self.
  3. 03:14 Compassion as Mission - Matthew briefly names Joseph Goldstein and the Insight Meditation Society to show how compassion became explicit in the culture of practice.
  4. 03:56 Shantideva's Vast Vow - A longer Shantideva passage expands the scope of the vow into images of healing, nourishment, and self-offering for the world.
  5. 05:16 The Weight of Grandness - He acknowledges how such vast intention can feel heavy, especially in the face of suffering, racism, oppression, war, and poverty.
  6. 06:26 Beyond Self-Improvement - Matthew explains how self-focused practice can become evaluative and tight, whereas compassion helps cut through the drama of asking how one is doing.
  7. 08:09 A More Useful Heart - He shifts from making oneself an artwork to making the heart useful, naming a quiet poignancy in that reorientation.
  8. 09:41 Enough, Then Others - The talk ends with the sense that practice can become less driven by personal happiness and more naturally directed toward others' welfare.
