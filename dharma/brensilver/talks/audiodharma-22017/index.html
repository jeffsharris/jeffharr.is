Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Dharmette: more of our personality in freedom than in our suffering

AudioDharma 14:07 2025-01-01

Matthew Brensilver reflects on a reversal of Tolstoy’s famous line: suffering can feel intensely personal, but freedom may reveal more of our actual uniqueness. Drawing on Buddhist teaching, Carl Rogers, Winnicott, and Bruce Tift, he explores how empathy, relinquishment, and reduced compulsivity allow a more spontaneous, distinctive self to appear. The talk stays grounded in the felt contrast between the performative quality of suffering and the quiet freshness of liberation.

Chapters

  1. 00:17 Tolstoy Reversed - He opens with Tolstoy and turns the famous line around to show that suffering may be more alike across people than it first appears.
  2. 01:48 Pain Feels Unique - He lingers on how suffering can feel like a private brand, while also pointing to its shared, disconnecting character.
  3. 04:55 Meeting Others Carefully - Using Carl Rogers, he describes empathic attunement as entering another’s world without losing perspective, and notes that another person’s freedom can feel surprising and instructive.
  4. 07:28 What Freedom Looks Like - He turns to the Buddha’s emphasis on cessation and asks what a life freed from greed, hate, and delusion might actually look like in ordinary personality and ethics.
  5. 09:28 True Self and Gesture - With Winnicott, he contrasts role-playing and melodrama with spontaneous gesture, suggesting that freedom lets deeper particularity come forward.
  6. 11:53 Already Enlightened - He quotes Bruce Tift on treating people as already enlightened and uses that line to reframe how we see ourselves and others.
  7. 13:27 Closing Questions - He ends by inviting reflection on how practice reveals both our own freedom and the uniqueness of others.
