Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Secrets, Shame and Transparency

AudioDharma 47:07 2014-02-20

Matthew Brensilver explores how Dharma practice can deepen inwardly while also broadening into more of life, making us less defended and more available to others. From there he turns to secrecy and shame, distinguishing privacy from concealment, and showing how hidden material can keep us stressed, isolated, and less transparent to ourselves. The talk draws on Foucault, Sam Harris, the Buddha’s teaching on hiri and ottappa, and Adam Phillips to frame honesty, vulnerability, and compassion as practical supports for a safer inner and social life.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Depth and breadth - He begins by describing practice as moving in two directions at once: deeper realization and a wider reach into ordinary life.
  2. 02:56 Becoming transparent - He develops the idea that Dharma can spread into more corners of experience, softening hidden places and making us safer for others.
  3. 05:09 Turning to secrets - He shifts from general practice to the subject of secrets, naming the stress of self-protection and the pull toward freedom and transparency.
  4. 07:57 Privacy and shame - He distinguishes secrecy from privacy and explores how moralism and shame can lead us to hide even from ourselves.
  5. 12:53 Honesty as a mirror - Using Sam Harris and a few playful examples, he argues that honesty reveals how we relate to reality and to one another.
  6. 16:26 Suppressing the hidden - Drawing on Daniel Wegner, he shows how trying to suppress thoughts or secrets tends to intensify preoccupation and anxiety.
  7. 23:06 Wholesome shame - He turns to the Buddha’s teaching on hiri and ottappa, distinguishing conscience from toxic shame and linking ethical discomfort to practice and behavioral change.
  8. 28:12 Kindness and vulnerability - He looks at the unproductive forms of shame, then emphasizes kindness, sangha, and compassion as ways to breathe around vulnerable places in the mind.
  9. 35:25 Questions on authenticity - In dialogue with the audience, he clarifies the relation between authenticity, self-inquiry, privacy, and transparency, ending with a short metta-style close.
