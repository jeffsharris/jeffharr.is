Reflections on Time in Dharma: Surrendering to Time, Healing Time, Not-Making Time (Retreat at Spirit Rock)

Dharma Seed 44:25 2025-10-20

What if the pressure to fill time is itself the problem? The talk begins with the sense that there is either too much time or not enough, and moves through boredom, grief, retreat structure, and the craving for “what’s next” toward a quieter question: how do we stop being pushed around by time and learn a wiser relationship to it? Along the way, the speaker uses references to Zadie Smith, Catherine Schultz, Shinzen Young, Bhikkhu Bodhi, and others to show how practice can slow experience down, reveal impermanence more directly, and soften the way memory and self get bound to the future.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening: time as the problem - Matthew begins by naming his own unease around time and the sense that practice arose as a response to it.
  2. 02:51 Busyness and boredom - He uses Zadie Smith’s lockdown reflections to explore the habit of filling time and the boredom that often drives it.
  3. 08:36 Grief widens the view - Catherine Schultz’s language about loss leads into a larger meditation on fragility, impermanence, and the emotional scale of life.
  4. 10:49 Retreat and the schedule - He turns to retreat life, showing how schedules and calendars can feel like attempts to control or buffer time.
  5. 15:17 Let time work - Recalling Shinzen Young, he reframes time as an ally that can soften grasping once mindfulness is established.
  6. 22:38 Impermanence in real time - He sharpens the teaching on anicca, describing not just endings but continuous arising and passing.
  7. 26:14 Presence and the past - Through T.S. Eliot and close phenomenological reflection, he explores how memory, fear, and the sense of self keep the present from feeling safe.
  8. 33:16 Ghosts into ancestors - He names healing as a process of blessing memory until it no longer hides instructions or secrets.
  9. 38:21 Beyond self and time - The talk closes by linking selfhood, prediction, and fear to the weight of time, then pointing toward a quieter refuge when the churning stops.
