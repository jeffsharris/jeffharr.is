Dharmette: Tranquility, View, Truth

AudioDharma 15:35 2026-02-04

If truth is tied to freedom, what state helps us actually see? The talk follows that question into the body: how mood, arousal, and balance shape perception, and why a settled mind may reveal more than a charged one. It then turns to tranquility and samadhi as a kind of quiet enoughness that narrows overwhelm, slows the world down, and makes clearer seeing possible without pretending everything is fine. From there, the speaker asks how to test whether that calmer view is really wise, and suggests trusting experience while refining the view.

Chapters

  1. 00:35 Truth and freedom - Matthew frames the talk with the Buddha’s teaching that truth and liberation belong together.
  2. 01:35 The body of knowing - He asks what state supports clear seeing and emphasizes that truth depends on the body, not just ideas.
  3. 02:37 Heart-mind investigation - The talk turns toward the intermingled mind-body complex and the need to examine how distress and ethics shape perception.
  4. 03:44 Mood shapes view - He describes mood as a kind of confirmation bias, where anger, ecstasy, and arousal change what the world seems to be.
  5. 06:02 Subtle pleasantness - Matthew lays out pleasantness and intensity as two dimensions and argues that subtle pleasantness is especially valued in Dharma.
  6. 07:47 The flood of life - He uses the image of sensory deluge and overwhelm to explain why concentration and tranquility feel relieving.
  7. 09:30 Samadhi and enoughness - The talk describes how stillness narrows time and possibility, reducing the world to one breath, one space, one love.
  8. 11:49 What tranquility sees - He says tranquility is not rose-colored; from that vantage point one sees dukkha and the folly of greed and hatred more clearly.
  9. 13:56 Testing the view - The talk closes by asking whether perceptions built on tranquility are wise, and whether they make more accurate predictions about happiness.
