Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Spirit Rock-Samadhi in the Context of Letting Go

Dharma Seed 48:44 2016-11-22

What if samadhi is not something to force, but what appears when grasping relaxes and experience becomes less frictive? The talk moves from the common idea that awakening will solve our problems into a quieter argument: settling the mind, making peace with the human condition, and learning to let go are what allow collectedness to arise. Along the way, it turns to the hindrances, the pull of wanting to be elsewhere, and the subtle ways clinging makes life more complicated than it needs to be. External references to William James, Suzuki Roshi, Shinzen Young, Wittgenstein, and a story about Joseph Goldstein help shape the arc from theory toward direct practice.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Reversing the Question - He begins by questioning the usual idea that enlightenment will solve our problems, and suggests that awakening may arise when there is less friction.
  2. 03:01 Samadhi and Letting Go - Matthew introduces samadhi as collectedness that comes through making peace with the human condition rather than forcing concentration.
  3. 07:23 Waiting for Nothing - He reflects on how practice can become a form of waiting, and invites the listener to consider living as if this moment were all that exists.
  4. 11:57 Beyond Ordinary Mind - A passage from William James helps him frame meditation as opening onto forms of consciousness that exceed everyday waking life.
  5. 16:55 The Uses of Samadhi - Using Shinzen Young and other examples, he argues that gathered attention is practical, healing, and deeply empowering without becoming a fetish.
  6. 24:01 Attachment and Confidence - He tells stories of getting attached to peak states, then turns to the way practice digestes the past and builds confidence and safety inside.
  7. 30:13 Letting Go of Reference Points - The talk broadens into ethics and orientation: living without regret, loosening the need to narrate experience, and softening around self-harshness.
  8. 37:34 Stillness Without Will - He explains why samadhi cannot be forced, using Ajahn Brahm and Wittgenstein to show how practice eventually releases control and even concepts.
  9. 44:35 What Is Not Yours - Matthew closes with the discipline of failing well and the Buddha's instruction to let go of everything that is not truly ours.
