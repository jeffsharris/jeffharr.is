Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Guided Meditation (Day 4): Emotion in the Body

AudioDharma 37:43 2016-04-07

What happens when emotion is treated not as a problem to solve, but as something to feel in the body? The talk begins by turning attention away from projects and plans toward the immediacy of experience, then guides a slow practice of resting with the body’s emotional signals—often felt in the face, throat, chest, and belly. It moves through noting, softening effort, and meeting pleasant, unpleasant, and neutral experience with relaxed, alert awareness. A brief reference to Shinzen Young’s line about suffering and freedom sharpens the teaching: the task is not to go elsewhere, but to stay with this life as it is appearing now.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Settling Into Life - The meditation opens by inviting a direct appreciation of immediate experience rather than chasing life in another time or place.
  2. 02:09 Opening the Heart - He encourages a gentle opening to what is here now, letting each moment become the whole of practice.
  3. 07:48 Suffering and Presence - A brief citation of Shinzen Young frames the human condition, then the talk returns to honoring this life exactly as it is.
  4. 14:40 Emotion in the Body - Emotion is described as a protective mechanism shaped by threat and opportunity, with both mental and bodily components.
  5. 18:35 Finding the Sensation - Attention is guided to the bodily fingerprints of anger, fear, and sadness, especially along the front axis of the body.
  6. 22:14 Resting With Change - The listener is invited to stay with the changing constellation of sensations and notice their moment-to-moment movement.
  7. 24:44 Relaxing Effort - Matthew points to unnecessary layers of effort that can be softened as one opens to emotional life.
  8. 28:52 Gentle Noting - He suggests simple noting when helpful, including naming emotion, intensity, or a vague sense of activity.
  9. 30:38 Equanimity and Sky - The practice settles into relaxed, alert awareness as the river of feeling passes through without needing to be obeyed, ending with the image of mind like sky.
