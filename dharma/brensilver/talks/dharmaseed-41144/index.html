Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Day 4: Metta Sit with Guidance (Retreat at Spirit Rock)

Dharma Seed 34:47 2017-05-26

How do we meet another person’s good fortune without immediately measuring ourselves against it? The talk begins with a simple story about a child’s fear of lions and turns toward mudita, sympathetic joy, as something that can arise naturally when the heart is less defended and less scarce. From there, the practice moves through phrases for a loved one, then turns carefully toward someone whose happiness feels threatening, using envy as a place to see self-clinging and soften around it. It ends by returning to tenderness for one’s own clinging and the relief of letting other people’s well-being mean no threat at all.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening into practice - Matthew settles the group into a guided sit and uses a story about his nephew to introduce the natural ease of sympathetic joy.
  2. 02:59 What mudita means - He names mudita as delighting in the well-being of others and as a fruit of practice when the heart is less defended or scarce.
  3. 04:35 Meeting envy - The talk turns to the comparing mind and the way other people’s happiness can trigger envy, defensiveness, and self-questioning.
  4. 07:11 Envy as teacher - A teaching from Ziggur Kangtrul Rinpoche is used to frame envy as a sign of egoic fragility and a wound to self-importance.
  5. 08:30 Settling the body - The group moves into formal practice, softening the body and preparing to work with the phrases of mudita.
  6. 12:59 Joy for a dear person - Listeners are asked to bring to mind someone they care about and repeat phrases wishing that person delight and continued good fortune.
  7. 19:51 Turning toward challenge - After a brief exchange, Matthew invites the practice to shift toward someone whose happiness feels threatening or provoking.
  8. 24:01 Working with comparison - The teaching emphasizes self-compassion, the threat felt by self-cherishing, and the possibility of extending mudita even when identification is active.
  9. 29:53 Opening the heart - The practice repeats the phrases for the difficult person and then returns to tenderness for one’s own clinging and vulnerability before closing.
