Dharmette: When Inspiritation to Practice is Lost

AudioDharma 15:53 2025-05-28

When inspiration to practice goes flat, the talk asks what is actually happening: is the path being asked to do something it cannot do, or has an old intention simply outlived itself? The speaker moves through tolerance for waning energy, the limits of technique, and the possibility that practice may need a new motive, a life move, or support from friendship, service, or another kind of wisdom. Near the end, a Zen warning about not wasting life and the Tibetan four thoughts turn the talk toward urgency, impermanence, and the defects of ordinary craving, before closing with the image of resting in confusion until a direction emerges.

Chapters

  1. 00:30 Making Room for Low Inspiration - He begins by normalizing waning inspiration and suggesting that the path includes cycles of comfort, loss, and renewed challenge.
  2. 02:43 Revisiting the Original Why - He invites listeners to examine what first drew them to practice and whether that intention is still alive or has been outgrown.
  3. 04:38 What Dharma Can’t Do - He warns against asking the Dharma to solve everything, including forms of suffering that may need therapy, medicine, service, or another lineage of wisdom.
  4. 06:59 When Action Is Missing - He turns to the way practice can stall when a needed life move is being avoided, and notes that sometimes one has to act before feeling ready.
  5. 08:44 Intrinsic Interest - He distinguishes skillful means from genuine interest, linking loss of inspiration to weakened investigation and rapt attention.
  6. 10:27 Love for Others - He suggests that practice can be reanimated by practicing for others, by letting its importance become palpable, and by drawing energy from spiritual friendship.
  7. 12:22 Urgency and the Four Thoughts - He briefly invokes a Zen warning not to waste life and then the Tibetan four thoughts that turn the mind toward Dharma as a counterweight to flatness.
  8. 13:56 Resting in Confusion - He closes with Ken McLeod’s account of resting in confusion until clarity and a direction slowly appear, without pretending the whole situation becomes clear.
