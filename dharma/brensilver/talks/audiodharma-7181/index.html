Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Morning instructions - Breath and Body

AudioDharma 51:37 2016-10-08

What if the first task is not to fix experience, but to meet it with steadiness, kindness, and a little less judgment? The talk begins by naming the roughness of early retreat—aches, fatigue, distraction—and then keeps returning to a simple instruction: rest attention on the breath, especially the exhale when the system feels overstimulated, and come back gently whenever the mind wanders. From there, it widens into a practice of inclusion, where sounds, thoughts, discomfort, and silence are all allowed to arise while the breath stays in the foreground; midway through, it turns to walking meditation as a formal, embodied continuation of the same mindfulness.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Opening the retreat - He introduces the morning instruction period and names a few attitudes to carry into retreat practice.
  2. 01:30 Discomfort and conditioning - He frames early-retreat pain, fatigue, fear, and other difficult states as part of the path rather than as mistakes.
  3. 03:31 Kindness and simplicity - He encourages blessing experience with awareness and kindness, then returns to the breath as the simple place where practice begins.
  4. 08:29 Working with the exhale - He suggests using the exhale, gravity, and the body’s settling as supports when the system feels overstimulated.
  5. 13:32 Breath counting practice - He offers a gentle breath-labeling practice, noting the beginning and end of each breath and returning whenever the mind wanders.
  6. 20:05 Including everything - He widens the field to sensations, sounds, thoughts, and images, while keeping the breath in the foreground and not making an enemy of experience.
  7. 26:05 Rilke and persistence - He warns against only trying to fix what is wrong, then uses Rilke to encourage steady practice and trust that no feeling is final.
  8. 31:36 Settling into silence - He invites a more precise resting with the breath and emphasizes surrendering into the present moment.
  9. 37:13 Noticing without judging - He describes the habit of evaluating practice by pleasantness, concentration, or energy, and encourages trusting the work done simply by sitting.
