Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

The What and How of Investigation

AudioDharma 13:54 2025-11-12

The talk begins with a familiar practice dilemma: when should investigation turn toward something, and when should it be left alone in the background? From there, it argues that the deepest move is not solving the mind’s questions but loosening the need to know, since sincerity matters more than a perfect algorithm for practice. The speaker then reframes investigation as an attentional practice—one that asks for patience, non-collection, and the willingness to let insight arrive without forcing a story too soon.

Chapters

  1. 00:16 The Question of Practice - Matthew opens with the common wish for a reliable rule about when to investigate and when to let experience rest.
  2. 01:25 Letting the Question Dissolve - He argues that many apparent forks in practice are illusory and that sincere practice matters more than having the perfect answer.
  3. 02:25 Answers and Letting Go - Investigation is contrasted with the mind's craving for certainty, and insight is described as a byproduct of cultivating mind rather than solving problems.
  4. 04:56 Sticky Thought - Using Kahneman and research on mind wandering, he describes how repetitive thought and psychological distress are linked, and why equanimity may mean not making an object of everything.
  5. 06:50 Attention, Not Answers - He defines investigation as an attentional practice and shows how the thirst for answers can be redirected into sustained, clear attention.
  6. 08:41 Relaxing the Need to Know - The talk turns toward unmotivated seeing, deep tolerance for not knowing, and the way new patterns appear only when grasping loosens.
  7. 10:57 Dhammavichaya - Matthew names dhammavichaya and explains it as magnifying and clarifying phenomena without turning them into a premature story.
  8. 13:27 Path and Goal - He ends by saying the path itself is the goal, leaving listeners with an image of openness and patient waiting.
