Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Reflections on Truth and Honesty

AudioDharma 41:21 2020-09-20

Truth is treated less as a final doctrine than as an honest way of looking. The talk moves from grief and the uneasy question of whether truth can free us, into a quieter claim: even if capital-T Truth is hard to pin down, we can still tell the difference between honesty and self-deception, and that matters for practice. From there it broadens into curiosity, the three marks, secrecy, shame, and lying, showing how much effort it takes to keep a false picture of ourselves in place. The talk closes by turning truthfulness into an ethical vow: to look more carefully, speak more transparently, and be less willing to defend what is merely conditioning.

Chapters

  1. 00:14 Beginning with grief - He opens by naming grief as a real part of this moment and links practice to a heart that can move more fluidly through heaviness and lightness.
  2. 02:37 Truth and liberation - A biblical line and classical Dharma framing lead into a question about whether truth frees us, and how ignorance gives rise to suffering.
  3. 05:56 From truth to honesty - He leans on Rorty and then pivots away from absolute Truth toward honesty, practical inquiry, and the courage to look closely at one’s life.
  4. 14:04 Curiosity as practice - Curiosity is presented as a non-transactional wish to look, one that can make practice feel alive and receptive rather than instrumental.
  5. 16:19 Delusion as default - Ajahn Chah and the Buddha’s teaching on delusion, the three characteristics, and evolutionary bias are used to show why clear seeing runs against our usual habits.
  6. 22:59 Refining the view - He emphasizes that practice is not doubting everything, but refining perception, stepping back from rationalization, and testing the stories we build about ourselves.
  7. 27:44 Secrets and shame - The talk turns to secrecy, borrowing from Freud, Wegner, and Bella DePaulo to show how hiding, lying, and impression management burden the self and entangle shame.
  8. 34:15 Transparency and freedom - Rilke’s image of unfolding gives way to a direct ethical argument: honesty helps free others and ourselves, supports humility, and closes with a vow to strive on in truthfulness.
