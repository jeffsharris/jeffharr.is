Dharmette: Reasons and causes

AudioDharma 13:49 2016-05-18

What if the story we tell about why we feel a certain way is not the same thing as the real cause? The talk starts with cause and effect as the backbone of practice, then turns to a psychiatric study on depression that complicates the easy link between reasons and causes. From there, it becomes a quieter inquiry into how much confidence we should place in our own explanations, and how that uncertainty can open into humility and wonder. The talk ends by bringing that question back to practice itself: how, exactly, does practice become a cause of less suffering and more well-being?

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening frame - He begins by framing the talk around cause and effect and the idea that understanding causality matters whether or not we think of ourselves as scientists.
  2. 00:42 Cause and effect in practice - Matthew links the Buddha’s teaching to the everyday work of noticing how the mind wanders and how we try to intervene in our experience.
  3. 02:14 Reasons versus causes - He introduces the distinction between the reasons we give for our feelings and the more objective question of what actually causes them.
  4. 04:32 The depression study - A psychiatric study by Ken Kendler is introduced as a way to test whether understandable reasons for depression line up with underlying risk and recurrence.
  5. 05:42 What the study shows - Matthew walks through the findings that understandable and apparently out-of-the-blue depression had similar risk profiles and similar later outcomes, complicating simple stories about causation.
  6. 09:44 Humility and wonder - He reflects on how this challenges self-knowledge and invites a more humble, even wonder-filled, relationship to our own lives.
  7. 11:38 Practice as inquiry - The talk turns back to Dharma practice as an honest experiment in learning how transformation happens and how to reduce suffering.
  8. 13:12 Closing thanks - He closes lightly, acknowledging the unusual topic and thanking the listeners for their practice.
