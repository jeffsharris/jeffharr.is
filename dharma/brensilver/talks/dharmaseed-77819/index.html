Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Cycling through All Four Divine Abodes

Dharma Seed 33:46 2023-05-10

The talk moves through the four divine abodes as a single practice of love, asking who or what most needs metta, compassion, sympathetic joy, or equanimity at each moment. It starts with the dead ends of hatred and apathy, then turns toward the heart’s capacity to take in suffering, rejoice in goodness, and meet what cannot be changed without clinging. Along the way, the speaker keeps returning to the question of how love can be steady enough to include grief, helplessness, and ordinary gratitude without closing down.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the cycle - Matthew introduces the idea of cycling through the four species of love and invites the listener to settle into posture and practice.
  2. 02:16 Metta and goodwill - He explains that mindfulness may not always generate love, but it can clear space for it, and asks who most needs loving-kindness now.
  3. 05:04 Receiving goodness - The practice of metta is broadened into noticing one’s own goodness and offering simple goodwill to self or another in ordinary terms.
  4. 10:18 Compassion for suffering - He turns to compassion, describing it as first feeling another’s suffering clearly and then pouring in love so the heart does not burn out.
  5. 14:56 When grief softens - Matthew speaks about unbearable absence of love and how a tender heart can move from grief and compassion into the possibility of joy.
  6. 19:09 Rejoicing in delight - He introduces mudita, including gratitude for one’s own good fortune, and describes rejoicing in another’s welfare without envy.
  7. 23:45 Equanimity and limits - The talk shifts to the question of helplessness and the end of our power, leading to a quiet love that can abide in gain and loss.
  8. 28:37 Refining the heart - Matthew says equanimity can remove clinging from compassion and opens into a vast, measureless mind that does not grasp or resist.
  9. 33:03 Closing reflection - He closes by saying Brahma-vihara practice can both steady the heart in love and draw what has not yet been loved into view.
