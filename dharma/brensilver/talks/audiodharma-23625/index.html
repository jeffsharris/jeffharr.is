Guided meditation in Style of Shinzen Young: Mental Images, Auditory Thinking and Emotion in the Body

AudioDharma 46:17 2025-08-16

How does a moment become suffering when sight, sound, feeling, and inner talk quietly harden into “me and my problems”? This guided meditation starts with settling the body, then moves through internal visual impressions, auditory thinking, and emotion in the body, showing how each sense gate can be noticed directly rather than followed. The practice keeps returning to a simple movement: hear thought as thought, feel feeling as feeling, and let experience unfold without taking it as a contract with the moment.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Settling the body - The practice begins by quieting together, easing the body, and finding a workable posture with some acceptance of discomfort.
  2. 02:27 Mindfulness and posture - The teacher names mindfulness as concentration, clarity, and equanimity, then invites a relaxed, upright embodiment.
  3. 05:31 Seeing mental images - Attention turns to the internal visual field, including fleeting images, visual thinking, and the sense of self carried in imagery.
  4. 11:58 Hearing inner thought - The practice shifts to auditory thinking, listening to verbal thought as sound rather than following its content.
  5. 17:33 Thought and orientation - The talk reflects on how language helps us track threats, opportunities, and time, and asks us to hear verbal thought without obeying it.
  6. 24:41 Feeling in the body - Matthew turns to emotion and affect as bodily sensation, noticing arousal, valence, craving, and clinging as changing physical patterns.
  7. 31:58 The three sense gates - Seeing, hearing, and feeling are gathered into one field of practice, with mindfulness meeting whatever appears and resting in blankness, silence, or peace when an object falls away.
  8. 37:34 Tracking the triad - A simple mental note of see, hear, feel is offered as support for moment-to-moment awareness across the three gates.
  9. 42:26 How suffering forms - The closing reflection investigates how suffering clusters around unnoticed sensory experience and how awareness can keep folding those patterns open.
