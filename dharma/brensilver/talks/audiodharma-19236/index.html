Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Intensity, Feeling, Thinking as Coping, Insight

AudioDharma 46:10 2023-10-31

Matthew Brensilver reflects on how retreat can make experience feel more intense rather than less, and how that intensity often gets managed through thinking, story, and explanation. He traces a quiet path through feeling, attention, equanimity, faith, and insight, suggesting that practice is often less about getting somewhere than about meeting what is already here without fighting it. Along the way he weaves in references to Ajahn Sucitto, the Buddha, Sharon Salzberg, Shinzen Young, and others to show how embodied feeling and nonconceptual awareness work together.

Chapters

  1. 00:03 Living dukkha - He opens with a novel line about living clichés and uses it to show how suffering feels singular, isolating, and hard to see clearly in the moment.
  2. 01:48 Retreat as intensification - Retreat is presented as a place where helplessness, energy, and intensity become vivid, even when the instructions are simple.
  3. 03:46 Energy and samadhi - He describes retreat as both stirring and tranquilizing energy, with samadhi deepening sensitivity, porosity, and the amplification of whatever attention highlights.
  4. 06:57 Naming the intensity - Matthew explores how the mind wants to explain strong feeling, how words can soothe or distort, and why practice means staying awake to phenomena without freezing experience into stories.
  5. 12:43 Feeling at the center - Drawing on Ajahn Sucitto, the Mula Sutta, and Lisa Feldman Barrett, he argues that feeling tone, arousal, and valence are central to both suffering and meaning.
  6. 18:08 Attention and soothing - He turns to the practical work of attention, showing how narrowing, widening, and softening the attentional spotlight can help metabolize intensity without suppressing it.
  7. 24:17 Do nothing practice - Using Shinzen Young's 'do nothing' instructions, he explores a less effortful way of being aware that reduces the strain of managing attention and interrupts egoic striving.
  8. 31:04 Equanimity and faith - He links deeper tranquility to faith and equanimity, describing them as a relaxation of compulsive preference and a willingness to let dukkha be seen without inner combat.
  9. 38:37 Stories and insight - The talk closes by returning to story: insight appears as a small, embodied story that arises from silence, then loosens and changes as practice continues.
