Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Equanimity 6

AudioDharma 21:31 2025-02-16

Matthew Brensilver describes equanimity as a real practice that first takes effort and then, at times, opens into a much deeper letting go. He distinguishes ordinary balance from the Mahasi-style stage of “equanimity with formations,” where experience feels less sticky, the three characteristics are vivid, and the sense of self loosens into space and groundlessness. Toward the end, he pauses on the risks of turning insight into a state to chase, and returns to the practical question of how this changes a life of commitments and compassion.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Cultivating Equanimity - He begins by distinguishing the gradual training of equanimity from a more ripened expression of it, emphasizing the work and energy the practice can require.
  2. 02:27 Beyond Forcing Letting Go - Matthew describes how practice can move from wrestling with pleasant and unpleasant toward a quieter state where letting go is no longer forced.
  3. 04:27 Mahasi Equanimity - He explains the Mahasi tradition’s account of equanimity with formations, where awareness seems to observe itself and the mind becomes extremely clear.
  4. 07:04 The Three Characteristics - The talk turns to how dukkha, impermanence, and not-self appear vividly in this phase, with experience feeling more neutral, coreless, and less personal.
  5. 10:11 Safety In Groundlessness - Matthew reflects on the relief and security found when nothing sticks to the mind, while also noting how this can resemble dissociation if it goes in the wrong direction.
  6. 13:14 Rumi And Letting Go - He uses Rumi’s language of living in the nowhere you come from to point toward equanimity, then cites the Buddha on safety being found in relinquishment.
  7. 15:09 Compassion And Nihilism - He considers how this kind of release can deepen compassion rather than nihilism, and how it changes one’s relation to the drama of suffering.
  8. 17:43 Not Chasing States - Drawing on Shinzen Young, Matthew warns against making spiritual practice into a quest for special experiences and says mature practice becomes less bound up with states.
  9. 20:31 A Dharma Life - He closes by admitting it is hard to trace exactly what causes freedom, then lands on the sense that practice becomes an ongoing Dharma life.
