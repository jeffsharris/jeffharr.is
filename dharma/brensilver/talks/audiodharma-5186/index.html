Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Metta: Cultivation, Concentration and Purification

AudioDharma 31:07 2014-10-23

What if metta is not mainly something to feel, but something that trains attention, purifies the heart, and slowly changes where the mind defaults? The talk moves from balancing wisdom and love to a threefold view of metta as cultivation, concentration, and purification, including the dry stretches where nothing tender appears and the practice still works. It then widens into an account of being seen—through an art performance, the human face, and a quoted teaching on not harming—so that kindness becomes tied to recognizing our own and others’ vulnerability and goodness.

Chapters

  1. 00:31 Wisdom and heart - Matthew opens by balancing insight and loving-kindness, suggesting that practice needs both wisdom and compassion rather than only one emphasis.
  2. 02:19 Why metta matters - He describes how metta can shape practice and character over time, drawing on the example of long-practicing teachers and the lasting effects of loving-kindness.
  3. 05:33 Defaulting to kindness - The talk turns to metta as a possible default state of mind, with the image of a mother cow and a warning not to measure practice only by dramatic warmth.
  4. 09:01 Three functions - Matthew names three ways metta works: cultivation, concentration, and purification, including times when the practice feels dry but still steadies the mind.
  5. 11:46 Purification and tears - He describes metta as opening the gates to sadness, grief, and relief, sharing his own experience of tears arising in practice and gradually softening.
  6. 15:40 Ringing the bell - Metta is compared to ringing a bell and listening for the body's response, with an emphasis on not expecting one particular inner sound.
  7. 18:32 Presence and being seen - Using Marina Abramović's The Artist Is Present, Matthew reflects on how sustained gaze and presence can evoke tears and make emotional life visible.
  8. 22:30 The face calls us - He brings in Stephen Batchelor and Emmanuel Levinas to deepen the reflection on the face as a call to responsiveness, empathy, and non-harming.
  9. 25:34 Self-regard and goodness - The closing section turns toward seeing one's own innocence and goodness, ending with the Buddha's teaching that self-love supports not harming others and a short sitting.
