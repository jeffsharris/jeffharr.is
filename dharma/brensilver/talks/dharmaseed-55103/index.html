Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Compulsive Thinking, Concentration & Equanimity

Dharma Seed 60:15 2019-03-08

What happens when the mind keeps reaching for control, even in meditation? This talk moves from the pressure of New York intensity and compulsive problem-solving into a careful look at worry, mind wandering, and the way thoughts claim too much authority. The speaker then turns toward concentration and equanimity as forms of letting go: not shutting experience down, but meeting it without interference so life can feel more settled, more poignant, and less driven by the need to optimize every moment.

Chapters

  1. 06:15 What experience becomes - Matthew begins by saying that our life is shaped by conscious experience and that gathered attention makes life feel richer.
  2. 10:54 Samadhi as letting go - He explains concentration as the fruit of release, and contrasts it with the restless habit of always trying to solve life.
  3. 18:51 Worry and modern life - The talk turns to worry, technology, and evolutionary vigilance, with references to Bhikkhu Bodhi and Borkovec supporting the argument.
  4. 28:27 A sheltered mind - Matthew describes samadhi as a temporary shelter from the storm and says a safe ethical community helps the mind settle.
  5. 33:52 Thought’s false gravity - He looks closely at mind wandering, self-evaluation, and the way thoughts seem more important than they are, drawing on Malia Mason, Jennifer Beer, and Daniel Kahneman.
  6. 40:03 Silence without defense - The discussion deepens into disorientation, autobiography, and equanimity as 'gazing upon without interference,' which keeps life poignant without making it melodramatic.
  7. 46:10 Pleasure, attachment, and restraint - Matthew warns against compulsively optimizing experience and tells Joseph Goldstein's story as a caution about attachment to blissful concentration states.
  8. 52:41 Enough, and waking up - He closes by reframing dana, quoting Suzuki Roshi, and using a leaf-and-stillness image to show that peace comes from non-interference rather than force.
  9. 59:10 Closing reflections - Matthew ends with a brief summary: letting go, equanimity, forgiveness, and making peace with the imperfection of being human.
