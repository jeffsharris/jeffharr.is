Matthew Brensilver, Sylvia Boorstein: Closing Session (Retreat at Spirit Rock)

Dharma Seed 1:51:03 2018-01-15

Beginning with the question of how to meet a hard public moment without turning away, the talk moves from quiet retreat silence into an explicit turn toward love, non-harming, and ethical responsibility. It draws on Martin Luther King Jr., Leonard Cohen, Norman Fischer, Nyanaponika, Thomas Merton, and Shantideva to show how practice is not escape but a way of living with suffering, aspiration, and uncertainty. The talk builds toward the image of the monk on the vine: precarious, held between loss and possibility, still finding the strawberry. It ends by widening that image into a communal dedication of merit, asking how retreat practice can become care for one another and the world.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Settling Into the Day - The session opens in shared silence and names the historical and emotional context of the day, moving gradually toward a deliberate orientation of the mind.
  2. 05:05 Love With Strength - Using Martin Luther King Jr. as a guide, the talk clarifies that the love being cultivated is not sentimentality but a strong, wise wish for non-harming that includes oneself.
  3. 13:38 Treaty and Ethical Grief - A Leonard Cohen lyric becomes a refrain for longing, weariness, and the wish to end conflict, leading into questions of responsibility, wealth, and what one owes others.
  4. 20:07 Refuge Becomes Practice - The teaching turns toward refuge, silence, and community, then into Norman Fischer’s Zen perspective that practice is not something added to life but something that gradually becomes life itself.
  5. 30:28 Zen Paradox and Freedom - A story about Bill Kwong Roshi and a closed zazen class illustrates the paradoxical logic of Zen and the release that comes when happiness is no longer tied to getting life to match desire.
  6. 36:58 Purpose in Daily Life - Nyanaponika’s teaching on clear comprehension of purpose leads into parenting, work, and the ordinary question of how to live so as not to cause harm, with compassion named as the answer to many questions.
  7. 53:30 Aspiration Without Control - Thomas Merton and Shantideva frame the balance between suffering and compassion, while the talk distinguishes wholesome aspiration from over-control and uses an astronaut metaphor for continual recalibration.
  8. 01:01:32 The Tiger and the Vine - A classic Zen story of a monk hanging over a gorge with a tiger below and a strawberry beside him becomes a way to contemplate precariousness, tenderness, and the sweetness still available in danger.
  9. 01:17:56 Dedication and Departure - The ending turns playful and ceremonial, describing a modern dedication of merit through standing in a circle, naming one another, and leaving with care, gratitude, and practical reminders for the road home.
