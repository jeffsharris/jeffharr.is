Dharmette: Moral Vandalism, Anger and Love

AudioDharma 16:04 2025-06-11

What happens when anger is not denied, but listened to for the movement it wants to make? The talk begins with a small somatic story about a meditation injury and a leg that wants to kick, then widens into a question about whether rage can be channeled without harm. From there it moves through the risks of anger, a Buddhist teaching on the “poisoned root and honeyed tip” of anger, and finally to Martin Luther King Jr.’s account of agape as active, redemptive goodwill. The result is a quiet but charged inquiry into how moral outrage might become courage and care rather than self-righteousness or collapse.

Chapters

  1. 00:09 Somatic starting point - A personal story about a bodyworker and a meditation injury frames anger as energy that may need movement rather than denial.
  2. 02:43 The taboo of anger - Matthew names the discomfort around anger in Dharma settings and considers its relation to fear, grief, and shame.
  3. 04:55 Danger and restraint - He cautions against anger’s destructive force while quoting the Samyutta Nikaya on the need to overcome it.
  4. 06:04 Wisdom inside anger - Matthew argues that anger is not the last word and suggests listening for the discernment, boundaries, and love hidden within it.
  5. 09:18 Moral outrage needs a path - He turns to present political harm and asks how the energy of no can be directed into something clean and useful.
  6. 10:51 King as model - Drawing on Jonathan Eig’s biography of Martin Luther King Jr., Matthew presents nonviolence as forceful, courageous action rather than passivity.
  7. 13:25 Agape, not sentiment - A long King quotation defines love as redemptive goodwill and helps Matthew land on a non-sentimental, active form of care.
  8. 15:28 Closing encouragement - He closes by inviting listeners to express what is wise in them and offers a brief blessing.
