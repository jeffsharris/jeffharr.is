Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Dharmette: Personality Disorder at the Individual and National Level

AudioDharma 13:51 2024-10-23

Matthew Brensilver uses the language of personality disorder to explore rigidity, defensiveness, comparison, and cruelty in self-view, then broadens that lens to American political culture. He speaks candidly about how such dynamics pull clinicians and ordinary people into counter-transference, frenzy, and moral confusion, while pointing toward non-clinging, wholesome action, spiritual friendship, and a longer view as ways to stay sane. The talk stays grounded in clinical observation but keeps returning to Dharma practice as a discipline of softening and clear-hearted response.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Opening With Candid Presence - He begins by greeting the audience plainly and naming that he is speaking from his own experience, not putting a polished Dharma bow on things.
  2. 00:36 Seeing Thick Suffering - A conversation with someone struggling with personality disorder leads him to reflect on layered suffering and the scale of clinical distress.
  3. 01:38 Ego Taken To Extremes - He connects personality disorder with the extreme and tragic end of egoic life, describing fragility as both problem and desperate solution.
  4. 03:08 Rigidity And Self-View - The talk turns to rigid identity, defensiveness, and the way self-view can lock people into strategies that no longer work.
  5. 05:19 Comparison And Cruelty - He explores how self-territory produces enemies and VIPs, comparison, envy, and a readiness for cruelty when self-image feels threatened.
  6. 07:10 Clinician In The Current - Using clinical experience, he describes how personality pathology can draw helpers into confusion, counter-transference, and a hard-to-escape emotional field.
  7. 09:03 From Patients To Politics - He moves from the consulting room to the national level, suggesting that the same greed, hatred, and delusion shape public life and political culture.
  8. 12:12 How To Stay Sane - He closes with practical counsel: do not cling, keep acting wholesomely, rely on spiritual friendship, and hold a long view on justice and love.
