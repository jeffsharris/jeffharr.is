Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Attention, Love, and Service

AudioDharma 40:27 2017-02-12

Matthew Brensilver explores how attention, love, and service are woven together in practice: we learn to notice the goodness already here, face the mind’s negativity bias, and open to the intensity of being human without flinching. He moves from mindfulness and equanimity into the question of how love becomes service when it loosens its grip on clinging, control, and self-importance. The talk closes with reflections on mortality, freedom, and the possibility of being radically available to what is needed.

Chapters

  1. 00:10 Goodness and Negativity - He begins by contrasting the mind’s tendency to fixate on what is wrong with the practice of deliberately noticing what is already wholesome.
  2. 03:34 It's Already Here - Using Joseph Goldstein’s phrase, he turns from striving toward learning to contact the goodness and support already present in life and community.
  3. 07:43 Shared Suffering - Marsha Linehan’s work and dedication are used to show how compassion, skillful means, and mutual responsibility can arise from recognizing shared vulnerability.
  4. 13:15 Attention as Love - He argues that awareness and love are not separate acts, but that paying attention itself can be understood as a form of love.
  5. 18:28 Seeing Human Life Clearly - The talk deepens into impermanence, the intensity of being human, and the longing for peace that quietly animates body and mind.
  6. 26:22 From Insight to Service - He links the noble truths to service, suggesting that clearer seeing frees energy and naturally turns the heart outward.
  7. 31:25 Love Without Clinging - Using Rilke and related reflections, he distinguishes love from possessiveness and shows how letting go can deepen care for self and others.
  8. 36:01 Mortality and Freedom - Mortality opens the heart, views are shaken off, and the talk moves toward a simple availability to what is needed.
  9. 38:46 Closing Reflection - He ends with a short period of silence and a three-part closing: to love what's already here, to transform ourselves, and to serve.
