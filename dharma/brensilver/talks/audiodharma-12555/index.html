Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Thursday Dharma Talk: Not-Self

AudioDharma 54:35 2020-12-10

Matthew Brensilver approaches not-self as a lived investigation rather than a philosophy to settle. He moves from the habit of making identity feel solid, through self-acceptance and the thinning of self-view, toward a more spacious kind of knowing that is still ethical and caring. Along the way he warns against spiritual bypassing, uses examples from research and contemporary teachers, and returns several times to the simple question of what clinging is doing in the moment.

Chapters

  1. 00:40 Why simplicity misleads - He begins by contrasting the appeal of simple models with the more nuanced reality of spiritual maturity and freedom.
  2. 02:54 Not-self as practice - He introduces anatta, names the reactive selfing it can trigger, and frames the talk as another period of practice rather than an argument.
  3. 06:22 Beyond views and fantasies - He warns against philosophical speculation, points back to suffering, and shows how clinging and habit obscure what is already here.
  4. 10:10 Misreading the teaching - Using examples of how we see others and ourselves, he clarifies that not-self does not cancel tenderness, conditioning, or dependence, and he cautions against spiritual bypassing.
  5. 16:59 Self-love and release - He traces a continuum from arrogance and self-hatred toward self-acceptance, forgiveness, and a softer relation to inner life.
  6. 21:53 Self-view and conceit - He distinguishes the grosser view of self from subtler self-making, drawing on comparison, shame, and egoic vigilance to show how fragile self-esteem is.
  7. 32:27 Narrative gravity - He turns to practice as a gradual softening of fixation, then uses Dennett’s center-of-gravity image to describe the self as a constructed center of narrative.
  8. 39:59 Fear and safety - He asks who we are without clinging, links self-fixation to fear and self-protection, and describes how safety in meditation can let the self thin out.
  9. 45:58 Empty center, open heart - He closes with spacious awareness, non-dwelling presence, and the reminder that the center can be empty while the heart remains full.
