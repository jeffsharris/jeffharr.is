Beyond Judging, Comparing and Fixing: Opening Session

AudioDharma 57:41 2025-05-31

What happens if a retreat begins by dropping the need to know how it will go? The talk frames the opening as an experiment in tolerating uncertainty, then turns toward three movements that support practice: attention that receives experience without force, renunciation of the endless pull toward “more,” and love that can stay steady without naivete. The second half shifts into the retreat container itself, with an explanation of the refuges and the five precepts, so the talk moves from orientation and reassurance into a shared ethical and ritual beginning.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Entering Not Knowing - The talk opens with the retreat as an adventure in uncertainty and the invitation to begin with reassurance rather than control.
  2. 02:28 Reassurance and Faith - Matthew recalls Michelle McDonald’s teaching that the first night of retreat is about reassurance and names faith in the many ways practice can unfold.
  3. 05:03 Attention as Prayer - He develops attention as the first facet of retreat, drawing on Simone Weil to show how simple, unmixed attention can be a form of prayer.
  4. 10:03 What Is Enough - Renunciation is presented as a direct answer to endless wanting, with Ajahn Sucitto and Albert King used to sharpen the question of enough.
  5. 15:33 Love in Hard Times - The talk turns to love as a necessity for meeting the intensity of human life, and to a less naive loving-kindness shaped by difficult conditions.
  6. 19:03 Group Sharing and Themes - Participants speak into the room, and the teachers listen closely to how judging, aversion, self-judgment, and the fear of being fundamentally wrong appear in practice.
  7. 26:34 Judging and Comparing - Dana reflects on the gift of arriving together, then names the retreat lineage and defines judging, comparing, and fixing as reactive habits rather than discernment.
  8. 38:31 Refuge and Precepts - The opening ritual is explained through the Buddha, Dharma, Sangha, and the five precepts, with emphasis on belonging, sincerity, and creating a safe practice field.
