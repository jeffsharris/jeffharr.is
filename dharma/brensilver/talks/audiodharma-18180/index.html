Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Guided Meditation: Deconstructing Sensory Experience

AudioDharma 30:32 2023-04-28

How do experience and selfhood loosen when seeing, hearing, and feeling are treated as separate events instead of one solid world? The talk first introduces a precise noting practice, then guides listeners through sensing visual thinking behind closed eyes, listening to the voice in the head without obeying it, and noticing emotional energy in the body as "feeling." It repeatedly returns to the idea that subtle shifts in attention can untangle the grip of identification, while also emphasizing surrender over the pressure to fix or improve practice.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Opening the method - Matthew frames the talk as a more technique-focused meditation and introduces the three-gates approach he learned from Shinzen Young.
  2. 01:43 Seeing, hearing, feeling - He distinguishes visual thinking, auditory thinking, and emotionally charged body sensation as three separate sensory events and explains why that discrimination matters.
  3. 05:50 Settling and surrendering - The guided practice begins with posture and relaxation, then shifts toward surrendering effort, perfectionism, and the subtle violence of self-improvement.
  4. 08:28 Noticing visual thought - Attention moves to images behind the closed eyes, the mental screen, and how visual thinking can soften into blankness and tranquility.
  5. 11:38 Listening to inner speech - He turns to auditory thinking, showing how the inner voice can be heard as sound rather than obeyed, and how identification with thought creates drama and then resolves.
  6. 17:25 Feeling in the body - The practice shifts to emotional circuits and affect, with a reminder that even subtle feeling tones are significant.
  7. 19:52 Opening to all three - He invites awareness of whichever of the three spheres is most prominent, noting that more than one may be present and that simple labels can suffice.
  8. 24:16 Cycling and untangling - The meditation cycles through seeing, hearing, and feeling again and again, allowing mindfulness and equanimity to loosen the tangle of selfing.
