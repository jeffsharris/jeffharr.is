Dharmette: Reflections on a RJ Davidson Quote

AudioDharma 15:47 2024-04-24

What if the real work of mindfulness is not to improve thought, but to become less captured by it? Drawing on a condensed account from R. J. Davidson and colleagues, the talk moves through sustained attention, meta-awareness, de-reification, non-reactivity, and the softening of self-story. Along the way, it treats impermanence as something the mind feels as risk, and shows how practice can loosen both the pull of thought and the grip of “me.”

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Opening the frame - Matthew introduces the talk as a reflection on interdisciplinary dialogue and the value of combining empirical care with contemplative insight.
  2. 01:03 Davidson's summary - He reads the condensed account of mindfulness training from Davidson and colleagues, naming the main capacities the quote describes.
  3. 03:08 Less mediated by thought - The talk turns from the quotation to Matthew's own framing of practice as moving toward a life less dominated by language and discursive thought.
  4. 04:19 Attention and distraction - He explains present-centered awareness, the meditation anchor, and the early signs of distraction as attention begins to fray.
  5. 06:40 Risk and re-entry - Using impermanence and a black-hole metaphor, Matthew describes why thought can feel necessary and how practice returns before absorption.
  6. 08:14 Seeing thought as thought - He develops de-reification as the shift from taking thoughts as reality to recognizing them as passing phenomena.
  7. 10:05 Equanimity and permission - The teaching moves into non-reactivity, with equanimity presented as a receptive, non-averse willingness to feel experience.
  8. 11:51 Self-story loosens - Matthew extends de-reification to self-concept, showing how the same process can weaken the grip of autobiographical thinking and self-judgment.
  9. 14:08 Less self, more life - He closes by saying the story of self becomes less sticky, leaving more room for life, and ends with a simple blessing.
