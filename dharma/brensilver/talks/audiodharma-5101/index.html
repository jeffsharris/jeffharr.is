Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Mudita

AudioDharma 40:23 2014-09-21

What happens when the heart stops treating another person’s good fortune as a threat? The talk moves from the ordinary claim that mindfulness gives us more choice into a close look at mudita, sympathetic joy, as a habit the mind can actually learn over time. Through a Maurice Sendak story, a passage from Sam Harris, and reflections on envy and jealousy, the speaker shows how comparison narrows the heart and how letting go of self-protection makes room for delight in others’ well-being. The talk ends with a quiet return to practice, suggesting that joy becomes steadier as the sense of territory loosens.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Practice and Momentum - Brensilver frames Dharma practice as a gradual reshaping of the mind, where repeated wholesome moments build momentum and change what the mind defaults to.
  2. 05:01 What Mudita Means - He introduces mudita as open-hearted delight in another's well-being, without a sense of encroachment or defense.
  3. 06:24 Sendak’s Eaten Card - A Maurice Sendak story illustrates sympathetic joy as delight so open that the gift is received without possessiveness.
  4. 09:59 Love Broadens Out - A passage from Sam Harris is used to show how love for one friend can open into a more universal and less territorial love.
  5. 13:36 Joy Can Be Trained - Brensilver turns to a canonical Buddhist statement and explains that mudita is cultivated by removing obstacles rather than forcing a smile.
  6. 18:36 Envy and Comparison - He distinguishes envy and jealousy, then traces how comparison and scarcity make another person's happiness feel threatening.
  7. 21:43 Evolution and Letting Go - A David Buss passage about jealousy's evolutionary logic helps him contrast biological fitness with the Buddhist path of letting go.
  8. 24:12 Self-View as Obstacle - Through examples and quotations from Zigar Kangtrul Rinpoche and Mathieu Ricard, he shows how envy exposes the wounds of ego and self-importance.
  9. 32:00 Less Territorial, More Free - The talk closes by linking insight into the First Noble Truth with a lighter, less possessive heart, ending in a short sit and a blessing for shared goodness.
