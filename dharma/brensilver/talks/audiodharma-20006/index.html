Dharmette: Faith in Letting Go (5 of 5)

AudioDharma 14:47 2024-03-15

How do you let go when letting go feels like risk, vulnerability, even neglect? The talk moves through that tension by arguing that Dharma practice asks for courage: to stop over-reading experience, to release familiar reference points, and to trust a descent into not-knowing. Drawing on William James, Nisargadatta Maharaj, and Ajahn Sucitto, it frames awakening as a gradual relinquishment of the personal world in favor of a quieter kind of power and dignity.

Chapters

  1. 00:09 James and tragic depth - Matthew opens with William James and a grim quote from James's father to frame Dharma as a response to the seriousness of life and suffering.
  2. 02:13 What the path offers - He asks what is actually on offer in practice and argues that meeting life with dignity requires training, not just ideas.
  3. 03:31 Trying something new - Using Akena's line about trying something new, he emphasizes that practice requires courage to leave familiar habits behind.
  4. 04:54 Risk of letting go - He describes how putting down thought and problem-solving can feel like neglect or vulnerability, which is why we hold on in meditation and life.
  5. 06:47 Power in helplessness - Matthew turns to the counterintuitive claim that real power and dignity can arise from opening to helplessness and the truth of dukkha.
  6. 07:55 Effective suffering - He distinguishes practice suffering that loosens suffering from the kind that compounds it, saying this requires trust and faith.
  7. 09:08 The rope of release - With Ajahn Sucitto's image of lowering oneself down a rope, he describes awakening as gradual relinquishment into the unconditioned.
  8. 11:31 Fewer signposts - He explores the loss of preferences, self-stories, and other familiar markers as the mind relaxes into a wider, less defended awareness.
  9. 13:09 Faith and gratitude - The talk closes by pointing to moments when awareness matters more than personal problems, and to the gratitude that can follow when the Dharma is inherited and shared.
