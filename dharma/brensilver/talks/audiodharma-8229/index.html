Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Dharmette

AudioDharma 12:44 2017-09-20

The talk begins with a familiar Dharma tension: practice can sound full of paradox, as if letting go and striving, self-cultivation and selflessness, all point in opposite directions. From there, the speaker uses a New York Times essay on “living in the present” to sort out a distinction between activities that aim at an endpoint and those that are complete in the doing itself. That thread leads into meditation, where Zen non-striving and insight-language about progress and awakening are set side by side, but the emphasis lands on how practice is actually lived moment by moment.

Chapters

  1. 00:15 Dharma and paradox - Matthew opens by naming the seeming contradictions in Dharma language around effort, non-doing, self, and selflessness.
  2. 02:08 One world, not two - He argues that many paradoxes are really matters of imprecision, not separate realities.
  3. 03:48 A present-moment essay - He introduces a New York Times op-ed that questions slogans like “live in the present” and sets up the telic/atelic distinction.
  4. 04:45 Telic and atelic - Using Aristotle and linguistic terms, he explains activities that aim at endpoints and those that are fulfilled in the doing.
  5. 06:54 What present means - He clarifies that living in the present does not dismiss purposeful work, but warns against valuing only projects and outcomes.
  6. 08:27 Practice and attention - Matthew connects the philosophy back to Dharma practice, noting that mindful attention can be brought to both goal-directed and non-goal-directed activity.
  7. 10:07 Meditation question - He asks whether meditation itself is telic or atelic and introduces Zen as a non-striving counterpoint.
  8. 11:16 Here, not elsewhere - He balances Zen and insight traditions, then lands on the claim that whatever practice yields, the way there is to be fully here.
