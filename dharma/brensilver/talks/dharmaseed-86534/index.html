Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Relaxing the Body and Mind

Dharma Seed 45:20 2024-09-27

What does it mean to truly relax the body without trying to make experience pleasant? The talk begins with simple instructions to soften the face, jaw, belly, shoulders, and attention itself, then turns to the subtler strain of the mind’s habit of acquiring, defending, and managing experience. From there, it opens into a fuller way of working with breath, body, and walking practice, including how to meet despair or restlessness with more space rather than more effort.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Begin with release - He starts with a simple invitation to relax whatever can be relaxed, beginning in the face, jaw, and other obvious points of tension.
  2. 01:01 Spine, shoulders, belly - The instruction expands through the spine, shoulders, and belly, using the body’s shape and contact with the earth to support softening.
  3. 03:21 Relaxing is not pleasant - He clarifies that relaxation is not the same as pleasantness, and that it means letting go of efforts to engineer experience.
  4. 05:36 The mind at ease - The teaching turns to the mind itself, naming the strain of acquisitiveness and the way meditation can become another anxious project.
  5. 10:08 Breath received - In a more relaxed field, the breath is presented less as an object to control and more as a tide that arrives and is received with delight.
  6. 14:54 Whole-body breathing - He guides attention through the front and back of the body, showing breath as a whole-body process rather than a narrow focus.
  7. 24:49 Notice the meditating mind - The practice widens to observe the stance of the meditating mind itself, including greed, aversion, agitation, and compression.
  8. 26:11 Walking practice and despair - The talk shifts into walking meditation, where discomfort, sadness, and the need for more space become central as he reflects on the practice period.
  9. 37:51 Space, sangha, awareness - He offers concrete help for walking practice, tells a Jack Kornfield story about standing still with difficult feelings, and closes by emphasizing care for each other and awareness in the shared container.
