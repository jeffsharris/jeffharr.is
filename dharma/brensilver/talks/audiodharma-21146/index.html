Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Thursday Guided Meditation

AudioDharma 41:27 2024-08-29

This guided meditation begins by checking for fear, bracing, and the quiet apprehension that can show up at the start of practice, then meets whatever is there with kindness rather than pressure. From there, the talk settles into breath as an anchor: noticing where breathing is clearest, feeling how it can soothe agitation and enliven numbness, and letting attention widen through the whole body. It also names the mind’s innocent habit of moving into memory and anticipation, and invites a softer relation to effort, curiosity, and the pull to control. Near the end, the emphasis turns toward surrendering into the present and letting scattered intentions line up through release.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Arriving with fear - The meditation begins by noticing fear, bracing, and the unease that can come with starting practice.
  2. 02:56 Common practice difficulties - Restlessness, sleepiness, doubt, and aversion are named as familiar conditions rather than signs of failure, with a brief reference to the Buddha.
  3. 04:37 Finding the breath - Attention is gathered on the breath at a chosen point in the body, with fuller breathing used to settle and clarify.
  4. 09:13 Breath through the body - The breath is explored as something that can soothe agitation, enliven numbness, and be felt through the whole body rather than only at one spot.
  5. 14:06 Willingness and curiosity - The instruction shifts from technique toward an attitude of willingness, curiosity, and interest in what attention and awareness are doing.
  6. 21:51 Grounded openness - The body is sensed as open yet grounded, with gravity and the belly becoming a place of stability.
  7. 28:02 Memory and anticipation - The mind’s movement into memory and future planning is described as innocent, along with the habit of using thought to stabilize experience.
  8. 37:36 Letting intentions align - The closing reflection notices background intentions, control, and aversion, then suggests that when we let go, the directions can come into alignment.
