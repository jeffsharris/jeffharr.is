Balance on the Path: Awakening and Cultivation

AudioDharma 43:50 2014-09-28

What happens when practice is pulled toward sudden awakening on one side and gradual cultivation on the other? The talk moves through that tension carefully, showing how an exclusive focus on enlightenment can turn into spiritual materialism, while a purely gradual approach can miss the radical possibilities of the path. Along the way, the speaker uses Gil Fronsdal’s “from A to A” framing, quotes Trungpa on ego, Leonard Cohen on emptiness not making a burden light, and Nisargadatta on freedom as a way of rethinking what practice actually changes. In the end, the two sides of the path are said to converge in harmlessness, service, and love.

Chapters

  1. 00:14 Practice Leaves Nothing Out - Matthew opens by describing what drew him to Buddhism: a path that includes the whole of life rather than leaving any part behind.
  2. 02:31 Two Sides of Change - He introduces the contrast between sudden awakening and gradual cultivation and explains why he thinks both are central forms of well-being.
  3. 04:53 The Middle Path - The talk turns to balance: acceptance and change, compassion and equanimity, wisdom and love, with a warning against overemphasizing one side of practice.
  4. 10:34 Spiritual Materialism - Using Trungpa’s language, Matthew describes how even awakening can be taken up by ego, turning practice into striving, clinging, and self-concern.
  5. 16:41 What Insight Does Not Buy - He reflects on the limits of insight experiences, including the painful fact that deep realization can still coexist with harmful behavior.
  6. 21:38 Emptiness And Longing - The talk shifts to the cultivation side, with Leonard Cohen’s poem and an exploration of dissatisfaction, longing, and why ordinary pleasure does not fully quiet the heart.
  7. 27:21 Freedom As Experience - Matthew cites Nisargadatta, then reframes the passage as a phenomenological description of freedom rather than a metaphysical claim.
  8. 29:28 Harmlessness And Service - In Q&A, he says the two paths converge in harmlessness, which includes not only refraining from harm but also a life of service, giving, and love.
