Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Equanimity (5 of 5):

AudioDharma 15:26 2021-08-13

What does equanimity have to do with indifference, cynicism, or saying that nothing matters? The talk begins by pushing back on those confusions and instead treats equanimity as a way of meeting what has already arisen without interference, so compassion can stay steady rather than collapse into overwhelm or shutdown. From there it moves through the tension between rest and action, showing how a rested heart can love more deeply, act more strongly, and then rest again.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Opening reflection - Brensilver introduces the final talk in the equanimity series and begins with a light, informal opening that leads into the main theme.
  2. 01:20 Not cynicism - He uses a bumper sticker and then directly addresses the common confusion between equanimity, cynicism, apathy, and passivity.
  3. 03:04 What equanimity is - Drawing on Shinzen Young, he frames equanimity as non-interference with experience and explains why meditation instructions should not be flattened into general life rules.
  4. 05:32 Heart with force - He describes matured equanimity as something that strengthens commitment and allows life to become more poignant rather than more melodramatic.
  5. 06:53 Middle path - Using the Buddha's concern about nihilism, he names two distortions to avoid: that everything means everything, or that nothing means anything.
  6. 08:46 Compassion purified - He shows how equanimity purifies compassion, making care less compulsive and more morally forceful, while acknowledging that the heart also needs rest.
  7. 12:04 Rest and renew - He returns to the cycle of opening, resting, and opening again, then broadens the view with a short Parfit quotation about future lives and reasons for gratitude.
  8. 14:23 Closing thanks - The talk ends with thanks to the group and a gentle wish for well-being.
