Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Sunday Dharma Talk

AudioDharma 49:23 2024-09-01

Is the Eightfold Path something to be followed, something to become, or something that only makes sense after the view shifts? The talk moves from the image of an ancient road to a steady unfolding of practice, pausing over Krishnamurti’s warning about rigid paths, a friend’s simple obedience to a teacher, and the reminder that an experience is not yet a path. From there it weaves wisdom, intention, ethics, and meditation into a single training of body and mind, asking how delusion is recognized, how ambivalence is softened, and how love and non-harming grow out of understanding suffering more clearly.

Chapters

  1. 00:30 Ancient path - Matthew begins with the Buddha’s image of rediscovering an ancient road and names the Eightfold Path as the teaching’s core direction.
  2. 01:51 Life’s hidden currents - He reflects on the mystery of a life’s unfolding, the limits of self-narrative, and why the Dharma feels like one current among many unseen influences.
  3. 06:46 Path and freedom - Krishnamurti’s warning about organized truth leads into a careful counterpoint: Dharma needs skillful means, not rigid procedure, but also not vagueness.
  4. 09:19 Trusting the teaching - A brief story about a friend who simply followed her teacher’s instruction becomes an example of how practice can unfold through ordinary trust.
  5. 11:25 Experience is not path - Using Joseph Goldstein’s response to drug-induced insight, Matthew distinguishes a powerful experience from the gradual context that makes freedom durable.
  6. 16:25 Wisdom and illusion - A Rumi image of mistaken captivity opens into wise view, delusion, and the way mindfulness makes hidden assumptions visible.
  7. 23:15 Refining view - He emphasizes that awareness alone is not enough: mindfulness is for understanding, and wisdom helps us make better predictions about happiness and suffering.
  8. 29:14 Ambivalence and aspiration - The talk turns to dukkha as a kind of ambivalence, the limits of perfectionism and fear, and the task of aligning aspiration with what is actually skillful.
  9. 34:18 Training the body - Matthew explains wise intention, renunciation, and effort as bodily training over time, using Mary Oliver to point toward new capacities learned through practice.
