Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Dharmette: Craving>Suffering, Suffering>Craving

AudioDharma 15:30 2024-07-31

Matthew Brensilver uses a listener question about Goenka to explore craving not just as a cause of suffering, but as something that suffering itself can call forth. He moves from Buddhist causality to Stephen Batchelor’s reversal, then brings in George Koob’s addiction research to show how craving, disappointment, and negative affect can reinforce one another. The talk ends with practical ways of working with craving: noticing its pain directly, renouncing for a time, and discovering that less may actually be enough.

Chapters

  1. 00:30 Opening question - Matthew introduces a question attributed to S. N. Goenka and uses it to frame the talk around craving as a condition, not just an object.
  2. 01:31 Dharma as causality - He turns to the Buddha’s teaching on cause and effect, placing suffering within a wider web of conditions and responses.
  3. 03:06 Suffering as cause - The talk shows how suffering can lead either to insight and purification or to confusion and further harm.
  4. 03:50 The reverse loop - Matthew highlights the usual formula that craving leads to suffering, then adds the reverse: suffering can also generate craving from within.
  5. 05:26 Craving from within - He explains craving as a tension and a response to not having equanimity, emphasizing that the seeds lie inside rather than in the object itself.
  6. 07:14 What we really want - The talk gathers around the wish to be free of craving itself, with a wry look at how often we try to satisfy that wish through more acquisition.
  7. 08:36 Addiction parallel - Matthew uses George Koob’s model of addiction and the anti-reward system to show a scientific analogue of the craving cycle.
  8. 11:47 Working with craving - He offers a few practical responses: seeing disappointment clearly, renouncing for a while, and staying present with intense feeling without acting on it.
  9. 14:09 Enough already - The talk closes with the possibility that greater fulfillment makes us feel less incomplete and that craving becomes unnecessary.
