Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Guided Meditation: Settling, Body, Breath

AudioDharma 56:26 2018-03-19

Matthew Brensilver offers a quiet guided meditation that moves from settling the body to resting with the breath, then into walking practice and the continuity of mindfulness through ordinary movement. He repeatedly invites a softer relationship to experience: relax the face and belly, let the body be dignified, and stop needing to sort every moment into right or wrong. The talk leans on practical instructions, with a few larger reflections on how practice can include boredom, uncertainty, and the mind’s habit of trying to protect itself.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Settling the mind - He begins by loosening fixed ideas about how meditation should go and reframes practice as something that can nourish goodness in many different conditions.
  2. 03:22 Modest responsibilities - The talk emphasizes that simply showing up with wholesome intention is enough, even when the mind is busy or concentration feels uneven.
  3. 04:47 Relax the face - Matthew guides a gradual softening through the eyes, jaw, shoulders, and belly, treating tension as a kind of bracing against the moment.
  4. 11:09 Upright and soft - The posture becomes the central instruction: spine lifted, lower body surrendered to gravity, body received as sensation in awareness.
  5. 19:06 Making peace with imperfection - He reflects on the human condition as something not to be fully sorted out, inviting a truce with difficulty before turning back to the breath.
  6. 24:54 The breath at close range - The guidance narrows to careful attention to the beginning and ending of inhale and exhale, while also noting how the mind protects itself by thinking ahead or revisiting the past.
  7. 40:04 Retreat as cooking - A retreat metaphor emerges: practice is like cooking one's life, and progress comes less from checking constantly than from steady surrender to the schedule and rhythm.
  8. 50:31 Walking and continuity - The final section turns to walking meditation, where attention shifts to feet, movement, and the visual field, ending with an invitation to carry this continuity into the rest of the retreat.
