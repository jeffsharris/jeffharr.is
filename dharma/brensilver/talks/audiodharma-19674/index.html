Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Not-self and self expression

AudioDharma 48:14 2024-01-26

What happens when the mask we wear to be someone starts to harden into a prison? The talk follows that tension from social performance and shame into a gentler reading of not-self: not as becoming nobody, but as loosening clinging so personality can be lived more freely. Along the way, it links inner rigidity to defensiveness and harm, then turns toward a quieter kind of self-acceptance that feels more real, more embodied, and more available for relationship.

Chapters

  1. 01:02 Masks and vulnerability - He opens with a novel excerpt about taking off masks and uses it to frame self-protection as shared human vulnerability.
  2. 02:52 Self-knowledge, not fixation - The talk shifts to the difference between knowing oneself and freezing the self into a finished thing, with performance and social presentation as a costly strain.
  3. 07:04 When masks harden - He develops the social and psychological consequences of self-presentation, including the idea that a mask can become a face and obscure deeper pain.
  4. 12:51 Self-view and relationship - Brensilver describes how self-fixation creates tension, burdening relationships, and connects inner work to becoming safe and non-harming for others.
  5. 17:34 Not being nobody - He warns against turning not-self into spiritual bypassing, and instead presents Dharma practice as flexibility rather than erasure.
  6. 22:18 Shame and innocence - This section treats anatta as shame-freeness, grounded in dependent origination and an appreciation of conditioning, wounds, and longing.
  7. 26:21 Interpersonal selfing - He shows how the self is forged and sustained through shame, judgment, and projection, then uses Ajahn Sucitto to link self-view with domination and coercion.
  8. 34:51 Freedom feels real - The talk turns toward the way not-self can make personality more available, more unselfconscious, and more alive, drawing on Winnicott and Maggie Nelson.
  9. 42:59 Seeing through and resting - He closes by returning to the retreat context: noticing self-clinging in practice, seeing its emptiness, and resting in a quieter willingness to explore.
