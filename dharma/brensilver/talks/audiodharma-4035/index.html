Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

The Non-Paradox of Self and Not Self

AudioDharma 43:20 2013-04-21

If self is not something to defend, what is actually being protected? The talk begins by setting self-love and self-forgetting side by side, then moves through the worry that they must be in conflict, before arguing that kindness, cultivation, and insight into not-self can support one another. It stays practical rather than philosophical, using the Buddha’s silence on speculative questions, a psychologist’s caution about ego language, and a series of images about retreat, memory, and the mind’s “home base” to show how practice loosens identification and makes inner life feel safer and less burdened.

Chapters

  1. 00:54 Two Path Tasks - He introduces the paired tasks of cultivating goodness and letting go of anything taken as I or mine.
  2. 02:41 Self-Clinging and Kindness - Matthew distinguishes harmful self-clinging from self-love and argues that kindness toward oneself supports insight.
  3. 07:05 Beyond Paradox - He explains why the subject feels paradoxical, then reframes self and not-self as experiences rather than metaphysical claims.
  4. 10:31 Buddha and Pragmatism - Using Vacchagotta’s question and Than Geoff’s comment, he sets aside speculative answers in favor of the end of suffering.
  5. 13:31 Ego and Development - A quote from Jack Engler helps separate psychological growth from spiritual language that turns ego into an enemy.
  6. 16:51 Mindfulness as Healing - He describes closing the eyes, meeting whatever arises, and letting practice digest memory, pain, and family history.
  7. 22:58 Suffering in the Self - Drawing on the Buddha and his own retreat stories, Matthew shows how self-clinging aims at safety, status, and feeling good.
  8. 27:47 Rumi and Sumedho - Rumi’s poem and Ajahn Sumedho’s line about personality and enlightenment shift the talk toward a freer stance.
  9. 30:46 Loving It to Death - He turns to subtle selfing, the sense of a home base of me, and closes with Norman Fisher on how practice quietly remakes a life.
