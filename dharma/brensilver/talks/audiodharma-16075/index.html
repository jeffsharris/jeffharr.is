Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Renunciation and Enjoying Pleasure

AudioDharma 13:36 2022-04-12

The talk asks whether Dharma is something to tack onto a comfortable life, or something that asks for real relinquishment. Starting from a William James quote about habit and training the nervous system, the speaker argues that renunciation is not grim self-denial but a way of loosening craving so pleasure can be felt without compulsivity. The middle of the talk turns on a careful distinction between craving and desire, and on the idea that equanimity with pleasure may look like uncomplicated delight rather than defensive caution.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Habit and training - Matthew opens with William James to frame Dharma as a disciplined shaping of habit and nervous-system training.
  2. 01:11 Renunciation and resistance - He names the tension between wanting the Dharma and wanting to keep one's ordinary life, pleasures, and routines intact.
  3. 03:12 A central practice - A brief reference to Zadig Amaro leads into his claim that renunciation is historically central, not a peripheral extra, in Buddhist practice.
  4. 04:40 Letting go eases fear - He describes how relinquishment can reduce the sense of inner lack and make us less afraid of not getting what we want.
  5. 05:31 Pleasure without defense - Matthew turns to the suspicion many practitioners carry around pleasure, and suggests the path should enliven rather than flatten us.
  6. 07:32 Craving and desire - He distinguishes bottomless craving from satisfiable desire and says practice can dismantle the first without squashing the second.
  7. 09:11 Equanimity with pleasure - The talk widens equanimity to include pleasure, describing enjoyment that is intense, uncomplicated, and free of compulsivity.
  8. 11:00 De-shaming and freedom - He closes by linking Dharma practice with de-shaming both pain and pleasure, and with loosening enough to enjoy deeply and meet life's difficulties.
