Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Living Alongside Questions (Retreat at Spirit Rock)

Dharma Seed 47:43 2025-10-23

What if practice means learning to live without freezing into final answers? The talk moves from impermanence and the mind’s hunger for certainty into a closer look at how views harden into greed, aversion, and fear, then opens into two recurring koans: did I love well, and why do I keep doing this. From there it becomes an extended inquiry into love, understanding, equanimity, and the stubborn patterns that keep reappearing even in long-term practice.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Settling into the room - He begins with a loose, humorous opening before turning the attention toward relaxation and the basic human tendency to settle in and assume continuity.
  2. 01:34 The illusion of permanence - Using the image of a river and a psychological study on predicted change, he shows how people underestimate how much they themselves will become different.
  3. 05:15 Impermanence and uncertainty - He frames anicca as central to the Dharma and argues that uncertainty is not a problem to solve but a condition to meet honestly.
  4. 07:30 Views and certainty - He warns that attachment to views can channel greed, aversion, and fear, and that certainty can make harmful action feel justified.
  5. 10:11 A life with questions - An Oscar Wilde line and a reflection on mistakes lead into the idea that mature practice is learning to live openly without demanding final answers.
  6. 12:29 Two koans of practice - Drawing on David Whyte and Sylvia Boorstein, he names two guiding questions: how to love, and why we keep repeating ourselves.
  7. 16:45 Love as understanding - He develops love as a widening moral and relational practice, emphasizing understanding, freedom, and the way compassion can become controlling when it cannot see the other clearly.
  8. 22:45 The circle of concern - The talk widens from close relationships to the growth of ethical life, the pain of self-deception, and the fact that love eventually meets helplessness and needs equanimity.
  9. 27:22 Compassion and equanimity - He explores the balance between caring and surrender, then turns to the depth dimension of practice: the dense habits, griefs, and repeated knots that return over time.
