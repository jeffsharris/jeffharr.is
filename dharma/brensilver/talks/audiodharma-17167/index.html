Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Restlessness, Worry & Compassion

AudioDharma 55:16 2022-10-27

Restlessness is treated here as more than a nuisance: it’s a forward-leaning, predictive energy that keeps trying to manage the future and turn the present into a test. The talk moves from ordinary worry and mental “false alarms” into Buddhist themes of anicca, dukkha, and bhava tanha, then slowly shifts toward what actually softens the mind: stopping, settling, and learning to stay. Along the way, it emphasizes that making peace with uncertainty takes compassion, and it closes by returning to a simple image from Ajahn Chah: if the cup is already broken, perhaps it can be enjoyed now.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Oatmeal and worry - Brensilver begins with a comic meditation on oatmeal as a window into his own restless mind.
  2. 02:29 Restlessness as the preface - He names restlessness and worry as the true topic and argues that practice often means stopping what we keep adding to the moment.
  3. 06:15 Freud and uncertainty - Using Freud as a bridge, he describes anxiety as a clue to mental life and restlessness as forward-leaning energy tied to samsara and incompletion.
  4. 13:08 Worry as control - He explores fear, prediction, and the mind’s attempt to govern uncertainty, linking this to the craving to become and the settling power of tranquility.
  5. 22:05 Roots, safety, and false alarms - With Ajahn Sucitto and a pain analogy, he shifts from getting things right to becoming rooted, distinguishing real danger from the mind’s false alarms.
  6. 27:02 Present-moment practice - A cited scientist’s account of meditation leads into his picture of practice as pruning counterfactual thinking and learning to stay with the present.
  7. 33:50 Compassion for groundlessness - Drawing on Pema Chödrön, he says impermanence has to be grieved before it becomes refuge, and that compassion is what lets the heart soften.
  8. 39:50 Regret and forgiveness - He turns to regret, accountability, and forgiveness, showing how to meet harm without building a heavier self-story around it.
  9. 49:23 The broken cup - He closes with Ajahn Chah’s broken cup teaching, using impermanence to clarify gratitude, love, and how we choose to spend what remains.
