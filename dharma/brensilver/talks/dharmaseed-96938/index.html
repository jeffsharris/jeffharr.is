Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

The Why of Practice: Three Possible Answers (Retreat at Spirit Rock)

Dharma Seed 42:27 2026-05-01

Matthew Brensilver opens with a skeptical look at meditation research and uses it to suggest that discomfort on retreat is not a sign that something has gone wrong. From there, he turns to a central question: not just how to practice, but why, and offers three possible answers—appreciation, improvement, and transcendence. The talk moves between personal reflection, Buddhist teaching, and a few literary and philosophical references, gradually narrowing toward trust, relinquishment, and the quiet sense that practice is about meeting the human condition directly.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Research and retreat discomfort - Matthew uses a meditation study and the reality of unpleasant retreat experiences to set a grounded tone about what practice is and is not for.
  2. 02:06 Why we practice - He shifts from the question of how to the deeper question of why, then uses Nicole Krauss and a film reference to describe Dharma as a wordless recognition and a kind of homecoming.
  3. 06:55 Dukkha and no escape - The talk broadens into impermanence, finitude, and the fact that suffering cannot be sidestepped, making courage and patience central to the path.
  4. 12:06 Three possible whys - Matthew names Shinzen Young's threefold frame—appreciation, improvement, and transcendence—and begins unpacking appreciation through pleasure, gratitude, and attentive contact with the world.
  5. 20:04 Acceptance and growth - He quotes Kōun Chino Roshi and Suzuki Roshi to hold self-acceptance together with the wish to change, then turns to inherited patterns and the work of transforming 'ghosts into ancestors.'
  6. 26:02 Care in the world - The middle of the talk describes how mindfulness reveals clinging, softens greed and hatred, and makes even small gestures part of a wider web of care.
  7. 31:34 Letting the world go - Matthew develops the third why—transcendence—through Ajahn Chah, the Buddha, and the Bhagavad Gita, pointing toward relinquishment, trust, and a more intimate view of consciousness.
  8. 39:22 Closing reflections - He closes by returning to the three whys as a living cycle and leaves listeners with an invitation to keep whatever is worthy of investigation and let the rest go.
