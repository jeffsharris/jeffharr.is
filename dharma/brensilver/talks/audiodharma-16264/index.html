Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Sympathetic Joy

AudioDharma 15:03 2022-05-12

Matthew Brensilver explores mudita, or sympathetic joy, as something more relational and less forced than ordinary happiness. He begins with the difficulty of “being joyful,” then uses a quoted teaching from a colleague to show joy as something we can receive through other people’s well-being, even alongside grief. The talk closes by turning toward the main obstacles to mudita—comparison, envy, possessiveness, and egoic identification—and by asking what it means to let another person’s happiness be unthreatening.

Chapters

  1. 00:14 What mudita is - Matthew introduces sympathetic joy and names why it can feel harder than it sounds.
  2. 01:30 A guide’s example - He describes a colleague he sees as especially skilled at teaching mudita and sets up a quoted passage from her.
  3. 02:15 Joy through connection - The quoted teaching reframes joy as something available through others, not something we have to manufacture alone.
  4. 05:22 Joy and grief - Matthew reflects on how joy can arise without canceling grief and how the emotional life can become more supple.
  5. 06:42 Removing obstacles - He shifts from creating joy to noticing what blocks it, especially the habits that make delight feel difficult.
  6. 07:17 Envy and ego - Matthew names possessiveness, comparison, and the ego’s need to appropriate goodness as the main barriers to sympathetic joy.
  7. 10:13 Against the stream - He brings in evolutionary psychology to show how jealousy can be understandable while still running against the Dharma path.
  8. 13:09 Nothing is taken - A quotation from Mathieu Ricard sharpens the point that other people’s happiness does not diminish us, and the talk ends in a quieter sense of sufficiency.
