Dharmette: Reflections on Sexuality

AudioDharma 17:13 2024-09-11

How do desire, tenderness, shame, and harm all belong to the same human terrain? The talk takes up sexuality as a neglected Dharma topic and moves through its complications without pretending it is simple: the risks of longing, the pain built into intimacy, and the possibility that attention can bring more freedom, less self-consciousness, and more honesty into sexual life. Along the way, the speaker draws on Freud, Jung, Leonard Cohen, a story by Lydia Davis, and Stephen Mitchell to ask whether erotic connection can be something other than compulsion. By the end, the question is less whether sex is peaceful than whether wisdom and love can still be found inside its disorienting pull.

Chapters

  1. 00:13 Opening the topic - He introduces the theme of sexuality as part of Dharma and names the need for caution because experiences and meanings vary widely.
  2. 01:40 Desire and instability - He explores how love and desire can destabilize, using Freud and Jung to frame sexuality as both craving and longing for the sacred.
  3. 03:13 Lay life and pleasure - He turns to the difficulties of translating monastic ideas into lay life and reflects on sexual desire as often mixed with unease rather than peace.
  4. 04:59 Cost of intimacy - Through U Pandita and Lydia Davis, he emphasizes that lust and love carry real suffering, and that the pain is part of the whole bargain.
  5. 07:46 Attention in intimacy - He asks what practiced attention might make possible in sexual connection, including greater openness, embodiment, and a less defended self.
  6. 09:50 Seeing and being seen - A friend's definition of intimacy leads him to consider shame, self-consciousness, and the possibility of being unencumbered in relationship.
  7. 11:53 Mutuality and disorientation - He describes sexuality as a deep form of doing something together, where power, generosity, non-duality, and even disorientation all become part of the path.
  8. 14:48 Precepts and romance - He closes by returning to ethical caution and citing Stephen Mitchell on how erotic passion unsettles selfhood and resists control.
