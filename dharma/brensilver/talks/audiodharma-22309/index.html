Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Equanimity 3

AudioDharma 56:49 2025-02-16

Matthew Brensilver offers a careful, practical talk on equanimity as a way of meeting pleasant and unpleasant experience without bracing, suppression, or over-identification. He moves from broad reflections on how suffering shapes our narratives into a more detailed account of sensory balance, then brings in several contemporary and traditional teachers to sharpen the definition. The second half turns more inward and practical, with questions about difficult emotions, shame, jealousy, and the slow ripening of practice over time.

Chapters

  1. 09:00 Beginning with affect - Matthew starts with the raw fact that much of our suffering and social narration grows out of how we meet pleasant and unpleasant feeling.
  2. 11:15 What equanimity means - He lays out several definitions of equanimity, distinguishing it from numbness and showing how it widens perspective.
  3. 14:13 Not self-absorption - The talk turns to how suffering narrows us and how equanimity can soften the compulsion to fix, tweak, or brace against experience.
  4. 17:00 A workable sequence - Using Alyssa Mrazek’s four aspects of acceptance, he breaks the practice into acknowledging, allowing, non-judgment, and non-attachment.
  5. 19:53 What it is not - He warns against over-reading moments of experience, and clarifies that equanimity is not apathy, passivity, or tolerance of injustice.
  6. 23:56 Compassion with balance - Matthew shows how equanimity supports more effective compassion by reducing compulsivity, desperation, and the urge to force change.
  7. 27:42 Love and gratitude - In dialogue with the room, he describes radical permission as a form of love and cites Stephen Levine on acceptance as gratitude.
  8. 32:40 How it ripens - He answers a question about conditions for equanimity by pointing to causes and effects in the awakening factors, repeated practice, and the mind being gently kneaded by practice.
  9. 41:14 Shame, mortality, and support - The closing questions move into shame, jealousy, and other hard zones, ending with the need for patience, small openings, and sometimes the steady gaze of a spiritual friend.
