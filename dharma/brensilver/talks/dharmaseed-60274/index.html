Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Death and the Poignancy of Life

Dharma Seed 61:37 2019-12-17

What happens when death is not pushed away but brought close enough to change how we live, love, and practice? The talk moves from a wry Onion joke and a contrast between “writing” and “dancing” into a steady inquiry into finitude, grief, and the ways meditation can become a kind of grasping. It then widens through Buddhist remembrances, hospice stories, psychology, and poets to ask whether a life shaped by mortality can become softer, more responsible, and somehow complete.

Chapters

  1. 00:23 Opening the topic - He names death as the evening’s theme and uses a satirical Onion story to point to the acquisitive, grasping quality that can enter meditation.
  2. 03:07 Writing and dancing - A passage from Nicole Krauss becomes a way to contrast self-building and present-moment immediacy, recasting practice as more like dancing than narrating a life story.
  3. 10:03 Birth and mortality - He widens the frame to the rarity of being born at all, then introduces the five remembrances and Ajahn Lee’s praise of aging, illness, and death as treasures.
  4. 16:55 Practicing with death - He describes year-to-live practice, the social sensitivity of talking about mortality, hospice work, and the way one’s death shapes the lives of others.
  5. 25:30 Resisting and meaning - Stephen Jay Gould and Irvin Yalom help him balance acceptance with the reality that death can feel like an enemy and that death anxiety drives much human life.
  6. 29:40 Finitude and grief - Drawing on a book about secular faith, he argues that finitude makes responsibility possible and that dharma practice requires the courage to grieve, both personally and collectively.
  7. 36:54 Loss makes life vivid - Catherine Schultz and Walt Whitman are used to show that impermanence sharpens attention, makes ordinary life precious, and asks us to keep watch rather than keep control.
  8. 41:27 Fear, identity, and softness - He turns to Freud, Terror Management Theory, and Pema Chödrön to show how mortality can trigger defensive identity, or, when groundlessness is met well, awaken kindness.
  9. 50:53 Becoming and letting go - A Buddhist teaching on craving for becoming leads into an account of practice as surrendering the future, loosening self-clinging, and rehearsing the deathless by relinquishing control.
