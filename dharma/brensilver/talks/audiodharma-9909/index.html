Reflecting On An Ajahn Sucitto Quote

AudioDharma 44:54 2019-03-30

What if the restlessness of mind is not a personal failure, but the very shape of selfing trying to find ground? Starting from a quote by Ajahn Sucitto, the talk moves through busyness, meditation avoidance, self-comparison, and the subtle agitation of identity, using Pascal, David Foster Wallace, and other references to ask what happens when we stop trying to distract ourselves. It gradually turns toward a quieter practice movement: folding the self into awareness, meeting experience with loving acceptance, and finding that the real ground is less in self-strategy than in a kind of nonclinging tenderness.

Chapters

  1. 00:34 Ajahn Sucitto's quote - Matthew introduces the teaching and begins unpacking the claim that stopping is hard because self is always busy searching for ground.
  2. 04:09 Busyness as human condition - He broadens the theme into ordinary life, where even quiet time and retreat can feel organized around avoiding stillness.
  3. 09:10 Solitude and avoidance - Pascal and David Foster Wallace help frame the fear of being alone with the deeper pain that silence can expose.
  4. 12:26 Self, agitation, and comparison - Matthew turns to the subtle connection between selfing, defensiveness, and the habit of evaluating oneself as an object.
  5. 20:16 Mind and narrative self - He uses Nisargadatta and Dennett to suggest that what feels like a solid self is more like a narrative center folded out of experience.
  6. 25:19 Loving the mess - A long example from Heather Havrilesky and references to teachers like Ajahn Chah and Ajahn Sumedho support a gentler, less combative way of meeting our own messiness.
  7. 33:01 Non-dwelling and mortality - The talk shifts toward not dwelling in self at all, then toward death, Parfit, and the relief that comes when the self is less of a prison.
  8. 40:25 Closing blessing - Matthew ends with repeated invocation, a blessing for welfare, and a brief closing exchange with the group.
