Dharmette: Mindfulness: Acceptance and Change

AudioDharma 11:59 2024-12-11

Matthew Brensilver explores a common Dharma tension: whether practice is about accepting ourselves or changing ourselves. Using short teaching stories and quotes from Niels Bohr, Suzuki Roshi, Marsha Linehan, Kobun Chino Roshi, and William James, he argues that real mindfulness holds both truths together without turning acceptance into a strategy. The talk moves through shame, self-view, and the craving to become someone else, then settles into a quiet case for non-shame as the basis for honest transformation.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 The central question - Matthew opens by naming the tension between teachings of inherent goodness and teachings that emphasize effort and transformation.
  2. 00:55 A pointed teaching story - He tells a Larry Rosenberg story that sharpens the issue by showing how spiritual sincerity can still be entangled with greed and self-deception.
  3. 02:06 Two profound truths - Bohr and Suzuki Roshi are used to frame acceptance and change as companions rather than opposites.
  4. 03:08 Acceptance without bargaining - Matthew draws on Marsha Linehan to say that acceptance stops being acceptance when it is used as a means to a result.
  5. 04:59 Shame and self-view - He turns to the depth of shame in self-experience and explains why even ordinary conditioning can feel devastating from the perspective of self-view.
  6. 06:21 Sitting with reality - Kobun Chino Roshi’s words lead into a description of practice as returning to who and where we are, even when that is painful.
  7. 07:45 The craving to become - Matthew describes bhava tanha, the urge for the next version of ourselves, and warns that practice can become egoic adornment.
  8. 09:05 Freedom from shame - William James widens the horizon as Matthew says that when shame loosens, the heart can meet the Dharma reverently and without self-deprecation.
  9. 10:35 Change from acceptance - He closes by saying that change grows from a platform of acceptance, not from a desire to arrive at a fixed identity.
