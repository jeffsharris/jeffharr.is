Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Mortality and the Poignancy of Life

Dharma Seed 37:47 2014-11-03

How do we live when the world is slipping through our fingers? The talk begins with mortality as a practical question, moving from a personal moment in a market to Buddhist reflections on mindfulness of death, denial, and the ways fear of dying can quietly organize the rest of life. It then widens through psychology, post-9/11 kindness, hospice work, and poetry toward a quieter claim: remembering impermanence can clarify what matters, soften the heart, and make life feel more complete.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the topic - Matthew introduces the subject lightly, then moves into the personal feeling that sparked the talk: mortality as something intimate and worth looking at.
  2. 03:28 Mindfulness of death - He brings in the Buddha’s teaching on mindfulness of death and uses Blake’s image of kissing the joy as it flies to ask how to live with change.
  3. 05:52 Year to live practice - A memory of the Year to Live practice, his eulogy exercise, and a difficult response from a Holocaust survivor sharpen the ethical caution around speaking of death.
  4. 11:07 Death, dignity, resistance - Matthew sets Stephen Jay Gould beside Sam Harris to show two truths at once: mortality can provoke resistance, and it also exposes how much ordinary life is spent on the wrong things.
  5. 16:30 Mortality salience - Freud and Terror Management Theory are used to explain why death is so hard to face and how identity, worldview, and even prejudice can function as defenses.
  6. 21:58 Kindness and groundlessness - Bill Moyers, Pema Chödrön, and Matthew’s own 9/11 reflections show another possibility: when groundlessness is shared, softness and kindness can emerge.
  7. 25:50 Hospice and priorities - Drawing on hospice work, Mary Oliver, and Ajahn Lee, he points to the small number of things that matter at the end of life and the usefulness of mortality for clarifying value.
  8. 30:11 Self and surrender - The talk turns toward the self as something tenuous rather than solid, and suggests that surrender to impermanence can actually feel more complete than holding on.
  9. 34:51 Closing poem - He ends with NyoGen Senzaki’s poem about carrying a humble zendo and finding freedom within limitation, then offers a quiet closing blessing.
