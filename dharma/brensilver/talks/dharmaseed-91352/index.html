Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Landing in the Breath

Dharma Seed 53:41 2025-04-17

What happens when the mind keeps turning every sensation into a question about the future? The talk begins with a simple instruction to land in the body and relax the face, shoulders, and belly, then moves into the habit of loading experience with hope, self-consciousness, and interpretation. From there it settles into breath practice, emphasizing the actuality of breathing, the loose ends of a human life, and the discipline of putting some trust in this moment rather than in the next chapter. Later, the talk turns practical again with walking meditation, boredom, changing anchors, and the sense that the quiet work of practice may be building below the level of obvious feeling.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Arriving in the body - The talk opens with a gradual softening of the face, shoulders, belly, and the urge to perform or prove anything.
  2. 07:38 Possibility in experience - Matthew describes how sensations, thoughts, and emotions are quickly read as signs about where life is going.
  3. 11:31 Landing in the breath - He redirects attention from interpretation to the actuality of breathing and the simplicity of intimate contact.
  4. 20:47 Loose ends and uncertainty - The practice broadens to notice points within the breath cycle and to meet worry, loose ends, and uncertainty with equanimity.
  5. 33:19 Hope in this moment - A central refrain develops: caring for the future can mean placing all hope in the present moment rather than endlessly projecting forward.
  6. 40:46 Not overreading retreat - He speaks about first-day retreat intensity, resisting grand conclusions, and not turning each sit into a chapter of autobiography.
  7. 43:06 Walking with ease - The teaching shifts into walking practice, emphasizing a sustainable, relaxed awareness that can continue through movement and ordinary transitions.
  8. 48:18 Boredom and fresh anchors - Matthew brings in Stephen Levine on boredom, then shares a flexible sequence of attention anchors, including a suggestion attributed to Jesse Vega Fry, to keep practice alive and adaptable.
