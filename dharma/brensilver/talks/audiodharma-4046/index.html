Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Working with Anger: Part 3

AudioDharma 49:40 2013-05-02

How do you work with anger without turning it into self-blame, blame of others, or a rush to premature forgiveness? The talk moves from seeing anger as something conditioned and burdensome, to a careful definition of forgiveness that is not condoning, suppressing, or necessarily reconciling. It then turns toward a deeper practice: looking at how a fixed sense of self and a fixed image of others keep anger in place, and how clearer seeing can soften resentment into goodwill. Questions at the end bring the teaching into practical territory, including self-forgiveness, guilt versus remorse, and anger in response to injustice.

Chapters

  1. 00:21 Reviewing the ground - He briefly recaps the earlier teachings on accountability, moral charge, and the burden anger places on the heart.
  2. 03:10 Forgiveness and clear seeing - Matthew frames forgiveness as a maturing understanding of harm, using Shantideva, a Sufi saying, and Longfellow to widen perspective.
  3. 06:28 What forgiveness is not - He carefully distinguishes forgiveness from obligation, suppression, boundarylessness, and reconciliation.
  4. 12:39 Concepts feed anger - A story about an evangelical encounter shows how abstract ideas can intensify aversion until a real person is seen.
  5. 16:36 Self-clinging and threat - He turns to how rigid identity and shame create ego pressure, making anger a defense of the self.
  6. 23:01 Not self, not other - The talk shifts to the way we essentialize others, then uses Ajahn Sumedho's advice to 'not create them' as a practical correction.
  7. 26:37 Suffering and tenderness - He describes how intimate contact with suffering can open compassion, reduce alienation, and make harm feel less attractive.
  8. 33:39 A gift loosens resentment - Matthew introduces the Buddha's advice to give a gift, then tells a personal story of giving to someone he had resented.
  9. 37:27 Questions, remorse, and closing - In Q&A he clarifies pardon versus forgiveness, distinguishes guilt from remorse, and reflects on anger at injustice before closing with a wish for the practice to support less suffering.
