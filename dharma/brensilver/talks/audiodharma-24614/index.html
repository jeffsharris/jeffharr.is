Orienting and ruling out the alternatives to love

AudioDharma 52:56 2026-01-17

What if the main alternatives to love are not villains to defeat, but habits to understand: ill will that feels charged with purpose, and apathy that comes from being too full? The talk opens by contrasting online distance with the fuller engagement of sitting together, then moves into a practical inquiry about how loving-kindness can be sustained without tipping into strain, detachment, or burnout. From there it becomes a quiet guided practice, asking what the heart needs right now and what kind of love is possible in this moment—sometimes tenderness, sometimes equanimity, sometimes simple rest.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the retreat - Matthew welcomes the group, checks in about the room, and orients everyone to the day of practice.
  2. 00:56 Engagement and presence - He uses research on online learning to argue that real engagement matters and that practice asks for bodily and emotional investment.
  3. 04:45 Why the day matters - A story about an online retreat in bed with snacks leads into a gentle reminder to honor the power of Sangha and the shared field of practice.
  4. 08:06 Entering the Dharma field - He reflects on ritualizing the moment of practice and noticing how sitting down changes the inner landscape and deepens sensitivity.
  5. 12:31 Love and its alternatives - Matthew names loving-kindness as the theme of the day and begins to examine the alternatives of ill will and apathy.
  6. 15:00 Understanding hate and apathy - He argues for a non-moralistic understanding of hatred and apathy, suggesting they are trying to do psychological work that love can do better.
  7. 20:50 Rest without apathy - The talk turns to compassion, nervous-system cost, and the need for rest and equanimity so care can remain sustainable.
  8. 25:01 Guided settling practice - Matthew guides a quiet practice of sensing the shape of love, noticing what the heart needs, and resting into a workable level of arousal.
