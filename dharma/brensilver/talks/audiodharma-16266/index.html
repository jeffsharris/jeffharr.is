Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Reflections on Lovingkindness

AudioDharma 15:19 2022-05-10

Can lovingkindness be real without pretending? The talk begins with that tension and follows it into a practical, unsentimental view of metta as something that can meet fear, wanting, and avoidance directly rather than papering over them. From there it moves through a brief turn to neuroscience, a reflection on attached love in family life, and then back to a quieter sense of warmth, goodness, and the way love can thaw what feels closed off. By the end, metta is presented both as a purification practice and a cultivation practice: a way of lowering resistance and becoming more attuned to goodness in ourselves and others.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Sincere practice - Matthew begins by cautioning against performative kindness and encouraging a way of practicing that feels intimate, alive, and true.
  2. 01:34 Fear and wanting - He links fear and craving as two sides of clinging, then brings in neuroscience to show the shared structure of positive and negative motivation.
  3. 04:13 Fear’s force - A few pointed quotations sharpen the felt power of fear and set up metta as the way out of both fear and wanting.
  4. 05:18 Approaching anxiety - Matthew describes mindfulness and love as forms of moving toward pain, so that anxiety gradually loses its charge.
  5. 06:29 Metta for fear - He names metta as a classical response to fear and distinguishes love that controls from love that simply gives.
  6. 08:12 Attachment and care - Using family life as an example, he acknowledges that attachment is often unavoidable while still pointing toward unconditional warmth.
  7. 09:56 Seeing goodness - Matthew lingers with the image of a mother cow and calf, then explains moral elevation as being moved by witnessing goodness.
  8. 11:37 Moved by goodness - He emphasizes that lovingkindness is not earned by deserving but arises from contact with goodness and can make the heart more tender.
  9. 13:25 Purification and blessing - The talk closes with metta as both purification and cultivation, lowering resistance to dukkha and opening the heart to signs of goodness.
