Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Guided Meditation: Not Fighting with Ourselves

AudioDharma 30:54 2021-08-09

How can practice become a kind of nonviolence toward experience, instead of a constant inner struggle? The talk introduces equanimity through a simple meditation frame: letting sensory experience arise without interference, “not fighting with ourselves,” and relaxing the body and mind enough to stop treating ache and contraction as a collision. From there, it returns again and again to the same movement—softening awareness like space, giving phenomena permission to come and go, and seeing that this calm is not numbness but a deeper responsiveness to suffering and joy.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Welcome and frame - Matthew greets the group and introduces the sit as a guided meditation with a theme to follow afterward.
  2. 01:26 Equanimity theme - He names equanimity as the week's theme and places it within the broader practice context.
  3. 02:05 Non-interference - Drawing on Shinzen Young, he describes practice as non-interference with sensory experience and the nervous system not fighting itself.
  4. 03:03 Sustainable posture - The meditation turns to finding a sustainable posture and sensing the spine and body in a way that supports ease.
  5. 05:47 Practice for all - He reminds listeners that practice is for oneself and others, and that its attitude is one of deep nonviolence toward experience.
  6. 09:22 Softening around collision - He points to the feeling of inner collision between ache and contraction, and invites awareness to soften like space.
  7. 11:44 Renouncing grasping - The reflection expands into renouncing greed and hatred, making peace with imperfection, and giving up the fight for this moment.
  8. 20:18 Letting go in practice - The guided section continues with repeated invitations to relax the body and mind and to simply meditate, with moments of emphasis on transcending the mind.
  9. 28:04 Equanimity deepens life - He closes by saying equanimity is not cold detachment but a deepening of poignancy, responsiveness, and attunement to both suffering and joy.
