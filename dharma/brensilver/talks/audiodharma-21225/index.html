Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Dharmette: Interpersonal Dharma (2 of 5): Not-Knowing and the Wildness of Loving Another Person

AudioDharma 14:55 2024-09-10

What happens when love is built on too much certainty? The talk moves through the tension between knowing and not knowing: how mindfulness can sharpen empathy, yet real intimacy also asks us to let others remain partly mysterious, even to ourselves. With brief turns through Simone Weil, Stephen Mitchell, and a passage from Philip Roth’s The Human Stain, the speaker returns to the same pressure point: predictability can soothe, but it can also flatten the wildness of loving another person.

Chapters

  1. 00:18 Who Am I? - Brensilver opens with the unease and wonder that arise when the familiar story of the self begins to loosen.
  2. 02:23 Empathy and Not-Knowing - He distinguishes interpersonal understanding from the deeper discipline of staying open, arguing that sincere practice includes surprise.
  3. 04:53 Forgiveness Opens Space - A Simone Weil passage helps him connect self-knowledge with forgiveness and the release of fixed identity.
  4. 06:16 Seeing Loved Ones Anew - He turns to family and close relationships, where concepts can thicken into distance and make others hard to reach.
  5. 08:20 Safety and Deadness - Using Stephen Mitchell, he shows how the wish for safety can preserve lifelessness and keep love from becoming too vulnerable.
  6. 10:01 The Limits of Knowing - A passage from Philip Roth's The Human Stain illustrates the hidden complexity of any biography and the always partial nature of understanding.
  7. 12:10 Wildness in Love - He closes by returning to routine, predictability, and the need to let go of the roles that freeze people into fixed identities.
