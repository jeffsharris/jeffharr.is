Buddhist Perspectives on the Serenity Prayer (Retreat at Spirit Rock)

Dharma Seed 39:30 2026-06-29

What if the Serenity Prayer is not mainly about accepting what cannot change, but about learning when not to fix at all? The talk moves from the familiar balance of serenity, courage, and wisdom into a Buddhist emphasis on non-fixing, showing how acceptance can weaken compulsive willfulness while making clearer action possible. Along the way, it uses ocean waves, retreat practice, and a series of Buddhist references to explore how practice shapes a life that is less driven by fear and more responsive to suffering.

Chapters

  1. 00:39 The Serenity Prayer - The talk opens with the prayer's familiar language and the claim that it can be read through a Buddhist lens.
  2. 01:44 What Cannot Be Changed - Using memories of waves and the limits of control, the talk distinguishes what can be influenced from what must be accepted.
  3. 04:23 A Deeper Kind of Fixing - A passage from the Majjhima Nikaya is used to shift the frame from conditions to the training of the heart-mind.
  4. 05:27 Practice Shapes Life - The speaker argues that practice should influence choices, relationships, and the conditions that support wisdom and love.
  5. 09:05 Respecting Our Limits - Equanimity is presented as real but limited, and the talk turns toward the need to acknowledge grief, finitude, and imperfection.
  6. 11:57 Acceptance Creates Change - The talk reframes acceptance as the basis of wisdom and shows how non-fixing can soften compulsion and strengthen love.
  7. 16:03 The Urge to Fix - Drawing on Buddhist and Tibetan references, the talk examines the restless habit of treating life as a problem to solve.
  8. 21:25 Feeling and Story - The speaker traces how arousal, language, and thought quickly build narratives that keep the fixing impulse in motion.
  9. 26:23 Hidden Agendas - With humor and retreat anecdotes, the talk exposes subtle bargaining in practice and points toward non-intervention and self-soothing.
