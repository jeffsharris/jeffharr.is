Reification and Dereification

Dharma Seed 41:48 2026-06-04

How do we practice without turning every insight, method, or identity into something fixed and unquestioned? The talk traces a careful path between reification and de-reification, showing how wisdom sometimes depends on grounding, reverence, and even deliberate solidifying, while at other times it depends on seeing that mind is shaping what seems obviously real. Along the way, it uses examples from meditation instruction, self-modeling, and Dharma language to show why practice keeps moving between holding on and letting go.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Teaching and Readiness - The talk opens by saying Dharma teaching is hard because people learn differently, and because real learning requires a mind that is soft, porous, and ready.
  2. 04:19 Mistakes and Context - Wisdom is presented as improvisational rather than algorithmic, with the Miles Davis quote framing mistakes as part of the path and Ajahn Chah used to balance letting go with compassionate engagement.
  3. 08:05 Naming Reification - The speaker introduces reification and explains it as taking thoughts, feelings, and perceptions to be unquestioned reality, especially through a naive realism that forgets the role of mind.
  4. 14:04 Mind Precedes Experience - Using the Dhammapada, the talk shows how de-reification means recognizing experience as mediated by mind and learning to see representations as representations.
  5. 16:58 Between Two Poles - Practice is described as moving gracefully between making things real enough to ground us and dissolving them enough to see clearly, with stability serving as the basis for deeper change.
  6. 19:44 Grounding and Reverence - An earthy-body instruction, reverence for signs and lineage, and references to Pascal, Maggie Nelson, and Roland Barthes are used to show why some forms need to be held while still remaining open to revision.
  7. 24:38 Method, Concentration, and Self - The talk turns to how concentration practice can intentionally reify an object, why different minds may need opposite instructions, and how the self-model can be seen as a representation rather than a solid essence.
  8. 33:05 Flexible Identification - The closing section explores how identification can become more wholesome and less painful, ending with the idea that the Dharma is about flexibility in the face of suffering rather than any fixed hierarchy of views.
