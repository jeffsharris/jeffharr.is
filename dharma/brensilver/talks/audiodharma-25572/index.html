Releasing Judging, Comparing, Fixing: Urge to Fix

AudioDharma 46:10 2026-05-26

What happens when the mind’s reflex to improve, manage, and explain is itself the thing causing strain? The talk explores the “urge to fix” as a subtle form of suffering, then turns toward a different stance: non-intervention, curiosity, self-soothing, and a more honest look at what motivates practice. It closes by widening the frame to illness, mortality, and the kind of care that can witness pain without pretending to solve everything.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 The Problem of Fidgeting - The talk opens with the sense that peace always feels just one fidget away, and questions the habit of restless self-improvement.
  2. 01:18 Fixing as a Reflex - The speaker distinguishes useful change from the deeper energy of fixing that keeps reaching for better conditions.
  3. 02:57 Progress and Fatigue - Human life as a story of progress is examined as both understandable and exhausting, especially when practice is pulled into that same momentum.
  4. 06:06 Alarm, Story, Self - The talk traces how feeling turns into alarm, language, narrative, and self-protection, creating a sense that everything must be managed.
  5. 11:58 Practice Without Answers - The speaker describes a form of awareness that is unfamiliar to the system: patient, non-interventionist, and not organized around explanations.
  6. 16:12 Secret Motives - The talk turns to hidden ambitions in practice, including the wish to get something from meditation or to improve the self indirectly.
  7. 20:20 Self-Soothing and Refuge - Rather than forcing change, the talk emphasizes creating enough safety through breath, space, love, ethics, and bodily regulation to let go more easily.
  8. 29:30 History, Samadhi, Softening - The discussion widens to healing the past, settling the mind into presence, and noticing how the boundaries between self and world soften when friction eases.
  9. 38:54 Mortality and Witness - The ending places non-fixing in the context of illness and death, with the ICU example showing that suffering may call more for presence and witness than solutions.
