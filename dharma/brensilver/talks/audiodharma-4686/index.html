Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Knowing and Not Knowing

AudioDharma 36:37 2014-03-23

Matthew Brensilver explores how practice depends on both knowing and not knowing. He moves from mindfulness as a way of seeing more clearly into the limits of self-knowledge, the role of curiosity, and the value of staying open when views harden into certainty. The talk closes by connecting this openness to relationships, practice, and the willingness to keep learning.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Knowing the path - Matthew opens by framing the Buddhist path as a way of knowing more deeply rather than remaining lost in vague experience.
  2. 01:33 Seeing through experience - He unpacks vipassana as seeing through the strands of experience and noticing the breath, body, feeling tones, and mind states more clearly.
  3. 03:27 Bare knowledge - Using Joseph Goldstein, he emphasizes the plainness of present-moment awareness and how easily we overlook it while chasing something special.
  4. 06:41 Self-knowledge and habit - He turns to the difficulty of knowing ourselves, naming habit, ego-protection, and the mismatch between our self-story and what is actually there.
  5. 11:28 What helps well-being - Matthew brings in Erica Carlson and related research, then asks what really makes us happy and why inherited models of success often fail.
  6. 14:09 False refuges - He names wealth, power, and fame as unstable refuges and ties the discussion back to the Four Noble Truths and a heartfelt path.
  7. 17:00 The value of not knowing - A Zen tea story and Stuart Firestein's writing on ignorance shift the talk toward wonder, openness, and the need to loosen attachment to views.
  8. 21:41 Views and practice - He describes how craving, aversion, and unconsciousness shape our theories, then argues that practice is an encounter with mystery that cannot be fully controlled or known in advance.
  9. 28:54 Relationships and learning - Philip Roth and Stephen Mitchell help him show why we never fully know another person, and he ends by saying meditation is ongoing learning rather than arrival.
