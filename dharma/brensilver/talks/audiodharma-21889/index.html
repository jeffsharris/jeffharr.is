Dharmette: The Divine Abodes (5 of 5) Equanimity

AudioDharma 16:17 2024-12-13

How do we keep caring without being worn down by the very fact that suffering is so large and so hard to govern? The talk develops equanimity as a kind of deep rest for the heart: still but not rigid, able to hold compassion without collapsing into compulsion, exhaustion, or helplessness. Along the way, it moves through the cost of caring, the danger of nihilism, and the middle path between thinking nothing matters and thinking everything matters. The result is a quiet case for refining love so it becomes more sustainable and more potent.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Opening gratitude - Matthew begins with a warm greeting and thanks the many people whose support makes the gathering possible.
  2. 01:57 Ease and release - He introduces equanimity through Ajahn Buddhadasa and reflects on the wish for beings to find ease as suffering keeps displacing suffering.
  3. 03:38 Still, not rigid - Using Ajahn Sucitto's phrasing, he describes equanimity as quiet, loving, and respectful, then sets it alongside the costly reality of compassion.
  4. 05:05 The cost of caring - A research study on compassion is used to explore why caring can feel taxing and why equanimity matters for making love sustainable.
  5. 07:27 Heart at rest - Matthew compares equanimity to sleep for the heart, warning against chasing stimulation and suggesting that rest does not require collapse.
  6. 09:40 Caring and not caring - With T. S. Eliot and a discussion of the Buddha's concern about nihilism, he frames the practice as a middle way between emptiness and over-significance.
  7. 11:31 Purifying compassion - He describes equanimity as a cross-check that refines compassion, reducing compulsive or self-righteous forms of care.
  8. 14:03 Helplessness and goodwill - The talk closes by naming helplessness directly and returning to a practical posture of resting without apathy, then ending with well-wishes and announcements.
