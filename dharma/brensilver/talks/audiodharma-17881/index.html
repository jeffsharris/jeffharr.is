Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Dharmette: Acclimatizing to Anicca

AudioDharma 16:01 2023-03-09

Matthew Brensilver explores anicca as something more than the fact that things end: it is the ongoing process of change, unreliability, and arising-and-passing away. He moves from the mind’s wish to stabilize life and avoid surprise, to the ways meditation reveals our habit of tracking self, story, and trajectory. The talk then turns toward a gentler response: stability, gratitude, equanimity, and a kind of resilience that can meet impermanence without hardening.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Opening the theme - Matthew introduces mindfulness of change and reframes attention as attention to process rather than to fixed things.
  2. 01:19 What anicca means - He cites Bhikkhu Bodhi to distinguish impermanence from simple ending and to emphasize the constant flux of conditioned phenomena.
  3. 02:17 Admitting resistance - He speaks personally about not liking change and the wish to make life hold still, then suggests practice prepares the heart for what cannot be held.
  4. 03:31 The permanence illusion - A study on how people misjudge their own future change leads into the common assumption that the present is a finished version of the self.
  5. 05:00 Prediction and surprise - He describes the mind as a prediction machine, always trying to avoid surprise, and shows how this appears in meditation as constant checking and interpretation.
  6. 07:11 Three characteristics - The talk widens to anicca, dukkha, and anatta, showing how the default fantasy is durable pleasure, fixed identity, and a final arrival that never really works.
  7. 09:30 Gratitude for conditions - Matthew suggests enjoying periods of stability by recognizing their tenuousness and the countless causes and conditions that make each moment possible.
  8. 11:35 Stability and refuge - He turns to the need for inner stability—sila, devotion, lineage, and the strong back, soft front posture—before we can meet impermanence without fragmentation.
  9. 14:08 Equanimity as refuge - The closing moves toward equanimity, where change is still heartbreaking but can also feel like refuge because we know ourselves as part of the river of change.
