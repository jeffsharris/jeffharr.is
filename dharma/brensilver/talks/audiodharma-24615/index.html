Functions of heart practice: cultivation, concentration, purification

AudioDharma 39:37 2026-01-17

How do heart practices work when love does not show up on command? The talk moves through metta as something improvisational rather than performative, arguing that the practice may show up as loving-kindness, compassion, joy, equanimity, gratitude, or even simple words that help the mind gather and settle. It also describes three functions of the practice—cultivation, concentration, and purification—showing how heart practice can lower resistance, bring grief to the surface, and gradually loosen the grip of self. The guided portion then asks listeners to settle into the body, sense what is needed, and let time be an ally while the heart finds its own native form of care.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the frame - Matthew introduces heart practice as improvisational and warns against pretending to feel what is not actually present.
  2. 02:57 Three functions - He lays out cultivation, concentration, and purification as different ways metta practice can function.
  3. 06:21 Cultivation and focus - The talk turns to resting in love, using simple phrases or a single word, and letting attention gather around them.
  4. 11:06 Open space of self - With an Ajahn Sucitto reference, he describes metta as moving out of the gridlock of self and softened measurement.
  5. 12:00 Settling into practice - The guided section begins with posture, body awareness, and a slow arrival into the practice field.
  6. 24:16 Words and reverberation - He invites listeners to sense what the heart needs, choose words or images, and listen for their effects in the body.
  7. 31:35 Purification and patience - The practice is framed as purification that may reveal pain, while time is described as an ally rather than something to rush through.
  8. 33:46 Goodwill and Sangha - He closes by widening the field to the goodwill of the room, the larger sangha, and the persistent force of goodness.
