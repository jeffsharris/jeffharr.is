Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Mindfulness, Dharma, Mental Health & Science Part 2 of 2

AudioDharma 1:25:21 2022-04-02

Matthew Brensilver explores how mindfulness and Dharma overlap with clinical ideas like attention training, cognitive therapy, exposure, and existential work, while insisting that the path is larger than any single technique. He keeps returning to dependent origination, the reduction of shame, and the way practice loosens avoidance and self-reification over time. In the final section, the talk opens into questions from the room about trauma, culture, sincerity, and how Dharma and mental health can genuinely inform each other.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Psychiatric complexity - He begins by describing the messiness of psychiatric suffering and the limits of simple diagnostic categories.
  2. 03:07 Causality as dependence - A twin-study diagram becomes an analogy for dependent origination, with many interacting causes rather than one clear source.
  3. 06:08 Dharma as multiple methods - He shifts from trademarked therapies to shared principles of change and describes the Dharma as a 'dirty drug' with many mechanisms.
  4. 09:16 Attention and right view - The talk turns to disrupted attention, then to the Dharma as a cognitive therapy grounded in right view and ways of seeing.
  5. 14:39 Thoughts, depression, and shame - He explains MBCT, de-stigmatization, and the early cognitive benefits of practice as ways of relating differently to pain.
  6. 19:58 Exposure and approach - Matthew develops the path of purification as a form of exposure, where practice trains the capacity to stay close to what is avoided.
  7. 29:10 Tranquility and mortality - He balances calming practices with the risk of using them as avoidance, then connects Dharma to existential questions and mortality salience.
  8. 37:54 Equanimity, self, and shame - Using stories and Buddhist language, he shows how mindfulness drains moralism, loosens selfing, and makes shame less solid.
  9. 59:18 Questions and integration - The Q&A explores sincerity, cultural translation, renunciation, trauma, and how Dharma and mental health can support each other.
