Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Loving the Self to Death

AudioDharma 39:55 2019-07-14

The talk begins with a small, ordinary fall in the rain and turns that moment into a question about how we meet difficulty without turning inward into self-attack. From there, the speaker moves through envy, the comparing mind, and the habit of making practice a battleground, then offers a different instruction: to “love it to death,” meaning to stay close enough to experience that it can’t harden into an object or enemy. The last part widens into memory, autobiography, and not-self, asking how practice can soften our relationship to the past and to the sense of “me” itself.

Chapters

  1. 00:30 Opening Story - Matthew begins with a vivid story of carrying a child in the rain and falling, using it to ask how we relate to our own falls and difficulties.
  2. 04:38 Envy as Teacher - He recalls a retreat with Shinzen Young and describes how envy exposed the architecture of self-view and comparison.
  3. 08:58 Love It to Death - He unpacks the phrase 'love it to death' as a way of being fully with experience instead of turning practice into a battle.
  4. 12:58 Peaks and Valleys - The talk turns to the ups and downs of retreat and daily practice, including helplessness and the softening that can come when nothing seems to work.
  5. 18:18 Acceptance and Love - Drawing on Ajahn Sucitto, Martin Luther King Jr., and Jessamyn Ward, he frames practice as inward nonviolence and deep acceptance rather than self-repair through force.
  6. 25:01 Working With the Past - Matthew shifts to habits, memory, and the way the present moment is shaped by what has been carried and not yet digested.
  7. 30:53 Blessing Old Stories - He explores autobiography, emotional memory, and the way practice can transform the past by meeting it with wisdom and love.
  8. 36:08 Self as Process - Using Dan Dennett and Buddhist teachings on impermanence and not-self, he closes by showing how loving the self deeply can reveal selfhood as a changing process rather than a fixed thing.
