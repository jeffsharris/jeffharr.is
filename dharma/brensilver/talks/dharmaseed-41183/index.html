Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Bringing Dharma Into Interpersonal and Communal Life (Retreat at Spirit Rock)

Dharma Seed 48:12 2017-05-28

Matthew Brensilver begins by questioning the habit of measuring retreat by surface-level mental states, and argues that practice is often changing more deeply than we can easily see. From there he widens the frame: retreat and ordinary life are inseparable, insight has to be lived, and silence has ethical implications in speech, relationship, and community. He closes by linking meditation to a broader bodhisattva-like responsibility, using examples from social ripple effects, effective altruism, and Norman Fischer on acting without expecting applause.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Measuring retreat - He opens by questioning the instinct to judge practice by the visible activity of the mind alone.
  2. 06:27 Fog and gradual change - Using Suzuki Roshi’s image, he describes practice as slow, often imperceptible, and still real.
  3. 09:00 Practice becomes life - Norman Fischer’s words help him show how commitment can turn practice from an addition into a way of living.
  4. 11:19 What silence reveals - He turns to Ram Dass to say that quieter attention makes beauty, suffering, and the world’s cries more audible.
  5. 12:42 Compassion must act - A story about Bhikkhu Bodhi leads into the claim that compassion needs expression, not only inner cultivation.
  6. 18:12 Speech and relationship - He explores how silence shapes speech, why honest communication matters, and how practice changes intimacy and self-protection.
  7. 26:38 Softening self-view - Through the vivid example of eye contact, he shows how clinging to self burdens both us and others.
  8. 32:31 Widening care - He extends selflessness into a broader refusal to essentialize others and a widening circle of empathy.
  9. 37:05 Justifying a life - A moral challenge about spending and effective help leads into practical questions of contribution, ending with Norman Fischer on acting without applause.
