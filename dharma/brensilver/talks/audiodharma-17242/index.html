Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Conversation about Insight

AudioDharma 44:10 2022-11-05

Matthew Brensilver explores insight as something more than analysis: a shift that is freeing, embodied, and often difficult to distinguish from ordinary knowing until it has become real. He contrasts rumination with realization, then brings in examples from Rob Burbea, Nicole Krauss, Jack Kornfield, Ajahn Chah, and others to show how practice changes perception, attention, and the stories we live inside. The conversation later turns to samadhi, motivation, and the way craving and wanting shape what we think we know.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Insight and Training - Matthew opens by questioning the idea that insight alone changes behavior, and reframes practice as a kind of training that works in more than one way.
  2. 03:19 What Insight Really Is - He develops Rob Burbea's view of insight as freeing, contrasts it with rumination, and describes insight as something that bubbles up rather than being forced.
  3. 07:58 Knowing and Realizing - The talk turns to the gap between knowing and making something real, with examples of dukkha, change, and the difference between propositional knowledge and practical wisdom.
  4. 12:27 New Feelings Enter - A long passage from Nicole Krauss is used to suggest that feeling itself can widen, and that insight may arrive like a new emotion or a new capacity for experience.
  5. 15:20 Enoughness and Not Knowing - Matthew argues that a sense of enoughness and a willingness to not know can broaden attention, loosen familiar interpretations, and make room for insight.
  6. 18:56 Samadhi and Consolidation - Drawing on Ajahn Chah and then a story about Jack Kornfield, he links insight with samadhi, embodiment, and the way insight becomes durable in the heart and body.
  7. 27:37 Accident-Prone Practice - In dialogue, he explains how to become more 'accident-prone' for insight by caring less about solving life and more about curiosity, openness, and loosened agendas.
  8. 36:40 Craving Shapes Perception - The conversation closes by stressing that wanting and knowing are intertwined, and that when craving softens the same experience can be seen differently, with a lighter relationship to narrative.
