Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Equanimity (3 of 5): Transmuting Dukkha

AudioDharma 15:52 2021-08-11

Matthew Brensilver explores equanimity as a way of meeting pain without wasting it. He contrasts this with self-denial and spiritual hardness, drawing on the Buddha’s middle path, Ajahn Sumedho, Thich Nhat Hanh, and Shinzen Young to show how difficulty can mature into love and a longing for liberation. The talk stays close to practice: soften, stay, and let dukkha become part of awakening rather than a source of more harm.

Chapters

  1. 00:14 Closeness Across Distance - He begins with a sense of shared presence, then points to the basic similarity of all our situations through sight, sound, thought, and feeling.
  2. 01:18 The Middle Path - Equanimity is framed through the Buddha’s middle path, rejecting both indulgence and self-mortification as ways of meeting life.
  3. 02:46 Grasping and Desire - Using Ajahn Sumedho’s line, he distinguishes desire from the grasping that turns desire into suffering.
  4. 03:46 The Limits of Austerity - A sutta-like story about burning off karma through pain is used to show the futility of spiritual self-punishment.
  5. 05:02 Not Squandering Dukkha - Thich Nhat Hanh’s 'no mud, no lotus' becomes a way of saying that suffering can be worked with rather than avoided.
  6. 06:32 Love Through Difficulty - He suggests that the way into deep love is often circuitous, passing through dukkha met by equanimity rather than bypassing it.
  7. 07:34 Softening to Conditions - Equanimity is described as softening to what cannot be controlled, with emphasis on surrender rather than passive resignation.
  8. 09:12 Pain Met Wisely - He widens the lens to social and personal harm, arguing that pain met without equanimity tends toward hatred, while wise attention prevents harm and supports purification.
  9. 11:54 Catharsis and Aftereffects - Shinzen Young’s phrase about equanimity as a cathartic factor leads into the final arc: practice can break open, release, and leave the heart quieter, harder to hate, and more able to love.
