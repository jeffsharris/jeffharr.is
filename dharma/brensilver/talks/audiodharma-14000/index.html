Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Equanimity (4 of 5): Skillfully Meeting Feelings Associated with Self

AudioDharma 15:13 2021-08-12

What happens when the stories that protect a sense of self start to feel thin? Using the image of planting a flag—claiming territory, making a case, keeping a mask in place—the talk traces how identity gets built through performance, defensiveness, shame, and the need to be seen a certain way. It then turns to equanimity as the way to meet those self-defining feelings directly, without making an enemy of them, so their charge can slowly drain and the heart can soften.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Planting a Flag - Brensilver opens with the image of claiming territory to show how we mark out a self for safety and reference.
  2. 01:25 Life as Performance - He develops the idea that social life often feels performative, using Goffman to show how masks can harden into a face.
  3. 03:12 The Self as Argument - The talk turns toward how our performances become a case for the value of me, and why Buddhism asks us to look deeper.
  4. 04:53 Pain Reveals Self-View - He describes how shame, arrogance, and self-righteousness illuminate the architecture of self-view and make its defenses visible.
  5. 07:23 Unconvinced by Story - Practice begins to loosen attachment to autobiography, opening a wider field of wonder and softness around identity.
  6. 09:01 Meeting with Equanimity - He emphasizes meeting identifications and their feelings with equanimity so their charge can gradually drain away.
  7. 10:30 Grief and Nonself - The talk acknowledges the grief that can accompany anicca, dukkha, and anatta before they become liberating.
  8. 12:24 Love Instead of Defense - Brensilver closes by stressing that the self needs love rather than aggression, and that surrendering ownership can bring ease and open-heartedness.
