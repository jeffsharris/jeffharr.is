Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Vulnerability, Porousness, Equanimity & Love (Online Retreat at Spirit Rock)

Dharma Seed 43:22 2020-05-14

What if vulnerability is not a problem to solve, but the condition that makes refuge, love, and practice necessary? The talk opens with the image of the human condition as wilderness, then moves through dukkha, the feeling of being always a step behind our real life, and the tension between modern ways of honoring fragility and the classical Buddhist emphasis on relinquishment. From there it turns to equanimity as a lived response: not numbing or denial, but a way of meeting pain without fighting it, widening perspective, and letting care become less sticky and more spacious.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Wilderness and refuge - Matthew opens with Ajahn Sucitto’s wilderness image and frames practice as learning how to take shelter without denying agitation.
  2. 02:18 The unlived life - Using Adam Phillips, he describes the poignant sense that life is elsewhere and that dukkha can be felt as loss of what never came to be.
  3. 06:04 Pain as the teacher - A brief Leonard Cohen reference leads into the idea that the diagnosis of suffering can itself feel like medicine and point toward refuge.
  4. 08:10 Vulnerability in practice - He asks how to hold human fragility, especially in a pandemic, and sets up the tension between honoring vulnerability and seeking freedom from conditions.
  5. 12:43 Fragility and animality - Matthew turns to modern Buddhist emphases on self-compassion and trauma, then to Martha Nussbaum’s argument that denying our animal vulnerability can deform ethics and compassion.
  6. 21:36 Classical release - Through the Subrahma sutta and related teaching, he presents the older renunciant model: awakening, restraint, and relinquishment as a response to fear.
  7. 26:00 Clinging and release - Bhikkhu Bodhi is quoted to show how the classical path traces anxiety back to clinging and treats relinquishment as the decisive step.
  8. 32:00 Equanimity and love - Matthew proposes equanimity as his own way of holding the two sides together: meeting pain with patience so the heart can soften rather than harden.
  9. 36:39 Wide perspective, quiet heart - He closes by widening the frame, encouraging a nonviolent relationship to experience, and ending with Leonard Cohen’s longing for peace between loves.
