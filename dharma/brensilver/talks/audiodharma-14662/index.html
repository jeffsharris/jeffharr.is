Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Mindfulness, Thinking, Mindlessness, Rumination

AudioDharma 42:37 2021-12-05

What counts as helpful thinking, and when does thinking become a place we get lost? The talk starts by distinguishing mindfulness from other forms of non-mindfulness, then moves through examples from retreat life, research on mind-wandering, and the felt difference between ordinary drift, rumination, and a slackening that may actually be restorative. It ends by pointing back to the body and to equanimity with unpleasant feeling, rather than trying to solve emotional distress by thinking harder.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Opening image - Brensilver begins with a light cartoon image to frame the day's topic as thinking itself rather than an abstract doctrine.
  2. 00:48 Kinds of non-mindfulness - He distinguishes different forms of mindlessness and suggests that not all non-mindfulness are equally harmful.
  3. 02:22 Thinking is not the enemy - He normalizes thinking, describes the embarrassment meditators often feel about it, and argues that the mind is just another sense gate.
  4. 06:04 Shame and thought patterns - A remark from Michelle McDonald softens shame, then Kalina Christoff's think-aloud research is used to show how fragmented ordinary thinking really is.
  5. 10:11 Evolution and vigilance - Brensilver reflects on why such habits may be conserved, then turns to the trust and safety needed for single-pointed practice.
  6. 15:13 Energy and slack - He makes a careful case for moments when attention can go slack, while warning that fatigue, retreat backlash, and passive consumption are not the same as restoration.
  7. 20:20 Attention economy - He explores the rhythms of exertion and renewal, the effects of what we consume during breaks, and the difference between wholesome release and drifting into defilement.
  8. 27:57 Loss of autonomy - Using Metzinger, he describes mind-wandering as a collapse of the knowing mind, where one becomes identified with thought until mindfulness reappears.
  9. 32:50 Rumination and the body - He turns to rumination as a narrower, more painful mode of thinking and ends by recommending a return to the body, patience, and equanimity instead of trying to solve feeling with thought.
