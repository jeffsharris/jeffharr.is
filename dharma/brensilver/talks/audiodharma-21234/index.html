Dharmette: Interpersonal Dharma (3 of 5): Assertion and Surrender

AudioDharma 16:24 2024-09-11

How do you stay open to another person without disappearing into them? The talk treats relationship as a dance of assertion and surrender, using the images of water and ice to explore when to melt toward connection and when to freeze into healthy boundaries. It moves from a retreat practice of walking toward a partner and signaling stop, slow down, or move forward into a careful reflection on seeing and being seen, the pressure of self-image, and the ethics of yes and no. Along the way, a brief reference to Charlotte Joko Beck and a retreat with Ajahn Sucitto helps ground the point that interpersonal practice is as necessary as meditation for communal life.

Chapters

  1. 00:21 Assertion and surrender - Brensilver frames relationships as a dance between holding power, sharing power, and being open enough to be affected.
  2. 02:27 Ice and melting - He uses Charlotte Joko Beck’s image of the ice cube to suggest that some softening is irreversible, but that individuation still matters.
  3. 02:54 Why community practices matter - He recounts Ajahn Sucitto’s view that meditation alone was not enough for community life, and that explicit interpersonal practices help harmony.
  4. 03:55 Meeting in the room - He describes the paired practice setup, with people walking toward one another and signaling stop, move forward, or slow down.
  5. 05:38 Shy, loving, and frozen - Matthew tells his own experience of feeling open, then hesitating, and sensing where connection begins to ask for a boundary.
  6. 08:05 Seeing and being seen - The talk turns to the pressure of another person’s gaze, the objectifying feeling of being seen, and the ethics that arise in the face of the other.
  7. 09:51 Self dissolving - He describes a spacious, metta-filled state where self-story falls away, then introduces Metzinger’s phrase for a less partitioned sense of knowing.
  8. 12:39 When freezing appears - A shift occurs as self-view grips the mind, and he notices how ideals about being open can turn into a harder, less flexible freeze.
  9. 15:13 Yes, no, and practice - He closes by emphasizing the dance of yes and no, saying that trust in relationship depends on being able to express both assertion and surrender.
