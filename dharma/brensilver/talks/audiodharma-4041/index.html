Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Working with Anger: Part 2

AudioDharma 50:17 2013-04-25

Matthew Brensilver continues a practical exploration of anger by framing it as something to own without collapsing into self-blame. He moves from an Ajahn Chah story and Buddhist teachings on investigation into Thich Nhat Hanh’s advice to care for anger gently, then turns to body-based practice, patience, and careful speech in close relationships and at work. The talk closes with questions from the group and a brief dedication of goodwill.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Framing the work - Matthew sets up the talk as a practical exploration of anger, emphasizing accountability without self-blame and the hope that practice can change reactivity.
  2. 04:07 Ajahn Chah's warning - He tells a story about Ajahn Chah’s counterintuitive hatred meditation to show how exhausting anger is and how much it depends on rehearsal.
  3. 07:28 Hope and path - Matthew names anger as a burden while also stressing the heart's capacity to change, and widens the frame from technique to the larger Dharma path.
  4. 12:13 Investigate instead - Using the Buddha’s four responses to pain, he argues for investigation over blame or despair and points to the way anger hides delusion.
  5. 16:51 Care for anger - He quotes Thich Nhat Hanh on treating anger like a part of the body or a suffering child, and recommends not acting immediately when anger arises.
  6. 19:45 Mindfulness and vulnerability - Matthew describes the middle way between suppression and acting out, especially in close relationships, where honest anger can reveal vulnerability.
  7. 23:27 Speaking from the heart - He presents short phrases for difficult conversations and reflects on rupture, repair, and the slow work of meeting anger without defensiveness.
  8. 32:22 Body and urgency - Drawing on Goenka, he emphasizes body sensations, breath, and patience as ways to notice anger early and bear its pressure without reacting.
  9. 39:24 Questions and closing - In questions, he addresses anger in work settings and sustained conflict, then ends with a brief reflection on goodness and goodwill.
