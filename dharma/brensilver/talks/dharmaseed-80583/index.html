Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Duality, Merging, Not-Self and Differentiation in the Interpersonal World (Retreat at Spirit Rock)

Dharma Seed 51:27 2023-10-15

How do we stay ourselves in relationship without turning inward and closing off, or dissolving into the other? The talk moves from self-acceptance and not-self into a long, intimate reflection on face-to-face contact, shame, empathy, and the subtle ways ego shows up in love and conflict. Through a retreat exercise of walking toward another person, plus references to Bahiya, Winnicott, Carl Rogers, and Octavio Paz, the speaker explores how freedom can include both merging and differentiation. It ends by asking what it means to love with space, equanimity, and enough distance to let the other remain free.

Chapters

  1. 00:35 Sitting and acceptance - Matthew begins with a saying attributed to Kobun and Chino, framing meditation as accepting life and oneself rather than striving for perfection.
  2. 01:48 Not-self and lucidity - He contrasts anatta with self-acceptance, then brings them together as radical acceptance paired with clear awareness.
  3. 03:22 Lay life and relationship - He reflects on the difference between monastic and lay practice, especially the way relationship becomes an end in itself in lay life and therefore brings both delight and grief.
  4. 07:55 Approaching another - A retreat exercise of walking toward a partner becomes a lived inquiry into boundaries, longing, awkwardness, and staying connected without abandoning oneself.
  5. 11:38 The face and ethics - Matthew turns to the ethical weight of looking at another person, drawing on Stephen Batchelor and Levinas to show how the face evokes care before concept.
  6. 17:29 Self in the gaze - He describes how being seen intensifies self-consciousness, then uses the retreat exercise to show how self-clinging can disrupt intimacy.
  7. 21:46 Seen as merely seen - Using the Bahiya teaching and Metzinger’s language, he describes a nondual mode where self-story falls away and knowing remains vivid and open.
  8. 25:23 Ego, safety, and refuge - He examines how identification collapses openness, how egoic pressure burdens relationship, and how being safe for others depends on deep insight into not-self.
  9. 31:10 Play and feeling real - Drawing on Nietzsche and Winnicott, he argues that loosening self-seriousness can restore play, aliveness, and a more embodied sense of being real.
