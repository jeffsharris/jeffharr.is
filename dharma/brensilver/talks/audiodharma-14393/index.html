Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Love and Its Relation to Wisdom

AudioDharma 47:35 2021-10-16

Matthew Brensilver reflects on how love and wisdom support each other, beginning with personal stories and literary references and moving toward a more practical Dharma question: how does a heart become large enough to meet suffering without closing down? He argues that love is not separate from clear seeing, and that understanding, attunement, and reverence are part of how practice matures. In the last part of the talk, he widens the frame to collective suffering, modern risk, and the limits of expecting Dharma practice to do everything at once.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Love Changes People - He opens with the claim that real change tends to happen through encounters marked by love, not hatred.
  2. 01:58 William James On Goodness - A William James passage is used to show how saints and tender practitioners can awaken dormant goodness in others.
  3. 03:42 Mortality and Urgency - Bringing in Dostoevsky, he turns the fear of permissiveness into an argument that finite life makes love more urgent, not less.
  4. 08:11 From Cynicism To Sangha - He shares his own aversion, then describes being changed by the warmth of practice communities and teachers.
  5. 12:24 Recognition And Dharma Love - A film and short story prompt a reflection on desire, recognition, and a more embodied, human form of love.
  6. 15:57 Metta And The Two Wings - He discusses loving-kindness practice and then asks how love and wisdom can be balanced without becoming lopsided.
  7. 22:14 Understanding As Love - Using Thich Nhat Hanh and the image of a river, he argues that expansion, understanding, and clear seeing are what let compassion hold suffering.
  8. 27:24 Path, Sangha, And Mirror - He speaks about falling in love with the path, the role of teachers and Sangha, and how others help reveal both our goodness and our roughness.
  9. 31:52 Practice In A Fractured World - The talk widens to transmission through ordinary conduct, the Buddha's response to sorrow, existential risk, and the question of how to spend the love we generate.
