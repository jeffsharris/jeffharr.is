Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Dharmette: Wholesome Qualities of Mind (5of5); The Parami of Energy

AudioDharma 13:56 2025-08-22

What if the energy needed for practice is not willpower, but a kind of willingness that gradually loosens the grip of self? The talk moves from the path of least resistance, through the difficulty of seeing conditioned life clearly, into a practical look at how energy rises and falls in retreat and ordinary practice. It then turns to the Tibetan four thoughts—precious human life, impermanence, karma, and the defects of samsara—as ways to rally the mind without glorifying striving.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening on effort - He begins with a light opening and turns quickly to the question of why any effort toward practice can feel hard at first.
  2. 02:02 Hard to see the Dhamma - Using Majjhima Nikaya 26, he frames awakening as something subtle, conditional, and contrary to the mind’s usual habits.
  3. 04:17 Against default programming - He brings in Thomas Metzinger to argue that inherited mental programming should be examined critically rather than glorified.
  4. 05:52 When energy rises and falls - He describes the changing energy of retreat practice and how low-energy periods can bring confusion, purification, and reduced self-regulation.
  5. 07:39 Four reflections - He briefly introduces the Tibetan four thoughts as a way to rally energy through reflection on human life, impermanence, and the need to ask what makes life complete.
  6. 10:30 Karma and samsara - He broadens the reflection to karma and the defects of cyclic existence, noting that ordinary pleasure still does not fully satisfy the heart.
  7. 11:57 Effort without self - In the close, he warns that effort can be tied to self-view and asks what practice looks like when energy is freed from that logic.
  8. 13:11 Closing blessing - He ends with appreciation, well-wishing, and a brief closing note from the center.
