Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Practice Questions

AudioDharma 11:20 2014-02-13

How do you keep practice steady when life is crowded, and how do you tell the difference between true meditation and the mind simply spinning? The talk moves from a practical question about fitting in practice with two young children to a set of clear suggestions: make a time and place, clarify the intention, and let meditation become as ordinary and non-negotiable as brushing your teeth. It then turns to the structure of practice itself, distinguishing breath- or body-based focus, open awareness, and times when thought is intentionally taken as the object of reflection rather than a distraction.

Chapters

  1. 00:09 Opening the question - Matthew invites questions about practice and hears a concern about inconsistency and how the mind should relate to thought.
  2. 00:32 Two parts of the concern - He names the two issues clearly: establishing regular practice and distinguishing meditation from ordinary thinking.
  3. 02:35 Make practice automatic - He offers practical advice about setting a time and place, reducing decision-making, and letting meditation become a settled habit like brushing teeth.
  4. 04:15 Intention and mortality - He points to sangha, renewed intention, and reflection on life’s finitude as deeper supports for returning to practice.
  5. 05:36 Clear instructions matter - Matthew explains that a vague sit invites the mind’s habitual tendencies, while a precise object such as breath, body, or sound helps define practice.
  6. 07:13 Noting and distraction - He describes noting as a way to mark focus and distraction clearly, and then distinguishes that from actively reflecting on a theme.
  7. 08:59 Thought as object - He contrasts breath practice, where thought is distraction, with open awareness or reflective practice in which thoughts can be included without solving anything.
  8. 10:06 Listener’s response - The questioner names what was helpful, especially the reminder to start simply and stay with one object rather than jumping around.
