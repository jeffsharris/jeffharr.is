Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Tranquility

AudioDharma 50:35 2016-04-08

How do you practice when the human condition itself feels intense, restless, and hard to rest inside? The talk begins with the image of the sick Buddha asking for the seven factors of enlightenment and then follows tranquility as a cooling, balancing force against agitation, hyper-arousal, and the urge to keep manufacturing the next moment. Along the way, it moves through retreat life, gentle anchors like the breath and sound, and the way quiet can loosen the grip of self and future-oriented striving. The closing tone is practical and modest: peace is not dramatic, but it changes how life is held.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 The Buddha at Night - Matthew opens with the sutta story of the ill Buddha asking for the seven factors of enlightenment, using it to frame tranquility as intimate and humane.
  2. 01:46 Human Intensity - He describes the sheer intensity of being human, the way distraction hides it, and how even a minute of sitting can reveal a longing for peace.
  3. 05:14 Restlessness and Leaning - The talk turns to the habit of piling intensity upon intensity and the subtle forward-leaning of the mind that waits for something to save it.
  4. 08:30 Balancing Energy - Matthew places tranquility alongside effort and energy as a balancing factor, showing how calm cools over-arousal and makes space for practice.
  5. 12:31 Soft Readiness - He draws on Suzuki Roshi, Thomas Merton, and a Jack Kornfield story to show tranquility as gentle, contagious, and easy to miss because it is not dramatic.
  6. 19:27 Settling the System - He offers practical supports for calm: simplifying retreat life, using the breath or other soothing anchors, and attending to the endings of phenomena.
  7. 25:02 Not Making a Self - The middle of the talk warns against turning practice into self-improvement, and against the disguised greed that tries to manufacture the next moment or future awakening.
  8. 31:50 Tranquility and Not-Self - Matthew explores how tranquility can reveal the vagueness of self, drawing on Bruce Hood, Dan Dennett, and a Buddha teaching that the ground is only more experience.
  9. 40:09 Peace as the End - He closes by naming peace as the highest good and recalling how practice eventually becomes life itself, less about improvement than about quiet liberation.
