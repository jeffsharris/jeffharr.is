Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Clinging & Letting Go

AudioDharma 39:42 2013-10-21

What if clinging is not just a problem to defeat, but a habit worth understanding for how much it seems to work? The talk moves from a simple claim about freedom as non-clinging, through poetry, personal examples, and Dharma teachings, into a careful look at the places clinging takes hold: sense pleasure, views, and self-view. It ends by turning toward the body, feeling, and the difficult work of staying open when letting go does not come easily.

Chapters

  1. 00:10 Happiness and clinging - Brensilver begins by calling us philosophers of happiness and asking what we actually believe brings it.
  2. 02:58 Freedom as non-clinging - He uses Joseph Goldstein and Rumi to show that freedom points toward a mind not clinging to anything, while avoiding the idea that clinging is simply an enemy.
  3. 06:00 Respect the habit - Drawing on Gil Fronsdal, he argues for reverence rather than harshness toward our own clinging because it is powerful and persistent.
  4. 08:06 Ajahn Chah's simplicity - He turns to Ajahn Chah and Ajahn Amaro for a plain instruction—let go—and a reminder that knowing we should let go is already much of the path.
  5. 12:33 Craving and desire - Brensilver distinguishes craving from ordinary desire, and contrasts feverish grasping with the steadier wanting of wisdom.
  6. 16:00 Four fields of clinging - He moves through sense pleasure and views, using pizza and the appeal of being right to show how clinging hides in ordinary satisfactions and opinions.
  7. 23:59 Wrongness and self-view - A quote from Oscar Wilde leads into the claim that spiritual practice often means discovering we were wrong about ourselves, our problems, and even the Dharma.
  8. 29:18 Feeling and release - The talk ends with self-protective patterns, dependent origination, and the feeling-craving-clinging cycle, emphasizing embodied equanimity and the confidence that nothing inside need be broken by feeling.
  9. 35:44 Compassion and surrender - After describing moments when letting go seems impossible, he points to helplessness as a spur to compassion and closes with Rumi on wholehearted surrender.
