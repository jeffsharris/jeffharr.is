Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Impressionistic vs Precise Technique

AudioDharma 39:00 2019-03-30

Is meditation best approached as a precise technique, or as something more impressionistic and responsive? The talk moves between those poles, first weighing the value of simple commitment and then opening into a detailed noting practice that distinguishes feeling in the body, mental images, and inner talk. From there it turns toward memory, suffering, and the possibility of letting experience be seen clearly enough that the past can be met with more wisdom and less struggle.

Chapters

  1. 00:30 Teaching and technique - Matthew names his ambivalence about teaching meditation and lays out the pull between vague, impressionistic guidance and precise, prescribed instruction.
  2. 05:28 Learning and loving - He offers the talk’s main orientation: practice is for learning and loving, and for not squandering suffering.
  3. 07:24 Memory shapes the present - Using Elie Wiesel, he argues that the present is formed by the past and that mindful attention can bless what is unfinished.
  4. 10:36 Three channels of experience - He introduces a more precise framework, drawn from Shinzen Young, distinguishing feeling in the body, internal images, and mental chatter.
  5. 20:14 Thought and selfing - Matthew shows how thought can dominate experience, create a worldview, and obscure impermanence and not-self.
  6. 25:14 Noting with clarity - He describes how to use casual or formal noting to track the three channels, including subtle emotions like impatience.
  7. 28:50 Blessing the past - The practice is framed as a quiet digestion of memory: meeting what arises now without compulsively rewriting the autobiography.
  8. 34:56 Questions and release - In the closing exchange he revisits redemption, allows room for sorrow with Leonard Cohen’s line, and ends with a gentler, less controlled sense of practice.
