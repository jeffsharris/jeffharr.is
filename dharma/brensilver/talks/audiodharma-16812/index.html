Dharmette: Dharma as an Attention Therapy

AudioDharma 14:18 2022-08-16

Matthew Brensilver frames Dharma practice as a kind of training for attention: a way of recovering agency, reducing fragmentation, and making insight more durable. He moves from the pressures modern life places on attention, through examples of attentional disturbance in depression, anxiety, addiction, and ordinary suffering, to the steadiness of samadhi and how it helps Dharma lessons sink in more deeply. The talk ends by emphasizing that concentration and insight belong together, and that deeper happiness is tied to open-heartedness rather than closed-hearted control.

Chapters

  1. 00:11 Opening the framework - Matthew introduces a framework for how Dharma practice works on us, then names attention as the first mechanism he wants to explore.
  2. 01:09 Attention as agency - He argues that attention is tied closely to volition and selfhood, making its training central to practice and to a sense of agency.
  3. 02:04 Modern life and mind - He describes how technology and modern life fragment attention, drawing on William James and Ajahn Chah to underscore how precious and vulnerable attention is.
  4. 03:23 Disturbed attention - He connects fragmented attention with depression, trauma, anxiety, OCD, addiction, ADHD, and the smaller but familiar restlessness of everyday suffering.
  5. 05:15 Training the attention - Matthew turns to the practice itself: placing attention on the Four Noble Truths, returning from discursive thought, and gathering the mind into something steadier and less shaken.
  6. 07:24 Poise and clarity - He describes the settled mind as less sticky, less mesmerized, and more at ease, and notes that samadhi and mindfulness are not rigidly separate in the Buddha's teaching.
  7. 09:08 Seeing suffering clearly - With stabilized attention, suffering becomes more vivid and the facts of clinging, hatred, and greed are harder to avoid or romanticize.
  8. 10:43 Insights that stick - He closes by saying that attention training gives the mind depth and lets insights leave deeper impressions, so what is understood in practice is less easily overwritten by later life.
