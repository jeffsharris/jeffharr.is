Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Taking the Backwards Ethical Step

AudioDharma 43:39 2020-06-21

What if ethical growth begins not by feeling better, but by becoming willing to feel what is uncomfortable, unresolved, and true? The talk starts with the idea of a “backward ethical step,” then moves from traditional Buddhist teachings on sila, hiri, and ottappa into moral psychology, asking how empathy, discomfort, and even grief widen the circle of what we can no longer ignore. It stays close to lived questions: how people rationalize harm, how sensitivity deepens with practice, and how a response to public suffering can become a personal responsibility.

Chapters

  1. 00:10 Teaching Style and Theme - Matthew opens with a self-aware reflection on how he teaches, then introduces the talk’s central question of taking a backward ethical step.
  2. 02:08 Wholesome Unpleasant - He challenges the idea that Dharma should mostly feel pleasant, and argues that the path includes the useful discomfort of ethical growth.
  3. 06:15 Ethics as One Fruit - Using Ajahn Chah and Bhikkhu Bodhi, he frames sila, samadhi, and paññā as inseparable and explains shame and moral dread as safeguards of morality.
  4. 09:08 From Avoiding Harm - The talk turns from refraining from harm toward a more active question of what it means to do good and to care beyond minimal non-harming.
  5. 13:42 The Backward Step - Dogen’s backward step becomes a wider image for ethical awakening, including the disorientation that comes when old habits no longer fit.
  6. 16:33 Moralization and Resistance - Matthew introduces moral-psychology research on how concerns become morally charged, and uses it to describe empathy, discomfort, and rationalization in ethical change.
  7. 24:24 Sensitivity and Vulnerability - With Krishnamurti and his own practice, he argues that ethical maturation depends on becoming more sensitive rather than harder or more defended.
  8. 29:45 Feeling, Renunciation, Courage - He ties the dynamics of hedonism and rationalization back to feeling, then names renunciation and courage as part of releasing moral incoherence.
  9. 36:09 In Place of the Friend - A passage from Norman Fischer leads into grief, shared responsibility, and a final call to act in the wake of George Floyd’s death.
