Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Affect and the Interpersonal Realm (5 of 5)

AudioDharma 17:46 2021-11-19

What if feeling, not thought, is the linchpin of how we know what matters, decide, and relate? The talk moves from a recent scientific argument for an “affectivism” phase, into a Dharma framing that “all dhammas converge on feeling,” and then into the risk and vulnerability of intimacy, where another person can feel like both threat and opportunity. From there it turns practical: how to hold pleasure without grasping, face shame without doubling down, and use the body—relaxing the hands, jaw, shoulders, and belly—as a place to discharge tension and regain steadiness.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Feeling shapes mind - Matthew opens with a recent article on 'affectivism' and uses it to show that emotions influence attention, learning, memory, and what matters most.
  2. 02:58 Feeling at the center - He draws the teaching into Dharma language, quoting Ajahn Sucitto to emphasize that all phenomena converge on feeling and that feeling drives motivation and urgency.
  3. 04:58 Threat and opportunity - The talk moves into the interpersonal realm, where other people are both support and challenge, and where emotion evolved for adaptation rather than enlightenment.
  4. 06:50 Love and vulnerability - Matthew speaks about the risks of opening the heart, grief, and intimacy as the willingness to see and be seen, with a focus on honest exposure.
  5. 08:53 Equanimity with pleasure - He extends equanimity beyond craving to include pleasure itself, then turns to ambivalence and the reminder that another person can never finish our seeking.
  6. 11:25 Being wrong gently - Matthew explores shame and defensiveness in relationship and offers Sylvia Boorstein's example of admitting error quickly and cleanly.
  7. 13:05 Body as stabilizer - The talk shifts to embodied practice, showing how the body can help discharge mental and emotional energy through relaxation, breath, and opening the places that hold tension.
  8. 15:05 Empathy deepens - He closes by suggesting that greater wisdom and equanimity in our own feeling life can deepen our ability to sense the inner life of others.
