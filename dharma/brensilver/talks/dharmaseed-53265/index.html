Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Sila as Our Practice in the World (Retreat at Spirit Rock)

Dharma Seed 45:15 2018-11-24

The talk begins with a plain question: how do we live ethically when we are vulnerable, dependent, and often ashamed of that dependence? From there, it moves from mindfulness to sila, arguing that the real way practice enters the world is through attention to how our choices affect others, and through the feedback that exposes clinging, self-protection, and fear. Along the way, the speaker uses brief references to Mother Teresa, Ajahn Sumedho, Ajahn Chah, and a closing Leonard Cohen lyric to keep returning to the same point: wisdom and love are not separate, and letting go can make us more responsive rather than less.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Vulnerability and dependence - Matthew begins with our basic incompleteness, the emotions that arise from it, and the way shame can cover our dependency on others and on the world.
  2. 02:23 Belonging and mindfulness - He links peace to interdependence, then shifts from trying to remember mindfulness to the deeper training that raises the baseline of awareness over time.
  3. 06:36 Ethics as the practice - Using Bob Thurman’s joke about practice and the concert, he argues that ethics is where practice enters the world most directly and where attention to suffering matters most.
  4. 10:30 Love, clinging, and letting go - He explores how sila exposes clinging, how love differs from control, and why goodness and joy are not manufactured so much as revealed when greed and aversion loosen.
  5. 16:00 Wisdom and humility - Matthew turns to the arhat-bodhisattva contrast and then to overconfidence, arguing for a middle way that combines courage, intellectual humility, and active open-mindedness.
  6. 24:25 Feedback and self-view - He describes ethical practice as an open system that learns through feedback, even when feedback stings or is imperfect, because it reveals how the self is constructed.
  7. 29:07 Freefall and courage - The talk deepens into the discomfort of having the self rattled, the defensiveness that follows, and the invitation to tolerate that freefall as part of ethical growth.
  8. 36:19 Moral incoherence - Drawing on Ajahn Chah and a personal story about money, Matthew reflects on the rare ideal of sainthood and the recurring moments when ordinary life no longer feels fully justifiable.
  9. 41:28 Radical hearts - He ends by reimagining hope as meaning rather than outcome, widening the frame to collective responsibility and closing with Leonard Cohen’s wish for a treaty between love and anger.
