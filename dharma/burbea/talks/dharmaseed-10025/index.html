Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Emptiness and Insight Meditation

Dharma Seed 62:25 2009-12-29

Rob Burbea explores emptiness as a practical way of seeing how suffering is built, not just as philosophy. He begins with the Buddha’s focus on dukkha and its root in delusion, then shows how mindfulness can reveal the mind’s habit of constructing selves, feelings, objects, and even time as if they had fixed essence. The talk gradually moves from ordinary examples and retreat life into a subtler view: letting go can make experience less solid, and emptiness is presented as opening toward joy, compassion, and freedom rather than bleakness.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 The root of suffering - He starts from the Buddha’s teaching on suffering and asks what lies deeper than ordinary causes.
  2. 03:51 Delusion and inherent existence - He explains delusion as mis-seeing reality, especially the habit of taking self and things to have their own essence.
  3. 10:18 What emptiness means - He defines emptiness as the lack of inherent existence and responds to common reactions of disbelief, fear, and nihilism.
  4. 15:41 Insight and convention - He compares insight meditation with other Dharma approaches and uses ordinary conventions like money, time, and place names to show how meanings are constructed.
  5. 22:08 Construction as practice - He chooses construction and fabrication as the most useful entry point for insight meditators and emphasizes the dependency on mind rather than on external causes alone.
  6. 27:22 The golden question - He turns the teaching into an inquiry: in any difficulty, ask how the mind is adding extra suffering and how that can be seen and released.
  7. 30:10 Five ways in - He gives five practical ways to notice emptiness through mindfulness, inquiry, self-view, impermanence, and the labeling of sensation.
  8. 39:37 From presence to letting go - He extends the analysis to the subtle construction of a stable self and points to Mipham Rinpoche to argue that raw experience is still shaped by delusion.
  9. 49:20 Joy, compassion, and service - He closes by showing how letting go dissolves solidity, makes appearances less burdened, and opens toward joy, love, equanimity, and service rather than disappointment.
