Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Working with Thought in Meditation

Dharma Seed 62:15 2008-11-09

Rob Burbea explores how meditation can meet thought without treating it as an enemy. He distinguishes ordinary planning and reflection from obsessive proliferation, drawing on Buddha’s teaching on thoughts that lead toward suffering or away from it, and he offers practical ways to work with thought through calm, the body, metta, and spacious awareness. The talk gradually shifts from discerning unhelpful thinking to seeing thought as fleeting, insubstantial, and not to be taken as self or authority.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Reframing thought - Burbea begins by rejecting the idea that meditation is mainly about suppressing thought and instead frames thinking as a complex part of human life.
  2. 05:53 Three aims with thought - He lays out three aspirations: being free with thought, discerning what to let go of, and learning to think well and creatively.
  3. 16:39 Working with harmful loops - The talk turns to entertainment thinking, boredom, and obsessive proliferation, with practical suggestions such as metta, returning to the breath, and using bodily attention.
  4. 20:14 What papanca does - Burbea explains how thought can spread from a simple moment into a whole story, using retreat examples and the Buddhist term papanca to show how proliferation inflates experience.
  5. 30:58 Finding the hook - He maps the chain from sense contact to feeling-tone, aversion, and proliferating thought, and shows how calmness lets one see where the process can be interrupted.
  6. 37:39 Emotion and well-being - The teaching widens to hidden assumptions, underlying emotions, and the Buddha’s advice on the right ending of papanca through wholesome forms of joy, grief, equanimity, and a deeper reservoir of well-being.
  7. 50:41 Thought as process - In the final section, he offers methods for the easier ranges of thought: noticing kinds of thinking, labeling when useful, and seeing thoughts as impermanent, unwilled, and without authority.
