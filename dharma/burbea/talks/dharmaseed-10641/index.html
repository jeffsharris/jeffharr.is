Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Development of Equanimity: Introduction and First Instructions

Dharma Seed 1:19:33 2008-10-03

How do you stay steady when life keeps shifting between praise and blame, gain and loss, pleasure and pain? The talk opens by rooting equanimity in the Buddha’s teachings, then carefully distinguishes it from indifference, showing it as something alive, warm, and spacious rather than shut down or cold. After setting the retreat in silence and trust, the speaker introduces a simple practice for meeting changing experience: stay with bodily sensation, notice impermanence, and repeat a phrase like “May I be at peace with the flow of experience.”

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the retreat - Burbea welcomes the group and honors the sincerity that brings people to retreat.
  2. 02:26 What equanimity means - He introduces equanimity as the retreat theme and distinguishes it from compassion-related balance.
  3. 04:53 Eight worldly conditions - The Buddha’s eight worldly conditions are used to show how life naturally swings between praise and blame, gain and loss, pleasure and pain.
  4. 09:14 The stone slab image - He recounts the Buddha’s image of equanimity as an unshakable stone slab, then adds that real equanimity also has flexibility and spaciousness.
  5. 11:58 Not indifference - Burbea draws a clear line between equanimity and the near enemy of indifference, stressing aliveness, openness, warmth, and love.
  6. 14:23 A spectrum of practice - He describes equanimity as gradual and relational, while holding it alongside the richness of emotional life.
  7. 21:01 Path, faith, and priority - The talk widens to the Buddha’s path as the context for the whole life, and to the shift in priorities that makes freedom and peace more central than fixing every problem.
  8. 30:54 Silence, trust, and practice - He speaks about intentions, surrender, noble silence, the five precepts, and the retreat container before introducing the evening’s practice.
  9. 41:18 Guided meditation on change - The long guided section trains attention on bodily sensation, fluctuation, and impermanence, using the phrase “May I be at peace with the flow of experience” as an orientation rather than an affirmation.
