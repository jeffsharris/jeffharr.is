Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Sila and Soul (Part 2)

Dharma Seed 2:52:46 2019-06-10

Rob Burbea explores how ethics, soulmaking, psychotherapy, and Buddhadharma shape what we notice, value, and act on. He argues that any framework has blind spots, and then uses examples from climate change, social justice, guilt, and ecological grief to ask what a fuller moral imagination might include. The talk gradually moves from critique toward imaginal practice, suggesting that images, virtues, and a more spacious sense of self and world can support a deeper ethical life.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the inquiry - Rob sets up the talk as an exploration of how ethics, soulmaking, and common development frameworks relate to each other.
  2. 04:12 Psychotherapy and Dharma - He compares psychotherapy and Buddhadharma at their best and at their limits, especially around self-preoccupation, ethics, and compassion.
  3. 12:12 Frameworks and blind spots - He emphasizes that every conceptual framework casts a shadow and can obscure important moral realities.
  4. 18:35 An imaginal misreading - A student’s therapy story about a monk image becomes an example of how imaginal material can be mistaken for denial instead of ethical calling.
  5. 29:34 Conscience and tradition - Rob asks what happens when conscience is absorbed into inner-critic language, and why living traditions need critique, dialogue, and care.
  6. 48:35 Ethics, time, and future - He turns to shadow, Kierkegaard, and the aesthetic-versus-ethical distinction to argue that present-moment spirituality can miss future commitment.
  7. 01:04:58 Dharma and moral courage - Rob questions apolitical Dharma, spiritual bypassing, and a dominant ethos of softness that may avoid necessary judgment or challenge.
  8. 01:16:16 Climate and responsibility - The talk deepens into climate ethics, fair share, and the difficulty of taking personal responsibility when harmful habits have been normalized.
  9. 01:32:24 Imagining the suffering - He uses imagined conversations with affected people and a critique of Four Noble Truths rhetoric to make ecological harm feel more real and morally immediate.
