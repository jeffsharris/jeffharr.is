Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Ways of Looking: (1) Anicca

Dharma Seed 1:11:52 2011-02-13

What happens when impermanence is treated not as an idea, but as a way of looking? The talk moves from simple examples of noticing change in sound, sensation, taste, and thought into a deeper inquiry into how perception builds solidity, self, and even suffering. It then turns to the practical question of how this lens interacts with metta and other forms of practice, with an emphasis on flexibility, direct experience, and what begins to loosen when the mind stops joining the dots so quickly.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the frame - Rob introduces the retreat context and distinguishes insight that arrives through reflection, intuition, or simple clear seeing from insight sustained as a chosen way of looking.
  2. 07:54 Three characteristics - He presents anicca, dukkha, and anatta as the Buddha’s teaching and, more specifically, as practice lenses that bring freedom in the moment.
  3. 11:40 Why this is subtle - A brief reference to the Buddha’s hesitation to teach dependent origination underscores how difficult these insights can be to see and why skillful methods are needed.
  4. 14:22 Learning to notice change - Rob explains how to deliberately tune attention to change itself, using everyday fluctuations and the ringing bell to train perception toward flux rather than fixed objects.
  5. 23:55 Sense-door experiments - He moves through body, taste, smell, and sight, showing how each sense door reveals arising and disappearing, and how the mind tends to turn momentary events into solid stories.
  6. 29:55 Seeing the visual field - Using Catherine McGee’s earlier point and an Escher comparison, he explores the visual field as shifting pattern and uses negative space to show how perception helps construct objects.
  7. 33:30 Subtle change and intention - The talk widens to include the subtler textures of visual stability, bodily sensations, thoughts, and intentions, while encouraging listeners to work both with narrow focus and the open field.
  8. 43:21 Letting go and self - Rob links seeing impermanence to spontaneous letting go, then turns to the felt sense of a fixed self and asks listeners to look directly for the one who seems to own experience.
  9. 50:42 Hard to overturn - He returns to the difficulty of dismantling self-view, including the sense of being the witness, and shows how sustained practice can gradually loosen identification.
