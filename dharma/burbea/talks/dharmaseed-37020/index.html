Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Gift and the Artifice of Self (Part 2)

Dharma Seed 1:14:01 2016-08-01

What if the self is not just something to be explained, but something to be re-enchanted? The talk moves through the ways reductionist and overly narrow spiritual views flatten experience, then turns to desire, mind, suffering, and imagination as dimensions that may need to be seen as divine, or at least held with far more room. From there it opens into a wider question of causality: whether the past, the present, the future, or the imaginal may all shape what we are becoming, and how practices and views can either close that down or let it unfold.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Why disenchantment happens - He begins by naming the flattening effects of reductionist views and some spiritual emphases that can narrow the sense of personhood and divinity.
  2. 04:14 What needs re-enchanting - He asks which layers of self need re-enchanting, and expands the question to include imaginal forms and the whole spectrum of self-fabrication.
  3. 08:33 Desire, eros, and legitimacy - He turns to emotions, desire, and eros, drawing on Corbin, Boehme, and Gregory of Nyssa to argue that longing can be holy and must be given room.
  4. 17:24 Mind and emptiness - He looks at mind, imagination, and the continuum with the divine, then contrasts this with anatta as a skillful way of looking rather than the only one.
  5. 21:46 The dark and the good - Using Jung, he asks about the order of practice: cultivate the good first, then learn how even darker or rejected material may be seen as divine.
  6. 26:07 Suffering and re-storying - He explores how judgment, blame, and inherited explanations can hold suffering in place, and how new stories can loosen and heal it.
  7. 36:49 Frames of causality - He widens the discussion to archetypal causality and then to dependent co-arising, distinguishing past causes from present fabrication.
  8. 46:34 Dreams, future, and imaginal life - He considers dreams and images as forward-moving as well as past-bearing, and draws on Corbin’s angel out ahead to describe imaginal transformation.
  9. 01:00:01 Artifice and soul-making - He closes by linking Jung, Nietzsche, Wilde, and metta practice to the idea that some artifice is necessary for healing, creativity, and soul-making.
