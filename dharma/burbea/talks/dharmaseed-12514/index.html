Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Ending Blame

Dharma Seed 54:55 2011-02-09

In this talk, Rob Burbea explores how blame narrows experience into a self-centered story, and how looking more closely reveals a web of conditions instead. He moves through everyday examples—mistakes, tiredness, food, mindfulness, relationships, and even difficult therapy experiences—to show how self-blame and blame of others can be softened by a wider view. The talk gradually brings together emptiness and metta, suggesting that repeated practice can loosen shame and make the heart and mind more flexible.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the problem - Rob introduces the connection between metta and emptiness, and frames blame as a major block to love and self-love.
  2. 07:22 Conditions, not self - He unpacks self-blame through examples from work, meditation, tiredness, and missed mindfulness, showing how many conditions shape what happens.
  3. 15:59 Food and fear - Using overeating as an example, he argues that what looks like greed is often mixed with fear, culture, and past conditioning.
  4. 19:22 Relationships as dynamics - He turns to couple dynamics and other relationships, showing how patterns of clinging, recoil, and blame are usually mutual and conditional.
  5. 27:34 Re-viewing the past - He broadens the teaching into re-inspecting past events, distinguishes remorse from guilt, and shares a therapeutic story about how outer conditions became inner ones.
  6. 38:05 Avijja and meaning - He names ignorance or delusion as the root problem and shows how quickly pain, thoughts, or images become evidence about the self.
  7. 41:50 Metta softens the view - He explains how metta makes perception more malleable and how changing the way we see can change what the moment becomes.
  8. 47:48 Practice, shame, and power - He closes by stressing repetition, trust, and the gradual dissolving of pervasive shame through metta, emptiness, and a felt sense of power.
