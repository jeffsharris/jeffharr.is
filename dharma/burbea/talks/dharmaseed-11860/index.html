Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Love/The Difficulties of Love

Dharma Seed 67:04 2008-03-07

What happens when the need to love and the need to feel loved start shaping almost everything we do? The talk moves from romantic love and cultural hype, through self-criticism, shame, rejection, and fear of being trapped, toward the quieter work of seeing how feelings are conditioned and how love is more than a passing emotion. It also turns toward metta practice as a way to soften the inner critic, open the heart, and discover that giving love can change what is actually perceived.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening Questions About Love - Burbea introduces the talk by naming love as a vital and often painful area of life, with special attention to the need to love and to feel loved.
  2. 05:28 Romance and Cultural Pressure - He examines how modern culture inflates romantic love, how that hype feeds longing, and how relationships can become burdened by expectation.
  3. 10:42 Self-Love and the Inner Critic - The talk shifts to self-criticism, the need to love oneself, and practical ways of meeting the inner critic through heartfulness, questioning, and mettā.
  4. 18:36 Worthiness and Hidden Shame - Burbea looks at how criticism becomes internalized, how shame blocks intimacy, and how relationships can offer healing when vulnerability is actually met.
  5. 26:44 Feelings, Intention, and Love - He explores how we shut down intimacy, why love is more than a feeling, and how emotions are fabricated by conditions, stories, and self-view.
  6. 35:31 Falling In Love - Using stories of romantic attachment, he questions clinging, fear, projection, rejection, and the solidified concept of being hurt or unlovable.
  7. 47:13 Relationship as Skill - The talk turns to the work of sustaining friendship and partnership, including the need for honesty, communication, and a shared purpose beyond dependency.
  8. 54:12 Metta, Perception, and Spaciousness - Burbea closes by returning to mettā, showing how giving love opens the heart, changes perception, and can loosen the tightness of self-definition and dependency.
