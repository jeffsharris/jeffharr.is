Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Talk Four: Jhanas One to Four

Dharma Seed 64:43 2008-08-11

What does it actually feel like when concentration deepens from a comfortable breath to full-body rapture, then to happiness, peaceful contentment, and finally still, bright awareness? The talk walks step by step through the four jhanas, using the Buddha’s images of bath powder, a mountain lake, lotus-filled water, and a white cloth to show the differences in each state and to stress that steadiness and whole-body suffusion matter more than dramatic intensity. It also pauses over practical questions: whether this is escapism, how precision can be embodied rather than intellectual, and how intense bliss can be worked with until it mellows.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the map - Burbea frames the talk as a map of the path and introduces the four jhanas as stages of deep concentration.
  2. 01:21 Listening and attitude - He asks listeners to notice their reactions to advanced practice and emphasizes that the talk is mainly a description, not a demand to do anything.
  3. 05:23 Pleasure gathers - The first movement is from a simple comfortable feeling into piti, with the breath helping the pleasant energy spread through the body.
  4. 09:37 First and second jhana - He reads the Buddha’s images for the first two jhanas and explains the shift from rapture to deep happiness as thought falls away.
  5. 13:50 Contentment and peace - The talk turns to the third jhana, where rapture fades and a tender, satisfying peacefulness becomes dominant.
  6. 17:24 Fourth jhana - Burbea describes the fourth jhana as pure, bright awareness and stillness, stressing refinement and steadiness over intensity.
  7. 23:07 Signs and mastery - He discusses nimittas, the importance of suffusion, and why steady familiarity with these states matters more than chasing peak experiences.
  8. 27:16 Escapes, not evasion - He answers the question of escapism by saying the jhanas are escapes from hindrances and grossness, not from life, and he links samadhi with love, confidence, and healing.
  9. 36:43 Prayer of practice - Burbea turns to the long arc of practice, including the Buddha’s teaching that these pleasures conduce to awakening, and the role of samadhi in insight.
