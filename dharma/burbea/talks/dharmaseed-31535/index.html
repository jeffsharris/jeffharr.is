Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Soulmaking (Part 2)

Dharma Seed 1:13:29 2015-08-10

Can Dharma be understood not just as lessening fabrication, but as a field where imagination, image, and soulmaking are already at work? The talk moves from a critique of “bare attention” as a complete account, to a wider framing in which practice can reduce suffering, but can also deliberately use images, mythos, and love to deepen perception and openness. Along the way, it asks whether disputes about what is “really Buddhism” are often also disputes about our own fantasies, assumptions, and the size of the conceptual box we are living in. By the end, the question is less whether image belongs in practice than whether our Dharma view is spacious enough for the soul.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Ways of Looking - He frames Dharma practice as developing flexible ways of looking and distinguishes approaches that fabricate less from those that unmake experience more deeply.
  2. 05:24 Three Practice Avenues - Burbea lays out three broad directions: bare attention, deeper unfabricating practices, and skillful fabrication that makes room for imaginal work.
  3. 07:39 Fantasy in Dharma - He shows how myth, image, and fantasy already shape our sense of Buddha, awakening, and the path, whether or not we notice it.
  4. 12:02 Self and Emptiness - Using dependent origination, he discusses the self as empty and then argues that this emptiness can allow more room for imaginal play.
  5. 15:14 Images and Dukkha - He turns to the Four Noble Truths and shows how images of suffering, compassion, and beauty can be used to meet dukkha skillfully.
  6. 20:01 Soulmaking as Priority - Burbea asks what the soul wants, and suggests that making soulfulness a priority changes the way practice is oriented and understood.
  7. 26:21 Dharma on Imaginal Ground - He treats disputes about what is 'really Buddhism' as psychological and philosophical questions about the fantasies and assumptions underlying any Dharma framework.
  8. 38:28 Concepts as Lenses - He reinterprets Dharma concepts as tools for perception rather than realities, and links that view to more flexible, open-ended practice.
  9. 53:13 Love, Eros, and Opening - He explores how love, eros, and conceptual frameworks feed each other, using Keith Jarrett as an example of image deepened by love and of soulmaking through perception.
