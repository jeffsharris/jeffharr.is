Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Energy, Image, Emotion (Part 2)

Dharma Seed 32:36 2015-08-08

When difficult emotion feels like a trapped vortex, what happens if image can give that energy a channel? The talk moves through anger, depression, and loneliness to show how an image can widen the field of experience, add depth or even beauty, and sometimes turn raw emotion into something more creative. It also keeps testing a basic question: is emotion primary, or do image, energy, and view shape one another in a more mutual way?

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Emotion and trapped energy - Rob begins by describing difficult emotion as energy caught in an unhelpful vortex and introduces the basic connection between emotion, image, and the energy body.
  2. 01:39 Anger and release - Using anger as the main example, he distinguishes anger from ill will and shows how recognizing anger can free energy and even expand awareness.
  3. 04:04 Default moods and hidden energy - He turns to habitual low energy or agitation, suggesting these states can hide unrecognized psychic energy that needs to be felt and allowed to move.
  4. 08:58 Coltrane and image - A story about John Coltrane and Ravi Shankar illustrates how intense expression can be mistaken for anger and how images can carry a larger emotional range than simple labels allow.
  5. 12:20 Meaning beyond emotion - Rob explains that images can hold shade, depth, duty, and soulfulness, and that they may transform loneliness or pain into something more spacious and meaningful.
  6. 16:52 Transformation and assumptions - He notes that emotions can dissolve, sweeten, or be held by an image, and invites listeners to notice the assumptions they bring to these shifts.
  7. 20:30 Causes and transpersonal feeling - The talk questions the habit of demanding a psychological cause for every feeling, bringing in William Blake and the idea that some emotions may be influxes from beyond ordinary personal explanation.
  8. 24:04 Mutual conditioning - Rob reframes emotion, energy, and image as dependently co-arising, emphasizing that views about what is primary affect what unfolds in practice.
  9. 28:51 Flexible Dharma views - He closes by contrasting a monistic conception of practice with a broader Dharma of many ways of looking, where non-clinging is understood as a range of modes of awareness that reveal emptiness and widen freedom.
