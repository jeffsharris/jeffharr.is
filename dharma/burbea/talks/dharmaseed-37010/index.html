Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

"I Sing The Body Electric": On Orientations in Practice

Dharma Seed 68:15 2016-07-28

Rob Burbea introduces the retreat by asking practitioners to emphasize the energy body: a subtler, more fertile mode of mindfulness that can support samadhi, metta, emotion work, imaginal practice, and insight. He then turns to five common Dharma assumptions he thinks can narrow practice, and contrasts them with flexibility, re-enchantment, and a more aesthetic, inventive way of looking. The talk closes by reframing the point of practice as loosening fixed views so that beauty, timelessness, and sacredness become more available in ordinary life.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening: Energy Body - Rob sets the retreat's main emphasis on sensing the energy body rather than repeating basic instructions about it.
  2. 02:25 What Energy Body Means - He defines the energy body as a subtler mode of mindfulness that overlaps with ordinary sensation but tunes attention differently.
  3. 08:31 Useful Across Practices - He shows how energy-body awareness supports samadhi, emotional life, imaginal work, metta, and insight by revealing shifts in contraction and release.
  4. 15:20 Body Electric - Whitman's line becomes a frame for the body as alive, electric, and shaped by attention, with perception itself understood as fabricated.
  5. 20:01 Walking in the Field - He gives practical guidance for walking and moving in a way that stays sensitive to texture, vibration, and pace.
  6. 23:26 Five Crass Assumptions - Rob names concentration, atomism, realism, solitude, and sanitization as common habits that can limit Dharma practice.
  7. 36:53 Crass to Scars - He sharpens the critique with the CRASS and SCARS mnemonics, then returns to the central role of subtlety and dependent origination.
  8. 42:45 Precision and Subtlety - Precision is redefined as discernment among ways of looking, and he uses the Anapanasati Sutta to show that breathing practice includes well-being as well as attention.
  9. 54:31 Loosening Into Beauty - In the closing movement, he contrasts passive openness with deliberate practice, and says the real point is loosening so that timelessness, beauty, and sacredness can arise more often.
