Chandrakirti's Chariot and the Unfindable Self

Dharma Seed 63:31 2009-01-31

Rob Burbea introduces Chandrakirti’s sevenfold chariot analysis as a practical way to investigate the self, beginning with the felt sense of inherent existence and then testing whether the self can be found in the aggregates, apart from them, or as their collection, possession, shape, or continuum. He keeps returning to the difference between empty, dead conceptualization and a living meditative inquiry that can soften into a direct sense of unfindability. The talk moves from general framing into detailed reasoning, then toward how the practice can alternate with samadhi and be used not only with self but with emotions and experience more broadly.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the practice - Rob frames the talk as part of a retreat sequence and distinguishes this method from the earlier anatta practice of seeing phenomena as not-me, not-mine.
  2. 03:10 Inherent existence - He introduces Chandrakirti and explains that the practice begins by clearly sensing the self as inherently existing, rather than blurring into abstract philosophy.
  3. 11:00 Why the chariot matters - Rob lays out the sevenfold framework and the chariot analogy, showing that a self would have to exist in one of a limited number of ways if it existed at all.
  4. 20:52 Sevenfold analysis - He works through the seven possibilities in detail, using cars, houses, muesli, and possessions to test each relation and show why none can hold.
  5. 39:25 Perceiver and perceived - The reasoning turns toward subject and object, and Rob probes whether perceiver, perceiving, and perceived can be the same, different, or separable.
  6. 46:23 Continuum and time - He examines the idea that the self might be a continuum in time, then questions whether the present moment or temporal ordering can support an inherent self.
  7. 54:00 Analysis and samadhi - Rob describes how reasoning and calm can support one another, so that analysis can bring the mind to a steadier, more settled sense of emptiness.
  8. 56:28 Using the method - He closes by explaining how the practice can be used with self, anger, fear, and sadness, and how it can either be taken up seriously or simply left for later.
