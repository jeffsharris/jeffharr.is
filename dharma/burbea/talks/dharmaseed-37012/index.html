Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Matter, Bodies, Worlds (Part 2)

Dharma Seed 1:14:54 2016-07-30

What if the world we take to be solid, single, and objective is partly shaped by the way we look at it? The talk moves from a critique of modern reductionism and mind-matter dualism into Buddhist, Kabbalistic, and tantric ways of seeing many worlds, many bodies, and different levels of time, always returning to experience rather than abstract belief. It then turns practical: how perception can be trained, how the body can be re-enchanted, and how sacredness may be approached in stages without rushing past what is actually workable.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Bottom-Up Assumptions - Burbea opens by examining the modern reductionist picture of reality and the hidden assumptions that make it feel self-evident.
  2. 08:16 Many Worlds, Many Traditions - He brings in Kabbalah, Buddhism, Islam, and related traditions to contrast modern flat materialism with older cosmologies of multiple worlds.
  3. 14:08 Quantum and Mind-Matter - The talk turns to quantum examples, the observer, and Descartes, showing how scientific ideals can harden into dogma.
  4. 21:01 Mind-Made Body - Burbea moves into Buddhist sources and a retreat anecdote to show how meditative practice can reveal other kinds of body and perception.
  5. 33:40 Phenomenology and Plotinus - He emphasizes experience over speculation, then uses Plotinus to illustrate a flexible way of conceiving matter and divinity.
  6. 45:28 Energy Body and Alchemy - The talk develops the energy body as one route to re-enchantment and then frames alchemy as a transubstantiation occurring in perception.
  7. 52:49 Mandala and Mantra - Burbea describes the world as sacred space, including mandala practice and hearing sound as mantra, with a looser, more creative retreat style.
  8. 58:40 Time and Timelessness - He turns to imaginal time, citing Corbett, Alex Wayman, and Walter Wink to describe a timeless dimension within appearance.
  9. 01:08:55 Pragmatic Closing - The talk ends by stressing stages, provisional dualities, and the recurring question of what is actually helpful in practice.
