Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Theatre of Selves (Part Three)

Dharma Seed 64:34 2013-11-24

What if the images and stories that move through practice are not just private material to be managed, but forces that shape how the world appears? The talk pushes past the usual idea of mindfulness as simply staying with experience, and instead turns toward conceptual frameworks, emptiness, and different ways of looking that can either flatten life or open it. Drawing on examples from Buddhist texts, Jung, Hillman, Rilke, myth, and psychotherapy, the speaker keeps asking whether an image is something to integrate—or something larger that asks something of us.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the series - He situates the talk as a continuation of a wider reframing of Dharma, noting that some listeners may want only small techniques while the larger movement is much farther-reaching.
  2. 02:43 Less fabrication - He distinguishes simple mindfulness and staying at contact from a deeper move toward less fabrication, while stressing that one cannot live permanently in an unfabricated state.
  3. 05:32 Ways of looking - The talk develops the idea that imagination, perception, and self are always shaped by assumptions, and that the Dharma can be understood as skillful ways of looking that reveal emptiness.
  4. 09:50 Concepts underneath experience - He shows how conceptual frameworks operate in ordinary perception, in emotional healing, and in religious traditions, using examples from the Pali Canon, Tantric Buddhism, and modern ideas of the unconscious.
  5. 15:16 Images are not parts - Burbea contrasts the common tendency to treat images as parts of the self with another way of conceiving them as presences with their own mode of being, drawing on Corbin, Robert Duncan, and gestalt therapy as points of comparison.
  6. 25:07 Archetypes and autonomy - He develops a more imaginal stance in which figures are not to be tamed or reduced, but enlivened and respected as living presences, with Rilke used to underline their autonomy and scale.
  7. 37:17 What the image wants - Using James Hillman, he argues that images make a claim on us, bear a kind of moral and aesthetic force, and may ask for service rather than integration, with examples from teachers, monks, angels, demons, and Dionysus.
  8. 48:50 Myth and soulfulness - He broadens the discussion to myth, Buddhist tradition, and even apparently realist views, suggesting that all are ways of looking that animate experience and can restore resonance, depth, and soulfulness.
  9. 01:00:46 Cautions and closing - He closes by naming the dangers on both sides: imaginal inflation or escapism, and on the other side flattening Dharma into realism; the talk ends as an invitation to notice the conceptual frame and try different ones with care.
