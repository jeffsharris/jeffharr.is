Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Soulmaking (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 1:15:46 2015-08-10

Rob Burbea introduces soul and soulfulness as active, relational qualities rather than as a fixed inner entity. He distinguishes soulmaking from panic, delusion, and bare attention, then opens a wider map of Dharma as many ways of looking, including skillful fabrication, image, myth, and fantasy. The talk moves through Buddhist, Kabbalistic, Jungian, and mythic references to show how imagination can be empty and still deeply meaningful.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the term - Burbea explains that he will unpack what soul and soulfulness might mean for practice and Dharma, rather than treating the word as a fixed doctrine.
  2. 04:10 Soul as making - He shifts from soul as an entity to soulmaking as a verb, using Keats to frame soulfulness as something nourished by ways of looking, relating, and acting.
  3. 11:29 Soulfulness and papanca - The talk distinguishes soulful image from hallucination and papanca, stressing that soulmaking has spaciousness, beauty, and a recognized sense of image.
  4. 17:15 Traditions and comparators - Burbea compares his view with Kabbalah, Buddha nature, and Jungian/archetypal language to show how different traditions authorize meaning and interpretation.
  5. 24:50 Three modes of practice - He defines Dharma practice as many ways of looking, then sketches three broad modes: bare attention, decreasing fabrication, and skillful fabrication.
  6. 30:31 Culture and modernity - The talk widens into a critique of modern objectivity and measurement, drawing on Tarnas to describe the loss of emotional, aesthetic, ethical, and imaginative reality.
  7. 36:04 Hospitals and mythos - Using medical settings as an example, Burbea asks what happens when soulfulness is crowded out, then turns to the unavoidable mythic and imaginal frameworks around practice, food, and renunciation.
  8. 46:21 Emptiness frees imagination - He argues that deep understanding of emptiness frees fabrication rather than opposing it, and that image, fantasy, and conceptual flexibility can be part of practice without literalism.
  9. 01:00:21 Mythic life and soul story - In the closing movement, Burbea draws on Jung, Hillman, Homer, Proclus, and Ibn Arabi to suggest that we live within a larger soul story, one that reconnects life with archetype, depth, and the vertical dimension.
