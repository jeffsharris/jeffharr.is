Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Cittamatra

Dharma Seed 61:46 2009-02-02

What if the suffering you take to be solid is being built, moment by moment, by the mind itself? The talk moves from a recap of three-characteristics practice into a more expansive inquiry: how self, emotions, pain, and even the world can seem substantial, then loosen into a spacious awareness that holds everything without strain. From there it opens into Chittamatra, using the language of mind-only, dream, and impressions in awareness to show one way this practice can be developed, while also warning that it is only a provisional step and not the final view.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening Questions - He begins by addressing confusion about the retreat's direction and whether emptiness requires unusual intellectual ability.
  2. 03:27 What Practice Is For - He reasserts that the point of Dharma is the ending of suffering and the increase of love and compassion.
  3. 05:38 Self and Suffering - He returns to the emptiness of self, then broadens the question to the kinds of things people actually suffer over in daily life.
  4. 10:03 From Things To Experience - He explains that emotions, pain, and even the world are seen as empty because the mind gives them solidity and fabricates experience itself.
  5. 20:02 Three Characteristics To Space - He recaps how the three characteristics can relax suffering and open into equanimity, listening, and a vast field of awareness.
  6. 23:31 Impressions In Awareness - He develops the image of all phenomena as impressions in awareness, including the self-sense, and notes how letting go can reduce drama and increase freedom.
  7. 33:32 Awareness, Names, And Rigpa - He explores the beauty and provisional status of this spacious state, mentions labels like ground of being and Rigpa, and cautions against reifying them.
  8. 45:02 Mind-Only Reasoning - He introduces Cittamatra more directly, using the dream analogy and the co-arising of subject and object to question an independently existing world.
  9. 53:00 Provisional View - He closes by comparing the space-and-awareness emphasis with the stream-of-consciousness emphasis, then contrasts both with Madhyamaka's move to empty mind itself.
