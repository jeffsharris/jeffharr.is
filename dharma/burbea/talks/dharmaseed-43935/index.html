Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Clinging, Craving, Eros (Q & A)

Dharma Seed 43:11 2017-03-28

What if clinging is not something to eliminate, but a range of push-pull movements that can become either contracted and suffering or open and creative? In this question-and-answer session, the talk moves from defining craving, clinging, and eros to practical ways of working with them: bringing in the energy body, loosening the sense of reality, and letting images become more imaginal. Later, it widens into emptiness, soulmaking, and the possibility of relating to anger, old patterns, and even one’s own history as part of a larger transformation rather than a problem to be fixed.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Clinging and eros - Rob defines clinging broadly and distinguishes contractive craving from the opening quality of eros.
  2. 06:07 Fabrication and suffering - He places clinging in dependent origination, discusses papancha, and asks whether clinging is always unwholesome.
  3. 10:10 Transforming craving - Rob explains two main levers for shifting craving: involving the whole energy body and loosening the reality of the object through emptiness and imaginal perception.
  4. 19:28 Emptiness without forcing - He widens the frame to include non-fabrication, samadhi, and metta, and encourages a flexible, non-obsessive relationship to dissolution and old patterns.
  5. 24:13 Soul and self images - Rob suggests that self-images can become imaginal over time and that practice can move gently rather than by force.
  6. 25:49 Anger as alchemy - A question about anger opens into tantric transformation: letting the image carry the emotion, trusting it, and finding what is alive and potent in it.
  7. 33:54 How to recognize imaginal - Rob responds to examples of light, story, and recurring figures, and clarifies that imaginal practice is recognized by its soulmaking qualities, not by the image alone.
  8. 41:11 Relational images - He finishes by noting that some images are brief while others become long-term relationships, and that the imaginal can spread into life and the wider world.
