Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Preliminaries, Regarding Voice, Movement, and Gesture (Part 3)

Dharma Seed 1:21:36 2020-03-03

Rob Burbea begins by using a personal period of being overwhelmed to question the ordinary idea of “centering,” then opens that term into a wider critique of selfhood shaped by interiority, boundaries, and singularity. From there he contrasts apophatic emptiness with an imaginal sense of angel, soul, image, and participation, and shows how those terms can change how difficulty, practice, and relationship are understood. The talk closes by introducing a dyad exercise in blessing through movement, gesture, and voice, first with meaning and then with a more stripped-down mode that lets expression move beyond ordinary language.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Opening and orientation - Rob previews the exercise to come and spends time clarifying the vocabulary he wants to examine first.
  2. 06:11 What centering implies - He uses a recent period of pressure and disorientation to question centering as a model of self, boundary, and inner core.
  3. 17:17 Emptiness and imaginal presence - He contrasts Christian centering prayer and apophatic emptiness with the angelic or imaginal mode he is emphasizing.
  4. 27:30 Stretch, devotion, and risk - He links soul, reverence, humility, eros, and antinomy to the conditions under which imaginal presence may arise.
  5. 37:24 Soulmaking and the person - He compares soulmaking language with aggregate-based analysis, arguing that soul, image, and angel keep personhood and relationality in view.
  6. 52:17 Meaning, participation, and image - He deepens the discussion of angelic and imaginal language as unfathomable, participatory, and not simply one or two.
  7. 01:00:14 First blessing exercise - He introduces the dyad practice in which one person blesses and the other receives through movement, gesture, and voice without known language.
  8. 01:14:13 Beyond ordinary meaning - He offers a second mode of the same exercise that drops any attempt to convey meaning, story, or emotion, and lets the practice move beyond blessing in the usual sense.
