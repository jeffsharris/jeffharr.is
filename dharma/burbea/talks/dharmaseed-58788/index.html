Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Phoenix of Metaphysics (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 2:30:30 2019-06-04

Rob Burbea traces how the classical scientific worldview came to feel like common sense, then shows how quantum physics and later philosophy unsettle that picture of objective, observer-independent reality. From there he turns toward the implications for emptiness, epistemology, and the question of how imaginal and soulmaking practices can be trusted without collapsing into either literalism or vagueness. The talk moves gradually from physics into philosophy, then into the practical problem of how to live with rigor, openness, and a more poetic way of knowing.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Opening the inquiry - He frames ontology and epistemology as the twin questions behind metaphysics and explains why they matter for practice and culture.
  2. 06:17 Knowledge and social order - Using Moscovici and Foucault, he argues that what a culture counts as real is tied to respect, hierarchy, and power.
  3. 11:28 Classical worldview - He sketches the scientific revolution and the mechanistic, clockwork picture associated with Galileo, Descartes, Locke, and Newton.
  4. 25:28 Quantum fracture - Wave-particle duality, superposition, and the observer’s role begin to break the old model of fixed, independently existing things.
  5. 34:52 What science reveals - With Victor Mansfield and related physics, he explores how observation, measurement, and the past itself become less fixed than common sense assumes.
  6. 54:43 Paradigms and truth - He turns to Kuhn, Marshall Walker, Laudan, and Lakatos to question whether science is moving toward truth or just solving problems within paradigms.
  7. 01:09:38 Truth, models, and realism - The talk develops distinctions between correspondence and coherence, then shows how anti-realist claims often smuggle realism back in.
  8. 01:34:15 Deconstruction and assumptions - He follows deconstruction, Descartes, Peirce, and Newton to show how hidden assumptions and motivations shape even claims to objectivity.
  9. 01:59:25 Epistemology as practice - He brings the discussion back to Buddhist epistemology, Yeats, Corbin, and the imaginal middle way, asking how soulmaking perceptions can be trusted and lived.
