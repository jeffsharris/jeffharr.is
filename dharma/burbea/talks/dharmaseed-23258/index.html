Right Action on Climate

Dharma Seed 49:44 2014-04-20

Ayya Anandabodhi speaks about climate change as an ethical and Dharma issue, beginning with the sense of Earth as sacred and moving through reasons Buddhists may feel hesitant to engage. She uses the Buddha’s care for a sick monk, Joanna Macy’s phrase about honoring pain for the world, and a series of concrete suggestions to show how grief can become steadiness and action. The talk then widens into practical organizing: speaking truthfully, joining groups, writing letters, divesting, and making personal and political commitments without losing compassion.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Earth as sacred - She opens with a felt sense of the living earth, the Welsh landscape, and the possibility of remembering sacred relationship with land.
  2. 02:01 Why Buddhists hesitate - She names the Buddhist tendency toward apathy or withdrawal around climate change and questions the idea that impermanence justifies inaction.
  3. 03:51 The sick monk - She recounts the Buddha bathing an abandoned sick monk as a model for insight joined with care and responsibility.
  4. 06:20 Climate as ethics - She parallels the monk’s abandonment with humanity’s treatment of the planet and asks whether practice can include turning toward harm rather than avoiding it.
  5. 09:47 Pressure and renewal - She places the crisis within a wider Buddhist cosmology of world cycles, then emphasizes that human choices can still speed up destruction or support renewal.
  6. 13:28 Resilience through practice - She speaks about congruence, self-respect, honoring pain for the world, and letting grief transform into strength rather than despair.
  7. 16:32 Concrete commitments - She introduces the shared action list and the Pali term adhiṭṭhāna as a firm resolve to choose a period of practice and action.
  8. 19:58 What to do now - She recommends resources, urges climate truth-telling, and gives practical examples of petitions, rallies, letters, government pressure, and organized collective action.
  9. 34:10 Questions and everyday choices - In the final exchange she discusses renewables, divestment, local banks, food choices, and the value of imperfect but sincere action guided by the larger systemic picture.
