Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Talk Five: Samatha, Nibbana, and the Emptiness of Perception - The Relationship Between Concentration and Insight

Dharma Seed 60:37 2008-08-12

Rob Burbea explores samatha as more than calm: a way of seeing how the mind builds suffering, self, and world. He moves from ordinary agitation and the inner critic into the relationship between concentration and insight, then into jhana, dependent origination, emptiness, and nibbana as progressively less fabricated experience. The talk closes with Rumi’s “Wean Yourself,” used as a quiet image of gradual release rather than sudden escape.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the Critic - Burbea begins by acknowledging the inner critic and setting a tone of making room for whatever is already present.
  2. 02:50 Samatha and Insight - He lays out concentration as more than preparation for insight, describing how calm nourishes the soil in which insight can actually take root.
  3. 10:59 The Mind Builds - Using dependent origination, he shows how the mind compounds suffering, perception, self, and world, and how that question reaches toward awakening.
  4. 19:45 Jhana as Perception - He turns to the jhanas as refined perceptions, tracing how the body and world can be experienced as pleasure, stillness, space, consciousness, and nothingness.
  5. 25:12 Release and Relief - Burbea emphasizes samadhi as a felt release from hindrances, story, and agitation, and describes how repeated calm undermines the apparent solidity of pain and emotion.
  6. 33:15 Perception Depends - He gives the retreat example of pain changing with attention, then draws out the deeper point that perception depends on mind state and is empty of fixed existence.
  7. 40:36 Fabricating Experience - The talk becomes more explicit about how attention, aversion, and identification glue experience together and make it seem solid.
  8. 45:13 Nibbana and the Unfabricated - He connects the quieting of fabrication with nibbana, the deathless, and the radical question of what is actually real and not built by mind.
  9. 58:04 Rumi and Weaning - Burbea closes with Rumi’s poem to suggest a gradual weaning from attachment, including attachment to states of samadhi themselves.
