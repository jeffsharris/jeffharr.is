Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

An Introduction to the Jhanas

Dharma Seed 1:10:31 2019-12-18

What is jhāna really for: a series of neatly labeled attainments, or a way of deepening well-being, freedom, and the training of perception? The talk moves from the Buddha’s four form jhānas and the formless attainments, through cautionary images about not rushing ahead, into a larger argument that practice should build sensitivity, discernment, and the capacity to let go. Rather than treating jhāna as a badge or a test of whether thought has stopped, the speaker keeps returning to what actually changes the being, and how that change relates to insight, emptiness, and the rest of the path.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the territory - Burbea frames the talk as a broad introduction and defines jhāna within the larger field of samādhi and samatha.
  2. 02:19 Buddha’s four form jhānas - He reads the Buddha’s descriptions of the first four jhānas and pauses to unpack the images of bath powder, a spring-fed lake, lotuses, and a body wrapped in white cloth.
  3. 09:38 The formless attainments - He briefly names the four formless jhānas and situates them as part of the same canonical map.
  4. 11:52 What really matters - Burbea turns from definitions to how he teaches: the importance of not getting stuck on category lines, the value of enjoyment, and the need to avoid reducing practice to a technical puzzle.
  5. 24:26 Mastery and marination - He explains what he means by mastery: thorough familiarity, the ability to enter and move among jhānas, and the need to stay long enough for the practice to change the mind and body.
  6. 33:45 Pitfalls and differing views - Burbea reviews common disputes about jhāna, uses the foolish cow image to warn against rushing on, and asks what is actually fruitful rather than merely impressive.
  7. 48:20 Why practice jhāna - He lays out several functions of jhāna: deep resource, insight into fabrication, and mystical opening, while also saying that jhāna alone is not enough for liberation.
  8. 55:04 Perception and emptiness - The talk shifts into Burbea’s larger framing: jhāna as training perception, not just concentration, and as something that belongs alongside emptiness practice, psychological work, and virtue.
  9. 01:04:30 Walking, rhythm, and closing - He gives brief guidance on walking meditation, posture, pacing, and how to let the day breathe, then closes the session and sends people to tea.
