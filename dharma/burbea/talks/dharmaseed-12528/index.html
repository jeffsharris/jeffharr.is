Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Impermanence, Love, Emptiness (Question and Answer Session 3)

Dharma Seed 1:14:54 2011-02-14

Fear opens the talk: a listener describes terror in the stomach after seeing that what seemed solid and true may be only a mental construction, and the reply begins by naming that as a very common edge of emptiness. From there the talk moves carefully between spaciousness and care—meeting fear directly when needed, but also using metta and samadhi as cushions, and learning when to back off rather than over-effort. It then turns to love, consciousness, and emptiness as provisional ways of seeing, and to the deeper question of how practice can also expose the self that is doing the looking.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Fear at the edge - A listener describes terror as assumptions about reality begin to fail, and Rob begins by normalizing the reaction.
  2. 03:03 Groundlessness and fear - He reframes emptiness as groundlessness and shows how fear arises when the usual sense of solidity drops away.
  3. 08:08 Kindness as cushion - Rob explains how metta and samadhi can steady practice, and how to switch gears when fear becomes too strong.
  4. 14:21 Love and consciousness - The discussion opens out into love, consciousness, space, and silence, with emphasis on experience rather than fixed definitions.
  5. 27:20 Concepts as rafts - Using the Buddha’s raft analogy, Rob says concepts like love, consciousness, and impermanence are useful tools that are eventually released.
  6. 37:33 The question of effort - He gives a sustained teaching on effort, warning against both strain and a false non-doing, and emphasizing felt sensitivity.
  7. 47:04 Turning inquiry inward - Rob shows how impermanence practice can be turned back onto the self-sense and the one who is looking.
  8. 52:19 Self, moments, and change - He works through examples of observer identity, the feeling of a bad day, and how looking closely reveals constructed solidity.
  9. 01:01:18 Naming the self - The final section explores self-definitions, how to write them down or question them directly, and how such inquiry can loosen identity.
