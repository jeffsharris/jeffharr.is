Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Death

Dharma Seed 63:39 2008-03-07

Death is treated here as something to look at directly, not a topic to avoid. The talk moves from the shock of mortality into practical ways of using its contemplation: to wake up urgency, clarify what matters, and loosen grasping at comfort, pleasure, and security. Along the way, the speaker folds in stories, poems, and Buddhist teachings to show how death practice can lead not only to sobriety, but sometimes to joy, wonder, and a stronger wish to give.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Why Contemplate Death - Burbea introduces death as a necessary theme if practice is to lead toward freedom, truth, and love.
  2. 04:12 Not Depressing, But Liberating - He shows how death contemplation can open joy, using a friend's encounter with a snake skull and his own nightly reflections after his father's sudden death.
  3. 09:00 Preciousness And Impermanence - A Merwin poem and Buddhist quotations frame death as a way to feel the preciousness of life and the urgency of practice.
  4. 17:02 Urgency And What Matters - Patrul Rinpoche, the sudden death of a yogi, and examples from ordinary life point to uncertainty, regret, and the question of how to live well.
  5. 25:59 Choices, Ethics, And Insight - He examines accumulation, generosity, moral regret, and delusion as factors that can make death easier or harder.
  6. 32:17 Refuge In The Deathless - The Buddha's encounter with aging, sickness, and death leads into the search for something beyond what also dies, including a discussion of rebirth.
  7. 39:14 Body, Earth, And Recycling - Burbea reflects on poetic and practical ways of seeing the body as part of a larger cycle, using Gibran, Thich Nhat Hanh, and simple elemental contemplation.
  8. 48:14 Impermanence In Practice - He turns to moment-to-moment change and the Buddha's verses on a life of virtue, discernment, and clear seeing of impermanence.
  9. 53:02 Experience, Emptiness, And Freedom - The talk closes by questioning what life and death are when experience itself is seen as fabricated, ending with the Buddha's images of the world as play, bubble, and mirage.
