Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Aspects of the Imaginal (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 53:02 2017-12-15

Rob Burbea opens a new short course on the imaginal by situating it as a clarification of earlier soulmaking teachings rather than a new system. He distinguishes ordinary imagination, papañca, metta imagery, and healing images from what he means by "imaginal," then introduces "sensing with soul" as an alternate way in. The talk ends by outlining a careful spectrum from ordinary imagination toward the fuller imaginal, with attention to the role of love, eros, beauty, autonomy, humility, grace, and soul-making.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Course framing - Rob frames the series as a supplement to earlier soulmaking talks and emphasizes pacing, digestion, and clarification over adding new material.
  2. 05:29 What imaginal means - He begins to define the imaginal and introduces "sensing with soul" as nearly interchangeable language that may make the teaching easier to hear.
  3. 11:57 Different practitioner entry points - Rob sketches three practitioner situations: those who do not get images, those unsure whether their images are imaginal, and those who sense the sacred in the world but not inner images.
  4. 15:39 Ontology and knowing - He turns briefly to what is real and how we know, arguing that imaginal practice questions ordinary assumptions about ontology and epistemology.
  5. 18:28 Imagination in practice - Rob distinguishes papañca from mindful, embodied imagination and gives examples from metta and healing imagery, including figures such as Tara, the Buddha, Jesus, or a grandmother.
  6. 23:55 Naming the tradition - He pauses to say that his use of imaginal is specific, noting overlap and difference with Henri Corbin, James Hillman, Tom Cheetham, Philip Pullman, and Mary Watkins, then begins listing core qualities.
  7. 31:30 Core qualities of the imaginal - Rob names a set of qualities such as beyondness, autonomy, two-ness, mutual mirroring, meaningfulness, theater, conceptual structure, timelessness, humility, reverence, grace, and soulfulness.
  8. 39:24 Why lists matter - He explains why the list is useful despite his ambivalence, using the Buddha as a model for enumerated teaching and saying these elements can act as ignition keys for practice.
  9. 47:34 A dynamic spectrum - The talk closes by stressing that imaginal experience is dynamic rather than fixed, and that the question is how to move toward the fuller, more fertile end of the spectrum.
