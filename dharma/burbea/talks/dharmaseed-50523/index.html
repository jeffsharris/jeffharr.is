Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

A Temenos Exercise (in threes)

Dharma Seed 1:23:19 2018-02-07

This talk offers a structured pair and small-group exercise that begins with grounding in the body, then widens into sensing others, metta, and the shared field created by speaking and listening. It moves gradually from simple presence to a more imaginal and relational way of noticing what happens in the body, heart, mind, and between people. Near the end, Rob links the exercise to a Primordial Cosmic Buddha chant, emphasizing that practice can include the full range of experience, not only what feels pleasant or clear.

Chapters

  1. 01:05 Settling Into Ground - The group is invited to settle and ground in bodily contact, earth, and sitting, with attention to how grounding can be sensed in multiple ways.
  2. 02:10 Opening the Energy Body - Awareness expands from the grounded body into a larger energy body space, with emphasis on bright, sensitive presence that may contract and reopen.
  3. 03:40 Sensing Self and Others - With eyes open, the practice turns toward holding one's own presence and the presence of the two companions at once.
  4. 05:47 Metta in the Circle - Rob introduces goodwill toward the other two, including giving and receiving metta while staying open through the senses.
  5. 08:36 First Round of Sharing - The exercise is explained: one person speaks briefly about their energy body while the others witness silently and track their own process.
  6. 18:45 Dependent Origination in Dialogue - Rob highlights how speaking, being seen, and listening alter experience moment by moment, and asks participants to include those effects in awareness.
  7. 27:06 Second Round: Emotions - The group returns to metta and then expands the sharing to include emotions, while witnesses remain fully present and sensitive.
  8. 32:05 Reverberation and Soul - After more rounds, the practice includes pauses, echoes, and then a deeper inquiry into what has touched the soul or feels meaningful.
  9. 47:00 Primordial Buddha Chant - Rob frames the final round and closes with the 'Om Ah Hum' chant, describing it as inclusive of light and dark, suffering and bliss, stillness and movement.
