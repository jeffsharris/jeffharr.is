Contemplating The 3 Characteristics (Pt 2) - Anatta (Not-Self)

Dharma Seed 56:21 2005-11-16

What happens when the sense of self is not taken as a fixed fact, but as something that keeps appearing, tightening, and releasing? The talk moves from the felt pain of self-referencing through the five aggregates, then into subtler territory: even awareness can become an identity unless it too is seen through. It stays practical throughout, with suggestions for noticing “not me, not mine” in ordinary experience, while also warning against turning anatta into another view to cling to.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Framing Anatta - He introduces this second talk on the three characteristics and sets up anatta as something to practice with, not just understand conceptually.
  2. 02:34 Why Self Matters - He shows how suffering, comparison, and self-referencing shape ordinary life and why investigating self is central to freedom.
  3. 06:22 Pain And Self-View - Through examples of success, failure, unworthiness, and inflated self-image, he describes how any self-view leads to fear and pain.
  4. 14:12 Fear And Appreciation - He distinguishes healthy practice from forcing, acknowledges fear of annihilation, and suggests moving slowly while appreciating any sense of release.
  5. 20:12 Buddha And Practical View - He tells the story of the Buddha refusing to answer self/not-self questions directly and uses it to emphasize freedom through a way of seeing.
  6. 24:10 Five Aggregates Practice - He introduces the five aggregates and the simple instruction to regard body, feeling, thought, and consciousness as not me and not mine.
  7. 30:04 Avoiding Experience Clinging - He warns against using the practice to chase a special state, then recalls his own resistance before finding the teaching deeply liberating.
  8. 35:48 Objects And Awareness - He extends the contemplation from objects to awareness itself, showing how identification can shift into witness consciousness and needs to be released more subtly.
  9. 48:14 Mutuality And The Self Agenda - He argues that self and world arise together, then closes by describing the self-oriented question 'What's in it for me?' and the radiance that appears when that agenda loosens.
