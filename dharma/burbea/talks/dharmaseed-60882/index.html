Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The First Jhana, and Playing and Working in (and out of) any Jhana

Dharma Seed 1:30:25 2019-12-22

How do you work with jhāna without turning it into a test of whether you’ve gotten it right? The talk starts with the first jhāna’s soap simile and then keeps returning to the same practical question: how to let pīti spread through the body, sustain it, and use it as a living basis for practice rather than a fixed achievement. From there it ranges into the meanings of key Pali terms, the role of signs like light, the place of walking meditation, and how to move in and out of jhāna without losing the sense of play. It stays grounded in concrete instructions for building familiarity, re-entering states, and noticing what actually deepens the mind.

Chapters

  1. 01:42 First Jhana Simile - Burbea reads the Buddha’s soap simile and uses it to frame first jhana as whole-body suffusion with pīti and sukha.
  2. 08:47 Later Terms, Earlier Practice - He contrasts later interpretive traditions with older meanings of vitakka and vicara, especially through Buddhaghosa and his own teacher’s translation choices.
  3. 12:08 Forget the Labels - Rather than over-analyzing terminology, he recommends entering the felt experience directly and treating mental commentary as secondary.
  4. 15:40 Pīti as a Sign - He explains piti as evidence that samadhi is deepening and broadens nimitta into any supportive sign, from happiness to light to stillness.
  5. 19:32 What First Jhana Needs - Burbea lays out how strong, sustained pīti becomes workable, how the body grows subtler across the form jhanas, and why pīti remains a resource even when deeper states appear.
  6. 27:05 Different Practice Grooves - He compares jhana practice with insight-style training, noting that some practitioners arrive closer to equanimity or later states and may need a different entry point.
  7. 34:51 Work, Play, and SASSIE - The talk shifts into a mnemonic for mastering jhana through suffusion, absorption, sustaining, subtle shifts, intensity, and enjoyment.
  8. 40:06 Malleability of Perception - Burbea shows how pīti can spread, how pain and light can be worked with as perceptions, and why absorption is less a fixed achievement than a flexible field of practice.
  9. 53:02 Enjoyment and Adjustment - He emphasizes maximizing enjoyment, noticing resistance to pleasure, and using light, responsive adjustments to deepen practice without self-consciousness.
