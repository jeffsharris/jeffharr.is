Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

No Self, No World

Dharma Seed 63:22 2009-05-15

What if the question is not whether the self exists, but whether a way of seeing is reducing suffering? The talk moves from a pragmatic reading of anatta into a close look at how self is built through story, past and future, and the subtle habits of push and pull that hold experience in place. From there it opens into the sense that self and world arise together, so that loosening one loosens the other, and the talk keeps returning to practice as a way of looking rather than a fixed view. It ends by widening this into dependent origination, with a strong warning not to turn emptiness into nihilism or a new philosophy to defend.

Chapters

  1. 00:42 Anatta as practice - Burbea frames not-self as a skillful way of reducing suffering rather than a doctrine to defend.
  2. 02:24 Self and not-self - He argues that self-view and not-self can both be useful, depending on what helps in a given situation.
  3. 05:54 Healing through self - A story about childhood criticism and psychotherapy shows why some forms of self-language can support healing.
  4. 12:37 Examining identity - Using examples from fashion, monastic life, and social status, he shows how quickly identity can become rigid and performative.
  5. 17:53 Mindfulness and story - Sustained mindfulness exposes gaps in self-definitions and shows how the self is built through past and future.
  6. 21:41 Ways of conceiving - He turns to the Buddha’s analysis of self in relation to body, feelings, perceptions, formations, and consciousness, and treats it as a meditation practice.
  7. 29:08 Self and world together - The talk broadens from not-me, not-mine to the co-arising of self and world, with the Buddha’s end-of-the-world story pointing to a deeper seeing.
  8. 45:13 Fabrication and lessening - Burbea explains that experience is fabricated and that seeing the building process more clearly lets self and world diminish together.
  9. 53:32 Emptiness beyond extremes - He closes by linking emptiness to dependent origination, rejecting nihilism and fixed views in favor of a quieter, freer understanding.
