Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Contact and The Construction of Self

Dharma Seed 60:09 2011-02-12

How does suffering get built, and what happens if you stop feeding it? The talk traces emptiness as the construction of self, moving from the ways selfing intensifies through comparison, views, and aversion to two practical responses: loosening the story and staying at bare contact. It then turns to vedana, showing how pain, labeling, and the push away can thicken into a whole drama, and how simply staying with sensation can sometimes let that loosen or even dissolve.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the theme - Burbea introduces the retreat focus on emptiness as a practical inquiry into how the self gets constructed.
  2. 01:35 Noticing construction - He returns to Catherine McGee’s prior talk and emphasizes seeing self-building in the midst of suffering and conflict.
  3. 04:04 Views and attention - The talk turns to how belief, interpretation, and mental complexity keep the construction process going, with the geranium example illustrating the point.
  4. 09:59 Body and aversion - He explains how bodily contraction and aversion are part of self-construction, and how allowing sensation can reduce the process.
  5. 15:28 Comparison and conceit - Burbea explores the comparing mind, the habits of measuring self against others, and how metta can soften that pattern.
  6. 20:32 Bare contact practice - He introduces staying at contact and bare attention as a second mode of practice, using hearing and simple sensation to stay close to experience.
  7. 25:51 Openness and luminosity - Questions lead him to distinguish directed and open attention, and to note that bare attention can reveal brightness, beauty, and ease.
  8. 31:15 Addiction to self-making - Burbea describes the habit of constructing a self as a kind of addiction rooted in mistaken ideas about happiness and reality.
  9. 41:50 Vedana and listening - He introduces vedana as the feeling tone present in every experience and shows how receptive listening can open the mind.
