Refractions: Of Body, Sensuality, and Sexuality (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 1:33:29 2017-02-13

What if the problem is not too much desire, but how quickly desire is pathologized and flattened into ideas of balance, health, or bare attention? The talk moves from intense spiritual longing, through examples like Thomas Merton and the Buddha, into a critique of modern Dharma assumptions about sensuality, practice, and the senses. It then opens into a wider argument about beauty, eros, soulmaking, and why a richer view of fabrication may matter for art, nature, and the ecological imagination.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the inquiry - He introduces the talk as a way of widening questions about body, sensuality, and sexuality rather than settling them.
  2. 03:08 Intensity and pathologizing - He argues that strong eros, whether spiritual, artistic, or sexual, is often misread as extreme or unhealthy.
  3. 07:32 Merton and total commitment - Thomas Merton's longing to give himself to God is used to show how devotion can look excessive from the outside.
  4. 11:31 Moderation and its limits - He questions the cultural elevation of balance and psychological health when it becomes a way of shrinking soulmaking.
  5. 18:06 Eros and the senses - He begins to connect eros with body, sensuality, and sexuality as fields of potentially endless opening.
  6. 28:42 Three views of practice - He lays out three ways of relating to sense experience: bare attention, transcendence beyond the senses, and a soulmaking or imaginal approach.
  7. 52:53 Beauty, depth, and belonging - He develops beauty as more than pleasantness, tying it to depth, intimacy, and the feeling of belonging to something larger.
  8. 58:44 Practice styles and flatland - He contrasts Mahasi-style insight and non-doing approaches with a view that preserves mystery, otherness, and erotic possibility.
  9. 01:20:18 Fabrication and sacredness - He closes by linking beauty to fabrication, poetry, nature, and ecology, arguing that recognizing illusion need not diminish the sacred.
