Connecting Inner and Outer Responses to Climate Change

Dharma Seed 37:28 2013-04-20

Donald Rothberg offers a grounded talk on climate change as a call to join inner practice with outer response. He moves from a short meditation invocation into a critique of spiritual escapism and reactive activism, then turns to the bodhisattva ideal as a model for sustained, compassionate engagement. The talk ends with practical reflections on personal, communal, and collective action, and with brief readings that hold urgency and tenderness together.

Chapters

  1. 02:14 Opening Invocation - Rothberg begins with a short meditation on spacious, steady awareness and uses it to dissolve the split between inner practice and the earth.
  2. 05:32 Why Integration Matters - He lays out the central theme: without connecting practice and action, Dharma can become escapism and activism can become reactive or self-righteous.
  3. 09:24 The Bodhisattva Ideal - Drawing on bodhisattva vows across traditions, he presents the bodhisattva as the figure who can hold awakening and service together.
  4. 14:29 Finding Your Calling - Rothberg uses Howard Thurman and Joanna Macy to suggest that people have different roles in the great turning and should find the work that makes them come alive.
  5. 18:16 Mindfulness Must Act - He quotes Thich Nhat Hanh to show engaged Buddhism as a decision to practice and help at the same time, especially in the face of suffering.
  6. 19:13 Dharma and Climate Practice - The talk turns to ethics, wisdom, and the need to widen practice beyond the individual into relational and collective life, including concrete examples from Spirit Rock and Berkeley.
  7. 24:27 Personal to Systemic Response - He moves through household changes, transportation, and larger climate strategies, arguing that personal discipline matters most when it supports systemic change.
  8. 28:44 Reflective Exercise - Rothberg invites listeners to name what calls them now, then asks them to share with a neighbor where they feel drawn to respond.
  9. 36:07 Closing Readings - He ends with short readings from Rabbi Tarfon, a Zen teacher, and Deena Metzger, emphasizing steady work, ordinary practice, and love in urgent times.
