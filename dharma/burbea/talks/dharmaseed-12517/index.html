Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Questioning Reality (Question and Answer Session 5)

Dharma Seed 1:28:18 2011-02-19

In this question-and-answer session, Rob Burbea explores how emptiness practice can feel freeing and unsettling at the same time, especially when it touches thought, emotion, and the sense of self. He keeps returning to balance: when to soften with metta, when to stay with anatta practice, and how to avoid confusing emptiness with flatness or disconnection. The talk also widens into how perception, knowing, and clinging all shape the way reality is built in experience.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the questions - Rob begins by orienting to the session and turns to the first question about a strong reaction to not me, not mine practice.
  2. 05:07 Balancing with metta - He reassures the listener that the practice is probably out of balance rather than wrong, and suggests metta or other calming supports.
  3. 11:46 What disidentifying means - Rob explains the logic of disidentifying with body, thoughts, emotions, intentions, and then even awareness itself.
  4. 17:17 Perception and knowing - He develops the distinction between perception and knowing, using sight and hearing to show how awareness and object arise together.
  5. 24:12 Anatta and ownership - The talk moves into the deeper meaning of not mine, including the Buddha's teaching on the body and a brief nod to analytical deconstruction.
  6. 28:45 Trees, beauty, and fading - A question about seeing trees leads into how less selfing can also mean less object-making, and eventually the world can seem to fade or dissolve.
  7. 33:13 Flatness or aversion - Rob addresses the fear that emptiness leads to blandness, suggesting that hidden aversion is often the real issue and that more metta may be needed.
  8. 46:22 Healthy clinging and desire - After a quiet pause, he turns to healthy clinging, explaining how desire, commitment, and practice energy can be gathered rather than scattered.
  9. 59:59 Three angles of practice - In the closing stretch he compares impermanence, emptiness, and anatta to three points of a triangle, encouraging a flexible, experimental approach to practice.
