Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Trust, Ethics, Image (Q & A)

Dharma Seed 34:37 2018-06-24

How do you work with aggression, erotic charge, and the fear that imaginal practice might spill into ordinary life? The talk answers by moving from caution and self-inhibition toward a slower, more spacious way of relating to images: holding them in metta, noticing their energy-body resonance, and letting them become less literal and more dimensional. A second question takes up whether an image that feels beautiful but unfinished should be pushed further or left alone, and the response keeps returning to pacing, grace, and not pressuring the psyche. Across the Q&A, the central thread is that trust grows gradually, and what seems dangerous or egoic may open into something more sacred when it is neither flattened nor forced.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 The question of aggression - A practitioner asks how to work with violent impulses, metta, and uncertainty about what is real or controllable.
  2. 02:33 Healthy doubt - Rob reassures that concern and discrimination are signs of health, and places the question within the ethical seriousness of practice.
  3. 04:46 Beyond repression - He explains how aggression and eros may become more imaginal and less pressured, rather than being flattened into action or simple inhibition.
  4. 09:23 Trusting the imaginal - Rob describes the slow development of trust, the role of body-sense over mental suspicion, and the need to give an image a limited field of attention.
  5. 12:24 Warrior and seduction - Using examples from his own practice, he shows how aggressive and erotic images can become holy, mysterious, and ethically contained.
  6. 17:10 A question of navigation - A second question asks how to work with an image that feels beautiful but not fully alive, and Rob warns against pressing too hard.
  7. 21:16 Grace and receptivity - He speaks about grace, resonance, and letting an image work on the heart and energy body rather than trying to force more from it.
  8. 24:36 Images and ethics - Rob addresses fears that imaginal work may conflict with personal ethics or strengthen unwanted energies outside practice.
  9. 28:39 Middle way of images - He closes by describing the imaginal as both me and not me, real and not real, and as a path toward freedom, sacredness, and a less ego-centered practice.
