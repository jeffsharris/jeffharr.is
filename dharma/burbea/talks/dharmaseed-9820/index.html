The Space of Awareness - Only Impressions in Awareness (3rd Guided Meditation)

Dharma Seed 40:25 2009-09-11

The talk begins by acknowledging that the afternoon meditations may feel different, then quietly shows how they are all ways of entering the same territory. It moves from kindness and settling in the body, to sensing stillness, opening to sound and silence, and then widening into an image of awareness as open sky in which thoughts, sensations, and even self-images pass like clouds, bubbles, waves, or reflections. Again and again, the emphasis is on letting experience arise and fade without clinging or resistance, and on noticing the undisturbed space that already holds it all.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Opening the retreat field - He begins by noting that the afternoon meditations may seem different, while pointing to them as different ways into the same territory.
  2. 00:42 Settling with kindness - The practice starts with posture, kindness, aspiration, and letting the body gather into calm.
  3. 02:29 Sensing stillness - Attention is guided toward bodily stillness and the stillness that can be felt in the whole field of experience.
  4. 04:33 Listening into silence - Awareness opens to sound, then to the silence around and between sounds, as if silence were the ground of hearing.
  5. 08:04 Sky of awareness - He introduces the image of awareness as open sky, with sensations and thoughts like brief lights, clouds, or fireworks passing through it.
  6. 12:13 Nothing to hold - The teaching emphasizes that whatever appears can be received naturally, without rejection, clinging, or interference, while awareness remains undisturbed.
  7. 22:23 Impressions in awareness - The imagery shifts to waves in the ocean and reflections on a lake, pointing to all experience as an impression that arises and fades within awareness.
  8. 31:53 Self as appearance - Even the sense of self and the image of oneself meditating are treated as further appearances, not something solid to identify with.
  9. 35:31 Qualities and aftercare - He closes by naming the vastness, peace, and receptivity of awareness, then suggests taking the practice into walking, standing, and ordinary settings.
