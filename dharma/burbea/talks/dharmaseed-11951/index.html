Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Exploring the World of Loving Kindness

Dharma Seed 1:11:25 2008-01-30

Can loving-kindness stay true when it meets need, hurt, anger, and the wish to be seen? The talk moves from the feel of metta in the body, through the near-enemy of attached love, into friendship, self-clinging, and the hard work of speaking honestly without harm. It then turns to anger, silence, and a gentler way of being in which love may feel less like something we make and more like something we learn to receive.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 What Metta Is - He begins by clarifying that metta is not just a feeling, but an intention that can show up in many textures and bodily sensations.
  2. 02:25 Compassion and Boundaries - He distinguishes metta from compassion, then explains the Buddha’s idea of the near-enemy as attached love that needs to be noticed without discouragement.
  3. 05:05 Self-Love as Foundation - He turns to self-metta, the cultural and personal reasons it can feel hard, and the Buddha’s line about finding no one more deserving of loving-kindness than oneself.
  4. 08:52 Friend, Benefactor, Near-Enemy - He outlines the practice categories and emphasizes that they are fluid tools meant to extend metta rather than fixed identities.
  5. 12:11 Friendship and Reparenting - Using friendship as an entry point, he reflects on fresh seeing, appreciation, being seen, and the way practice can function like a gradual reparenting.
  6. 17:12 Self, Clinging, and Identity - He moves into not-self, showing how personality is not denied but understood as conditioned, and how clinging builds the sense of a separate self.
  7. 22:39 Gibran on Friendship - A long passage from Khalil Gibran’s The Prophet is used to frame friendship as a place for generosity, honesty, and spiritual deepening rather than transaction.
  8. 26:08 Anger and Skillful Speech - He examines how clinging and anger reinforce selfing, then explores the hard work of hearing anger, saying no, and speaking what is true and helpful.
  9. 38:58 Conflict Without Harm - He gives concrete examples of careful confrontation, the fear of anger, and the practice of meeting hurt beneath reactivity without becoming a doormat.
