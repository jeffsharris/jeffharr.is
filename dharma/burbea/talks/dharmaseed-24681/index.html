Earth's Crisis - On the Edge of the Roof - (on climate change)

Dharma Seed 52:41 2014-09-17

Tara Brach explores climate crisis through Dharma, using Rumi, the Parsifal legend, and Joanna Macy to show how denial, grasping, and disconnection keep both people and the planet in a kind of trance. She moves from the scale of ecological loss to the inner work of feeling, presence, and belonging, then toward the question of what right action looks like without certainty about outcomes. The talk closes with a prayerful invitation to listen more deeply to the living earth and respond from care.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the crisis - Brach frames the climate emergency as both a planetary event and a spiritual challenge, linking ecological harm to the habits of indifference and waking up.
  2. 09:02 Rumi and Parsifal - Rumi's warning about being drunk at the edge of the roof becomes a bridge into the Parsifal legend, where one honest question restores life to a wasteland.
  3. 12:10 Trance and consumption - She describes the egoic trance as grasping, numbness, and objectifying the world, drawing on the Sutra on the Son's Flesh and the logic of endless consumption.
  4. 16:52 Separation and entitlement - The talk expands into a developmental view of separation, fear, and entitlement, including cultural patterns of growth, extraction, and a sense that humans stand apart from the earth.
  5. 25:00 Digital distance - A contemporary texting story leads into a reflection on how cyber-life can flatten depth, embodiment, and compassion, even while increasing information.
  6. 28:52 Earth as belonging - Through Joanna Macy and Chief Seattle, Brach emphasizes that the earth is not a supply source but a shared body, and that shutting down in fear is a central danger.
  7. 33:03 Feeling and action - She shifts from diagnosis to practice: deepening attention, feeling what is here, not relying on hope of results, and asking what the earth requires of us now.
  8. 42:31 Collective awakening - Brach points toward systemic change, community, and teachings from Paul Hawken, Darwin, and Emerson that reframe life as interdependent, urgent, and worthy of wonder.
  9. 50:33 Listening and prayer - The closing turns into a brief contemplative appeal to hear the animals, plants, and planet itself, and to let prayer become response.
