Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Between Ikon and Eidos: Image & Hermeneutics in Meditation (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 1:29:59 2018-01-09

Rob Burbea explores how ideas shape perception, practice, and even what feels possible in meditation. He moves from the cultural dominance of scientific materialism into a more nuanced account of imaginal perception, hermeneutics, and the role of emotion, value, and eros in knowing. The talk is part philosophical framing, part invitation to notice how assumptions can narrow or open the field of experience.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Why Grounding Matters - Burbea begins by explaining why sensing with soul can feel unfamiliar, risky, or illegitimate, and why it may need more grounding and space.
  2. 06:17 Ideas in Experience - He argues that ideas and concepts are always involved in perception, and that imaginal practice includes both images and values.
  3. 12:01 Eidos and Interpretation - The talk turns to eidos, hermeneutics, and the claim that we are always interpreting existence, whether consciously or not.
  4. 20:18 Science and Reality - Burbea sketches the classical scientific worldview, then shows how relativity and quantum mechanics unsettle its assumptions about objectivity.
  5. 32:04 Pauli, Jung, Symbols - He brings in Pauli and Jung to suggest that reality may be more symbolic and less straightforwardly material than modern habits assume.
  6. 40:15 Abstraction and Reduction - Using Feynman, Christian Fuchs, and William James, he critiques the habit of turning living experience into 'nothing but' abstract material causes.
  7. 51:05 The Moon Story - A personal example of a woman's relationship with the moon illustrates both the opening of imaginal perception and the doubts that can arise around it.
  8. 59:00 Epistemicide and Value - He argues that modern culture can invalidate emotion and imagination as ways of knowing, and shows how that affects how we understand suffering, beauty, and divinity.
  9. 01:14:33 Dharma and Eros - The talk closes by bringing the critique back to Dharma practice, warning against reductionist views and suggesting that eros and soulmaking widen the sense of awakening.
