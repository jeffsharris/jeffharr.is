Practice & Climate Change

Dharma Seed 48:46 2014-05-13

Thanissara reflects on practice as preparation for the moments when fear, grief, and collective crisis press on the heart. Using climate change as the main example, she draws practice toward refuge, resilience, and the possibility of responding from a steadier and less separative mind. The talk moves from basic mindfulness and ethics into samadhi, renunciation, and the quiet capacity to meet uncertainty without collapsing into reactivity.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Practice as preparation - The talk opens by framing retreat practice as training for the moments when strong experience hits the heart and we must choose between reactivity and wise pause.
  2. 02:46 Climate change and activation - Thanissara names climate change as a major pressure on the psyche and explains how it can trigger fear, overwhelm, and old patterns of distraction.
  3. 05:36 A shift in consciousness - She argues, with Einstein and Dharma language, that problems cannot be met from the same separative mindset that helps create them.
  4. 10:50 Refuge in Buddha Dharma Sangha - The talk unfolds the three refuges as living realities: inspiration, teachings, and the capacity to practice, rather than as mere formulas.
  5. 16:18 Ethics and conscience - Thanissara turns to the five precepts, conscience, and restraint as a grounded ethical base that protects both oneself and others.
  6. 19:30 Courage and resilience - Examples from Aung San Suu Kyi and the retreat context lead into a discussion of resilience as the ability to gather, steady, and return.
  7. 23:21 Knowing awareness directly - Ajahn Tate's distinction between awareness and mental activity becomes a way to practice with breath, recognize the listening heart, and hold grief without being ruled by it.
  8. 29:33 Simplify and let go - Using Ajahn Lee's bridge analogy and the principle of nekkhamma, she explains how ethics, samadhi, and wisdom support a life that can release grasping and consume less.
  9. 40:02 The peace that remains - The closing section returns to surrender, the naked moment of awareness, and a peace that can remain present even in unstable times, from which authentic response may emerge.
