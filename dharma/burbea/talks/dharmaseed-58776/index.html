Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Sila and Soul (Part 3)

Dharma Seed 1:50:31 2019-06-11

What if virtue is not just a means to an end, but something beautiful in itself? The talk begins by setting Buddhist sila beside an expanded discussion of moral values and virtues, then moves into a contrast between traditions that see the good as serving liberation and a view, drawn from Hartmann, that the morally good stands on its own. From there it opens into questions of human nature, the beauty of a life shaped by virtue, and the tension between doing what is effective and doing what is right when the outcome is uncertain. Examples from Bonhoeffer, the Titanic, Plato, and climate activism keep the discussion grounded in real situations where efficacy and soul-level integrity diverge.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Opening the question - Burbea introduces the talk’s focus on two ways imaginal practice can widen ethics, especially by bringing in ideas of values and virtues.
  2. 05:22 Values and virtues - He defines moral values, distinguishes them from virtues as dispositions, and begins to show why they matter beyond abstract theory.
  3. 11:27 Intention over result - The talk turns to the Buddhist emphasis on intention and disposition rather than outcome, with examples of harm, generosity, and practical wisdom.
  4. 20:23 Paramitas and release - Burbea compares virtues with the Buddhist perfections and argues that, in one traditional reading, the qualities serve liberation rather than existing as final ends.
  5. 28:52 Hartmann’s intrinsic good - He introduces Nikolai Hartmann’s claim that the morally good is good in itself and uses it to question a purely instrumental view of ethics.
  6. 40:05 Beauty, language, and soul - The discussion widens into etymology, the beauty of virtue, and the sense that virtues can be cultivated as part of a beautiful life and a fuller humanity.
  7. 01:07:38 Modern shifts in virtue - Using Paul Kugler, Jung, and MacIntyre, Burbea explores how language and modern moral thought have changed the way virtue is understood.
  8. 01:24:26 Virtue over efficacy - Bonhoeffer, the Titanic, and climate change illustrate cases where doing the right thing may matter even when results are limited or unclear.
  9. 01:41:01 Double intention - In the closing movement, Burbea questions Hartmann’s sharp divide between concern for the other and concern for one’s own moral being, suggesting a self-reflexive dimension in ethical action.
