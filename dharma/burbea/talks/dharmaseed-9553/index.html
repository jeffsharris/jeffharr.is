Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Subtlety of Dependent Origination

Dharma Seed 1:11:31 2010-02-13

Rob Burbea explores dependent origination through the lens of time, showing how past, future, and even the present can lose their apparent solidity in meditation. He connects this to selfing, object-perception, attention, and the subtle ways dukkha is built into experience, then uses Nagarjuna, Gampopa, Ajahn Mun, Mipham, and the Buddha’s awakening story to press the analysis deeper. The talk stays practical throughout: the aim is not abstraction, but usable ways of seeing emptiness that soften grasping and open freedom.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the Map - Rob sets the direction of the talk, linking emptiness, fearlessness, and the capacity to give before turning to dependent origination.
  2. 02:17 Why Time Matters - He introduces dependent origination at a subtler level and explains why time, not just self and habit, needs to be examined.
  3. 04:08 Time and Clinging - Using everyday and meditative examples, he shows how clinging makes time feel solid, immediate, and stressful.
  4. 09:40 Past, Future, Present - He tracks how past and future often fall away first, then questions whether even the present has inherent existence.
  5. 13:21 Seeing Through Now - Rob argues that the present cannot stand alone and that language, concepts, and the idea of a moment all depend on each other.
  6. 20:44 Nagarjuna on Arising - He uses Nagarjuna’s four alternatives and a related argument about the present and the past to show why arising cannot be inherent.
  7. 26:13 Self, Thing, Time - Rob shifts into a phenomenological model in which self, object, and time form a tripod that supports dukkha.
  8. 33:41 The Tripod of Dukkha - He develops the mutual dependence of self, thing, and time, then brings in the Buddha’s awakening story and the role of knowing, attention, and intention.
  9. 46:09 Attention and Conceiving - Rob unpacks how attention, object, and moment fabricate each other, and how conceiving subtly wraps time into perception.
