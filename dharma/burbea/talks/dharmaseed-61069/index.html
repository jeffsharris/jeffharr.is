The Ontology of the Soul, and the Soul of Ontology ( ... inevitably)

Dharma Seed 2:09:55 2020-02-12

What if the crucial question is not whether something is real, but what a given view of reality lets us see, value, and do? The talk moves from the Buddha’s middle way of emptiness, through the imaginal middle way, into a broader inquiry about conventional truths, soulmaking, and the limits of reductionist psychology. It then widens again to include ethics, eros, time, and healing, asking how our assumptions shape the worlds of self, other, and practice.

Chapters

  1. 00:43 Two Middle Ways - He opens by distinguishing the ordinary middle way from the ontological middle way in the Kaccayana Sutta.
  2. 02:39 Imaginal and Emptiness - He contrasts the imaginal middle way with the middle way of emptiness and clarifies why the two are not the same.
  3. 06:22 Practice and Ontology - He explains why emptiness arises from careful inquiry, while imaginal practice leaves ontology more open and flexible.
  4. 18:53 Conventional Reality - He turns to conventional truths, ethics, karma, and modern physics to show why emptiness does not settle every ontological question.
  5. 25:55 Soul and Reductionism - He defines soul as a way of looking, then critiques reductionist materialism and narrow phenomenology as inadequate accounts of human life.
  6. 38:43 Eros and Disclosure - He develops the idea that ontology can be soulmaking, drawing on John Milbank, Catherine Pickstock, and Descartes to argue that eros can disclose reality.
  7. 01:02:58 Conventional Truths at Work - He treats conventional truths as provisional, using examples from science, his own illness, and the language of duty, discipline, and sacrifice.
  8. 01:31:47 What Models Open - He tests psychological models by asking what they open or close, and presents the soulmaking dynamic as a useful predictive framework.
  9. 01:51:59 Time and Liberation - He closes with caution about shared images, then moves into Plotinus, Alexander Aphrodisias, and the possibility that time is dependently originated.
