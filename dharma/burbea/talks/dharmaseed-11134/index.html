Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Sense of Self

Dharma Seed 60:39 2009-01-25

What is the self actually doing when it feels so solid, and when does that solidity begin to loosen? The talk moves from a traditional distinction between the emptiness of self and phenomena into a detailed, practical investigation of selfing: self-story, inner critic, identity, guilt, and the habit of making everything mean something about “me.” It keeps returning to the question of suffering, showing how different ways of looking—at actions, at dependent arising, at moments when practice quiets the mind—can expose the self as constructed, useful in some contexts, but not inherently existing. The closing movement turns toward practice instructions: not identifying, not clinging to definitions, and learning to see where freedom begins.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Framing emptiness - He begins by situating the talk within the retreat’s larger exploration of emptiness and the distinction between self and phenomena.
  2. 02:36 Self as a tool - He argues that the Buddha’s primary concern is suffering, so self-language is only useful when it helps reduce suffering and support ethics, communication, and care.
  3. 10:37 Story and identity - He turns to the way identity is shaped through life stories and roles, showing that a useful narrative can guide practice if it is not taken as fixed truth.
  4. 16:40 Seeing self-definitions - He examines how practitioners define themselves through labels, comparisons, and social patterns, and how retreat reveals those definitions as malleable.
  5. 22:31 Inner critic and the early texts - He discusses self-conclusion, the inner critic, and a brief return to early Buddhist questioning of what can truly be called self.
  6. 27:46 Spectrum of selfing - He develops the idea that self-sense ranges from gross and reactive to subtle and light, including the effects of metta and the caution that subtle selfing can remain.
  7. 35:44 Samadhi and fabrication - He looks at how deep concentration quiets thinking and reveals how both self and world are fabricated, not simply discovered.
  8. 41:19 Conceiving self differently - He surveys several ways self is conceived, then introduces anatta practice and the shift from self-view to action, especially in relation to guilt.
  9. 54:11 Dependent arising and release - He closes by showing how clinging and emphasis build self-view, and how different ways of looking can gradually reveal the self as conventionally present but not inherently existing.
