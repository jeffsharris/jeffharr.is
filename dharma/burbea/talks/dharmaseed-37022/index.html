Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Imaginal and the Middle Way (Q & A)

Dharma Seed 51:08 2016-08-01

In this Q&A, Rob Burbea explores how imaginal practice can be held without sliding into belief or dismissal. He moves from encouragement about practicing in ordinary life, to the distinction between self-oriented practice and practice for something larger, and then into a wider middle way that holds multiple views, archetypes, and callings at once. The later questions turn toward renunciation, sacredness, and the role of darker imaginal material, with Rob emphasizing trust, flexibility, and a broader path that includes cooling, equanimity, and soul-making.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the Q&A - Rob begins by setting up the question period and answers the first concern about how to continue imaginal practice when the surrounding culture does not support it.
  2. 02:40 Support off retreat - He emphasizes nourishment, small openings, and the value of short moments of practice in daily life rather than relying on long continuous retreat conditions.
  3. 06:15 Practice and intention - In response to a question about intention, he distinguishes practice for self-improvement from practice for God, the Buddha, or the bodhisattva path.
  4. 09:34 A spectrum of views - Rob expands the frame from humanist service to a more multidimensional view, including deity practice, the cosmic Buddha, and the idea that practice can move through different perspectives over time.
  5. 15:32 Middle way and wobble - He turns to the middle way between reification and dismissal, saying that apparent razor-edge questions often become more spacious with practice and that wobbling is normal.
  6. 20:37 Renunciation as imaginal - A question about renunciation leads to a discussion of archetypes, fantasy, and how the monk, artist, or renunciate can remain alive as an image without becoming a rigid life identity.
  7. 28:39 Permission and practice - Rob stresses the role of permission in retreat, showing how group practice can legitimize hidden sensibilities and make them feel shared rather than private or strange.
  8. 33:13 Cooling intense experience - He addresses fear around strong experiences by placing them in the context of the wider path, recommending more access to coolness, space, and equanimity alongside bliss and intensity.
  9. 38:35 Making the sacred - In the discussion of pragmatism, he explains that sacredness often begins with bounded ritual or places before widening into an all-pervasive sense of holiness.
