Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Renunciation and Joy

Dharma Seed 54:27 2006-02-09

Renunciation is usually treated as deprivation, but the talk keeps asking whether it might actually open the heart. Moving from the Buddha’s Eightfold Path to the rich young man, from consumerism to retreat life, the speaker argues that letting go is not about grim self-denial or willpower; it is about freedom, love, clarity, and a deeper capacity for joy. The talk keeps testing that claim through stories, examples from ordinary life, and a long closing poem by Mary Oliver that points to amazement and enoughness.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the question - Burbea introduces renunciation as an often-avoided topic and frames the talk around its surprising connection with joy.
  2. 01:56 Path and tradition - He places renunciation within the Eightfold Path and draws on Jesus’ encounter with the rich young man to show its uncompromising side.
  3. 06:13 Beyond stereotypes - He pushes back against dismissive or heroic views of renunciation, including monastic machismo and the idea that it is outdated.
  4. 09:41 Freedom, not self-control - Burbea defines renunciation as letting go for the sake of a more meaningful freedom, not as willpower, ascetic image, or self-improvement.
  5. 13:27 Consumerism and the path - He links modern consumption to spiritual distraction, then explains how renunciation and qualities like mindfulness and joy feed one another.
  6. 16:59 What renunciation gives - The talk traces the gifts of renunciation: spaciousness, simplicity, clarity, honesty, love, and a stronger capacity to give.
  7. 21:58 Hunger and depth - Burbea examines how renunciation affects confidence, sensitivity, and spiritual urgency, using Krishnamurti to question whether we ask enough of life.
  8. 27:04 Joy in ordinary life - He explores what actually produces happiness, contrasting shallow rewards with the quieter joy of presence, nature, and simple moments.
  9. 37:30 Wise company and amazement - The closing section turns toward community, deeper tastes, and Mary Oliver’s poem as an image of wonder, enoughness, and life held lightly.
