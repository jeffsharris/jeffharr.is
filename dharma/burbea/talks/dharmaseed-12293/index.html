Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Love and Emptiness

Dharma Seed 1:22:52 2007-02-04

What changes when love stops being aimed only at people and begins to meet experience itself? The talk starts by reviewing mettā and compassion as ways of reconditioning the mind, then moves into a more subtle question: whether love can open perception toward emptiness rather than merely improving the self. A teaching from the Aksayamati Sutra, a Lotus Sutra line about seeing all things with compassion, and extended discussion of grasping, fear, and dependent origination gradually push the practice from heartfelt nourishment toward non-clinging and freedom.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening Review - Burbea reviews the retreat's lovingkindness and compassion practices and frames them as cultivation of the heart.
  2. 03:27 Nourishment and Insight - He presents the Buddha's twofold approach: wholesome nourishment and investigation, and explains why practice is not self-improvement.
  3. 07:19 Conditions and the Unconditioned - The talk turns to how some wholesome conditions can lead toward unbinding, not just more conditioning.
  4. 11:48 Seeing Things with Compassion - A Lotus Sutra line and the Aksayamati Sutra lead into the new practice of directing love and compassion toward dharmas and experience itself.
  5. 17:47 Acceptance and Perception - He emphasizes genuine welcoming, the color of mind states, and how love and equanimity change what appears.
  6. 29:18 Ease and Relationship - Burbea argues that practice is about the relationship to experience, not just the object, and that a skillful relation removes suffering.
  7. 40:32 Fear and Present Time - He uses fear, the past, the present, and future to show how reified views shape experience and how emptiness weakens that grip.
  8. 48:10 Grasping and Fading - The teaching deepens into dependent origination, non-grasping, and the way love can make objects and self-states fade.
  9. 01:01:11 Questions and Practice Guidance - In the Q&A, he clarifies how to choose between mettā, compassion, and the new practice, and how to work with internal and external phenomena.
