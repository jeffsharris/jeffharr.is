Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

What is an 'Image'? (Part 2)

Dharma Seed 61:56 2015-08-09

Rob Burbea continues a careful unpacking of what he means by “image,” moving from simple definitions into a more subtle view of how images shape perception, identity, and practice. He contrasts imaginal seeing with analytic reduction and with bare attention, and he uses examples from Jewish mysticism, Lurianic Kabbalah, Greek myth, and tantric practice to show how images can be both fabricated and deeply real. The talk gradually opens into a broader claim: that the way we look participates in what appears, and that this can support soulfulness rather than flatten experience.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 What image means - He begins by clarifying that “image” here is not limited to visuals, and may involve resonance, dialogue, ambiguity, and poetic depth.
  2. 02:36 Poetic and archetypal - He distinguishes one-off images from recurring constellations, and introduces the tendency toward archetypal, irreducible, soul-bearing images.
  3. 06:22 Against flattening - Using dependent origination and modern reductionism as examples, he argues that analytic breakdown can be useful but can also flatten what is alive and complex.
  4. 14:39 Fabrication and divinity - He uses emptiness, the Zohar, and a story about writing a book to show a middle way between taking imaginal figures as objectively real and dismissing them as mere products of the mind.
  5. 20:08 Shards and views - Through Lurianic Kabbalah he develops the idea that perception and interpretation help create new faces of the divine, and that image arises together with the way of looking.
  6. 25:06 Telos and destiny - He explores how images can carry a future-facing pull, citing James Hillman and discussing how practice may involve more than dissolving suffering.
  7. 33:44 Myth, memory, papanca - He broadens the scope of image to include myths and memories, and contrasts this with papanca, where experience becomes literalized, contracted, and shorn of resonance.
  8. 44:01 Stages and cross-pollination - He describes how images unfold gradually, seed one another, and can appear through encounters with people, stories, and cultural material that become imaginally alive.
  9. 51:59 Seeing as image - He links imaginal perception with tantra, contrasts it with bare attention, and ends by suggesting that lightly held conceptual frameworks can deepen soulfulness and soul-making.
