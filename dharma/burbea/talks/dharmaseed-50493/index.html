Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Sensing with Soul (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 1:27:16 2017-12-21

What if the point of practice is not just to notice experience, but to sense the world, the self, and even perception itself with soul? The talk traces how imaginal practice can spill beyond an inner image into a wider cosmopoetic way of seeing, then distinguishes this from oneness perceptions and more reductionist forms of mindfulness. It moves toward a careful, practical map of sensing with soul: how it can include the senses, thought, reverence, discernment, play, and a deepening equanimity even in the face of illness and death.

Chapters

  1. 01:30 From Image to World - Burbea recalls earlier imaginal teaching and explains how an inward image can spill outward into the world as cosmopoiesis.
  2. 05:36 Cosmopoiesis and Oneness - He distinguishes imaginal cosmopoiesis from perceptions of universal oneness and shows how both can shape experience without being reducible to illusion.
  3. 10:40 Introducing Sensing with Soul - He names sensing with soul as the larger frame that includes imaginal perception, oneness-perceptions, and the movement between them.
  4. 17:56 Why It Matters Most - Burbea argues that the real point is not fascinating imagery, but the opening, healing, and re-enchantment of existence itself.
  5. 25:29 Philosophy and Examples - He quotes J. N. Findlay on mysticism and beauty, then uses examples from music and lovers to show that sensing with soul can be embodied and ordinary.
  6. 41:17 Soul in All Senses - Using B.B. King, touch, thinking, and the six or seven senses, he shows how the whole field of experience can be sensed imaginally.
  7. 57:22 Four Modes of Contact - He outlines everyday, dharma-lensed, oneness-based, and imaginal modes of sense contact, clarifying what changes in each one.
  8. 01:08:28 Mindfulness and Dependence - Burbea links sensing with soul to mindfulness, appropriate attention, and dependent origination, emphasizing responsiveness rather than passivity.
  9. 01:19:33 Reverence and Play - He closes by showing that reverence, discernment, and play are part of sensing with soul and of practice itself.
