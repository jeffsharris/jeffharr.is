Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Heart Art and an Introduction to Image

Dharma Seed 56:08 2018-06-24

How do emotion, self, and image change the way experience unfolds? The talk begins with energy-body practice and then widens into several ways of meeting difficulty: resting in samadhi, letting dukkha be held in metta, allowing emotion to reveal its own intelligence, and noticing when a self-sense shifts into image. A long example of confusion, emptiness, and then an inner music image shows how suffering can remain present while taking on a more personal, poetic, and holy shape. The second half turns more explicitly to what 'image' means here: not just visual fantasy, but a relational mode of perception that can arise through any sense and that is judged by whether it is soul-making.

Chapters

  1. 00:11 Two aims for the session - Burbea outlines the talk's two themes: extending energy-body practice in relation to emotion, and beginning a discussion of image.
  2. 05:35 Samadhi and difficulty - He describes how to rest in pleasant energy, work skillfully with contraction, and then consider a different attitude that simply stays with what is difficult.
  3. 11:27 Self, story, and healing - Using Catherine McGee's no-self practice as a contrast, he explains that emotions may need self, story, and varying degrees of openness for healing to unfold.
  4. 13:24 Image meets dukkha - He develops the idea that an emotion can be held by metta or an image, and tells the old king and baby monkey story as an example of dukkha remaining present while another harmony enters.
  5. 22:36 Holy roots of feeling - Burbea broadens the frame to include sanctifying rage, fierceness, hatred, and frustration, and suggests that allowing emotional energy to move can loosen stuckness without forcing catharsis.
  6. 28:30 Dynamism and movement - He emphasizes the energy in difficult emotion, the way self-sense can shift as energy moves, and how this differs from a more standard jhana-style allowing.
  7. 30:49 What image means - The second half defines imaginal experience as a relational, not merely visual, mode marked by beauty, eros, autonomy, humility, and duty, and includes a long personal example where emptiness practice gives way to the sense 'I am this music.'
  8. 46:02 Not every image is imaginal - He contrasts soul-making image with a ghost story and a deva photo, showing that unusual perceptions are not enough by themselves; what matters is the quality of relationship.
  9. 50:52 How to invite images - Burbea closes with practical ways to support image: practices that liquefy fabrication, working from emotional energy, deliberately imagining places, and drawing on people, memories, dreams, or taught images.
