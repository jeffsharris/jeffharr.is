Dilemmas and Delineations: How did we get <em>here</em>?! (Part 2)

Dharma Seed 42:31 2017-01-19

How can eros be named without flattening it into sexuality, pleasure, or a fixed definition? The talk begins by situating eros inside a larger practice framework, using the hermeneutic circle to show why any single term has to be read in relation to the whole path, the whole Dharma, and the larger web of imaginal and soulmaking teachings. From there it offers a working description of eros as a desire for more contact, more knowing, and more intimacy with the beloved or erotic object, then tests that definition against tension, juiciness, dynamism, arousal, delight, and the difference between soulmaking delight and meditative bliss.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Why This Delineation - Burbea explains why eros matters on the path and why it belongs within imaginal practice, sacredness, and sexuality rather than being reduced to a side issue.
  2. 02:20 Definitions and Context - He pauses before defining eros to reflect on the use of words in general and to insist that the definition is serving a practice purpose, not claiming final authority.
  3. 05:46 Hermeneutic Circle - Using the idea of parts and wholes, he shows how eros, mindfulness, and the Dharma can only be understood in context and through movement between small and large views.
  4. 10:12 Eros as Desire - He begins a simple working definition: eros is a desire for more contact, more connection, more touch, and more knowing of the erotic object or beloved.
  5. 15:56 The More and Otherness - Through Pothos and related ideas, he emphasizes eros as an impulse toward more, while also preserving otherness rather than collapsing into fixed union.
  6. 24:10 Recognizing Eros - Burbea describes how to notice eros in practice through sensitivity to mind states, the energy body, attraction, tension, and different levels of intensity.
  7. 30:10 Juiciness and Arousal - He turns to libido, moisture, and fruitfulness to point toward the juiciness of eros, then links eros with the imaginal, soulmaking, and a broader dynamism of interest.
  8. 37:03 Delight and Bliss - The talk closes by distinguishing soulmaking delight from meditative bliss, using jhana as a comparison and returning to the idea that eros enriches and expands experience.
