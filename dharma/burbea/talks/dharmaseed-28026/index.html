Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Releasing the Self, Freeing its Demons (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 23:18 2014-10-05

What if the self is not something to erase, but something to relate to more wisely? The talk begins by questioning a familiar Buddhist simplification about self and aggregates, then argues for a more flexible approach that can make room for complexity, eros, art, and the patterns of modern personality. From there it turns to the inner critic as a concrete example, showing how loving-kindness, deconstruction, or even dialogue can change its force—and sometimes reveal a hidden protector or a grief-struck image of the mother beneath it.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Questioning the usual view - Burbea begins by naming the common teaching that ego is the problem and self is illusory, then starts to test that framing.
  2. 02:08 Limits of aggregates - He argues that reducing self to a process of aggregates is not something the Buddha said and can flatten deeper understanding.
  3. 03:41 A skillful way of looking - The aggregates and emptiness are presented as perspectives that can be used meditatively, not final descriptions of reality.
  4. 06:55 Why simplicity fails - He turns to modern complexity, eros, art, and personality, suggesting that simplistic teachings can fail to nourish the richness of life.
  5. 11:01 Modern suffering and self - Burbea notes that much contemporary suffering sits at the level of personality, and asks for other ways of opening the notion of self.
  6. 12:26 Meeting the inner critic - He introduces the inner critic as a familiar structure and names loving-kindness and insight into emptiness as two ways of softening it.
  7. 14:46 From deconstructing to relating - The talk shifts to an imaginal move: treating the critic as a figure, entering dialogue, and allowing relationship to change the experience.
  8. 16:50 The clumsy protector - Burbea suggests the critic may be a protector in disguise, and tells a retreat story in which turning toward it reveals unexpected kindness.
  9. 19:43 Grief and the mother image - A second story shows how a dead mother can remain alive as an image in the psyche, and how responding to that image can open a freer and more beautiful relation.
