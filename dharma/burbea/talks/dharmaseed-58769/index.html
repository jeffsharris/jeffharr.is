Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Dharma(s) Discovered and Created (Part 3)

Dharma Seed 1:34:15 2019-06-08

In this talk, Rob Burbea explores how contemporary culture shapes the way Dharma is understood, especially the tension between modernist ideas of one truth and postmodern suspicion of universal claims. He then asks whether Buddha Dharma, and Soulmaking Dharma in particular, should be taken as universally true, or as frameworks that are partial, plural, and historically situated. The talk gradually shifts from critique of universality toward a more open view of Dharma as art, with room for borrowing, interpretation, and multiple valid stories about its place in the world.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Cultural context - Burbea begins by tracing the clash between modern and postmodern ways of thinking and how that tension colors contemporary Dharma.
  2. 05:58 What counts as universal - He asks whether Dharma claims, especially the Four Noble Truths, are being treated as universal in a way that should be examined more carefully.
  3. 13:23 Gender and structure - The talk turns to the Pali Canon, renunciation, patriarchy, and whether some Buddhist styles reflect a strongly male-shaped inheritance.
  4. 17:32 Soulmaking and plurality - Burbea describes Soulmaking Dharma as elastic and self-aware, then distinguishes it from superficial eclecticism and perennialist mixing.
  5. 26:38 Borrowing across traditions - He considers the use of the Old Testament, Jesus, Rumi, Kuan Yin, and even missionary stories to ask what is really being borrowed and what remains distinct.
  6. 35:01 Postmodern limits - The discussion widens to the limits of postmodernism, Richard Rorty, and the need for practice that can actually shift perception rather than merely critique ideas.
  7. 43:53 Dharma as art - He proposes Dharma, especially Soulmaking Dharma, as an art with poetic and imaginal truth, irreducible purpose, and a third kind of knowing.
  8. 51:39 Culture and spirit - Burbea reflects on how views arise from historical and cultural contexts, bringing in Hegel while rejecting any closed or final philosophy of spirit.
  9. 58:03 Two ways to place it - He sketches different ways to locate Soulmaking Dharma in relation to mainstream Buddhism, ending with the idea that the relationship may be seen from either side.
