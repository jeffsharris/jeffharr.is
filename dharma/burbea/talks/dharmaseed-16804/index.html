Stewardship and Intention as Gaia Practices

Dharma Seed 52:25 2012-07-19

James Baraz offers a Dharma-inflected reading of ecological responsibility through Bob Doppelt’s book From Me to We, focusing on two commitments: acknowledging trustee obligations and choosing one’s destiny. He moves from interdependence, karma, and moral responsibility into a practical model of trusteeship, then closes with a change process for moving out of denial and into action. Along the way he uses Buddha references, Ram Dass, Julia Butterfly Hill, and a remembered Andreas Vollenweider concert to make the ethical stakes feel personal and concrete.

Chapters

  1. 00:35 From Me to We - Baraz introduces Bob Doppelt’s framing book and reviews the first three principles that lead toward a shift from individualism to interdependence.
  2. 05:13 The Law of Trusteeship - He develops the fourth principle, arguing that seeing oneself as part of larger systems naturally gives rise to benevolent responsibility.
  3. 09:58 Benevolence and No Ownership - Using the Buddha’s post-awakening compassion and Ram Dass’s line about being accountants in the firm, he emphasizes that ownership is ultimately provisional.
  4. 12:56 Power as Responsibility - A remembered Andreas Vollenweider concert becomes a vivid example of being called to use power wisely and in service of others.
  5. 16:39 Duties of a Trustee - Baraz lays out the duties of loyalty and prudence, connecting them to fairness, public goods, sustainability, and climate preparation.
  6. 25:17 Taking the Commitment - He invites listeners to take in the commitment to acknowledge trustee obligations and help sustain life on earth.
  7. 27:45 Choosing Destiny - The fifth commitment shifts to free will and intention, with the reminder that what we choose in each moment shapes our world.
  8. 32:00 Stages of Change - Baraz outlines a practical process for moving from disinterest through deliberation into action, maintenance, and a stable trustee identity.
