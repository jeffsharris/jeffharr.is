Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Dharma(s) Discovered and Created (Part 2)

Dharma Seed 1:16:44 2019-06-07

Rob Burbea continues a close inquiry into how traditions are shaped by worldview, personal inclination, cultural pressure, and the kinds of tensions that keep a Dharma alive. He compares several broad cosmologies, especially different ways emptiness can be understood, and asks what each one allows or excludes in practice, ethics, beauty, and sacredness. The talk also returns to imaginal practice, suggesting that emptiness and the imaginal can support each other rather than stand in opposition.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 What Shapes Tradition - He recaps the earlier thread: what a healthy tradition needs, and how culture, error, conflict, and tension all participate in shaping it.
  2. 03:34 Personal Inclinations Matter - The talk turns to the human side of choice, including soul style, preference, fear, and how these silently steer which Dharma people notice and adopt.
  3. 06:55 Four Worldviews - Rob outlines four broad models—physicalist, transcendent, all-one, and soulmaking—and shows how each one frames suffering, awakening, and what counts as real.
  4. 10:54 Awakening Through Models - He keeps unpacking the implications of those cosmologies, especially for ethics, the meaning of practice, and the different kinds of human being they produce.
  5. 17:30 Images as Stepping Stones - Using a conversation about philosophers and Winnicott's transitional object, he examines the view that images are only provisional and contrasts it with a soulmaking reading of the imaginal.
  6. 26:37 Practice and Neuroscience - Rob considers a modern physicalist account of awakening as neurological change, and asks what that leaves out about emptiness, sacredness, and the range of human possibility.
  7. 34:02 Many Meanings of Emptiness - He surveys different Tibetan and modern construals of emptiness, from protecting conventional reality to using emptiness as an anti-spiritual or anti-sacred stance.
  8. 50:09 Deepening the View - Drawing on Nagarjuna, he argues that emptiness is deeper than impermanence or process, and that a deeper understanding can open both soul and imaginal practice.
  9. 58:14 Honesty, Authority, and Inquiry - The talk ends by returning to the need for honesty about what we really want, the role of beauty, and the balance between autonomy and authority in a living tradition.
