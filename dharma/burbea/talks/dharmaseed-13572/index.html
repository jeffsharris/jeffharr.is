Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

For the Sake of Life on Earth, part 2

Dharma Seed 42:35 2011-07-24

How do you stay open to the pain of the world without collapsing into fear, numbness, or isolation? The talk moves through the spiral of “the work that reconnects,” beginning with gratitude, then honoring pain for the world, and then shifting into a practice of seeing with new eyes through poetry, indigenous wisdom, and a guided outdoor exercise. From there it turns toward action: if we are part of the earth rather than separate from it, then ecological and social change calls for discipline, courage, and a willingness to go to the limits of our longing.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening Spiral - Macy begins by situating the talk within the spiral of the work that reconnects and names the movement from gratitude into grief.
  2. 00:44 Honoring Pain - She explains why fear and grief for the world are natural responses to interdependence, not signs of failure.
  3. 04:21 Great Unraveling - Macy names ecological collapse and argues that activism alone is not enough unless our view of the earth changes.
  4. 07:17 Seeing With New Eyes - She invites deconditioning from individualism and reads Rilke’s widening circles as a way of finding belonging in the mystery.
  5. 12:35 Through the Separate Self - Using the Buddha’s teaching, she describes the self as a convention and points to radical interdependence as the deeper truth.
  6. 17:51 Earth as Kin - A long passage from Big Bill Neidjie presents the earth, trees, grass, blood, wind, and body as mutually alive and related.
  7. 22:03 Guided Sensing Practice - Macy gives instructions for a silent two-person outdoor exercise that uses touch, sound, smell, and sight to renew felt connection with the living world.
  8. 28:10 Going Forth - She returns from the practice to encourage open-hearted action, reads Rilke’s going-forth poem, and emphasizes that beauty, terror, and uncertainty belong together.
  9. 36:32 Courage and Discipline - Macy closes with Cesar Chavez, a critique of complacent middle-way talk, and brief lines from Rumi, a Navajo elder, and Gandhi to frame disciplined action as sacred work.
