Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Image, Ethics, and Awakening (Q & A)

Dharma Seed 1:32:46 2019-03-29

What happens when ethics, eros, ritual, and imaginal practice are all held in the same room? In this Q&A, the talk moves from precepts as the ground of practice to a broader claim that ethics must keep growing as Soulmaking Dharma deepens, with repeated concern for how images shape action, speech, and responsibility. It also turns practical and personal: ritual, sexual eros, the risks of misunderstanding, the place of service, and a closing question about what kind of life is actually worth living. The tone stays reflective but direct, with several vivid examples and a clear resistance to easy answers.

Chapters

  1. 06:38 Opening Questions - Rob begins by taking up retreat questions and setting the tone for a practical, wide-ranging discussion.
  2. 10:23 Precepts as Basis - He reflects on his first Dharma talk and explains why the precepts are not a side issue but the ground of practice.
  3. 13:29 Ethics Deepens - He argues that ethics becomes more organic and more demanding as Soulmaking Dharma unfolds, rather than less important.
  4. 22:42 Ritual and Sacredness - Rob turns to ritual, describing how it can sanctify ordinary things and widen the sense of what can be sensed with soul.
  5. 27:15 Imaginal Attention - He explains that imaginal practice is a way of looking, balancing skill, receptivity, and grace rather than relying on the object itself.
  6. 38:56 Eros and Boundaries - The discussion moves through sexual eros, consent, and the care needed when imaginal practice enters relationships and community life.
  7. 58:15 Dark Night Reframed - Rob rejects fear-based ideas about the unconscious and the dark night, and instead links difficult practice effects to dependent origination and the style of practice.
  8. 01:07:34 The Sister Image - A detailed imaginal reading of an image of his sister becomes a living example of grief, beauty, eros, and compassion in practice.
  9. 01:18:23 Service and the Path - He closes by reflecting on the bodhisattva ideal, rebirth, awakening, and the need to ask what one truly wants from a life of practice and service.
