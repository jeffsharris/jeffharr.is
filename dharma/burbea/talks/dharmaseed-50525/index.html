Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Discernment in Soulmaking (Q & A)

Dharma Seed 57:05 2018-02-08

This Q&A explores how Rob Burbea uses “imaginal” in relation to soulmaking, and how that language differs from Hillman, Corbin, and Jung. He then turns to practical questions about the active and receptive ends of image work, the role of eros and sexuality, and why ontology matters when practice begins to shift one’s sense of what is real. Throughout, he returns to a quiet criterion: what matters is not forcing a theory, but whether a way of seeing becomes more soulful, spacious, and alive.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 What Is Imaginal? - He begins by clarifying that images should not be required to carry fixed meanings, and that his use of imaginal treats psyche itself as image.
  2. 03:03 Genealogy of the Term - He situates the word imaginal in Corbin, Hillman, and Jung, then explains that different traditions use the term differently.
  3. 07:02 Image as Perception - He shifts from terminology to practice, arguing that seeing everything as image or perception can open insight, spaciousness, and freedom.
  4. 17:01 Soulmaking and Skill - He distinguishes liberation from soulmaking, then names the practical foundations needed for the latter, including bodily awareness, emotional skill, and kindness.
  5. 24:43 Active and Receptive - He answers a question about the spectrum between making an image happen and receiving it, encouraging flexible effort rather than a fixed style.
  6. 30:32 Autonomy and Trust - Using Coltrane as an example, he says the point is not how deliberately something arose, but whether it feels soulmaking and can be trusted as such.
  7. 34:02 Eros and Sexuality - He defines eros as movement toward the beloved and explains why sexuality is one manifestation of it, but not its whole range.
  8. 40:46 Horizontal Desire - He contrasts craving that stays horizontal with a soulmaking eros that gains dimensionality, space, and more choice.
  9. 46:52 Ontology and Reality - He closes by discussing emptiness, the unsettled question of reality, and why ontology can be practiced as part of soulmaking rather than as a search for final certainty.
