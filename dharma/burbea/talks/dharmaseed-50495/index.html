Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Sensing with Soul (Part 3)

Dharma Seed 28:36 2017-12-23

In this talk, Rob Burbea looks closely at how perception can shift into sensing with soul, and how ordinary experience becomes imaginal. He moves through the dynamics of change, the balance among Self, Other, and World, and the need at times to linger with the energy body, desire, or emotional life rather than treating them as distractions. Near the end, he returns to reification as a common source of difficulty, while also warning against becoming rigid or self-policing about it.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Framing the inquiry - He introduces the talk as a close look at how perception becomes soul-sensing, while encouraging an attitude of play rather than rigidity.
  2. 02:33 Soulmaking as dynamism - He describes soulmaking as a living process with different tempos, from sudden shifts to gradual consolidation, and uses the hawk gliding on thermal currents as an image of responsive practice.
  3. 06:00 Different tempos of change - He develops the idea that conceptual frameworks, like Logos, often change more slowly than other elements, and that change can unfold in stages rather than always dramatically.
  4. 08:00 Self, Other, World - He turns to the Self-Other-World trinity, showing how practice may need to lean toward one pole to counter habit and restore balance.
  5. 11:37 The energy body - He suggests that some people need to spend more time with the energy body, moving between gross and subtle registers and noticing where attention has become one-sided.
  6. 15:11 Desire and eros - He discusses blocked desire, encouraging listeners to notice it, allow it, and eventually recognize it as beautiful or even divine.
  7. 17:29 Habit or soul's call - He asks what is merely habitual and what is genuinely given by soul, applying that question to emotion, energy, and the broader shape of practice.
  8. 23:39 Reification and looseness - He returns to reification as a root problem in stuck or distorted soulmaking, but ends by emphasizing a light, art-like way of teaching and practicing that allows for exceptions.
