Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Beyond the Search for Freedom

Dharma Seed 61:09 2016-07-27

Rob Burbea explores a question beneath the usual Dharma frame: is practice mainly about reducing suffering, or can it also be about beauty, sacredness, and soulmaking? He moves from a reflection on re-enchantment and the modern disenchanted worldview, through a close look at mindfulness as “coping,” into a broader vision of emptiness, meaningfulness, and open-ended freedom. The talk ends by turning to Jung for support in the idea that neither freedom nor self-realization is a finished project.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Retreat frame - Burbea introduces the retreat’s themes and situates re-enchantment within the larger context of practice and contemporary life.
  2. 05:04 Matthieu Ricard question - He uses Matthieu Ricard’s unusual happiness as a pivot to ask whether joy comes more from technique or from inhabiting an enchanted cosmos.
  3. 09:25 Coping or opening - The talk contrasts mindfulness as coping with a wider possibility: practices that deepen sensitivity, reveal sacredness, and loosen fabrication.
  4. 16:18 What practice is for - He asks listeners to examine their own aim on the path and considers a more radical orientation toward beauty and sacredness rather than only suffering reduction.
  5. 24:25 Buddhism and modern models - Burbea reflects on whether this emphasis still counts as Buddhism, and how Buddhist, medical, and modern spiritual framings tend to center suffering relief.
  6. 31:41 Self, God, and meaning - He speaks more personally about what he cares about in practice, then develops the role of self-orientation, divine orientation, and meaningfulness.
  7. 39:54 Priority reversal - The talk explores the complex effects of reversing priorities from self-healing to beauty and sacredness, including why that may be healing in itself.
  8. 48:03 Who practice serves - Burbea addresses the people for whom art, soulfulness, and sacredness matter most, and links these concerns to the open-endedness of the path.
  9. 55:13 Jung and open-ended freedom - He closes with Jung’s remarks on individuation and life’s problems as support for a path that stays unfinished, alive, and meaningful.
