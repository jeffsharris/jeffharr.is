Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Approaching the Dharma: Part Two - Liberating Ways of Looking

Dharma Seed 61:14 2012-11-22

Rob Burbea develops the idea that insight is not mainly a special experience, but any understanding that lessens dukkha. He then shifts from insight as a result of practice to insight as a method: ways of looking through not-self, impermanence, unsatisfactoriness, metta, and awareness itself. The talk moves gradually toward a deeper claim that self, object, and even the sense of fabricating are empty and mutually dependent, so practice becomes learning how to use and release different ways of perceiving.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Setting the frame - Rob explains the long arc of the two talks and why this material may only make sense when held alongside the earlier discussion of emptiness.
  2. 03:17 Insight, loosely defined - He gives a broad definition of insight as any realization that decreases dukkha, and distinguishes it from mere mindfulness or knowing.
  3. 08:03 Insight from experience - Personal and universal examples show how insight can arise from one’s own experience and bring relief in the present or later on.
  4. 13:49 A way of looking - The talk turns to insight as a starting point and then to the Buddha’s teaching on not-self as a perception to train deliberately.
  5. 19:00 Impermanence and letting go - Rob develops impermanence and unsatisfactoriness as practical lenses, showing how sustained attention to change and dissatisfaction loosens clinging.
  6. 25:41 Metta and the Four Noble Truths - He widens the field to metta, mindfulness, and awareness, then frames these as present-time enactments of the Four Noble Truths through the way of looking.
  7. 32:09 Repetition and deeper insight - Repeatedly sustaining a perception consolidates insight, and then opens further shifts, including the fading of self and object through the same practice.
  8. 39:41 Fabrication and emptiness - The discussion deepens into fabrication, dependent origination, and the idea that ordinary ways of looking reinforce dukkha, while skillful ones unbind it.
  9. 48:03 Practice as experimentation - He closes by stressing that practice is the experimental cultivation of different ways of looking, each one empty, provisional, and capable of bringing freedom.
