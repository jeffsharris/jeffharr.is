Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Practicing with the Three Characteristics

Dharma Seed 1:16:45 2009-01-26

Rob Burbea develops the three characteristics as practical lenses for meditation: impermanence, dukkha, and not-self. He keeps returning to the same point that these are not doctrines to believe, but ways of seeing that support letting go, especially when meditation meets difficulty. Later he connects the practice to dependent arising, the five khandhas, and a more subtle release from identification altogether.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the framework - He returns to the morning's meditation instructions and frames insight practice as a way of seeing that leads to letting go.
  2. 03:17 The three characteristics - He names anicca, dukkha, and anatta and explains that these are lenses, not mere results, with several ways of working each one.
  3. 07:06 Balancing samadhi and insight - He stresses that precision is not the point here; what matters is a spacious balance between concentration, metta, and insight.
  4. 12:02 Working with difficulty - He shows how tiredness, pain, scatteredness, and other difficult states can become the very material for practice.
  5. 17:04 Impermanence and dukkha - He develops impermanence into a way of seeing unsatisfactoriness and contrasts this with a reductive view of tiny ultimate particles, citing Nagarjuna.
  6. 23:41 Not-self through letting go - He explains how relaxing clinging reveals the changing, dependent nature of self-sense and makes phenomena easier to see as not-self.
  7. 34:23 The anatta practice - He distinguishes direct not-self practice from looking for a self, then maps the five khandhas as a practical way to examine experience.
  8. 50:59 Subtlety and support - He warns against turning the practice into aversion or a struggle to get rid of self, and shows how samadhi, dependent origination, and gradual training deepen it.
  9. 01:03:57 Nothing owned - He ends with a broader contemplative note, bringing in Jesus, Dogen, and a Buddhist passage to point toward non-possessiveness, holy disinterest, and emptiness.
