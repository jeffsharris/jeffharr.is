Active Hope

Dharma Seed 49:37 2013-04-20

Joanna Macy reflects on Active Hope as a practice of meeting ecological and social crisis without panic or paralysis. She reworks hope as something we do, then traces how uncertainty can deepen compassion, widen responsibility, and draw people into collective action. The talk moves between Bodhisattva language, personal memory, activism, grief, and a culminating reading from the Avatamsaka Sutra that expands the sense of time, body, and interconnection.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening and the book - The talk opens with a song reference and introduces Active Hope as the frame for the evening.
  2. 01:28 Hope as a verb - Macy explains why she no longer treats hope as wishful thinking, but as an active way of stepping forward.
  3. 03:59 Bodhisattva and bodhicitta - She defines Bodhisattva and bodhicitta as caring, interdependent intention rather than private salvation.
  4. 06:59 The plankton shock - A memory of Cousteau’s warning about plankton becomes the turning point that changes her sense of ground and purpose.
  5. 13:37 Panic and paralysis - She describes the two ditches of responding to crisis and the need to stay present without checking out.
  6. 18:41 Working together - The story of Ralph Nader’s movement and the tar sands flyover shows how action, witness, and companionship change despair.
  7. 24:42 Grief into action - A song about the tar sands leads into reflections on mourning, renewable rebuilding, and being moved by a larger purpose.
  8. 34:07 Service across time - Macy quotes a climate scientist on what service requires now and expands responsibility across generations and lifetimes.
  9. 42:05 One hair, all worlds - She ends with the Avatamsaka Sutra’s vision of interpenetration, where one body, one moment, and one place contain the whole web of life.
