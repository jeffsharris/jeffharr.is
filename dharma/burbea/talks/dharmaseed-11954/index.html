Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Birth of a Bodhisattva

Dharma Seed 1:13:46 2008-02-12

Rob Burbea explores the bodhisattva ideal as a way of uniting compassion, wisdom, and equanimity. He moves from Shantideva’s vows and the feel of radical service into the practical question of what makes compassion sustainable when suffering does not resolve. The talk then widens into emptiness, faith, and examples from Christian and Buddhist traditions to show how a heart can stay open without becoming overwhelmed.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Bodhisattva Meaning - Rob introduces the bodhisattva as an aspiration shaped by compassion and wisdom, and sketches how the term developed in Buddhist history.
  2. 02:56 Shantideva's Vows - He reads Shantideva’s vows of universal care, using them as a vivid picture of compassion at the center of life.
  3. 05:55 What Sustains Compassion - The talk turns to whether compassion arises suddenly or gradually, and why equanimity is needed to keep it steady.
  4. 10:29 Balance in Practice - Rob explains the balance between receiving suffering and sending healing, and how samadhi and joy support a stable heart.
  5. 17:00 Self, Doing, and Space - He explores the range from effortful compassion to more effortless presence, including the role of self-concern, attachment to results, and spaciousness.
  6. 25:17 Holding Suffering - A Rilke poem and Buddhist devotional imagery open a wider sense of being held, followed by questions about needs, recognition, and co-dependency in caregiving.
  7. 33:59 Equanimity and Karma - Rob critiques spiritual bypassing, then uses karma teachings to show how a larger, unrestricted heart changes how suffering is felt and held.
  8. 46:41 Faith and Devotion - Stories of Maximilian Kolbe and Damien of Molokai illustrate compassion sustained by faith, surrender, and a transformed view of suffering.
  9. 59:14 Emptiness and Compassion - The talk closes by showing emptiness as a gradual softening of view, warning against using it to dismiss pain, and bringing compassion and emptiness back together as complementary tools.
