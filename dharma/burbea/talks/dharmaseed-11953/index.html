Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Love and the Emptiness of Things

Dharma Seed 1:30:39 2008-02-10

How does love change when it stops being aimed only at a person and starts meeting experience itself? The talk moves from basic loving-kindness and compassion, through the idea of wholesome nourishment and investigation, into a subtler account of emptiness where what we call objects, selves, and even suffering are seen as dependent on relationship and perception. Along the way, the speaker keeps returning to practice questions: what happens when we welcome pain instead of struggle with it, and how do love and non-grasping begin to make things dissolve, soften, or lose their grip?

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Reviewing the path - Burbea reviews the earlier practices of loving-kindness and compassion, framing them as slow reconditioning rather than self-improvement.
  2. 03:31 Nourishment and insight - He presents the Buddha’s path as two wings: wholesome nourishment through qualities like metta and samadhi, and investigation for insight.
  3. 08:31 From practice to perception - The talk shifts to how the senses, questioning, and samadhi fit together, and how practice softens the sense of self without reinforcing ego.
  4. 16:25 Love beyond beings - Drawing on the Lotus Sutra and the Aksayamati Sutra, he explores love directed toward beings, toward dharmas, and eventually toward nothing in particular.
  5. 27:25 Technique and freedom - Burbea pushes back against a narrow idea of vipassana, emphasizing that the Buddha cared about freedom and that methods are only useful insofar as they lead there.
  6. 40:08 Perception through relationship - He argues that what we call objects are shaped by our relationship to them, and that love, equanimity, and fear all alter what appears.
  7. 54:39 Love and emptiness - Using the Aksayamati Sutra, Rumi, and dependent origination, he develops the idea of love without an object and shows how grasping helps build the world it seems to face.
  8. 01:08:03 Not stopping at states - He warns against reifying spacious meditative states, explaining that awakening means understanding the nature of perception itself, not settling for refined experiences.
  9. 01:18:49 Questions and practice - In the Q&A, he speaks about anxiety, hidden agendas in metta practice, and the distinction between conditioned states and the unconditioned.
