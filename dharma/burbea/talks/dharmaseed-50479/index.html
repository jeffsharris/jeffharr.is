Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Between Ikon and Eidos: Image & Hermeneutics in Meditation (Part 3)

Dharma Seed 57:36 2018-01-11

What if the point of practice is not to secure one true picture of reality, but to notice how every picture is shaped by desire, feeling, and angle of view? The talk moves from Nietzsche’s perspectivism to stories of Burmese teachers, then into a critique of reductionist meditation styles that deconstruct beauty, emotion, and the self in order to stay unshaken. From there it asks what emptiness, truth, and care of the soul might mean if there is no single objective view to cling to, and whether practice can open into a wider, more soulful range of sensing rather than a narrower one.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Pluralism and perspective - Burbea introduces perspectivism through Nietzsche, arguing that seeing is always shaped by affects, interests, and multiple possible angles.
  2. 03:07 Knowing is interpretive - He develops the case that knowledge is embedded and interested, using Berlin, Hawke, and his own language of practice to distinguish pluralism from relativism.
  3. 12:18 Practice and emotional scope - Two Burmese meditation stories raise the question of whether dharma practice should diminish emotion and beauty or keep them available in a different way.
  4. 17:52 Reductionism in meditation - Burbea describes his own dot-to-dot method and then turns to Marcus Aurelius and Foucault to show how analytical deconstruction can be used to undo enchantment.
  5. 23:17 Beauty, soul, and humility - He asks when reductionism becomes too narrow, and contrasts a posture of mastery with sensing the soul, where beauty, holiness, and humility remain alive.
  6. 29:45 Power behind ideas - The talk widens into the social force of epistemology, the hidden ways teachings and worldviews can become aggressive, and the subtle limits they place on experience.
  7. 40:09 Truth and care of self - Using Foucault, Burbea examines modern assumptions about truth, then asks what care of the self means when truth is not treated as a single objective given.
  8. 47:55 Opening the maps - He returns to emptiness, the plurality of self-views, and the way theories, images, and desires mutually shape practice and perception.
  9. 55:09 Space for soulmaking - Burbea closes by suggesting that questioning our assumptions can create more room for sensing the soul, and sets up the move into specific practice possibilities.
