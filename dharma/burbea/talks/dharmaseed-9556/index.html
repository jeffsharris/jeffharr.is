Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

'To see no-thing is to see excellently'

Dharma Seed 64:47 2010-02-06

Rob Burbea explores the emptiness of phenomena, moving from the felt fading of body, feeling, perception, and emotion into more analytic Madhyamaka reasoning. He repeatedly returns to how experience is fabricated by the mind, why that matters in meditation, and how seeing the interdependence of parts, wholes, and consciousness can loosen suffering. The talk stays practical even when it becomes philosophically rigorous, ending with the quiet claim that seeing 'no thing' can be a deeper way of seeing reality.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the field - Burbea names self-doubt as a common obstacle and invites listeners to take in only what is useful from a talk that will cover many angles.
  2. 02:20 Phenomena are empty - He shifts from emptiness of self to emptiness of phenomena, framing the talk with Buddhist statements about seeing reality by not reifying appearances.
  3. 09:43 Dependent arising deepens - Using the Buddha and the Ananda story, he emphasizes that dependent arising is subtle and that emptiness reaches beyond obvious psychological selfing.
  4. 18:47 Fading and fabrication - He develops a phenomenological practice of seeing body, feeling, emotion, and perception fade when support is withdrawn, and warns against turning emptiness into a metaphysical thing.
  5. 33:27 Porridge, body, and parts - Through the porridge example and the chariot-style analysis, he shows how body and things lack clear boundaries, and how wholes and parts depend on each other.
  6. 40:24 Ways of looking - He explains that the appearance of things depends on how they are looked at, including the role of mental labeling and the mutual dependence of consciousness and object.
  7. 44:40 Aggregates arise together - Drawing on Prajnaparamita and a Majjhima Nikaya reference, he argues that the aggregates, perception, feeling, and awareness cannot really be separated.
  8. 50:50 Walking and motion - He turns to Nāgārjuna's analysis of motion, using walking meditation and the walking path to show how movement cannot be found as inherently existent in past, present, future, or any separate place.
  9. 01:02:36 Seeing no thing - The talk closes with the consequences of this reasoning: careful analysis exposes fabrication, and canonical lines about seeing no thing are presented as pointing toward freedom rather than nihilism.
