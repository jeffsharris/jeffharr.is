Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Self and the World

Dharma Seed 54:27 2007-09-20

What if the question is not whether the self exists, but where suffering is being built? The talk moves from the usefulness of self-story for healing, through the practice of not identifying with body and mind, into a subtler inquiry: how self and world are co-created by concepts, moods, and the push-pull of experience. Along the way, it uses examples of childhood harm, inner-child healing, and everyday self-images to show how loosening identification can bring lightness, not blankness. It ends by pointing toward dependent origination and nibbana as unbinding, with less self, less world, and more freedom.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Setting the question - Burbea introduces the theme and reframes self or no-self as secondary to suffering and freedom from suffering.
  2. 02:12 When self-story helps - He shows how self-concepts can be healing when they help with trauma, inner-child work, boundaries, and cherishing what was wounded.
  3. 07:57 Choosing the right lens - The talk turns to practice maturity: knowing when to use self-language and when to use not-self, without fear or rigidity.
  4. 10:09 Mindfulness loosens identity - He describes how quieting and continuous mindfulness puncture fixed self-views by exposing how they depend on time, mood, and repetition.
  5. 19:05 Not me, not mine - Burbea summarizes a Buddha teaching on the aggregates, using it as a present-moment strategy for seeing bodily and mental events as not-self.
  6. 25:55 Self and world co-arise - He widens the lens to show that any self-conception comes with a world-conception, such as victim and victimizer or knower and known.
  7. 30:35 Helpful concepts, harmful concepts - The talk distinguishes concepts that lead to entanglement from those that lead to freedom, including tikkun olam and the Four Noble Truths.
  8. 41:47 Fabrication and empty experience - Burbea develops the idea that consciousness is always colored by mental factors and that self and world are fabricated together through push and pull.
  9. 48:47 Dependent origination and unbinding - He closes with Buddha references on the origination and cessation of the world, dependent origination, and nibbana as unbinding.
