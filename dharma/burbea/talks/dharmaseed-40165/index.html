Dilemmas and Delineations: How did we get <em>here</em>?! (Part 3)

Dharma Seed 1:19:12 2017-01-20

How do emptiness, imaginal practice, soul, divinity, and eros fit together without collapsing into either metaphysics or reductionism? The talk moves by way of a careful glossary: it starts from a phenomenological method, then unfolds terms like ways of looking, fabrication, emptiness, image, soul-making, logos, cosmopoiesis, theophany, divinity, and Buddha nature. Rather than treating these as abstractions, the speaker keeps returning to how they alter perception, deepen meaning, and open or close experience in practice.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Entering the framework - He situates the talk within a wider sequence of teachings and explains why the material needs to be heard in order.
  2. 05:33 Why these words matter - He reflects on reactions to terms like soul, divinity, and God, and argues for approaching them through experience rather than assumptions.
  3. 18:09 Phenomenology and looking - He defines the phenomenological method, then introduces ways of looking and fabrication as the basis for the whole framework.
  4. 24:27 Emptiness and freedom - He shows how emptiness follows from fabrication and how freedom lies in recognizing that there are only ways of looking.
  5. 29:02 What counts as imaginal - He distinguishes imaginal images from mere fantasy or projection, emphasizing otherness, autonomy, beauty, sacredness, and meaning.
  6. 38:00 Soul-making and eros - He defines soul-making as any way of being that generates soulfulness, then begins to introduce eros, libido, psyche, and logos as related terms.
  7. 45:06 The eros-psyche-logos dynamic - He expands on the mutual fertilization of eros, psyche, and logos, showing how concepts shape perception and practice.
  8. 58:55 Cosmopoiesis and theophany - He describes how imaginal practice can spill over into a transformed sense of the cosmos and how particular things can appear as faces of the divine.
  9. 01:06:53 Divinity and Buddha nature - He broadens divinity as a category of mystical quality, then closes by sketching a non-dual view of Buddha nature, Dharmakaya, and cosmic awareness.
