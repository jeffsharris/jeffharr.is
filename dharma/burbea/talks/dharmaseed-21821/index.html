Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Theatre of Selves (Part Two)

Dharma Seed 62:01 2013-11-17

Rob Burbea explores imagination as a genuine part of practice, not as decoration, and keeps returning to the question of what different ways of looking make possible. He moves from the emptiness of self, through devotion, archetype, shame, and lineage, toward the sense that images can open a more soulful and less reductive way of meeting life. The talk stays practical throughout, emphasizing mindfulness of body, emotion, and relationship to the image itself.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Practice and emptiness - He reviews the earlier talk and frames imagination within a larger Buddhist understanding of practice aimed at seeing emptiness, not merely coping better.
  2. 03:56 Images in practice - He surveys uses of imagination in metta, samadhi, and inner-critic work, then shows how different archetypal tones shape practice.
  3. 08:27 Archetype and presence - Through Kuan Yin, Ajahn Mun, Ajahn Maha Bua, Jimi Hendrix, and Bob Dylan, he shows how figures can embody and transmit imaginal force.
  4. 13:30 Love and shame reframed - A Mary Oliver passage and several retreat stories lead into the idea that love vivifies images and can recast shame in a more healing way.
  5. 21:47 Self as mythic field - He argues that self-images are not fixed realities, but part of a web of mythic relations, lineages, and possible identities.
  6. 29:51 Deliberate imaginal practice - He distinguishes his approach from simple identity affirmation and explains how to work with images through attention to body, mood, and dialogue.
  7. 39:17 Relationships with images - He explores many possible relationships to an image, including tenderness, eroticism, and guidance, then shares vivid retreat visions of healing.
  8. 50:14 Iconic, not reductive - He contrasts narrative images with timeless, iconic ones, warns against flattening them into psychology, and closes with a retreat image of the wind of enlightenment.
  9. 59:50 Concepts and closing - He ends by stressing that assumptions and concepts are always active in practice and says he will return next time to how they open or block the path.
