Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Into the Depths of Silence

Dharma Seed 59:40 2007-11-17

How can silence be more than a retreat rule, and what opens when we actually listen to it? The talk moves from the strangeness of a silent retreat into silence as a living practice: it reveals priorities, brings remorse and judgment into view, softens the push and pull around experience, and can even change the way sounds, thoughts, and the self are felt. Along the way, the speaker uses Rumi, Thomas Merton, Pablo Neruda, Wordsworth, and Dōgen to point toward a silence that is not empty deprivation but spacious, receptive, and quietly loving.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 What Silence Is For - Rob begins by reframing retreat silence as something more than a rule or feat of self-control.
  2. 02:52 Responses to Quiet - He names the range of reactions silence can evoke, from awe and relief to fear, disorientation, and taking it for granted.
  3. 06:03 The Ocean and the Puddle - Using Rumi’s frog story and a phone anecdote, he asks what we miss when we stay inside a small, familiar world.
  4. 09:46 Listening and Priority - With Thomas Merton and several practical examples, he describes silence as a listening that lets priorities, remorse, and care come into view.
  5. 24:05 Neruda on Quiet - He reads Pablo Neruda’s 'Keeping Quiet' to point to silence as a shared stillness that can interrupt self-absorption and widen concern.
  6. 26:21 Sound, Thought, and Space - The teaching turns toward simple listening, the space between sounds, and a gentler relationship to thought and experience.
  7. 40:14 Beyond Objects and Self - Rob deepens the inquiry into what happens when the push-pull with experience relaxes, bringing in Dōgen and Rumi to point beyond self-definition.
  8. 52:43 The Immeasurable - He closes by describing a silence that can feel textured, loving, and expansive, and by using Sariputta to show how greed and aversion manufacture significance and measurement.
