Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Matter, Bodies, Worlds (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 62:01 2016-07-30

What changes when matter is not taken as a fixed, self-evident reality, but as something shaped by view, imagination, and practice? The talk begins with a bird-garden story to contrast a reductionist, survival-based reading of life with more enchanted ways of seeing, then moves through self and world, psychotherapy, and the question of whether practice is oriented toward the self or toward the divine. From there it opens into matter, body, space, and time, bringing in emptiness, dependent origination, and critiques of scientism to ask what kinds of worlds our assumptions actually create.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Birds and reductionism - A garden story introduces the contrast between a harsh reductionist reading of life and a more open, less deluded way of seeing.
  2. 03:53 Self and world together - Rob shows how views of self and world co-arise, and how healing is limited when it sits inside a soulless background story.
  3. 09:46 Re-enchantment and devotion - He develops the idea that self and world can be re-enchanted together, with practice oriented either toward self-improvement or toward the divine.
  4. 18:43 Objects as theophanies - Using amulets, charms, and ordinary objects, he contrasts self-centered infatuation with seeing things as faces of the divine or as appearances rather than fixed realities.
  5. 27:26 Matter, mater, origins - The talk turns to matter and its links with mother, origin, and fantasy, showing how assumptions about embodiment are often concretized without being noticed.
  6. 31:53 Mature imaginal vision - Rob argues for a mature imaginal enchantment in which particulars disclose divinity in and through matter rather than outside it.
  7. 36:48 Nietzsche on scientism - He brings in Nietzsche to criticize scientism, measurable truth, and the flattening of existence into calculation, while defending ambiguity and reverence.
  8. 45:24 Horkheimer, Adorno, and limits - Horkheimer and Adorno deepen the critique by linking mechanistic reason to domination, alienation, and a diminished understanding of nature.
  9. 53:19 Science, space, and openness - He closes by noting how science itself can unsettle fixed ideas of matter, space, and time, and by returning to emptiness, dependent origination, and resistance to single vision.
