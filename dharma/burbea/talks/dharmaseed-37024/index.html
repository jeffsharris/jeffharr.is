Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Movement of Devotion (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 53:34 2016-07-29

If the point of practice is not to win a battle with thought, what actually changes the mind? The talk moves from samadhi as a more subtle, less grasping relation to thought into a wider experiment with openness, flexible attention, and the energy body, then into imaginal practice as a way of perceiving the world differently. From there it maps the conditions that let devotion, surrender, and imaginative perception become alive: less fabrication, a receptive heart, and a way of sensing that blends body, image, and world. The talk closes by sketching how imaginal practice can move from supplication to harmony and, at times, to a felt union that changes how the cosmos is perceived.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Practice Directions - He outlines the retreat’s flexible orientations—samadhi, emotional work, and imaginal practice—and cautions against reducing meditation to suppressing thought.
  2. 09:12 Beyond Concentration - The talk distinguishes true practice from chasing blankness, emphasizing subtlety, openness, and agility of attention instead of rigid focus.
  3. 15:45 Experimental Practice - He frames practice as inquiry into what feeds or starves wholesome states, and extends that experimental stance to the retreat’s broader methods.
  4. 23:00 Imaginal Opening - Rob introduces imaginal practice and disagrees with the idea that it only arrives as spontaneous grace, suggesting it can be deliberately supported.
  5. 25:09 Four Supporting Factors - He lays out four conditions for imaginal and cosmopoetic perception: less fabrication, synesthesia, an open heart, and imaginal perception itself.
  6. 33:43 Humility and Surrender - Using his illness as an example, he shows how acknowledging helplessness can open the heart into devotion, surrender, and encounter with the divine.
  7. 40:16 Preliminaries and Devotion - He compares the imaginal path to tantric preliminaries, stressing that the heart’s posture must be prepared before the imaginal can feel alive.
  8. 43:57 Three Modes of Relation - Rob maps imaginal practice as invocation, harmonizing with qualities, and deeper union, using Avalokiteshvara and Yamantaka as contrasting examples.
  9. 51:28 Transubstantiated Perception - He closes by comparing imaginal communion to Eucharistic transubstantiation, where the world itself is sensed as altered and divine.
