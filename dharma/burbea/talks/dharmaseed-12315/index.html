Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Working with the Hindrances

Dharma Seed 61:04 2007-03-31

The talk starts with a simple retreat-day reality: practice often meets mixed conditions, not uninterrupted calm, and that is exactly where the work begins. From there, the speaker moves through the five hindrances one by one, treating them not as failures but as familiar human energies that can be recognized, softened, and worked with through posture, breath, attention, and honest questioning. The emphasis stays practical: cultivating steadiness by learning when to stay with difficulty, when to widen awareness, and when to choose a more nourishing object. Along the way, images like the hungry ghost, the burning coal, and trading candy for gold make the path feel concrete rather than abstract.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Retreat and difficulty - Burbea opens by naming the mixed experience of retreat and setting up the talk around the difficult side of practice.
  2. 02:00 The five hindrances - He introduces the five hindrances as common human states that show up in meditation and in life.
  3. 05:58 Active practice - He turns to the Buddha’s more active framework, including the four right efforts and the idea that working with difficulty is part of developing calm.
  4. 10:20 Craving and hunger - Burbea explores craving for sense pleasure, using the hungry ghost image and questioning how desire affects the heart and sense of fullness.
  5. 15:13 Pleasure and nourishment - He distinguishes ordinary gratification from deeper nourishment, including the role of wholesome pleasure and the body in loosening craving.
  6. 22:52 Aversion and anger - He discusses aversion from obvious anger to subtler irritation and boredom, emphasizing body awareness and physical relaxation.
  7. 27:40 Boredom and energy - Burbea treats boredom as a loss of sensitivity, then offers ways to re-energize attention through breath, spaciousness, and refined awareness.
  8. 40:22 Sloth and torpor - He explains dullness and sleepiness, then gives practical methods such as posture, light, breath, standing, and walking meditation.
  9. 46:25 Worry, doubt, and release - He closes with restlessness, worry, and doubt, connecting them to ethics, clarity, and firm but gentle persistence in practice.
