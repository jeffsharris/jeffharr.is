Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Logos in the Garden of Souls (Part 2)

Dharma Seed 48:12 2017-02-03

Rob Burbea explores how any Dharma framework also shapes what we can notice, value, and become. He argues that a useful logos must support soulmaking: imagination, eros, freedom, beauty, and the capacity for frameworks to break open rather than harden into dogma. In the second half, he turns to interpretation itself, questioning claims of neutral access to the Pali Canon and tracing how modern, scientific, and secular assumptions can quietly narrow Dharma and erase other ways of knowing.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 What a logos must hold - He returns to the problem of how to build a conceptual framework that can make room for soulmaking, dependent origination, emptiness, and multiple ways of knowing.
  2. 04:00 Eros and interpretation - He shows how ideas about desire and eros directly change experience, and why some views support soulmaking while others constrain it.
  3. 09:07 When Dharma narrows - He warns that a narrow Dharma can become entrenched dogma, quietly limiting freedom, creativity, beauty, and the range of experience.
  4. 11:58 Whose Pali Canon? - He asks who is really being heard when people appeal to the Pali Canon, and argues that every reading comes with assumptions, agendas, and a fantasy of the Buddha.
  5. 14:58 Scholarship and tradition - He questions the claim that traditional language guarantees authenticity, and suggests that scholarship and interpretation are always creative and historically situated.
  6. 20:26 Origins and dialogue - He looks at how Mahayana and Vajrayana traditions claim continuity with the Buddha, then reframes the relationship to tradition as dialogue rather than fixed authority.
  7. 26:29 Epistemicide in Dharma - He introduces epistemicide to name the erasure of embodied, imaginal, and culturally diverse ways of knowing, including within some secular Dharma settings.
  8. 31:57 Mindfulness is not enough - He argues that stripped-down secular Dharma can function like a missionary worldview unless it also opens deeper insight, dependent origination, and liberating ways of looking.
  9. 40:18 Training ways of looking - He closes by using contemporary philosophy to show that epistemology is always lived, then returns to meditation as the training of flexible states of mind and ways of looking.
