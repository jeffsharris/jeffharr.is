Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

An Ecology of Love (Part 4)

Dharma Seed 64:52 2015-12-21

Rob Burbea explores how love, eros, metta, and Dharma shape one another, with a strong focus on our view of the earth and the sacred. He looks at how self-image, Buddhist ideas about non-clinging, and cultural habits of materialism can narrow perception or activism, and he argues for a broader, more imaginal field. The talk closes by returning to ecology as a lived relationship between perception, love, emptiness, and action.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Eros and self-view - Burbea begins by describing eros as involving psyche and logos, and shows how any view of self and other already shapes love and action.
  2. 01:57 Berry and Confucius - He turns to Wendell Berry and Confucius to frame activism as preserving qualities of heart and remaining true to conscience.
  3. 04:20 Images of nobility - He considers how words like nobility and practitioner can carry archetypal images that either open or constrain the self.
  4. 07:07 Duty and archetype - Using climate activism and an example from Luca Jacquet, he shows how duty can arise from imaginal self-views and generate energy.
  5. 13:46 Dharma and engagement - He contrasts narrow Dharma images with a broader emptiness teaching that can allow revolutionary, warrior, or otherwise disallowed self-images.
  6. 18:40 The Four Noble Truths - The talk asks whether the Four Noble Truths are read only individually, or whether they can include institutions, systems, and the suffering they cause.
  7. 24:49 Clinging and fabrication - Burbea develops the idea that letting go of clinging changes perception itself, revealing the emptiness of self, world, and nature.
  8. 30:27 Science and materialism - He critiques the modern habit of treating scientific method as worldview, drawing on Francis Bacon and Owen Barfield to question physicalism.
  9. 38:52 Two kinds of activism - He distinguishes instrumental activism from soulful, imaginal activism, arguing that climate work needs both levels and not just measurable effect.
