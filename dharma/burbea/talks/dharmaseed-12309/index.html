Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Developing Samatha/Concentration

Dharma Seed 65:49 2007-04-01

What if concentration is less about narrowing down and more about a calm, collected depth that can hold the whole life of practice? The talk moves from working with hindrances and softening tight effort to a broader case for samatha as a full path, not just a preliminary technique. Along the way, it keeps returning to the practical supports that let calm deepen: appreciation, body sensitivity, ease, and the willingness to stop making practice into a struggle. In the later part, the focus shifts toward pīti, fear around pleasure, and the surprising way deepening calm can reveal that emotions and problems are more condition-made and less fixed than they seem.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 What Samatha Means - Burbea distinguishes samatha from a narrow idea of concentration and describes it as calm, collectedness, depth, and unification.
  2. 05:25 A Lifelong Practice - He widens the view to samatha as an ongoing exploration of consciousness, perception, rest, and receptivity to wonder and beauty.
  3. 10:18 Why It Matters - Using everyday examples, he argues that samatha is a skill worth developing and that it supports freedom, steadiness, and service.
  4. 15:13 Insight Needs Support - He shows how samatha strengthens insight practice, makes realizations last, and softens the self's grip on experience.
  5. 20:45 Happiness as Basis - Burbea turns to the Buddha's teaching that happiness is the proximate cause of samatha and encourages gratitude, appreciation, and connection as fuel for practice.
  6. 26:16 Working with Hindrances - He revisits obstacles, impulsive reactivity, and self-judgment, then explains how non-involvement and inner resource make letting go possible.
  7. 32:56 Softness, Not Strain - The talk moves into objections about tightness, effort, and bodily sensitivity, with guidance to keep samatha tender, spacious, and kind.
  8. 39:16 Goals and Attachment - He examines striving, goal-orientation, and attachment, arguing for intelligent aspiration without brittle self-pressure.
  9. 46:45 Pīti and Fear - Burbea introduces pīti as bodily pleasure or rapture, discusses fear around enjoyment and selflessness, and suggests a gradual, trustful opening to it.
