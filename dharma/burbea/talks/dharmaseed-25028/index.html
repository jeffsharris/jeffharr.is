The Four Noble Truths of Responding to Climate Change

Dharma Seed 66:01 2014-10-08

Donald Rothberg frames climate change through the Four Noble Truths, moving from an honest reckoning with suffering into the causes, possibilities, and pathways for response. He draws on Buddhist ethics, Thich Nhat Hanh, Joanna Macy, Bhikkhu Bodhi, Bill McKibben, Lester Brown, and the Dalai Lama to connect inner practice with collective action. The talk balances sober climate facts with practical encouragement, especially around staying connected, avoiding despair, and taking local and political steps.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Ethics and Integrity - Rothberg opens by situating climate work inside ethical practice, integrity, and the three trainings of Buddhism.
  2. 04:25 Non-harming and Earth Care - He widens the first precept through Thich Nhat Hanh and an Earth Care Week statement to frame ecological harm as a moral concern.
  3. 06:33 Four Noble Truths Framework - He lays out the Four Noble Truths as the structure for the talk and thanks several teachers who shaped the presentation.
  4. 10:44 The Bad News - He names climate change as a grave collective suffering, reviews basic climate science, and describes the scale of likely impacts and injustice.
  5. 23:22 Meeting Distress - After pausing with the weight of the topic, he turns to practices for facing fear, grief, numbness, and shock together.
  6. 25:53 Roots of the Crisis - He outlines outer and inner causes, including fossil fuels, consumer culture, privilege, fear, distraction, and denial.
  7. 32:40 Possibility of Transformation - He shifts to the third noble truth, arguing that a sustainable and more just world is possible and pointing to rapid changes already underway.
  8. 40:29 The Path of Response - He describes personal, community, and national responses, including meditation, ethical living, local organizing, divestment, and broad mobilization.
  9. 51:58 Questions and Closing - In discussion he returns to compassion, polarization, and staying connected, then closes with dedications and a reminder of shared interdependence.
