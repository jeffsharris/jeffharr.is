Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Releasing the Self, Freeing its Demons (Part 3)

Dharma Seed 56:37 2014-10-05

Rob Burbea explores how images, fantasies, and inner figures shape practice far more than we often notice. He moves from common ways of treating imagery—ignoring it, psychologizing it, or folding it into a tidy self-story—toward a more imaginal approach in which images are met as meaningful, sometimes demanding presences. The talk keeps returning to a quiet test: what happens in the body, the heart, and the sense of self when an image is taken seriously without being literalized?

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Setting the frame - Rob introduces the theme of image and fantasy, and explains that the talk will extend the earlier teaching.
  2. 02:27 Common responses - He surveys the usual Dharma attitudes toward images: ignore them, notice and release them, or use them to soften judgment.
  3. 04:31 Psychological readings - He turns to psychotherapeutic and integrative ways of understanding images as past material, compensation, or parts of a self to be balanced.
  4. 07:07 Conceiving and experimenting - He shifts from true-versus-false to what different ways of looking lead to, emphasizing mindfulness, bodily resonance, and meaningfulness.
  5. 10:57 Image as living presence - He asks what changes when images are not judged by their content and when their relation to the body reveals whether they are fruitful.
  6. 16:20 From emptiness to freedom - The Buddha's teaching on emptiness, together with Eliot and Rilke, frames an experiment in letting images carry more power than the ego usually allows.
  7. 21:26 Daemons and angels - He develops the idea that images may not come from personal history at all, drawing on Corbin and Hillman to speak of service to psychic persons or daemons.
  8. 28:32 Risk, myth, and ethics - He addresses the danger of this approach, distinguishes mythic truth from literal belief, and asks how to tell ego-proliferation from something deeper.
  9. 36:19 What is not just ego - Through examples from Hendrix, Senna, Buddhist polemics, the Buddha's own words, and the myth of Horus, he questions how quickly we dismiss grandeur, desire, and force as mere ego.
