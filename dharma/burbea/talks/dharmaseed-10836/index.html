Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Approaches to the Hindrances

Dharma Seed 1:30:46 2008-11-06

How do you work with the hindrances without making meditation feel like a grim battle? The talk moves from a clear map of the five hindrances into a practical, almost workshop-like exploration of how they show up, how to tell them apart, and how different responses—softening, inquiry, firmness, relaxation, or more energy—can change the sitting. It also keeps returning to a bigger question: whether meditation is best understood as a craft, a form of doing, or a kind of non-doing, and what subtle habits are hidden inside each view. In the later discussion, the talk gets very specific about jealousy, self-worth, imagery, and how to make practice more personal and alive.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the subject - Burbea introduces the hindrances as common features of practice and outlines the five classical types.
  2. 03:38 Meditation as art - He steps back to ask how meditation is being viewed, presenting it as an art that involves skill, craft, and subtle development.
  3. 07:13 Questioning practice views - The talk explores tensions between doing and non-doing, and asks what kind of stance keeps practice honest, open, and alive.
  4. 14:20 Mindfulness and release - He turns to the Satipatthana Sutta and the Buddha's language of abandoning and liberating oneself from hindrances, not just noticing them.
  5. 20:56 Sloth and torpor - A detailed set of approaches is offered for dullness, including posture, light, breath, space, movement, and checking for avoidance.
  6. 27:02 Restlessness and craving - He gives practical ways to work with agitation and sense desire, emphasizing relaxation, grounded attention, nourishment, and not getting caught in fantasy.
  7. 36:54 Aversion and doubt - The discussion broadens to the subtler forms of aversion and then to doubt, distinguishing healthy questioning from paralysis.
  8. 45:15 Personalizing practice - Burbea encourages listeners to ask what they actually want from practice and to make the work meaningful rather than dutiful.
  9. 54:32 Not taking the ride - He closes the main talk by stressing recognition, non-identification, and not taking hindrances personally, then opens the room for silence and questions.
