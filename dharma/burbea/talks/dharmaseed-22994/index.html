The Eco-Crisis as a Spiritual Crisis

Dharma Seed 54:27 2014-04-20

David Loy explores the ecological crisis as a spiritual crisis, arguing that the same sense of separation that Buddhism addresses in the individual self also appears at the level of human civilization. He moves from climate change, biodiversity loss, and industrial growth to Buddhist questions about awakening, activism, and why mindfulness can become a coping strategy rather than a response to suffering. The talk closes by reading poems from Rumi and David Whyte to point toward non-separation, intimacy, and a different way of living with the earth.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Fragile Earth - Loy opens with Earth Day framing and simple images of thin water and air to show how fragile the planet is.
  2. 02:01 Seeing the Iceberg - He introduces the two-icebergs metaphor and broadens the ecological crisis beyond climate change to fisheries, plastic, and mass extinction.
  3. 08:50 Reading Extinction - A poem by Sam Taylor brings the loss of species into a more immediate, emotional register.
  4. 11:20 Beyond Climate Alone - Loy turns from visible symptoms to the deeper causes in industrial growth society and then uses the second iceberg to question Buddhism's public face.
  5. 20:08 What Awakening Means - He contrasts escape-oriented and fit-in versions of Buddhism with a third view in which awakening deconstructs the separate self and opens compassion.
  6. 33:46 Growth and Alienation - The talk draws precise parallels between the individual sense of lack and civilization's obsession with endless growth, technology, and economic expansion.
  7. 43:10 Intimacy With the World - Loy shifts to the felt realization of non-separation through examples from nature and two poems that point toward belonging rather than isolation.
  8. 49:45 A Spiritual Crisis - He returns to the ecological crisis as a collective spiritual problem and closes by insisting that inner transformation and social transformation cannot really be separated.
