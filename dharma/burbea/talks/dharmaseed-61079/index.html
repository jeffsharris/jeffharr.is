Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Image of Ethics (Part 4)

Dharma Seed 1:51:51 2020-02-17

What if ethics is not mainly about restraint, but about what kind of human being we take ourselves to be? The talk moves from remembered moments of soulmaking and imaginal experience into a historical inquiry, contrasting a flattened secular-modern sense of humanity with traditions that saw the human as dimensional, related to divinity, cosmos, and a larger telos. Along the way it uses chemo, shame, hiri and ottappa, the Protestant Reformation, Plato, and modern utilitarian or pragmatic ethics to ask whether moral life can still be felt as beautiful, noble, and soul-making rather than merely necessary.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the inquiry - Rob sets up the question of how Soulmaking Dharma might support ethics and why the talk depends on a sense of what a human being is.
  2. 03:29 Soulmaking and humanity - He invites listeners to compare ordinary secular self-understanding with moments when the self becomes imaginal and humanity feels deeper and more charged.
  3. 12:13 Kinds of suffering - Using his chemotherapy experience, he distinguishes bodily pain from soul suffering and argues that ethics must notice different levels of hurt.
  4. 17:25 Flattening the human - Rob examines utilitarian, voluntarist, and rationalist accounts of ethics, asking how each one shapes or shrinks the human being.
  5. 24:46 Reformation and rupture - He turns to the Protestant Reformation, the loss of intermediaries, and the cosmic imagery of refraction to show how Western history altered our sense of soul and divinity.
  6. 33:07 Grace and ethics - Through Luther, Calvin, Melanchthon, and related figures, he traces a historical split between grace and virtue that relegates ethics to civic order.
  7. 49:47 Telos and soulmaking - He contrasts a grace-only model with Soulmaking Dharma's more balanced view of participation, creation, and a possible human telos toward the divine.
  8. 01:03:44 Shame, eros, and dignity - Rob brings in modern pessimism, hiri and ottappa, and the role of shame and guilt, while insisting that ethics also needs love, eros, and soul.
  9. 01:23:54 What ethics is for - He ends by asking what kind of image of ethics we live by, and whether Soulmaking Dharma can restore a sense of nobility, blessedness, and a larger purpose for human life.
