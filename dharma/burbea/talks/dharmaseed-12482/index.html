Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

From Feelings to Freedom (Exploring Vedana)

Dharma Seed 51:55 2006-11-04

How can feeling tone be the doorway to freedom rather than another trap? The talk begins with bodily pain and the ordinary urge to endure, adjust, or recoil, then moves into a careful exploration of vedana as pleasant, unpleasant, or neutral across breath, sound, thought, and movement. From there it turns to the practical question of what feeds feeling tone and what lets it fade, ending in a deeper inquiry into dependent arising, non-self, and the groundlessness of experience.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Working with bodily pain - Burbea begins with guidance for handling pain in sitting practice and emphasizes that meditation is not an endurance test.
  2. 02:52 Turning toward difficulty - He encourages curiosity, honesty about one’s edge, and changing posture when needed while staying mindful of relief.
  3. 04:19 Relaxing the whole body - The instruction widens to notice tension, recoil, and the larger field of the body around painful sensation.
  4. 06:11 Microscope and space - He offers two skillful modes of attention: fine-grained investigation of pain and spacious awareness that gives it context.
  5. 09:21 What vedana means - The talk shifts from bodily pain to vedana as feeling tone, and Burbea explains how every experience can be met as pleasant, unpleasant, or neutral.
  6. 15:08 Sensitivity to feeling tone - He describes how subtle vedana is often overlooked and how it can support calm, metta, and greater attentiveness in practice.
  7. 21:44 Seeing vedana as not self - Using the Buddha’s phrase 'see vedana in vedana,' he shows how feeling becomes less personal when self-judgment is not added to it.
  8. 28:43 Grasping and aversion - Burbea explains the three ways we react to feeling tone and shows how suffering drops when the relationship to experience is relaxed.
  9. 33:03 Conditions and groundlessness - He turns to samudaya and dependent origination, asking what feeds vedana and what lets it fade, and points toward the groundless, interdependent nature of experience.
