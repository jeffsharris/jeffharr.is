Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Way of Non-Clinging (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 1:18:28 2017-01-15

What if the common teaching to “just let go” is true, but still too simple to carry the whole path? The talk starts by sorting through the overlapping Pali language of desire, craving, clinging, and fetters, then uses dependent origination to show how these movements actually build suffering in ordinary life. From there it moves into practical ways of meeting craving in real time—through attention to feeling tone, notice of bodily tension, reflection, and the quiet recognition that papanca, or proliferating story, keeps the whole thing spinning. By the end, the talk opens a larger question: whether non-clinging is not only release from suffering, but a doorway into a deeper freedom, emptiness, and a wider sense of beauty and sacredness.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the context - Burbea explains that this talk is a preface to a later exploration of eros and practice, and sets up the question of what kind of Dharma framework best serves liberation.
  2. 04:36 Pali terms and fetters - He surveys overlapping Pali words for desire and clinging, using the early texts to show both the usefulness and the limits of fine conceptual distinctions.
  3. 13:59 Dependent origination map - The talk turns to the twelve links as a practical map for seeing how craving and clinging move into suffering.
  4. 16:52 Everyday craving examples - Chocolate cake and cigarette craving are used to make the abstract sequence feel immediate and embodied.
  5. 19:20 Anatomy of clinging - Burbea walks through craving, self-building, and proliferation as processes that tighten experience and reinforce suffering, while recommending actual entry points for practice.
  6. 40:44 Simple letting go - He considers the familiar instruction to let go of everything, then shows how mindfulness, reflection, and recognizing papanca can support it in limited but real ways.
  7. 52:56 Working with dukkha - The talk becomes more phenomenological, showing how craving feels in body and mind, including aversion, grasping at pleasure, and the pressure that can be tolerated and released.
  8. 01:04:04 Impermanence and its limits - Impermanence is presented as a powerful doorway to letting go, but Burbea says this alone still leaves a narrower view of practice than the Dharma allows.
  9. 01:11:32 Beyond simplism - The closing section points toward deeper insight into the unfabricated, emptiness, and the relation between appearances and timelessness, preparing for the next talk in the series.
