Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Sila and Soul (Part 7)

Dharma Seed 1:19:56 2019-06-15

What happens when moral life is not a clean choice between right and wrong, but a series of real tensions between goods that both matter? The talk moves through Hartmann’s idea of antinomies of value, contrasting simple oppositions like kindness and unkindness with harder conflicts such as harmony and conflict, individuality and community, or love of the nearest and love of the remote. It then turns to Dharma practice, asking how sila, the seven factors of awakening, and even the image of the Buddha can be understood when values do not all fully fit together at once.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Simple oppositions - Burbea begins with straightforward value oppositions like kindness and unkindness, honesty and dishonesty, then distinguishes these from harder conflicts among goods.
  2. 01:55 Hartmann and antinomies - He introduces Hartmann’s idea of antinomy and develops examples where values can support one another yet still pull in different directions.
  3. 10:21 Conflict as a value - Through Heraclitus and Hartmann, he argues that conflict can be ethically productive, sharpening discernment and opening new possibilities.
  4. 17:26 Classical virtue and tragedy - Burbea contrasts Hartmann’s view with Plato, Aristotle, Aquinas, and Sophocles, emphasizing tragic situations where good meets good and choice becomes unavoidable.
  5. 26:50 Balancing practice qualities - He turns to the seven factors of awakening and to the felt tension between energizing and calming qualities, including a tender-heartedness he says is not simply metta or compassion.
  6. 30:03 Three kayas and beyondness - The Mahayana three bodies are used to suggest a transcendent synthesis of qualities that cannot all manifest at once in ordinary human life.
  7. 35:50 Absolutes and mysticism - Burbea brings in John Findlay, Hartmann, Jeffrey Kripal, and Jorge Ferrer to question easy claims about moral mysticism, perennialism, and shared spiritual conclusions.
  8. 53:21 Plural psyche, simple rules - Using James Hillman and then Hartmann again, he reflects on a polytheistic psyche and the way commandments and precepts give a useful minimum without exhausting ethics.
  9. 01:09:44 Minimums and hard choices - He closes by reading Hartmann on justice as a minimum condition and returns to the impossibility of perfectly guiltless moral life, even for the Buddha.
