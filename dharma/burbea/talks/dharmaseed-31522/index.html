A Sacred Universe: Insight, Theophany, Cosmopoesis (Part 3)

Dharma Seed 1:35:33 2015-08-12

How can words like God, divine, and theophany be opened up so they do not collapse into loaded ideas or simple belief-versus-disbelief debates? The talk moves from the unfabricated and the deathless, through emptiness and dependent origination, toward a broader account of how perception becomes less rigid and how meaning can still be real without being fixed. From there it widens into tantric and imaginal territory, including Buddha nature, Luria’s tzimtzum, and a river-side experience where the cosmos seemed to speak, so that personal and impersonal senses of divinity can be held without forcing them into one frame.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the Question - He begins by asking what words like God and divine might mean, and why they so often provoke strong reactions.
  2. 03:33 Language and Conceptual Frames - He broadens the vocabulary through Tibetan, Christian, Jewish, Islamic, and philosophical references, and explains why conceptual frameworks matter in practice.
  3. 10:06 Why Frameworks Matter - He argues that soul and soulmaking need spacious, fertile concepts that do not narrow experience or shut down possibility.
  4. 15:27 The Unfabricated - Using multiple mystical traditions and the Buddha's language, he describes the unfabricated, the deathless, and consciousness without attribute as a first major opening.
  5. 22:34 Less Fabricated States - He distinguishes the fully unfabricated from other beautiful but still conditioned states such as cosmic love, universal awareness, and cosmic oneness.
  6. 28:15 Universal and Particular - He contrasts universal, impersonal divinity with a fourth possibility: theophany, where divinity appears in a particular person, thing, or imaginal figure.
  7. 31:13 Fabrication and Insight - He ties these experiences back to emptiness, dependent origination, and delusion, showing how less fabrication opens deeper perception.
  8. 43:38 Buddha Nature and Gnosis - He uses not-self, Mipham, and Buddhist Tantric language to describe an empty awareness that knows emptiness and perceives divine appearances.
  9. 53:19 Luria and Divine Contraction - He turns to Isaac Luria's tzimtzum as a mythic parallel to fabrication and unfabrication, emphasizing that these are perspectival teachings rather than final truths.
