Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

400 and Rising

Dharma Seed 42:10 2013-05-17

What do we do when the climate crisis feels too big to hold, and how do we stay open without becoming numb? The talk begins with a recent trip through monastery communities and the sight of eagles, then turns toward the sharper question of rising carbon, collective denial, and the feeling of living inside a damaged world. From there it moves through the four Brahma Viharas—loving-kindness, compassion, appreciative joy, and equanimity—as ways to meet grief with clarity and action, without losing sight of the vulnerability of sentient life. A Buddha story about caring for a sick monk finally grounds the whole talk in ordinary responsibility: ultimate truth does not cancel the need to help.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Arriving in community - She opens with recent travels to monasteries and reflects on long practice, community life, and the softening that comes from sustained mutual adjustment.
  2. 02:23 Hermitage and the eagles - A visit to the Pacific Hermitage leads into images of kayaking, birds, and the felt sense of being quietly part of nature rather than separate from it.
  3. 06:36 The illusion of control - She widens into reflection on human vulnerability, modern control, and the ways city life and technological mastery can obscure our dependence on the natural world.
  4. 12:27 400 parts per million - Using Bill McKibben and 350.org, she frames climate change in measurable terms and names 400 ppm as a serious threshold the world has already crossed.
  5. 14:17 Listening for the feminine - A brief account of hearing the Dalai Lama at an environmental summit emphasizes compassion, nurturing, listening, dialogue, and education as urgently needed qualities.
  6. 19:18 Seeing the larger truth - She brings in impermanence, no-self, and collective responsibility, asking how Buddhist practice meets the reality of climate grief and shared harm.
  7. 22:44 Four Brahma Viharas - Loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity are presented as inner supports that steady the heart without denying the crisis.
  8. 28:59 Equanimity and karma - She explains equanimity through the Buddha’s reflection on karma, emphasizing intention, responsibility, and the freedom to shape how we meet the present.
  9. 33:56 Care for what is sick - The talk ends by holding ultimate truth together with relative care, using the sick monk story to argue that impermanence never excuses neglect.
