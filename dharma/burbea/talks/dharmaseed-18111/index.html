Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Necessity of Fantasy

Dharma Seed 68:38 2012-12-31

What happens when the image of practice narrows to calmness, non-involvement, and personal freedom, while the wider world is asking for responsibility? The talk moves from ethics as a base of the path, through climate change, species loss, and the silence of Dharma communities, into a larger claim that our lives are shaped by the stories and images we live by. By the end, the speaker is asking whether practice needs a wider range of fantasies, myths, and forms of awakening that can honor grief, outrage, engagement, and the scale of our shared life.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Setting the frame - He explains that this talk will move differently from ordinary teaching, taking up the bigger picture and the role of view and imagination in practice.
  2. 06:29 Ethics as foundation - Burbea redefines ethics as how we live in relation to others, contrasts it with inner critic culture, and places it at the base of the Buddhist path.
  3. 14:41 Climate and responsibility - He widens ethics beyond the personal to species loss, climate change, and collective responsibility, using contemporary crisis as the pressure behind the talk.
  4. 22:18 Silence and Buddhist culture - He asks why Dharma communities stay quiet, brings in the language of commodification and capitalism, and questions the cultural image of spiritual practice.
  5. 28:33 Fantasy and image - Burbea argues that Buddhist practice is shaped by fantasy and image whether or not we notice it, and that one cannot simply leave fantasy behind.
  6. 32:52 Narrowed images - He critiques a constricted Dharma imagination, asking what has been excluded from the image of awakening, teacherhood, and a meaningful path.
  7. 41:08 Dukkha beyond the self - He examines precepts, omission, social structures, and the Four Noble Truths, suggesting that suffering and its causes may be far wider than individual patterns.
  8. 50:31 Stories that shape culture - Burbea turns to Norman Brown, Ben Okri, and Carl Sagan to show how stories and images alter collective feeling, values, and responsibility.
  9. 58:23 Widening the range - He closes by calling for a larger plurality of fantasies and images in Dharma, including more room for wildness, outrage, Bodhisattva aspiration, and a more engaged modern lion’s roar.
