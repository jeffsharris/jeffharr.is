Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Sila and Soul (Part 8)

Dharma Seed 1:54:15 2019-06-16

In this talk, Rob Burbea explores ethics through the lens of soulmaking, arguing that moral life is not only about universal values but also about the particular callings of individual souls. He uses Hartman’s language to distinguish ordinary goodness from rarer forms such as nobility, radiant virtue, and a duty to one’s own ideal personality, then turns to hatred, powerlessness, and how blocked soul-making can harden into contraction. The talk closes by widening ethics again: toward love of the remote, responsibility to future generations, and a more open-ended view of the good life as a quest.

Chapters

  1. 05:04 Universal values and soul - He introduces the tension between shared ethical foundations and the individual soul’s different callings and emphases.
  2. 16:31 Radiant virtue - Using Bob Marley and Hartman, he explores uncommon virtues that can shine through a person even when other moral expectations are judged differently.
  3. 32:42 Nobility and new values - He reads Hartmut Rosa on nobility, suggesting that some souls are drawn to the uncommon and may help disclose newer ethical values.
  4. 42:26 Hatred and powerlessness - He defines hatred carefully, shows how it can arise from blocked desire and helplessness, and links it to the need for soul-making and differentiation.
  5. 01:07:35 Polemic and courage - Through James Hillman and the protest on the deportation flight, he shows how conflict, when transformed, can become power, clarity, and imaginal action rather than cruelty.
  6. 01:23:30 Love of the remote - He turns to Nietzsche and Hartmann’s Fernstenliebe, contrasting care for those nearest to us with responsibility to the far future and to the most capable among future generations.
  7. 01:35:18 Creative spirit and sacrifice - He reflects on the hard, lonely quality of work oriented toward future generations, with Beethoven, Mahler, and the idea of duty to the angels as examples.
  8. 01:46:35 Narrative unity of a life - He closes with MacIntyre’s account of life as a quest, linking moral goodness to an open-ended search that gradually reveals both the goal and the self.
