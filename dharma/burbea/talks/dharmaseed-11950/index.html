Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Compassion

Dharma Seed 69:55 2008-02-04

What does compassion actually ask of the heart: to open to suffering, or to close down out of fear? The talk moves from defining compassion as a natural response to suffering, through its many qualities—empathy, giving, steadiness, wisdom, listening, joy—to the subtle ways it gets distorted by fear, anger, pity, and grief. From there it turns practical, showing how compassion can be cultivated through inquiry, the company we keep, service, self-compassion, and a wider sense of shared vulnerability and non-separateness.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 What compassion is - Burbea introduces compassion as more than a feeling, describing it as the heart’s response to suffering and outlining its basic movement toward alleviation and care.
  2. 02:35 Many strands of compassion - He names compassion’s components—empathy, giving, equanimity, wisdom, kindness, acceptance, humor, listening, opening, joy, and faith—and explains why it is active and not merely emotional.
  3. 06:25 Ambivalence and longing - The talk turns to the common fear that compassion will burden or overwhelm us, alongside the deeper intuition that compassion brings reconnection, sweetness, and a more spacious life.
  4. 15:45 Questions and practices - Using the Gertrude Stein story, he emphasizes asking the right questions, then sketches several ways compassion is cultivated: devotional tuning, insight practice, and a life organized around compassion.
  5. 21:22 Conditions that nurture it - Burbea explores what supports compassion: willingness to touch suffering, guarding against numbness, the softening of self-view, acting compassionately, associating with the compassionate, and caring for happiness.
  6. 27:51 Four near enemies - He examines fear, anger, pity, and grief as distortions of compassion, using stories and examples to show how blame, identification, and overwhelm can block the heart’s natural movement.
  7. 44:04 Empathy and giving - The talk balances compassion’s receptive side with its active side, including the image of Avalokiteshvara as one who listens and reaches out, and the need to keep taking in and giving out in proportion.
  8. 53:49 Shared vulnerability - Burbea widens the frame through spaciousness, self-compassion, and Naomi Shihab Nye’s poem 'Kindness,' pointing to the commonality of sorrow, uncertainty, and bewilderment.
  9. 01:04:55 Emptiness and interpenetration - He closes by reframing anatta as fullness and interconnection, moving through body, breath, stars, and perception to suggest a non-separate, interpenetrating view of self and world.
