Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Healing the Imagination (Q & A)

Dharma Seed 40:24 2016-07-29

What does it mean to trust what works in practice when the mind insists one thing and the body says another? In this Q&A, the talk moves from that question into a practical compass of release, relief, harmony, and loosening in the energy body, especially in imaginal and mindfulness practice. It then widens into questions of lingering with images, the balance of soul and spirit, and how different people may need different paths over time. The closing exchange turns to a strong metta experience and how to work with it when it feels overwhelming, boring, or deeply alive.

Chapters

  1. 00:33 What works in practice - Rob first clarifies that “follow what works” is usually about meditation in the moment, not only a life path or career decision.
  2. 01:55 Energy body as compass - He explains how the energy body can show harmony, openness, or loosening even when the mind is skeptical about an image or practice.
  3. 04:26 Release and relief - Rob describes subtle release, relief, and ease as signs that insight or mindfulness practice is moving in the right direction.
  4. 06:21 Working with images - He responds to questions about rapidly changing imaginal material, suggesting steadiness, patience, and attention to whether apparently separate images may be aspects of one unfolding image.
  5. 13:00 Gestures and posture - The conversation turns to mudras and how small changes in hand position can alter the felt tone of practice, sometimes making a marked difference.
  6. 16:58 Tantra and emptiness - A question about Tantra leads into a broader reflection on pleasure, grasping, and the Buddhist teaching on kilesas, including the deeper move of seeing them as empty.
  7. 24:10 Soul, spirit, and fit - Rob develops a nuanced account of soulmaking, spirit, discipline, and transcendence, arguing that different people need different proportions of imagery, eros, purity, and spaciousness.
  8. 32:04 Metta as energy - In the final question, he offers practical ways to work with strong metta: letting energy move outward, opening to its non-personal or divine dimension, and bringing it into contact with pain.
