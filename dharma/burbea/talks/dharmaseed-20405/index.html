Learning from Climate Change

Dharma Seed 42:00 2013-07-12

Ayya Santacitta reflects on climate change as both a practical emergency and a spiritual challenge: a situation that asks for honest attention, emotional digestion, and a wider sense of belonging. She moves from the difficulty of speaking about climate without shutting people down, to a critique of the Enlightenment-era worldview, to teachings on grief, resilience, and ecological identity. The talk closes by placing the climate crisis in a larger evolutionary frame, where discomfort can become a path into deeper responsibility and practice.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Naming the dilemma - She opens with the challenge of teaching climate change honestly without pushing people away.
  2. 02:28 Shock and denial - Climate disruption is compared to traumatic news that first meets disbelief, anger, and shutdown.
  3. 06:00 Discomfort as guidance - She argues that discomfort carries information and can help communities adjust course.
  4. 08:25 Universe as scripture - Thomas Berry’s line becomes a way to trust the larger intelligence of evolution beyond the thinking mind.
  5. 14:17 Outgrown worldviews - She traces the Enlightenment and mechanistic thinking, suggesting they no longer fit current conditions.
  6. 19:58 Grief and ecological self - Using Arne Næss and Joanna Macy, she describes how sitting with climate grief can open a wider identity and a more natural response.
  7. 24:54 Attention and resilience - She returns to the need for samatha, clear attention, and collective resilience in the face of overwhelming information.
  8. 32:36 Learning from collapse - Easter Island and Rumi frame the danger of overshoot, while also pointing to humility, practice, and the possibility of waking up.
  9. 39:53 Closing appeal - She ends by saying the crisis can catapult us into a new worldview if we are willing to meet it and keep listening.
