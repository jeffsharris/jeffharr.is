Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

"A Body At Peace With Itself"

Dharma Seed 58:18 2007-11-03

How do you stop fighting the body and begin to meet it as a source of practice rather than a problem to solve? The talk moves from direct bodily awareness, through pain, comfort, and loving-kindness, into a subtle argument that perception itself can be trained: sensations are not just endured, but seen, softened, and sometimes re-seen altogether. It then widens into impermanence, not-self, and the body’s fragility, using images of foam, mirage, and giving the body back to nature to loosen identification without turning cold or dismissive. Quietly and steadily, the talk keeps returning to the same practical question: can this body be met with less pressure and more freedom?

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the theme - Matthew introduces the talk as a set of possible ways of working with the body in meditation and frames mindfulness of the body as one among several valid approaches.
  2. 05:40 Body at peace - He quotes Tsongkhapa on the preciousness of a body at peace with itself and uses that to invite a kinder relationship to embodiment and impermanence.
  3. 06:53 See body as body - The talk turns to the Buddha’s instruction to see the body as body, not as a mirror of self-worth, appearance, or comparison.
  4. 11:22 Bare attention - He describes direct, non-conceptual attention to bodily experience and the range of sensations that open when the body is felt closely and gently.
  5. 16:07 Working with pain - Matthew offers practical ways to meet discomfort: relaxing the rest of the body, exploring the edges of pain, widening awareness, and softening aversion.
  6. 22:16 Pleasure and metta - He shifts to comfort, well-being, and loving-kindness, emphasizing that body sense is central to metta and that pleasure can be learned and explored without clinging.
  7. 28:54 Body as deep refuge - Using Ananda’s response after the deaths of Sariputra and the Buddha, he presents mindfulness immersed in the body as a deep resource, even in illness and grief.
  8. 40:07 Letting body go - The talk moves into relinquishment and the three characteristics, including a contemplation of the body as not-self, not controllable, and returning to nature.
  9. 49:19 Foam and deathless - Matthew quotes the Buddha’s foam simile and closes by linking body mindfulness to emptiness, aging, sickness, death, and the possibility of realizing the deathless.
