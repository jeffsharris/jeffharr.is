Dharma practice and the climate Crisis

Dharma Seed 61:12 2013-04-17

The talk begins with a plain dilemma: meditation practice is compelling, and the climate crisis is compelling, so how do we not choose one against the other? From there, the speaker moves through examples, from Thich Nhat Hanh’s engaged Buddhism and Merton’s monastic activism to Joanna Macy’s “Great Turning,” to show that inner practice and outer response can belong together in different ways. The second half turns practical and personal, asking where one’s gifts lie, how to avoid burnout and self-righteousness, and why climate action without Dharma can distort, while Dharma without the world can also narrow. It ends with a brief poem about the world being on fire and “no time not to love,” holding urgency and steadiness in the same breath.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Retreat and urgency - Rothberg opens by naming the contrast between the beauty of retreat and the urgency of the climate crisis.
  2. 04:43 The climate dilemma - He lays out the apparent choice between deep practice and worldly action, then shows why climate change makes that tension impossible to ignore.
  3. 14:18 Engaged Buddhism examples - Thich Nhat Hanh, Thomas Merton, Buddhadasa, Dorothy Day, Aung San Suu Kyi, and Gandhi are offered as models of different ways to combine inner and outer life.
  4. 17:31 Joanna Macy's framework - Rothberg turns to the Great Turning and its three modes of response: holding actions, shifting institutions, and shifting consciousness.
  5. 20:24 Vocation and critique - He asks listeners to find where their gifts meet the world's need, while also warning that meditation can become a refuge from responsibility.
  6. 26:38 Practice needs ethics - Using Gary Snyder's remarks, he argues that wisdom, meditation, and ethics belong together and that Dharma must include social awareness and non-harming.
  7. 36:08 Why activism needs practice - He names problems in activism such as anger, self-righteousness, burnout, and demonization, and says these are exactly the places where practice matters.
  8. 41:23 Concrete climate responses - The talk moves into practical examples, including household reductions, city plans, organizations, and Buddhist ecology groups.
  9. 50:18 Questions and closing - In dialogue with the audience, Rothberg discusses differences within climate work and ends by returning to urgency, equanimity, and the poem that says there is no time not to love.
