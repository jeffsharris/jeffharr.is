Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

In Praise of Restlessness

Dharma Seed 1:15:58 2014-11-25

What if the problem is not restlessness itself, but the way we keep confusing agitation, inquiry, and a deeper kind of fire? The talk moves from the hidden metaphysics and myths inside any Dharma view into a set of contrasting images: the seeker, the fugitive, the settled sage, and the Hermes-like wanderer who does not want a final theory. From there it opens into a wide argument that desire, beauty, and even spiritual longing may be part of the path, not obstacles to it, and that practice may be less about reaching a fixed end than about staying with an open-ended work.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Setting the frame - Rob briefly recaps the first two talks and introduces the central claim that Dharma always carries hidden assumptions about what is real and how it is known.
  2. 06:06 Metaphysics and myth - He unpacks how metaphysics and mythos quietly shape practice, then asks what happens when those hidden supports are brought to the foreground.
  3. 15:14 Homelessness and inquiry - The talk turns to images of homelessness, seekerhood, and fugitive running, before distinguishing agitation from the more fruitful restlessness of inquiry.
  4. 24:35 Creative unrest - Using Critchley, Picasso, and alchemy, he argues that some disturbance is part of real work and that the path may need heat rather than premature settling.
  5. 30:38 Desire on the path - He explores desire as a legitimate force in science, religion, and practice, drawing on Jung, the Buddha, the Dalai Lama, St. John of the Cross, and others.
  6. 39:55 Pothos and endless work - The talk widens into longing, beauty, and the sense that practice can be driven by an unending task rather than a final endpoint.
  7. 50:43 Beauty and enchantment - He suggests that people often practice not only to reduce suffering but for wonder, beauty, and a more enchanted relation to the world.
  8. 01:03:37 Dharma as art - The closing section develops the strongest form of the argument: Dharma as art, with poetic truth, multiple readings, and a liberating openness that cannot be boxed in.
