Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Dimensionality, Humility, Duty, and Love

Dharma Seed 1:43:19 2019-05-31

What changes when an image is not just something seen, but a presence that can be noticed, ignited, and answered? The talk moves from the practical point of the imaginal elements—how they help discern what is genuinely imaginal and how they can be worked with in practice—into revisions on twoness, particularity, and the danger of reducing an image to one meaning. From there it widens into dimensionality, humility, duty, prayer, and love, including a long reflection on how imaginal love differs from metta and how it can reveal the ideal or deepest dimension of a person.

Chapters

  1. 01:17 Purpose of the lattice - He explains that the imaginal elements are meant both for discernment and for active practice, where noticing or tweaking an element can ignite others.
  2. 09:43 Two revisions - He offers two corrections to the list: images should not be reduced to one meaning, and twoness means retaining particularity rather than dissolving into oneness.
  3. 14:16 Dimensionality and depth - He develops dimensionality as depth and other-level being, using Corbin to suggest that a person can be a refraction or emanation of an angel.
  4. 20:52 Anchors in depth - He argues that imaginal depth matters because it gives the soul a different kind of anchor than ordinary solidity, and can include cosmic love and awareness.
  5. 40:38 Humility and self - He redefines humility as a relation to mystery, love, and divinity, not self-denigration, and ties it to a lighter, less fabricated sense of self.
  6. 51:52 Prayer and desire - He shows how prayer can increase separation if desire hardens the self, then suggests a subtler understanding in which personal desire participates in divine desire.
  7. 01:02:17 Duty and rightness - He explores how the rightness of an image can carry a felt duty, sometimes through difficult or stark figures, and how necessity may be woven into imaginal life.
  8. 01:15:52 Particular love - He contrasts metta with the particular, demanding love of imaginal figures, then uses Hartmann to describe love as seeing the ideal or highest possibility in another.
  9. 01:33:28 Love as knowing - He closes by emphasizing that imaginal love is also a mode of knowing: it sees through the empirical surface, recognizes the ideal, and can guide and anchor a life.
