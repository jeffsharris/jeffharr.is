Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Meditator as Revolutionary

Dharma Seed 1:10:02 2011-07-31

Rob Burbea asks what practice is actually for, then widens the question beyond private calm to the social and ecological conditions shaping suffering. He moves from spiritual archetypes and the fantasy of awakening to climate change, consumer culture, and the ethical responsibilities that come with meditation. By the end, the talk argues for a Dharma that can hold grief, resist numbness, and make room for a larger love and a longer view.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 What practice is for - He opens by asking what practice is leading toward and whether we are conscious of the fantasy that motivates us.
  2. 03:06 Archetypes of awakening - He contrasts the serene Buddha image with Jesus overturning the tables to show how spiritual symbols shape practice and attitude.
  3. 06:13 Sensitivity and suffering - He reflects on increased sensitivity in practice, the meaning of suffering, and how traditions inherit different assumptions about what hurts.
  4. 12:15 Widening the picture - He traces a widening understanding of suffering from Freud through family wounds to social, environmental, and global causes.
  5. 25:41 Values in a global age - He questions whether the five precepts are enough for contemporary life and brings in inequality, climate data, and the shape of modern culture.
  6. 33:58 Culture shapes the mind - He critiques detached objectivity, advertising, and consumerism, arguing that values and emotions are being trained by the surrounding system.
  7. 43:14 Turning in and out - He develops the idea of the meditator as revolutionary: inwardly meeting pain, outwardly responding to collective suffering and injustice.
  8. 55:34 Justice, courage, and long view - He draws on Hillman, Bhikkhu Bodhi, and others to argue that Dharma should speak about values, justice, risk, mortality, and emptiness.
  9. 01:08:05 Creative maladjustment - He closes with Martin Luther King Jr., framing the needed response as principled nonconformity and creative maladjustment to the times.
