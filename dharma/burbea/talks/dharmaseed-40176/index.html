From Mindfulness to Divinity: Towards the Tracing of a Phenomenology of Soul (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 1:51:30 2017-02-19

What happens when mindfulness is widened enough to notice that eros, fantasy, and soul-making are not distractions but part of the texture of being human? The talk moves from that opening claim into a close reading of image, showing how the imaginal can feel theatrical, mysterious, timeless, and relational, and how it can reshape the sense of self, other, and world. From there, it turns to the tension between reducing suffering and allowing soul-making, then widens again into a phenomenological account of divinity, the unfabricated, and the ways practice can open both transcendent and immanent dimensions of experience.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Eros and soul-making - He begins by saying that meaningfulness, devotion, eros, fantasy, and soul-making are not optional extras but recurring features of human life.
  2. 04:30 What image reveals - He unpacks the imaginal as theatrical, timeless, mysterious, and relational, with love, eros, and sacredness implicit in it.
  3. 10:13 Subtle fantasies in practice - He shows how life and fantasy interpenetrate, how ordinary moods and identities can hide imaginal structures, and how practice can call forth rather than impose images.
  4. 17:55 Life as image - The talk widens the frame so that meditation, relationship, work, and culture can all be sensed as part of a single imaginal field.
  5. 24:48 The Buddha as fantasy - He turns to the ways practitioners construct a Buddha, arguing that this imaginative relationship can be more fruitful than a flat historical one.
  6. 32:14 Ways of looking - He asks how we choose between perspectives, contrasting the usual Buddhist aim of reducing suffering with the added question of what brings soul-making.
  7. 39:31 Pathos and suffering - He explores how soul-making can involve tension, pathos, and even pain, drawing on James Hillman and Ajahn Chah to keep the issue unsettled.
  8. 46:52 Suffering and interpretation - He examines how retreat culture, discipline, and ideas of purification or awakening can wrap suffering into fantasy, and how the meanings of dukkha vary.
  9. 59:20 What divinity means - He distinguishes divinity as something inexhaustible, relational, and beyond full capture, with experiences and concepts both unfolding over time.
