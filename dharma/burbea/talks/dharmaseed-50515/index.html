Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Transformation in Soul (Q & A)

Dharma Seed 36:42 2018-02-05

In this Q&A, Rob Burbea explores how imaginal practice, healing, and emptiness can fit together without reducing any one of them to the others. He contrasts a flattened reading of selflessness with a more dimensional view in which self-images, personality, and difficult emotions can all become part of soulmaking. The talk moves from the question of what is being healed, to the role of anger and grief, and then toward a working definition of soul as something flexible, relational, and not fully capturable by concepts.

Chapters

  1. 00:24 Images, healing, emptiness - A listener asks how imaginal self-images relate to Buddhist emptiness and healing, and Rob begins by framing the whole problem.
  2. 02:34 What is being healed? - Rob examines whether the work is to deconstruct the self, strengthen it, or open another way of understanding what healing means.
  3. 04:46 Divine uniqueness - He describes a middle way in which a person's uniqueness, including pain and difficulty, can be sensed as having sacred depth rather than being dismissed.
  4. 08:27 Multiplicity and freedom - Rob explains how soulmaking can loosen rigidity by revealing many possible modes of self, drawing on James Hillman’s language of psychic plurality.
  5. 12:21 Emptiness and play - He returns to emptiness as a basis for flexible self-construction and says that self can be shaped in the service of soulfulness as well as dukkha reduction.
  6. 14:58 All directions are good - Rob reflects on boundaries, self-shoring, and psychological work, stressing that the error is not any one path but being trapped in only one view.
  7. 18:25 Anger, grief, image - In response to a question about difficult emotions, he shows how grief and anger can open into images, duties, and a more vivid sense of soul.
  8. 25:30 Sharpness without cruelty - Rob distinguishes forceful or cutting expression from cruelty and describes how anger can carry a wider ethical or archetypal purpose.
  9. 30:19 What soul means - He closes by offering two ways of understanding soul and by showing how practices can expand or contract soulmaking depending on their limits.
