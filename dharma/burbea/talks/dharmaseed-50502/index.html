Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

What is Awakening? (Part 3)

Dharma Seed 59:15 2018-01-07

Rob Burbea questions the usual desire to fix a single, historical definition of awakening and instead asks what different views of awakening produce in practice. He moves through ethics, tradition, genealogy, and the cultural shaping of Dharma, then sketches a trajectory of awakening through fabrication, emptiness, and participatory knowing. In the final third, he turns personal, using a cancer diagnosis and later imaginal reflections on death to describe what actually brings freedom, equanimity, mystery, and a sense of sacredness.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Questioning the Bar - Rob begins by questioning why we argue over the proper threshold for stream entry instead of asking what such standards are doing in the psyche.
  2. 05:21 Ethics and Awakening - He examines ethics as part of awakening, using climate change, carbon use, and shifting moral norms to show how cultural assumptions shape our judgments.
  3. 15:11 What Awakening Means - The talk widens from ethics to the larger question of how awakening relates to tradition and what kind of relationship practice has to precepts and conduct.
  4. 16:53 Tradition and Genealogy - Rob draws on Nietzsche, Foucault, and Einstein to argue that traditions are layered, mixed, and historically altered rather than pure or single in origin.
  5. 27:29 Western Dharma Questions - He turns to the West's postmodern and non-transcendent mood, suggesting that the question is not only what awakening is but what it can mean here.
  6. 33:44 Fabrication and Emptiness - Rob outlines a trajectory of awakening through exploring fabrication, seeing self and phenomena as empty, and eventually recognizing the emptiness of any final conceptual framework.
  7. 40:30 Open-Ended Awakening - He broadens awakening to include healing, kindness, and the ending of inner suffering while insisting that awakening itself remains empty and open-ended.
  8. 48:15 Facing Death Personally - In a personal section, he describes receiving a cancer prognosis and reflects on which insights and imaginal ways of seeing actually changed his relation to dying.
  9. 57:20 Doors and Gates - He closes by asking which ways of looking matter most and returning to awakening as a movement that opens doors, gates, and possibility without fixed end.
