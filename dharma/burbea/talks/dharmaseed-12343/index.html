Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Passion, Desire, and the Path

Dharma Seed 62:22 2007-03-16

Can desire actually belong on the path, instead of being treated only as something to eliminate? The talk moves from examples of fierce renunciation and quiet devotion, through questions of death, practice energy, and the four factors of balanced effort, toward a more searching look at desire in sexuality, romance, fatigue, and retreat life. It ends by contrasting the work of cultivating well-being with the subtler practice of letting go, asking what happens when aspiration, effort, and even the idea of a fixed goal are seen more deeply.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Passion on the path - Rob introduces passion and desire as central questions for practice and frames the talk against the common tendency to demonize them.
  2. 02:12 Renunciation with fire - Using the Buddha, Kalu Rinpoche's teacher, and an Antarctic expedition, he shows that deep renunciation can still be driven by strong energy and interest.
  3. 06:36 Urgency and possibility - He turns to the possibilities of practice, the desire for happiness, mortality, and Krishnamurti's image of coming to life with a thimble.
  4. 09:59 Quiet passion sustained - The talk asks what feeds endurance in practice, then describes passion as something that rises and falls and must be cared for rather than assumed.
  5. 13:32 Goals, aspiration, and effort - Rob examines the discomfort around goals, then uses Shantideva and the four factors of effort—aspiration, confidence, joy, and rest—to sketch balanced practice.
  6. 20:10 Where desire moves - He reflects on how desire shapes life, draws on the Upanishads, and warns against squandering energy through distraction, trivia, and scattered attention.
  7. 25:47 Sex, romance, and retreat - This section opens up desire in sexuality and romance, including retreat attraction, and asks whether practice is opening the heart or shrinking into fixation.
  8. 34:10 What feeds experience - Rob repeatedly asks what is feeding a desire or relationship, considering loneliness, cultural pressure, alcohol, and the way energy can be lost or distorted.
  9. 43:14 Energy, tiredness, and release - He revisits effort, sleep, laziness, and tiredness, then shows how craving and aversion exhaust the system while non-craving can free energy.
