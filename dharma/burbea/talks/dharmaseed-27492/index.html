Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Image, Mythos, Dharma (Part Two)

Dharma Seed 35:23 2014-12-07

What if the real obstacle is not imagination itself, but the habit of taking experience too literally? The talk moves from common fears about imaginal practice—madness, confusion, losing touch with reality—into a careful argument that mindfulness, poetry, and mythos all operate with assumptions that shape practice whether we notice them or not. It then traces a path from early Buddhist death contemplation and recollection practices, through the Buddha’s transcendent thrust toward the unfabricated, to later Mahayana and Vajrayana ways of validating image and emptiness together. By the end, the speaker suggests that meaningfulness and love are already saturated with image and fantasy, and that exploring that can open something practical rather than merely theoretical.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Setting Up the Question - Burbea introduces the theme, names the discomfort some people feel, and begins by describing imaginal practice as something that often arises in a sensitive one-to-one setting.
  2. 04:19 Healthy and Unhealthy Imagining - He addresses fears about madness by contrasting nuanced imaginal relationship with literal hallucination, drawing on Mary Watkins and Percival's Narrative.
  3. 06:35 Metaphor, Canon, and Presence - He uses examples of holy war, death contemplation, and recollecting the Buddha to argue that imagination is already present in Buddhist practice and that the issue is often literalism.
  4. 09:47 Reality and Assumptions - Burbea distinguishes objective and experiential reality, then breaks down the hidden metaphysical assumptions carried by ontology, epistemology, and cosmology.
  5. 14:05 Modernism and Matter - He critiques the modern assumption that matter is the only reality and uses physics to suggest that even science does not support such a simple picture.
  6. 19:04 Reading the Pali Canon - The talk turns to the Buddha's path as a transcendent one that fabricates less, with mindfulness as support rather than the final aim and the deathless as the end point.
  7. 25:54 Three Paths of Practice - He lays out three possibilities: bare mindfulness, stronger practices that reduce fabrication further, and practices that openly honor image, fantasy, and mythos.
  8. 29:59 Mythos, Love, and Meaning - Burbea concludes by showing how emptiness can open imaginal practice, how mythos is already part of religious life, and how love and meaningfulness often depend on imaginative amplitude.
