Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Practice of Compassion

Dharma Seed 1:25:46 2007-02-01

Compassion here begins as the heart’s response to suffering, then keeps widening through empathy, wisdom, and action. The talk traces what helps it grow—metta, confidence, wise company, discernment, and a willingness to touch pain without collapsing into fear, anger, pity, or grief. It then turns toward practice questions, self-compassion, and the way emptiness can loosen the sense of a separate self so compassion becomes less burdensome and more natural.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 What compassion is - Burbea defines compassion as the heart's response to suffering, distinct from metta and shaped by empathy and a wish to heal.
  2. 02:19 Compassion's qualities - He describes compassion as active, kind, accepting, humorous, joyful, and more than a feeling alone.
  3. 07:21 Why the heart resists - The talk turns to our ambivalence about compassion, the fear that it is a burden, and the deeper yearning that makes it fulfilling.
  4. 12:27 How it develops - He surveys several ways compassion can be cultivated, from phrases and tonglen to insight and association with the compassionate.
  5. 18:31 What nurtures compassion - Burbea emphasizes touching suffering directly, understanding cause and condition, and seeing how compassionate action and well-being support one another.
  6. 31:04 Near enemies - He works through the near enemies of compassion—fear, anger, pity, and grief—and shows how discernment and balance keep compassion from hardening.
  7. 38:58 Balance and spaciousness - The talk moves into the balance between empathy and healing energy, then into a more spacious form of compassion that can hold suffering without burden.
  8. 47:08 Self, emptiness, and oneness - He explores compassion for oneself, then brings in emptiness and anatta as ways of loosening the sense of a solid self and opening to shared humanity.
  9. 58:32 Interpenetration and practice questions - After poetic and philosophical reflections on non-separateness, the talk ends with questions about practice, action, equanimity, and compassion in the world.
