Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Maps for the Journey (A Brief Overview)

Dharma Seed 1:12:38 2010-02-05

How does practice move from settling the mind, to looking at experience, to seeing how self, object, and even time are built? The talk begins as a review of earlier retreat themes—samatha, metta, the three characteristics, and open awareness—then widens into a map of emptiness, with detours into phenomenology, the Pali Canon, and later Buddhist philosophy. Later, the emphasis becomes very practical: how clinging changes pain, how perception fades when self-sense loosens, how the visual field can become less solid, and why ethical discernment still matters when emptiness deepens.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Framing the Review - Rob explains that this talk is an overview of the retreat’s thread rather than new material, and he names the value of keeping each teacher’s line of teaching coherent.
  2. 02:25 Samatha and Looking - He reviews samatha, metta, and the three characteristics as ways of looking that reduce suffering in the moment and open the door to insight.
  3. 05:51 Open Awareness and Methods - He describes open awareness as a pleasant abiding and places it alongside seven-fold reasoning, emphasizing that different practices can support one another.
  4. 11:20 Finding What Works - Rob stresses clarity in practice, the importance of bringing questions into interview, and the need for each meditator to find the ways of working that actually fit.
  5. 15:13 Levels of Selfing - He turns to the spectrum of selfing, from obvious identity stories to subtler constructions that remain active even in calmer states.
  6. 20:32 Phenomenology and Emptiness - Rob links the Pali Canon’s practical approach to a phenomenological stance: bracket metaphysical questions and investigate how suffering and experience are fabricated.
  7. 26:29 Maps of Meditative Absorption - He outlines possible paths into deeper subtlety, including the eight jhanas, and describes them as increasingly less fabricated forms of perception.
  8. 34:06 Pain, Pīti, and Sound - In dialogue with questions, he contrasts samatha and insight approaches to pain, then uses sound and absorption to show how separation and substantiality can fade.
  9. 41:24 Grass, Field, and Projection - A walking meditation story about the grass becoming radiant leads into a teaching on mutual dependency between self-sense and object-perception.
