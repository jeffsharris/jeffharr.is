Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Sila and Soul (Part 4)

Dharma Seed 1:13:56 2019-06-12

What if values are not just human preferences, but real ideals that call to be lived? The talk moves from Hartmann and Plato to mathematics, using pi, irrational numbers, imaginary numbers, and the wave function to make a case for a realm of ideal being that shapes physical reality without fully appearing in it. From there it turns practical and intimate: an anesthetic near-death moment, the phrase “Metta, metta,” and the question of what we can actually trust, bow to, and give ourselves to when life falls short of the ideal. The closing movement brings in guilt, devotion, and eros, suggesting that our pain may point back to the values and images we cannot perfectly embody, yet still orient our lives by.

Chapters

  1. 00:59 Ontology of values - Burbea opens by laying out Hartmann’s unusual view that values are real and independent, existing in an ideal sphere rather than being made by culture or preference.
  2. 04:03 Participation and ideas - He connects this view to the Zohar and to Plato, using participation to show how subject and object, or value and action, can co-create one another.
  3. 10:03 The ideal calls forth action - Kierkegaard and Hartmann help him sharpen the moral point: values are not automatic forces, but when the ideal is unmet it can become the basis for ethical action.
  4. 20:29 Mathematics as analogy - He spends time with irrational numbers, pi, and imaginary numbers to show how something can be real, governing, and yet not physically manifest.
  5. 36:04 Values and transcendence - The talk returns from mathematics to the question of whether values, like images, may be both immanent and beyond full manifestation.
  6. 40:06 Metta near death - Burbea recalls a hospital anesthetic moment in which he turned toward metta and later wonders whether he was orienting himself toward an ideal that could serve as an anchor.
  7. 52:03 Dimensionality and soul - He argues that without some sense of depth or transcendence, values become thin, and he links this loss of dimensionality to a poverty in ethics and in soulmaking.
  8. 01:01:23 Faith beyond outcomes - A long Hartmann passage supports his claim that one can devote oneself to an ideal even when it will not be fully realized in one’s lifetime.
  9. 01:06:56 Guilt, devotion, and healing - He closes by showing how guilt can be re-read as a sign of love and devotion, and how the gap between the ideal and the actual can become a source of wisdom rather than despair.
