Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Mystery of Vedana: A Key to Liberation

Dharma Seed 69:19 2010-11-06

What if the small textures of pleasant, unpleasant, and neutral feeling are not minor details but the place where suffering first takes hold? The talk begins by widening the idea of freedom, then turns to vedana as a practical way of noticing how raw sensation becomes grasping, aversion, boredom, and papanca. From there it moves between simple instructions—stay with the bare feeling, loosen the clinging, let the body soften—and a deeper inquiry into how feeling, relationship, and even the sense of separate things may not be as fixed as they seem.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Why vedana matters - He introduces the second foundation of mindfulness and places vedana in the larger question of freedom and liberation.
  2. 05:28 Stationing attention - Burbea explains satipatthana as a place to stay, and suggests vedana can be a root-level investigation rather than just general mindfulness.
  3. 10:21 Three feeling tones - He defines vedana as pleasant, unpleasant, and neither, then shows how ordinary sensations, thoughts, and moods all carry feeling tone.
  4. 16:25 Reactivity and the second arrow - He turns to the Buddha’s teaching on understanding vedana, then describes how craving, aversion, boredom, and tension arise around feeling tone.
  5. 31:22 Stories that proliferate - A Thai monk’s magnet demonstration and the porridge anecdote show how unconscious reaction and papanca amplify a simple sensation into a larger crisis.
  6. 39:45 Staying with bare feeling - He describes the practice of remaining at vedana to stop the chain of reactivity, with the second-arrow simile and dependent origination in view.
  7. 47:55 Changing the relationship - Burbea explores how seeing impermanence, relaxing bodily tension, and deliberately allowing experience can loosen clinging and soften suffering.
  8. 55:17 From experience to emptiness - He follows the inquiry into the mutual dependence of vedana and clinging, then opens into the more mysterious possibility that experience is not as independently real as it appears.
  9. 01:06:13 Come and see - A brief comparison with quantum theory and the Buddha’s ehipassiko invites direct investigation, and the talk ends by encouraging further inquiry rather than settled belief.
