Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Ending the Inner Critic (1)

Dharma Seed 34:44 2010-12-04

What happens when the inner critic turns practice into pressure, shame, and a constant sense of not being enough? The talk begins by naming that voice clearly, then shows how it distorts meditation into “should,” blocks inquiry, and even creates an inner rebel in response. From there, the speaker lays out a path for loosening its grip: acceptance, questioning, reclaiming inner power, and especially metta, or loving-kindness, as a way to retrain the whole pattern over time.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 What the critic is - He defines the inner critic as a harsh, self-putting-down inner structure marked by shame, aversion, and contempt.
  2. 02:49 Not alone in this - He reassures listeners that this pattern is common, explains why people often feel isolated in it, and names the despair that can come from misunderstanding it.
  3. 05:49 Where it comes from - He traces the critic to family, education, and broader cultural forces, including individuality and consumer culture.
  4. 08:45 How practice gets pressured - He shows how the critic turns meditation into 'should,' draining joy and sometimes producing an inner rebel in response.
  5. 12:07 Judgment blocks inquiry - He explains how the critic constricts genuine questioning and turns even ordinary curiosity into self-judgment.
  6. 15:00 Acceptance before inquiry - Using the Four Noble Truths, he argues that acceptance of dukkha has to come before we can investigate its causes.
  7. 19:15 Challenging the critic - He contrasts modern Dharma language with the Buddha's style, then says the inner critic needs to be challenged carefully and kindly.
  8. 23:50 Metta as first practice - He introduces metta as the first of five approaches, describing it as a patient retraining of thought, belief, and habit.
  9. 30:19 Love from others - He broadens the practice to include giving loving-kindness outward and receiving the healing impact of others' faith and true seeing.
