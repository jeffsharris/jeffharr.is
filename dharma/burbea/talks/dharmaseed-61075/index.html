Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Image of Ethics (Part 3)

Dharma Seed 2:22:29 2020-02-16

What if ethics needs more than rules, more than happiness, and more than a flat picture of the world? The talk moves through older ways of grounding value — divine command, scripture, cosmic hierarchy, rational order — and keeps asking what each one makes possible and what each one leaves out. It then turns to Soulmaking Dharma as a way of restoring dimensionality: not by asserting dogma, but by asking how eros, participation, beauty, and a sensed order might actually shape ethical life.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Ethics and dimensionality - Burbea introduces the problem of grounding ethics in some other dimension and reviews how imaginal figures, angels, and daemons have historically provided that depth.
  2. 09:18 Voluntarism and scripture - He explains voluntarism as ethics grounded in divine command and scripture, then compares that with Buddhist appeals to scriptural authority.
  3. 14:26 Reformation and modern flattening - The talk traces how the Protestant Reformation, Ockham, and later secular modernity reshaped ethics around divine sovereignty, ordinary life, and the loss of intermediaries.
  4. 28:37 Augustine and ordered love - Burbea turns to Augustine's idea that virtue is the order of love and explores how a hierarchical cosmos can support ethical valuation.
  5. 39:56 Modern cosmos, participatory reality - He contrasts the modern flattened universe with older hierarchies and brings in John Wheeler's participatory universe to suggest a non-naive ontology.
  6. 55:42 Soulmaking and the world - The talk links sensing soul, eros, and trust in divinity with ethical orientation, then moves through Bacon and Taylor to show how the instrumental stance narrowed modern life.
  7. 01:26:59 Rationality and the Logos - Burbea distinguishes procedural from substantive rationality, using Plato, Philo, John, Genesis, and Origen to explore reason, Logos, and the soul's longing for truth.
  8. 01:51:08 Limits of rational ethics - He questions Enlightenment faith in rationality alone, then tests utilitarian ideas against hierarchy, suffering, and the limits of purely arithmetic moral thinking.
  9. 02:14:16 What lies behind reduction - The talk closes by asking whether the wish to reduce suffering may hide a deeper longing for beauty, holiness, and mystery, which Soulmaking Dharma tries to bring into view.
