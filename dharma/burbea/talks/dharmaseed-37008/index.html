Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Hearing All Sounds as Mantra: A Jewelline World

Dharma Seed 57:06 2016-07-30

What if the point of practice is not to hold one object, but to learn how attention can move: from body well-being, to emotion, to image, to the sensed world, and finally to sound itself? The talk begins by laying out those overlapping directions, then turns into a long guided experiment with Om Mani Padme Hum, first as chant, then as a way of opening the heart, the energy body, and the imagination. From there the listening widens further: sounds in the room, in the wind, in speech, even in pain and awkward noise are invited as mantra, so the practice becomes an exercise in re-sensing the world as a kind of living mandala. The talk ends by stressing that this is not a formula but an invitation to play, test, and find what actually opens perception.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Retreat directions - Rob briefly reviews five overlapping practice directions, including samadhi, emotion, imaginal practice, cosmopoiesis, and the energy body.
  2. 10:29 Mantra as experiment - He introduces Om Mani Padme Hum and explains that the point is not a fixed doctrine but a flexible, creative use of chant.
  3. 15:53 Settling into chant - The group is guided into posture, heart-centered awareness, and shared chanting led by Catherine McGee.
  4. 23:12 Spell and blessing - Rob deepens the chant as enchantment, divine speech, and a field of blessing woven into the cosmos.
  5. 28:16 Imaginal listening widens - The meditation turns toward cosmopoiesis, with the mantra held lightly and the sense of sacred sound extending beyond the spoken phrase.
  6. 36:21 Walking as mandala - Participants move around the room while keeping awareness of the energy body, the heart, and the room as a kind of mandala.
  7. 48:14 All sounds as mantra - Rob extends the practice to voices, birdsong, coughing, wind, and even difficult sounds, as a way of re-enchanting perception.
  8. 53:32 Sound, meaning, and play - He reflects on speech as miracle and closes by urging practitioners to experiment freely and find their own way.
