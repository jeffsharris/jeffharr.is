Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Phoenix of Metaphysics (Part 2)

Dharma Seed 2:15:32 2019-06-05

What if the urge to know reality, rather than collapse into certainty or shrug into skepticism, opens into a wider middle way? The talk moves from critiques of classical science and positivism into emptiness, imaginal practice, and the claim that concepts can be fruitful even when they are unproven or perhaps untrue. Along the way it brings in physics, metaphysics, and figures like Rorty, Yeats, Corbin, Smolin, and Goethe to ask whether inquiry can stay rigorous without becoming rigid. By the end, the emphasis has shifted from proving final truth to letting eros, beauty, and conceptual frameworks support soulmaking.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Framing the problem - Burbea recaps the talk's central questions about knowledge, reality, and the scientific worldview, then introduces the tensions that will structure the discussion.
  2. 08:42 Shrugging and default views - Using Richard Rorty as an example, he describes the common move of refusing ontology altogether and falling back into inherited assumptions.
  3. 10:50 Imaginal middle way - He turns to Yeats and Corbin to outline an imaginal way of seeing that keeps sacredness, poetic meaning, and multiple levels of interpretation alive.
  4. 17:31 Sacredness in emptiness - Burbea deepens the discussion of emptiness, subject-object participation, and the way sacredness can reappear through a subtler understanding of fabrication and perception.
  5. 29:57 Higher truths and physics - He moves into examples from philosophy and physics, using other dimensions, mystical absolutes, and quantum theory to show how models can try to integrate paradox.
  6. 47:41 Metaphysics in science - The talk traces the decline of positivism and the continued role of beauty, intuition, and speculative models in scientific discovery.
  7. 01:04:51 Fruitful falsehoods - Burbea argues that untrue or unproven ideas can still be generative, especially when they function as scaffolding for practice, imagination, and soulmaking.
  8. 01:22:20 Beauty as cause - Through pseudo-Dionysus and later physics analogies, he develops the idea that beauty, correspondence, and duality can organize experience without collapsing it into a single fixed truth.
  9. 01:53:53 Inquiry as soulmaking - The talk closes with Goethe, Cohen, and a final return to epistemology: the point is not to solve everything, but to keep inquiry alive in ways that nourish the soul.
