The Anatomy of Ignorance 2 - The Social Roots of Ignorance and climate Disruption

Dharma Seed 64:06 2013-10-02

Climate disruption is taken up first as a problem of ignorance: personal, social, and universal. The talk moves from a sobering account of the science and the scale of the carbon budget to a closer look at denial, emotional overwhelm, and the ways individualism can keep larger systems out of view. It then turns practical, asking what can be done at the levels of personal conduct, community, and collective action, with brief attention to divestment, institutional change, and the role of practice in staying open to fear and grief without being paralyzed by them.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Reviewing the Three Ignorances - Rothberg recalls the previous week’s framework of personal, social, and universal ignorance and explains why naming ignorance can be sobering and hopeful at once.
  2. 06:47 Social Conditioning - He develops the social dimension of ignorance through race, gender, individualism, and the ways culture keeps larger systems out of view.
  3. 14:35 Climate Disruption - The talk turns to the climate crisis, using the IPCC report and Bill McKibben’s carbon-budget framing to show the scale of the mismatch between science and current plans.
  4. 24:59 How We Don’t Take It In - Rothberg explores how people know the facts but do not fully receive them, describing climate denial as intellectual, emotional, and tokenistic.
  5. 28:50 Grief and Processing - Drawing on Joanna Macy, he describes the need for shared settings where fear, sadness, shock, and overwhelm can be felt and transformed into energy for action.
  6. 33:11 Personal and Local Action - He moves through practical responses at the individual and institutional level, including simplicity, transportation choices, local carbon footprints, and examples such as Berkeley’s climate planning.
  7. 40:09 Collective Mobilization - Rothberg argues that technical solutions already exist, but they require a committed minority, and he presents Joanna Macy’s three-part model of preventing damage, shifting institutions, and shifting consciousness.
  8. 47:59 Practice and Motivation - He emphasizes that climate action needs Dharma resources such as compassion, equanimity, and a different motivation rooted in care for future generations.
  9. 51:33 Questions and Final Counsel - In discussion, he addresses the realities of fossil-fuel dependence, the limits of individual virtue, and the difficulty of speaking about crisis in a Dharma setting, then closes by returning to the ethical contradiction of harming one’s own children and grandchildren.
