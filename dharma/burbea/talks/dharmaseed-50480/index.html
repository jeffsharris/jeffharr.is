Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Between Ikon and Eidos: Image & Hermeneutics in Meditation (Part 4 - The Three Characteristics)

Dharma Seed 63:53 2018-01-12

Rob Burbea explores the three characteristics—dukkha, impermanence, and not-self—not as fixed doctrines, but as meditative ways of looking that can shape perception. He moves from dependent origination and attention to vedanā into a broader argument that Dharma concepts are skillful lenses, not final truths. The talk then opens toward emptiness, soulmaking, and Vajrayana, where deconstruction can eventually give rise to a very different sense of appearance, beauty, and sacredness.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Ideas Become Ways of Looking - Burbea introduces the larger theme of ideas shaping sensing with soul, and explains why concepts need to become lived ways of looking rather than abstract theories.
  2. 14:15 Dependent Origination Revisited - He uses dependent origination as the main example of a Buddhist framework that can be translated into subtle practice, including emptiness and imaginal dimensions.
  3. 19:33 Working with Vedanā - The talk turns to stationing awareness on feeling tone, showing how close attention can attenuate craving while still remaining an incomplete picture of awakening.
  4. 25:11 Relative Truth and History - Burbea cites Ajahn Mahabua and the historical context of the Buddha’s teaching to emphasize that dharma categories are raft-like and conditioned, not ultimate realities.
  5. 29:56 Three Marks Introduced - He names the three characteristics and begins reframing them away from facts to be believed and toward lenses to be applied in meditation.
  6. 34:12 Lenses, Not Doctrines - Impermanence, dukkha, and not-self are treated as sustained ways of attending that reduce fabrication and reveal emptiness more deeply than conceptual filing systems.
  7. 42:18 Turning Toward Their Opposites - Through soulmaking and Vajrayana-style reversal, Burbea shows how the characteristics can open into seeing things as bliss, eternal, or self-like, not as fixed truths but as possibilities of view.
  8. 50:44 Perception as Practice - He widens the frame to perception itself, describing it as deconstructive work, healing, gift, grace, opportunity, and participation in the divine.
  9. 01:01:15 Deconstruction and Reconstruction - The talk closes by returning to the three characteristics as doors that can dissolve themselves when taken deeply enough, making room for sacred perception to emerge.
