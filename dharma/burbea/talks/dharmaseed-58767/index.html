Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Dharma(s) Discovered and Created (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 2:24:45 2019-06-06

What makes a tradition alive: shared texts, practices, values, and authority, or the tension between all of these and the freedom to differ? The talk moves through mindfulness, the Buddhist canon, and then wide-ranging comparisons with Judaism, Christianity, and philosophy to show how traditions are shaped by culture, interpretation, and even productive misunderstanding. It keeps returning to a harder question: when does difference enrich a living lineage, and when does it become a fracture or a counterfeit teaching?

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Opening the question - Rob frames the talk as an invitation to reflect on tradition with mind, heart, and soul rather than rush to conclusions.
  2. 06:26 Mindfulness and tradition - He uses Stephen Batchelor's Bodhi College example to contrast mindfulness as technique with a deeper relationship to history, teachings, and belonging.
  3. 26:52 What traditions share - The talk lays out the shared elements that may make a living tradition coherent: community, values, practices, stories, vocabulary, and beliefs.
  4. 35:13 Mystery and canonical depth - Rob turns to the Zohar, tantric texts, and John's Gospel to suggest that opacity, poetry, and unresolved depth can nourish eros and commentary.
  5. 47:49 Tradition as exchange - He broadens the frame to historical borrowing, showing how Judaism, Christianity, Islam, and modern Buddhism have all been shaped by outside influences.
  6. 01:02:01 Cultural conditioning - The talk examines how even core Buddhist schemes, such as the three fires, dependent origination, and the aggregates, may reflect the culture they emerged in.
  7. 01:12:26 Mindfulness revised - Rob returns to contemporary mindfulness and argues that popular presentations often truncate the Satipatthana Sutta and remove its active, discerning edge.
  8. 01:25:10 Origins and history - He questions the fantasy of origins, bringing in Nietzsche, Vattimo, and Howard Zinn to suggest that history is always shaped by perspective and selection.
  9. 01:41:44 Conflict as vitality - Drawing on MacIntyre and Whitehead, Rob argues that healthy traditions often contain internal argument, epistemological tension, and creative friction.
