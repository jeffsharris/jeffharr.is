Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Equanimity

Dharma Seed 54:28 2006-12-28

Equanimity is introduced as steadiness of mind and heart in the face of all conditions, then tested against the eight worldly conditions: praise and blame, success and failure, gain and loss, pleasure and pain. The talk moves carefully away from the idea that equanimity means indifference, showing instead that it can be open, alive, and spacious rather than cold or dull. From there it turns practical: returning to the breath, contemplating impermanence and death, cultivating metta, compassion, and spiritual joy, and examining how push and pull create suffering. It closes by pointing toward a quieter freedom in which things are seen as less measurable than they first appear.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 What Equanimity Means - Burbea introduces equanimity as a common Buddhist word that in practice means steadiness of mind and heart in relation to all conditions.
  2. 02:01 The Penguin Story - He tells a story about penguins ducking a helicopter to show that being unmoved is not the same as equanimity.
  3. 03:34 The Eight Worldly Conditions - The talk turns to praise and blame, success and failure, gain and loss, pleasure and pain as the ordinary tests of balance.
  4. 06:06 Open Heart, Not Indifference - Burbea distinguishes true equanimity from the near enemy of indifference and names the fear that equanimity will make life dull.
  5. 09:09 Cultivation and Practice - He argues that states of mind are not random or merely event-based, and that steadiness grows through small acts of practice and showing up.
  6. 15:49 Impermanence and Death - The talk develops impermanence as a major support for equanimity, moving from daily change to uncertainty, vast time, and mortality.
  7. 21:48 Happiness From Inner Qualities - Burbea explains that equanimity deepens when happiness is rooted in inner qualities such as metta and compassion rather than outer conditions.
  8. 31:09 Wonder and Spiritual Joy - He brings in mudita, wonder, nature, art, and the critique of self-obsession as ways to open receptivity and nourish joy.
  9. 39:55 Reactivity, Pain, and Spaciousness - The closing section examines how worldly conditions lose power when reactivity softens, how pain changes with aversion, and how equanimity becomes a spacious, almost non-grasping openness.
