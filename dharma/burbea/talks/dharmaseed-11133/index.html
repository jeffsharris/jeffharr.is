Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Freedom of No-thing-ness

Dharma Seed 53:22 2009-02-09

What if the solid thing you’re trying to work with in meditation is only being held together by the mind’s habit of naming, linking, and believing? The talk moves from a reminder to care for the heart on retreat into a detailed analysis of emptiness: how whole and parts depend on each other, how labels fix experience into fear or pain, and how the body, movement, and even time become harder to pin down than they first appear. Near the end, the reasoning is brought back to practice, with a suggestion to use these insights as portable ways of looking — while also knowing when to return to simple samadhi and metta.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Taking Care of Heart - Burbea begins by reminding listeners to protect their heart on retreat, keep up gratitude and love of Dharma, and not let the mind get lost in the difficult side of practice.
  2. 03:22 Seeing No Thing - He introduces the theme of no-thing-ness with a Prajnaparamita-style quote and an early canonical line, then turns toward emptiness as a way of seeing rather than a doctrine to hold.
  3. 05:12 Wholes and Parts - Using chairs, bodies, pain, and fear, he shows how the mind imputes wholes on parts and parts on wholes, making ordinary experience seem more solid than it is.
  4. 16:11 Applying the Analysis - He explains how the same reasoning can be used in retreat and daily life, and when needed, extended from objects to the sense of self that takes them personally.
  5. 19:59 Body Boundaries - He questions where the body begins and ends, using water, breath, food, shape, and the subtle body to show how bodily identity is conceptually drawn rather than fixed.
  6. 25:55 Aggregates and Labels - Drawing on Nagarjuna, he argues that the aggregates and self co-arise, then looks at how feeling, perception, labeling, and reaction interlock in fear and pain.
  7. 30:07 Dependence in Experience - The talk widens to consciousness, the aggregates, and dependent origination, stressing that a strong intellectual grasp matters, but direct seeing of fading matters more.
  8. 38:05 Walking and Motion - He turns to Nagarjuna’s analysis of walking to show how motion cannot be pinned down in the path, the moment, or the present, and how this reveals conceptual construction.
  9. 44:58 Portable Practice - Burbea closes by framing Nagarjuna-style reasonings as portable tools for meditation, while warning against overthinking and recommending a return to simplicity, samadhi, and metta when needed.
