Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Instructions: Opening to the Current of Desire

Dharma Seed 30:12 2017-03-29

Rob Burbea introduces a practice thread he calls “opening to the current of desire,” distinguishing it from both renunciation and imaginal practice with eros. He lays out three steps: notice dukkha or desire, ask what is really wanted at a deeper level, and then open the body to the energetic current of desire itself. The talk then moves through several examples, showing how this can reveal freedom, connection, aliveness, or a broader sense of being without requiring any external change.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Practice threads - Rob sets the retreat context by naming the main threads of practice and where desire fits among them.
  2. 02:12 Introducing desire practice - He distinguishes imaginal eros from the new practice of opening to the current of desire and emphasizes the role of trust.
  3. 04:50 Three-step method - Rob gives the basic sequence: notice dukkha or desire, ask what is really wanted, and trust that desire may hold something worthwhile.
  4. 09:22 Opening the body - He explains how to open bodily and energetically to desire rather than merely observing it mindfully.
  5. 12:28 Freedom and joining - Two examples show desire opening into felt freedom from constraint and then into a sense of joining that does not depend on the object.
  6. 16:23 Craving and hype - A new-romance example leads into a more standard Dharma analysis of craving, papancha, and the mind’s exaggeration of here and there.
  7. 20:18 Deeper wants - Rob works through a case where the surface object gives way to wider wants such as being heard, heart-opening, connection, and love.
  8. 23:58 Lack and fabrication - He warns that desire is often entangled with lack, grief, and frustration, and that attention can help fabricate the whole pattern.
  9. 27:27 Trusting the current - He closes by restating the practice and noting that opening to desire may lead into imaginal manifestation or into a fuller internal fulfillment, with action sometimes relevant and sometimes not.
