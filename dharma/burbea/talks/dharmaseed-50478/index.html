Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Between Ikon and Eidos: Image & Hermeneutics in Meditation (Part 2)

Dharma Seed 1:38:14 2018-01-10

Rob Burbea continues a wide-ranging inquiry into how meditation, meaning, and worldview are shaped by the ideas we inherit and the ways we look. Drawing on Husserl, Heidegger, Rorty, Quine, Vico, and Midrash, he questions the assumption of a single objective reality and argues that mindfulness itself always carries metaphysical commitments. The talk moves toward a quieter but sharper claim: interpretation is not detached analysis, but something lived, participatory, and bound up with what truly matters to us.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Why ideas matter - Burbea opens by revisiting how inherited cultural assumptions can quietly constrain imaginal and meditative practice.
  2. 05:24 Scientific inheritance - He reviews the legacy of scientific worldview and its tendency toward abstraction, reduction, and doubt about soul-making practice.
  3. 10:07 Husserl and crisis - Using Husserl, he argues that positivistic science leaves out value, freedom, and the questions that matter most in human upheaval.
  4. 13:10 The life-world - He develops Husserl’s idea of the life-world as the taken-for-granted context of presuppositions, and links it to phenomenology and Dharma.
  5. 17:49 Apperances and reduction - The talk shows how close attention can deconstruct experience and also slide into reductionism if it treats appearances as final facts.
  6. 35:37 Ways of looking - He argues that experience varies with historical context, mood, and especially the current way of looking, so mindfulness is never neutral.
  7. 59:13 No raw data - With Sellars, Quine, and Rorty, he challenges givenness and the idea of raw data, then turns to truth as pragmatically and existentially conditioned.
  8. 01:20:39 Plurality and participation - Vico and Isaiah Berlin are used to support a plurality of cultures and questions, and to show that interpretation belongs to a living historical process.
  9. 01:30:18 Interpretation must matter - He closes by emphasizing that hermeneutics, like Dharma practice, only works when it matters deeply and carries ethical and social consequences.
