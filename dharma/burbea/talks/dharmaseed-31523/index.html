Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

A Sacred Universe: Insight, Theophany, Cosmopoesis (Part 4)

Dharma Seed 1:24:17 2015-08-13

What if the same person, sound, or world can be seen on more than one level at once? The talk builds a careful case for a "vertical spectrum of the imaginal," showing how perception, eros, and conceptual frameworks can open an image into theophany and even a larger sense of world-making, rather than collapsing it into a flat, self-referential reading. Along the way, the speaker pauses to distinguish this from the Buddhist spectrum of fabrication, warns against turning any view into a rigid creed, and returns again and again to the need for conceptual structures that stay loose enough to keep experience open.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Concepts as openings - Burbea frames the talk as an open-ended offering of conceptual structures that can support practice without closing experience down.
  2. 04:01 Circular understanding - He brings in Paul Tillich and argues that spiritual understanding is necessarily circular, partial, and unable to contain the Divine.
  3. 08:27 Structures that break - Using Lurianic Kabbalah, he describes how frameworks are needed, then outgrown, broken, and re-formed as practice and life expand.
  4. 11:27 Perception is central - He rejects the idea of non-conceptual experience and shows how conceiving and perceiving are always woven together, including in imaginal practice.
  5. 20:06 Vertical imaginal spectrum - He introduces the tentative idea of a vertical spectrum of the imaginal, illustrating how a person can be seen at several levels at once, including as a theophany.
  6. 31:18 From image to world - The theophanic sense spreads outward into cosmopoiesis, where self, other, and world are co-created in a different felt cosmos.
  7. 40:20 Eros stretches the logos - He explains how eros wants more contact and more depth, which enlarges the image, the conceptual frame, and the sense of soulmaking.
  8. 51:15 Dynamism and emptiness - Burbea contrasts the vertical imaginal with the Buddhist spectrum of fabrication, then returns to Kabbalistic images of creation, shattering, and mutual making.
  9. 01:05:51 Meaningfulness and lightness - He closes by connecting these views to meaningfulness, calling for a light touch, and warning against reducing the divine to something tidy or easily grasped.
