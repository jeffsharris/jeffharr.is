The World and More: Immanence, Tantra, and Transcendence (Part 2)

Dharma Seed 1:15:22 2017-02-12

What if eros is not a distraction from awakening but part of its movement, reshaping how a tradition understands the path, the body, and the world? The talk moves through Buddhism, Tantra, Christianity, Kabbalah, and a few intimate examples to test three possibilities at once: that erotic language is symbolic, that it is a vehicle, and that it may also be a real re-sanctification of sexuality itself. Again and again, the speaker returns to the same pressure point: whether practice should strip the world down to literal meaning, or open it into a layered, imaginal, and cosmically participatory field.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Eros at the Center - He opens by naming eros as central to tantric symbolism, re-enchantment, and the relationship with sense experience and awakening.
  2. 06:19 Origins or Telos - Using Nietzsche and historical examples, he contrasts the myth of origins with a teleological way of reading religious development.
  3. 09:48 Tradition as Growth - Michelangelo and Buddhist history are used to suggest that traditions may be moving toward something fuller rather than decaying from an original purity.
  4. 14:28 Sophia and Imaginal Prayer - Gichtel and Thomas Merton illustrate a Christian line in which feminine divine presence permeates the world and devotion becomes imaginal participation.
  5. 20:37 Ambiguous Sacred Sex - Gnostic and tantric materials are read as deliberately multivalent, raising the question of whether sexual imagery is symbolic, literal, or a doorway into practice.
  6. 27:56 Three Tantric Readings - He lays out symbolic, instrumental, and re-sanctifying readings of tantra, then argues that all three are active in a soulmaking approach.
  7. 36:41 Sun, Moon, and Cosmos - An imaginal episode with a woman, a heart-sun, and later a moon image becomes a way of describing erotic polarity as cosmic harmony rather than personal psychology.
  8. 44:06 Sex as Divine Mystery - With the Hermetic Asclepius and related material, he argues that sexual union can reveal and participate in divine and cosmic dimensions when seen reverently.
  9. 55:50 Ritual, World, and Thirst - Kabbalah and the Zohar are used to show ritual as world-making, and the talk ends by holding together the sacred world and the pull beyond it into the transcendent.
