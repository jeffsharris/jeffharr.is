Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Ways of Looking: (2) Dukkha

Dharma Seed 39:28 2011-02-15

What if dukkha is less a statement about life and more a way of seeing how experience fails to fully satisfy? The talk moves from lightly labelling things as “unsatisfactory” into more practical instructions: noticing aversion and clinging in the body, using impermanence or dissolution to loosen the grip, and then getting close enough to allow or welcome what is there. It keeps returning to a quiet claim: most of the suffering comes not from the thing itself, but from the relationship with it, and that relationship can soften into relief, stillness, and a more subtle sensitivity.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Reviewing ways of looking - He briefly reviews the idea of training in different ways of looking and sets up the second characteristic.
  2. 01:44 Dukkha as unsatisfactory - Dukkha is reframed as unsatisfactoriness: anything conditioned cannot fully fulfill, so the practice becomes lightly recognizing that and letting go.
  3. 04:22 Meister Eckhart's parallel - He brings in Meister Eckhart's 'let go of creatures' as a contemplative parallel to releasing attachment to the created and conditioned.
  4. 06:48 Not limiting life - He clarifies that this is not life-denying or cold, but a mode or gear in practice that loosens fixation without rejecting experience.
  5. 12:55 Feeling push and pull - The talk turns to vedana and how to notice clinging or aversion through bodily contraction, then relax it repeatedly.
  6. 17:47 Using impermanence - He offers several ways to work with dukkha: remembering impermanence, seeing phenomena dissolve, or silently reflecting 'unsatisfactory.'
  7. 19:49 Allowing and welcoming - A more intimate instruction follows: get close to the experience, then allow, open to, or welcome it, including its feeling-tone.
  8. 25:17 Release and subtle dukkha - He emphasizes that suffering comes from the relationship to experience, then shows how letting go can reveal subtler layers of tension.
  9. 29:20 Direct or open practice - He contrasts working directly with an object and opening the field more broadly, saying both reveal different aspects and can be switched when needed.
