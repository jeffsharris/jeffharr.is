Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Wisdom and Desire

Dharma Seed 52:39 2018-06-27

Rob Burbea explores desire as a central force in shaping self, life, and world, and asks whether Dharma language has made that force too simple. He contrasts craving with eros, then develops a more nuanced picture in which imaginal perception, subtle letting go, and embodied practice can open desire into beauty, mystery, and sacredness. The talk moves from critique of reductionist views to a practical map of how desire can be worked with skillfully, without pretending it can be mastered completely.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Desire Shapes Everything - He opens by arguing that our relationship to desire shapes our being, our life, and the worlds we inhabit.
  2. 02:10 Beyond Simple Renunciation - He questions the idea that the path is simply about getting rid of desire and asks what freedom we actually have with it.
  3. 03:58 A Wider Psychology - He critiques a narrow Buddhist framing of desire, then turns to the attraction of emptiness as beauty, mystery, and sacredness rather than only suffering reduction.
  4. 12:03 Practice and Soulmaking - He points to the imaginal dimension already present in practice, names earlier teachings on desire, and introduces a meditative way of working with desire's energy.
  5. 19:29 Craving Into Eros - He draws the core distinction between craving and eros and describes how skill, mistake, and embodied attention can open one into the other.
  6. 27:01 From Flatness to Imaginal Depth - Using a narrow definition of eros as a starting point, he shows how desire becomes trapped in horizontal wanting unless imaginal perception and richer ideas are allowed.
  7. 35:41 Dependent Arising of Desire - He explains how desire, body, image, and view condition each other, and offers practical ways to widen energy and intention so craving can open.
  8. 40:39 Sacredness Beyond Objects - He reframes what is really wanted beneath object-desire, pointing toward soulmaking, grace, humility, and an ever-widening sense of sacredness.
  9. 51:42 Closing Distinction - He closes by returning to the need for discernment, skills, and differentiated language if certain gates of practice are to open.
