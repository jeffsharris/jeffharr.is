Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Non-Duality and the Fading of Perception

Dharma Seed 1:43:36 2010-02-04

What if the world of experience is built by the way attention clings? The talk moves from a Buddha story about “the end of the world” into a close, practical examination of dualities, dependent origination, and how grasping helps make perceptions feel solid. It then follows that thread into meditation itself: pain, fear, boredom, and vividness are all treated as places where less clinging can let experience soften, fade, or even disappear. Along the way, the talk brings in Nagarjuna, Faith in Mind, the Buddha’s instruction to regard things as empty, and a few concrete examples from daily life and practice.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening intention - Rob sets out the aim of the talk: a practical account of emptiness that can be used in meditation.
  2. 01:19 The end of the world - He introduces the Buddha’s riddle about seeing the end of the world as a way into liberation and emptiness.
  3. 03:20 Dependent origination broadened - He expands dependent arising beyond the twelve links into mutual dependence, relativity, and the emptiness of opposites.
  4. 08:00 Dualities in experience - A long section on how the mind constructs pairs like silence and noise, pleasure and pain, and how push-pull reinforces them.
  5. 17:38 Practice without preferences - He turns to Prajñāpāramitā and Faith in Mind, using them to frame non-picking-and-choosing as a meditative practice.
  6. 21:33 Clinging and solidity - Through examples of restlessness, loneliness, and security, he shows how clinging builds self-sense and object solidity.
  7. 28:35 From non-clinging to fading - He explains that letting go can make pain, fear, and other experiences fade, and that this is the deeper insight beyond impermanence.
  8. 41:37 Web of causes - He clarifies dependent origination as a web rather than a line, and uses the talk itself, nidanas, and a Meister Eckhart quote to stress mutual dependence.
  9. 50:34 Spectrum of fading - Using poker chips, stacked levels of delusion, and Hui Neng, he describes perception as a spectrum that can thin out into emptiness.
