Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Logoi and Leanings

Dharma Seed 1:45:00 2019-06-02

How do you steer when practice opens a fork: move toward the calm of samadhi, or stay with the imaginal depth and its soulmaking pull? The talk moves through navigation, pacing, and the subtle cues of the energy body, then widens into the role of logoi, conceptual frameworks, and how they can either limit or release what practice can become. Near the end, the speaker turns to symbolic time, causality, and a reading of experience that is less flat and more open to multiple dimensions.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the navigation - Rob introduces the talk as a set of pointers about steering in imaginal practice and between practices.
  2. 02:51 Subtle shifts in practice - He describes the art of moving between practices, comparing it to leaning a hawk, bike, or boat rather than making abrupt moves.
  3. 09:09 The samadhi fork - He contrasts wide awareness with microscopic attention, then develops the major fork between following pleasant feelings into samadhi or staying with the image and its wider soulmaking possibilities.
  4. 18:53 Pacing and balance - He explains that eros and practice have rhythms, sometimes needing slowing, stability, or counterbalance so experience can be digested rather than rushed.
  5. 26:16 Logos and causality - He widens the discussion to logos, showing how different causal stories and perceptions of time shape what is possible in practice.
  6. 37:37 Energy body and ideas - He returns to the fountain image and argues that both bodily resonance and the underlying ideas of practice are necessary for soulmaking to open fully.
  7. 48:10 Present and future causes - He contrasts past-cause explanations with present-moment dependent origination and a teleological sense of being called by the future or the angel out ahead.
  8. 59:20 Water, time, and scripture - Using Corbin and the Moses story, he reads water as time and argues against flattening sacred stories into only literal history.
  9. 01:10:47 Concepts as support - He insists that imaginal and emptiness experiences need a supporting conceptual framework, and illustrates how a missing or limiting framework can make practice stagnate.
