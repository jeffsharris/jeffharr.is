The Seven-Fold Reasoning (The self cannot be found anywhere)

Dharma Seed 1:46:53 2010-01-29

Rob Burbea introduces the sevenfold reasoning as a practical Madhyamaka method for investigating the self, starting from the felt sense of an inherently existing “I” and moving through seven ways it cannot be found. He emphasizes that this is not philosophy alone: the reasoning is meant to be taken into meditation until it gives way to a quiet, meaningful emptiness rather than a blank or abstract conclusion. Along the way he contrasts this approach with other ways of working with anatta, touches Chandrakirti, the Pali Canon, Nagarjuna, and related Indian and Tibetan self-theories, and keeps returning to the question of what actually holds up in direct experience.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Why This Matters - He opens by saying the issue is not whether one can repeat that the self is an illusion, but how to see it deeply enough to transform experience.
  2. 02:02 From Anatta to Inquiry - He situates the talk among earlier retreat practices and introduces a different approach: instead of only looking at phenomena, look directly for the self.
  3. 04:58 Chandrakirti and the Chariot - He places the method in Chandrakirti’s sevenfold reasoning, with roots he says reach back into the Pali Canon, and frames the chariot as the model for the self and the aggregates.
  4. 09:38 Preparing the Mind - He explains why the sense of an inherently existing self must first be made clear before analysis, and why conviction in the method matters.
  5. 20:20 Three Steps of Practice - He lays out the structure of the meditation: identify what is being negated, establish certainty that only seven possibilities apply, then examine them one by one until the self is not found.
  6. 29:43 The First Two Reasonings - He summarizes the seven possibilities and begins working through them, showing how the self cannot be found as one or many, as the parts, or as something standing apart.
  7. 42:13 Body, Feeling, and Possession - He continues through the aggregates with examples from body, feeling, and possession, using familiar images like illness, shaving, and owning a notebook to loosen the intuition of self.
  8. 01:06:44 Continuity and Consciousness - He turns to the self as a temporal continuum, then to consciousness itself, arguing that neither a stream of moments nor a knower apart from what is known can be the self.
  9. 01:28:05 Emptiness, Not Blankness - He closes by stressing that the aim is a felt understanding of unfindability, not nihilism, and answers questions about safety, tradition, and how the practice fits with samadhi and later retreat themes.
