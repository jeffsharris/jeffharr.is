Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Daimon, Refracted

Dharma Seed 2:08:07 2019-05-30

What if deep emptiness does not flatten the self, but opens a wider range of selves that can be played with, inhabited, and seen as empty? The talk moves from Nagarjuna and the Buddha’s middle way into a long exploration of imaginal practice: a fountain of soul flowing into self, other, and world; a wounded blackbird that becomes an image; a queen in a blood-trailing robe; and a naked goddess in a tree. From there it turns to the inner critic, desire, imprinting, Corbin’s angel out ahead, the sefirot, and a short guided meditation on metta and the voice as a way of letting the self become more dimensional and soul-suffused.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Emptiness and self-views - Burbea begins by showing how a radical understanding of emptiness changes the way selfhood can be understood.
  2. 05:45 Middle way, not nihilism - He brings in Nagarjuna and the Buddha’s middle way to clarify that emptiness does not mean blankness or the disappearance of all self-conceptions.
  3. 10:45 Soul’s balance and spread - Using the fountain analogy, he explains how soulmaking needs a balance across self, other, and world rather than an over-focused enchantment in one direction.
  4. 19:54 Desire as entry point - He turns to desire and eros, suggesting that what looks like dukkha or self-contraction can become a doorway into imaginal practice.
  5. 25:44 The wounded blackbird - A retreat story shows how care, compassion, and loss can settle into an image that becomes more vivid, less proliferating, and more healing.
  6. 31:04 Images as shared qualities - He develops several examples, including the blood-trailing queen and the naked goddess, to show how images can reveal qualities, mirror the self, and preserve a light but real two-ness.
  7. 47:07 Imprint, genius, and soul - A concern about wanting to make an imprint leads into a broader argument that soul wants to touch and be touched, and that creativity may be more than ego-display.
  8. 01:00:11 Corbin, Souriau, and angels - Burbea uses Oscar Wilde, Stravinsky, Corbin, Souriau, and Hillman to widen the framework into imaginal persons, the angel of the work, and a more polytheistic psyche.
  9. 01:13:08 The inner critic - He gives several ways to work with the inner critic, including feeling its charge, imagining its destruction, and finding the eros underneath its tightening.
