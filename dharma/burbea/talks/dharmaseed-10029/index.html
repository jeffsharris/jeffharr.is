Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

'There are hundreds of ways to kneel and kiss the ground' (A Talk on Devotion)

Dharma Seed 62:55 2009-12-31

What if devotion is not a foreign add-on to meditation, but something already shaping how we live, choose, and bow? The talk moves from the many forms devotion can take — praise, awe, surrender, aspiration, ritual, prayer, even attention itself — into a broader question of what we are actually aligning with. Along the way, it brings in Christmas imagery, Mary Oliver, Rumi, and stories about Copenhagen and Mother Teresa to show how devotion can become service, witness, and a quiet opening of the heart. It ends by asking whether practice is helping us love more widely, let go more deeply, and recognize the many ways to “kneel and kiss the ground.”

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the Heart - Burbea begins by naming devotion as one of the heart’s most beautiful and important capacities, and by noting that the Insight Meditation tradition often undernames it.
  2. 03:07 Many Forms of Devotion - He broadens devotion into sacredness, awe, praise, aspiration, yearning, and bodily expression, showing that it can have clear objects or remain unnameable and inward.
  3. 12:38 Ritual and Culture - The talk distinguishes ritual from devotion and then questions Western assumptions about stripping Buddhism of cultural baggage, scientific materialism, and Romantic individualism.
  4. 18:19 Attention as Prayer - Burbea turns to Christmas imagery and Mary Oliver’s 'The Summer Day' to show devotion as reverence for human potential and for the simple act of paying attention.
  5. 23:15 Pilgrimage in Action - Using the Copenhagen climate march, he describes devotion as prayerful action, witness, and service that matter regardless of outcome.
  6. 31:44 Objects and Embodiment - He examines devotional objects such as Avalokiteshvara, Kuan Yin, and Jesus, and tells a story about Mother Teresa to illustrate devotion as demanding love and surrender.
  7. 41:13 Longing as Message - Rumi’s 'Love Dogs' reframes longing as a response from the divine or Buddha nature, and Burbea encourages nurturing the devotional flame rather than chasing intensity.
  8. 45:50 Feeding the Flame - He draws on the Buddha’s language of feeding and starving wholesome qualities, then asks what conditions nourish devotion and how it should translate into daily life.
  9. 53:00 Letting Go of Forms - The talk closes by connecting devotion with renunciation and apophatic practice through Mother Teresa, the via negativa, St. John of the Cross, Meister Eckhart, and Simone Weil.
