Dukkha and Soulmaking (Part 3)

Dharma Seed 1:44:12 2017-12-30

What happens when an image is beautiful, meaningful, and still carries dukkha? The talk continues its close look at realism in imaginal practice, showing how images can be over-literalized into self-judgment, prediction, or duty, and how that rigidity can produce swings between grandiosity and self-abasement, even religious or secular dogmatism. From there, the speaker turns to personal examples of creative loneliness, changing archetypal figures over time, and the way imaginal work can open an image without trying to fix it by force. The later movement widens into harder questions: what healing and liberation mean, how many callings can coexist, and whether eros points to reality, creates it, or participates in it.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Realism and dukkha - He begins by naming realism as the main way imaginal practice gets mishandled, especially when images are taken too literally or made into fixed claims about the self or the future.
  2. 07:20 Polarized swings - He shows how believed-in realism often produces pendulum swings between grandiosity and self-abasement, including clinical language around mania and depression.
  3. 13:28 Creative loneliness - He turns to a personal imaginal history shaped by solitude, marginality, and struggle, using it to show how an image can be both deeply meaningful and painful.
  4. 27:17 Images evolve - He traces how solitary warrior and wanderer images gradually widened into figures of rest, healing, and nourishment, suggesting that imaginal transformation often happens organically.
  5. 33:02 Images ask for images - He argues that conventional frameworks did not change these images; rather, repeated imaginal engagement opened them, and he stresses the importance of not trying to improve them too quickly.
  6. 38:10 Healing and liberation - He widens the discussion with Jung, Hillman, and soulmaking language, asking what healing or liberation would even mean for an image, a self, or a soul.
  7. 45:17 Impossibility and duty - He develops Hillman’s challenge to simple liberation talk, then describes the dukkha built into imaginal life: the impossibility of fully manifesting every image, calling, or duty at once.
  8. 01:04:48 Full intention, less craving - He returns to eros and intention, explaining how dukkha drops when desire stays spacious, includes logos, and remains oriented to the whole of soulmaking rather than a single object.
  9. 01:31:47 Auden and ontology - He reads Auden on eros as a religious vision, then sketches a spectrum of ontological views and ends by proposing a participatory way of understanding sensing the soul.
