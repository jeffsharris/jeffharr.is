Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Samatha (Concentration Practice) - Introductory Talk

Dharma Seed 40:00 2007-03-30

What if calmness is not a side benefit of practice but a skill the heart can deliberately learn, deepen, and live from? The talk opens by placing samatha inside an ancient human current of retreat, then argues that calm, insight, and the heart practices are complementary paths rather than rivals. From there it turns practical: how intentions slip, why silence and simplicity matter, how breath practice can be approached, and why ethics and care for others are the ground that lets calm actually take root.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the Retreat - He welcomes people to Gaia House and situates retreat as a precious opportunity to turn inward.
  2. 02:18 Samatha in Human Life - He frames calmness as an ancient human capacity, not a narrowly Buddhist possession, and says it should be cultivated deliberately.
  3. 05:24 Why Calmness Matters - He describes samatha as nourishing, healing, and a strong basis for insight, then places it alongside the other main Buddhist paths.
  4. 08:13 Three Practice Paths - He recounts Ananda's three kinds of practitioners to show that samatha and insight can develop in different orders or together.
  5. 09:21 Inner Resource and Method - He answers concerns that retreat is selfish, then turns to the practical question of how calmness is developed through meditation and technique.
  6. 16:17 Breath, Control, and Letting Go - He reflects on breath practice, subtle control, and the limits of simply 'being with things as they are' as a complete path.
  7. 19:52 Intentions on Retreat - He emphasizes that retreat is not passive observation but a training in intention, especially noble intentions toward freedom, truth, love, peace, and happiness.
  8. 25:11 Gratitude, Silence, Simplicity - He points to the conditions that nourish practice at Gaia House, especially gratitude, silence, and a simple life of very few demands.
  9. 31:26 How to Support Calm - He asks retreatants to surrender to silence and the schedule, balance posture and movement, and keep ethics and the precepts as the foundation for deepening calm.
