Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Dukkha and Soulmaking (Part 2)

Dharma Seed 44:04 2017-12-29

What does it take for soulmaking practice to deepen instead of simply generating more movement, more intensity, or more dukkha? The talk keeps returning to pacing: when to open, when to stay with one image or desire, and how to balance imaginal work with steadier practices like samadhi, metta, mindfulness, and emptiness. It also addresses common worries about danger, fantasy, and losing touch with reality, including a discussion of Jungian sub-personalities, the Kabbalistic idea of the breaking of the vessels, and why questioning can be part of growth rather than a threat to it.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the path - He begins by naming the basic concern of making soulmaking practices sustainable rather than dukkha-producing.
  2. 03:40 Pacing and steadiness - Rob explains why the path needs both opening and staying with what has been revealed long enough for it to deepen.
  3. 07:10 Retreat balance - He turns to how retreat life can mix imaginal practice with other forms of practice instead of doing only one thing all day.
  4. 10:54 Two practice currents - He sketches the balance between practices that build equanimity and practices of eros, and how they can support one another.
  5. 14:27 Working with fear - Rob addresses ambivalence, concern about danger, and the importance of not forcing oneself into practices one cannot yet hold.
  6. 21:22 Imaginal figures and autonomy - He distinguishes his sense of the imaginal from a safer Jungian sub-personality frame and stresses the autonomy of imaginal figures.
  7. 26:38 Breaking and rebuilding - Using the Kabbalistic idea of breaking vessels, he describes how practice can fracture old structures and later require integration.
  8. 31:42 Questioning and sexual imaginal - He says inquiry can be risky but is often more dangerous not to do, then turns to sexual imaginal practice and its icon-like, non-escalating quality.
  9. 37:23 Darker images and realism - The talk closes by encouraging trust in darker images, expanding sacredness, and returning to the deeper question of realism and the imaginal middle way.
