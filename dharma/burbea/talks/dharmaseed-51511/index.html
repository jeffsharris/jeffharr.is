Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Opening Talk ('To Kiss a Falcon')

Dharma Seed 27:41 2018-06-22

What if the foundations of practice are not a fixed doctrine, but a willingness to question what we think we already know? The talk introduces Soulmaking Dharma as something to be condensed, distilled, and approached through skills, qualities, and understandings rather than through an exhaustive list of rules. It then moves into a wider reflection on art, beauty, and the need for discernment, using a birthday poem and a brief reference to John Coltrane to show how something can change our sense of value and belonging without stating a thesis outright.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Setting the Foundations - He introduces the retreat as a condensation of foundations rather than a new teaching and names the skills and attitudes the path asks for.
  2. 03:56 Questioning Assumptions - He stresses that this approach includes the courage to question inherited ideas, while also respecting pacing, digestion, and individual readiness.
  3. 06:39 Opening the Field - The talk turns to what soulmaking can open up: beauty, art, human possibility, and a wider sense of where the path may lead.
  4. 09:46 Art, Truth, and Reality - He unpacks his use of the word art and explains the path’s unusual relationship to truth and reality as something that opens experience rather than fixes doctrine.
  5. 12:35 The Birthday Poem - A poem about red poppies, falcons, belonging, and secret languages becomes a vivid example of relational, imaginal, and embodied teaching.
  6. 16:51 Culture and Crisis - He reflects on Western inheritance as both brilliant and brutal, linking the talk to ecological and anthropological crises.
  7. 19:50 Discernment and Precision - He argues that soulmaking requires discriminations, conceptual care, and a balance between precision and elasticity.
  8. 25:18 Playing the Practice - He closes by encouraging play, experiment, and first-hand experience, with practice and understanding enriching each other over time.
