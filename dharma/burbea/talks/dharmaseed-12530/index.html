Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Ways of Looking: (3) Anatta

Dharma Seed 51:19 2011-02-17

What if the point is not to prove whether there is a self, but to learn how identification is built and how to loosen it? The talk moves from the Buddha’s refusal to answer metaphysical questions directly into a practical exploration of anatta: noticing how body, feeling, thought, intention, and even awareness are quietly taken as me or mine. It then turns to a very concrete method—starting with the body, using light labels, and widening the field of disidentification—while warning against turning the practice into aversion or a project of wiping the self out.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 The middle way - Burbea frames anatta through the Buddha’s refusal to collapse experience into ‘is’ or ‘isn’t,’ pointing instead to a practical middle way.
  2. 03:03 How self gets built - He turns to the self-sense as something constructed through habit, especially through aversion, clinging, and identification.
  3. 07:19 Practicing disidentification - The talk traces how mindfulness, metta, impermanence, and relaxing the relationship each make identification less solid.
  4. 12:26 Five aggregates map - Burbea introduces the five aggregates as a way to break experience into workable parts and practice non-identification.
  5. 16:02 Light labels and practice - He offers gentle labels like ‘not me’ or ‘not mine’ and explains that they are only tools for supporting a way of looking.
  6. 20:16 Body and ownership - Using Dogen and then a reflection on ownership, he explores how sustained looking at possessions and the body can loosen the sense of mine.
  7. 27:15 Expanding the range - He describes how practice gradually extends from body sensations to feelings, thoughts, intentions, perceptions, and awareness.
  8. 34:06 Common pitfalls - Burbea warns against trying to crush the self-sense and clarifies that the practice is about letting phenomena be, not getting rid of them.
  9. 40:36 Playful experimentation - He closes by emphasizing that insight comes through light, patient experimentation, with gentleness, metta, and practical adjustment over time.
