Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Today’s Climate Reality

Dharma Seed 27:36 2014-04-20

What happens when a climate talk begins by treating the facts as something to look at directly, even if they are hard to bear? The talk moves from the immediate evidence of warming, ice loss, drought, fires, and acidifying oceans into the human systems tied to them: violence, food insecurity, fossil-fuel economics, and the barriers that keep people from acting. It then shifts toward a practical response, arguing for emissions cuts, carbon pricing, keeping most fossil fuels in the ground, and broader changes in poverty, education, and restoration.

Chapters

  1. 00:10 Look directly - The talk begins by placing climate reality in the frame of the First Noble Truth: face suffering clearly before trying to transform it.
  2. 00:49 What is happening now - She reviews present-day climate evidence from the AAAS report, including warming, ice loss, sea level rise, acidification, flooding, drought, storms, and fire.
  3. 04:13 Warming and wildfires - The talk continues with the causes of the crisis, the human role in rising CO2, and the growing scale of wildfires and megafires.
  4. 06:39 Impacts on life and conflict - Using the IPCC and a reference to Years of Living Dangerously, she describes how climate disruption affects ecosystems, health, food, water, and the risk of violence.
  5. 09:17 Maps of impact - She returns to the IPCC’s global picture and explains how water, marine systems, terrestrial systems, and food production are already being affected across continents.
  6. 15:38 California drought ahead - Michael Werner’s comments localize the talk to California, with Sierra snowpack, reservoirs, and a hotter, drier future.
  7. 17:18 The carbon budget - Drawing on Bill McKibben’s climate math, she explains the remaining carbon budget and the scale of continued fossil-fuel exploration.
  8. 20:14 Why action is blocked - She names the economic interests, misinformation, and subsidies that keep fossil fuels in place, and argues for a carbon tax and an end to new extraction.
  9. 24:53 What to do together - The closing turns to Lester Brown’s broader response—cut emissions, stabilize population growth, reduce poverty, restore ecosystems, and keep working together.
