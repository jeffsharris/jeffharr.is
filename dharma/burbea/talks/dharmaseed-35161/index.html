Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Ending the Inner Critic (2)

Dharma Seed 46:27 2010-12-04

How do you meet the inner critic without feeding it more aversion? The talk moves through a set of practical approaches: softening with kindness, giving the critic space, feeling the bodily hurt it leaves behind, and then investigating what is actually being believed, joined together, and protected. From there it turns to dialogue, doubt, and finally reclaiming a sense of power through the body, the heart, and the thinking mind. A brief reference to mindfulness as “affectionate curiosity” helps frame the whole inquiry as a way of befriending what usually feels hostile.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Opening the approach list - He returns to the earlier list of methods for loosening the inner critic and frames them as different ways of working with the same painful pattern.
  2. 00:45 Mindfulness with kindness - He develops mindfulness as affectionate curiosity and befriending, emphasizing that we can let the critic be there instead of trying to get rid of it.
  3. 03:25 Space and bodily tenderness - He explains how spacious attention prevents the critic from taking over and makes room to feel the hurt in the body with warmth and care.
  4. 06:16 Investigating what is there - He shows how clearer mindfulness can reveal the critic’s component parts, such as rage, pain, and tone of voice, rather than one vague cloud.
  5. 10:33 What the critic believes - He turns to questioning: what is being believed, what is being protected, and what the critic may be giving us, including identity, familiarity, or a false sense of safety.
  6. 15:49 Teasing apart the dots - He uses the image of joining and separating dots to show how consciousness constructs the critic from many factors, and how precise attention can weaken that construction.
  7. 19:48 Dialogue and skeptical inquiry - He describes dialoguing with the critic as a way to make it specific, slow it down, ask whether it would really be satisfied, and use intelligence as an ally.
  8. 25:33 Protection, insight, and practice - He explores how the critic can be trying to protect us from shame or rejection, and how insight needs to be reused as a living practice rather than a one-time glimpse.
  9. 30:59 Reclaiming power - He moves into embodied power: naming the critic, invoking inner figures of strength, setting boundaries, opening the heart, and finding power through centeredness, intelligence, and vitality.
