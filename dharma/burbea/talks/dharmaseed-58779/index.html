Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Sila and Soul (Part 6)

Dharma Seed 67:56 2019-06-14

Rob Burbea explores whether moral values are subjective, culturally contingent, or in some sense objective, using Nikolai Hartmann as a guide and then testing those ideas against Dharma practice. He moves from ontology and conscience into free will, guilt, and the possibility of seeing self more flexibly, before opening into a subtler discussion of how virtues can require counterweights and syntheses. The talk closes by asking what it means to remain open to moral complexity without collapsing into flaccidity, relativism, or a rigid moralism.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Framing the inquiry - Burbea introduces the question of moral values and sketches the larger ontological issue he wants to revisit before moving into ethics proper.
  2. 03:34 Hartmann on values - He presents Hartmann’s view that values are objective and hierarchically ordered, reading a long passage about conscience, guilt, and the rank of values.
  3. 09:57 Culture and analogy - Burbea compares the objective-value view with cultural relativism, then turns to mathematics and abstract entities as an analogy for how moral values might exist.
  4. 15:39 Practicing with ideas - He suggests trying on the idea of moral values as ideal forms in meditation, then asks whether image and idea can both serve as gateways into ethical life and soulmaking.
  5. 21:41 Soul, self, and guilt - The talk broadens into cultural change, the world soul, and the non-separation of subject and object, then shifts to free will, guilt, responsibility, and using self-view skillfully when it helps.
  6. 30:19 Freedom and moral being - Burbea explores Hartmann’s argument that free will matters because it grounds personhood and moral responsibility, while also suggesting a more flexible practice relation to these views.
  7. 39:42 The good as higher value - He follows Hartmann into the idea of the good, where goodness is framed not as an abstract essence but as the capacity to recognize and choose the higher value in a situation.
  8. 48:55 Conflicting virtues - Using Mandela, Aristotle, and MacIntyre, Burbea shows how values can conflict and how virtues may need counterweights, rather than existing as single traits in isolation.
  9. 01:03:06 Beyond easy answers - He considers flaccid moderation, postmodern fragmentation, and a dim intuition of a beyond of value, then pauses with the unresolved complexity still alive.
