Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Way of Non-Clinging (Part 3)

Dharma Seed 66:37 2017-01-17

What if the problem is not clinging itself, but a too-small idea of what non-clinging is for? The talk starts by questioning the familiar ideal of simply “letting go,” then widens the frame to ask what that ideal means for art, love, activism, family, and the full range of human passion. From there it contrasts ordinary craving with subtler investigation, and finally sets two broad directions side by side: deepening insight into emptiness through clinging, and opening to eros as a movement that can deepen soulfulness, beauty, and sacredness.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Returning to non-clinging - He briefly recalls Part 2 and reopens the question of what non-clinging means when applied as a practice and a view.
  2. 02:37 What the ideal leaves out - He questions whether a life without clinging is even the right aim, especially when considering engagement, personhood, art, family, and love.
  3. 10:26 Concepts and Dharma fantasy - He contrasts a narrow Dharma framework with broader ways of living, and shows how fantasy and image shape our sense of awakening.
  4. 15:39 Why simple frames fail - He argues that reducing romantic love, sexuality, and other deep commitments to a small set of concepts cannot adequately hold lived experience.
  5. 22:48 Three directions of inquiry - He distinguishes ordinary sense desire, subtler clinging tied to fabrication, and a third movement he begins to call eros.
  6. 27:41 What practice is for - He asks what each kind of investigation can reveal, and whether ease, happiness, or stress reduction are really the whole point of practice.
  7. 37:09 Mindfulness and poetry - He introduces discrepancy-based processing, then tests it against poetry, beauty, music, and the lived force of wanting.
  8. 47:40 Eros and soulmaking - He defines eros as a movement that opens depth, beauty, meaning, and sacredness, and asks whether a framework serves soulmaking.
  9. 55:02 Two broad paths - He closes by saying some practitioners need more work with craving and letting go, while others are being called toward eros and a wider, richer practice.
