Opening, Tuning, and Relating to the Imaginal: Instructions (2)

Dharma Seed 1:15:40 2015-08-10

What does it mean to stay mindful with an image without collapsing into daydreaming or treating it as a symbol to decode? The talk lays out a practical way of attending, tuning, and opening to imaginal experience: holding an image steadily, noticing its body and energy resonances, and sensing when a figure wants to be seen, entered, or related to differently. Along the way, the speaker uses examples like a river scene with an angelic presence, birdsong heard as sacred text, a wounded bird, and encounters shaped by love and gratitude to show how an image can be known more by its living effect than by visual detail alone.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Mindfulness of Images - The talk begins by defining imaginal practice as mindfulness applied to images, not fantasy, and by setting up the basic orientation of attending, tuning, and opening.
  2. 05:20 How an Image Is Sensed - Burbea outlines the first three ways of working with an image: the sense modality through which it is known, whether it wants to fill out, and the character or flavor that can be felt in it.
  3. 15:05 Specificity in Experience - Using birdsong as sacred text, he shows that the important thing may be the precise depth, beauty, and world-quality of an image rather than any literal meaning or tradition.
  4. 21:46 Detail and Resonance - He contrasts focusing only on visual detail with focusing only on emotional and energetic resonance, and explains why imaginal practice holds both together.
  5. 27:14 Signals in the Energy Body - Burbea lists the kinds of energetic cues that suggest an image is 'right' for practice, including alignment, opening, softening, energizing, well-being, harmonization, and contoured body responses.
  6. 40:25 Posture and the Wounded Bird - A story about a wounded bird image shows how bodily posture can matter, and how a feared image may reveal unexpected love when met in the right way.
  7. 46:21 Views and Conceptual Frames - He turns to thoughts and views, warning against reducing images to psychology, labels, or advice, and emphasizing play, responsiveness, and the power of the conceptual frame.
  8. 56:18 What the Image Wants - The final movement asks what the image wants, with examples of trust, benevolence, compassion, and the possibility that an image invites a different perspective or a different kind of love.
  9. 01:12:58 Three Ways to Relate - He closes by sketching three stances toward an imaginal figure: behold it, enter into it, or feel oneself being seen by it.
