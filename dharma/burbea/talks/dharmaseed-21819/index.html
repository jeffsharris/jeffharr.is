Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Theatre of Selves (Part One)

Dharma Seed 56:18 2013-11-10

What if the self is not a single solid thing, but something constructed in different ways of looking? The talk begins with emptiness and not-self, then moves through seven practice entry points for loosening identification, before turning to a more surprising claim: imagination is not a side issue, but one way the Dharma can reveal and soften selfhood. From there it opens into examples from metta, samadhi, inner critic work, and imaginal dialogue, where figures and voices are treated as active perspectives rather than literal truths.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Why Imagination Matters - Burbea frames the retreat as a long inquiry into the place of imagination in Dharma and in human life.
  2. 05:04 Emptiness and Not-Self - He sets emptiness and anatta as the core direction of practice, while distinguishing that from a narrow idea of mindfulness.
  3. 06:39 Seven Ways Through Self - He sketches seven practice approaches for seeing through self, from personality definitions to not-me contemplation.
  4. 12:18 Limits of Simplifying - He questions the view that the aggregates are the whole story, and shows why simplification can fail in romance, sexuality, and eros.
  5. 18:04 The Imaginal Opening - He names the imaginal as another way of seeing emptiness, one that does not flatten experience and can open the Dharma more widely.
  6. 21:54 Images in Practice - He gives familiar examples from metta, recollection, tantric visualization, and samadhi to show how images can support compassion, inspiration, and bodily ease.
  7. 26:59 Working With Inner Critics - He turns to the inner critic as an imaginal formation that can sometimes be deconstructed and sometimes engaged in dialogue.
  8. 35:51 Myths, Parents, and Voices - Through examples of duty, perfectionism, and heroic drive, he shows how images can carry depth beyond simple psychological labels.
  9. 46:46 Plurality and Dialogue - He closes by showing how imaginal persons can loosen a singular self and lead toward dialogue, transvaluation, and new ways of seeing.
