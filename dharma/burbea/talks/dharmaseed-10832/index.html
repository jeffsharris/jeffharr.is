Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Maya and Nirvana (Beyond the Measure of Mind)

Dharma Seed 63:48 2008-11-27

What if suffering begins not with what happens, but with the mind’s habit of measuring, dividing, and believing in what it has made? The talk moves from the Buddha’s magic-show simile for maya into dependent origination, showing how contact, feeling, perception, conceiving, and clinging feed one another until the world of self and things feels solid. From there it turns to practice: the three characteristics, no-preference, and other ways of seeing that quiet the measuring mind until the built-up world begins to fade and the unmade can be glimpsed.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Framing the inquiry - He introduces the talk as a difficult, non-linear exploration of something profound that will spiral around a central theme.
  2. 03:25 Maya and the magic show - Using the Buddha’s magic-show simile, he explains illusion, emptiness, and the sense that the whole of experience can be hollow of essence.
  3. 07:15 Papancha and fabrication - He describes how the mind proliferates experience, making many forms out of what is then taken as solid reality.
  4. 12:16 Signs and measurement - Drawing on Sāriputta and related terms, he connects greed, aversion, and delusion with measuring, sign-making, and the felt sense of self and world.
  5. 23:42 Dependent origination - He traces how contact, name-and-form, feeling, measuring, and craving mutually reinforce one another, and how Mahākaccāyana’s language points to this constructed tangle.
  6. 28:59 Three characteristics - Impermanence, unsatisfactoriness, and not-self are presented as practical lenses that quiet preference and begin to thin the solidity of experience.
  7. 33:12 No preferences, no duality - He brings in Faith in Mind and a story of Vepacitti to show how non-preference and the use of dualities as rafts can loosen selfing and conceptual fixation.
  8. 43:33 The subtler prison - The talk turns to the most refined self-view, the building of time and subject-object, and the radical claim that even mind and objects are dependently built.
  9. 56:55 Beyond measure - He distinguishes ordinary immeasurables from nibbana as beyond space and time, then closes with a poem on false discrimination, suchness, and freedom from measures.
