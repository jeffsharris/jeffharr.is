Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Experience Of Self (Personality and Beyond)

Dharma Seed 1:19:56 2010-01-22

The talk starts with a careful turn away from slogans like “the ego is the problem” toward a more practical question: what actually leads to suffering, and what leads to freedom? From there, it moves through the ordinary ways self is helpful in life, then into the ways we get locked into personality, story, comparison, and the inner critic. The speaker keeps returning to a gentler inquiry: can a particular self-definition be seen as not quite me, or not quite mine, and can loosening it bring a little more space? Near the end, the talk broadens into dependent arising, the role of actions rather than self-view, and the idea that emptiness does not erase functioning but can make it feel lighter and more open.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Framing selflessness - Rob sets up the retreat context and clarifies that the aim is to understand the self’s emptiness, not to destroy the self.
  2. 01:26 Self and suffering - He ties selflessness to the basic Dharma question of what leads to suffering and freedom, while showing why self-language still matters in ordinary life.
  3. 05:11 Healing the person - He turns to psychotherapy, responsibility, tenderness, and self-cherishing as useful ways the personality-level self can heal.
  4. 08:16 Story and identity - The talk examines how life stories are told, revised, and used to trap or empower us, including the example of the Buddha’s own narrative.
  5. 12:25 Personality-level suffering - Rob focuses on the social and psychological level where most suffering clusters: self-definition, comparison, projection, jealousy, and measurement.
  6. 17:11 Questioning definitions - He offers practical questions for loosening identity, including whether a trait is really me or mine and whether clinging to it actually causes suffering.
  7. 20:30 The spectrum of self - Mindfulness reveals gaps in self-image, the role of the inner critic, and the fact that selfing ranges from gross distress to subtle identity.
  8. 25:34 Meditation as selfing - He shows how spiritual practice can become self-improvement, how samadhi changes self-experience, and why the self-sense is far subtler than ego games alone.
  9. 33:45 Appreciation and familiarization - A remembered Cambridge talk becomes a springboard for appreciating lighter states of self and learning to inhabit them without force.
