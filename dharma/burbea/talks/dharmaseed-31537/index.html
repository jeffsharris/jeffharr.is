Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Love and Demands of the Imaginal (Part 2)

Dharma Seed 65:12 2015-08-11

What if practice is not for the self at all, but for the unfolding of an imaginal angel, and then for drawing something larger back into the world? The talk moves from Corbin’s phrase about the angel’s individuation into Hasidic, Buddhist, Vajrayana, and Kabbalistic parallels, using ayin, the unfabricated, and emptiness to show how perception can be loosened and made more malleable. From there it turns to the demand of imaginal figures, the open-endedness of soul-making, and the way healing may mean widening what we can see, value, and serve.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Reframing the Aim - Burbea returns to Corbin’s line about the angel’s individuation and uses it to shift practice away from self-improvement toward a larger imaginal orientation.
  2. 02:25 Hasidic Twofold Movement - He introduces a Hasidic pattern of dissolving into divine nothingness and drawing divine influx back into the world to spiritualize matter.
  3. 07:20 God Beyond Belief - The talk develops the idea that practice can be for God without requiring fixed beliefs about existence, and then bridges into the Buddha’s teaching of the unfabricated, cessation, and the end of subject-object construction.
  4. 17:03 Beyond Being and Nonbeing - Burbea compares the Pali Canon, Plotinus, and Jewish mystical language to show how the ultimate is spoken of as beyond ordinary concepts of being, non-being, and description itself.
  5. 24:12 Buddha Nature and Tantra - He turns to Vajrayana Buddhism, where emptiness and luminosity are joined, and explains tantric seeing as a skillful way of perceiving the world and beings as divine and empty.
  6. 29:30 Emptiness Makes Perception Malleable - The talk follows how emptiness practice loosens fixed constructions, using examples from the Maggid of Mezeritch and other sources to show how perception can become more fluid and sacred.
  7. 38:39 Healing World and Purpose - Burbea brings in Tikkun Ha'olam, Corbin’s imaginal faculty, and Nietzsche to suggest that perception, interpretation, and life practice can be part of a larger healing and completion beyond the self.
  8. 52:35 Demands of the Imaginal - In the closing section, he names three invitations of imaginal practice: to open to the imaginal realm, to welcome multiplicity, and to recognize that image, myth, and fantasy already saturate ordinary life.
