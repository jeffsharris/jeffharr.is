Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Wise Action for the Earth

Dharma Seed 34:53 2014-04-20

The talk asks why the Buddha still matters in the face of ecological crisis, and answers by tracing the same basic pattern from personal suffering to collective climate breakdown: a mistaken sense of separation. From there it turns toward practice, arguing that inner work, wisdom, and compassion have to be joined to outer response if action is not to collapse into burnout or abstraction. It closes with images of awakening into intimacy with the whole earth, through Rumi, Shantideva, and a call for a new bodhisattva-like way of living.

Chapters

  1. 00:04 Buddhism and crisis - David opens by asking why the Buddha still matters for understanding the ecological crisis and proposes a parallel between personal dukkha and our collective situation.
  2. 02:56 Separate self, shared suffering - He develops the idea that the normal sense of a separate self is insecure, internally ungrounded, and tied to the feeling that something is missing.
  3. 05:26 Forgetting the self - Using Dogen and Nisargadatta, he describes awakening as moving beyond self-concern into intimacy, wisdom, and compassion.
  4. 06:50 From awakening to action - The talk turns toward bodhisattva responsibility, the earth as our larger body, and Rumi’s image of the worm waking into the vineyard.
  5. 14:14 A collective predicament - A Shantideva passage and Bill McKibben’s climate argument are used to show that the crisis is systemic and cannot be solved by individual effort alone.
  6. 26:49 Three kinds of transformation - Drawing on Joanna Macy, he outlines holding actions, building alternatives, and shifting consciousness, then closes with Howard Thurman on vocation and aliveness.
  7. 30:17 Questions and closing images - In the Q&A, he reflects on language, resilience, contagion, and a Spanish song about singing together so they cannot stop us.
