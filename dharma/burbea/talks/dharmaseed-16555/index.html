Karma and Sila as Gaia Practices

Dharma Seed 53:57 2012-07-05

James Baraz uses Bob Doppelt’s From Me to We as a lens for reading Dharma through climate and social responsibility. He moves from interdependence and not-self to karma, then into a concrete look at how ordinary habits ripple through ecological systems and organizations. The talk ends with a quiet, practical invitation to mindfulness, accountability, and moral clarity without being overwhelmed by guilt.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Framing the talk - Baraz introduces the series and sets up the question of how Dharma practice meets planetary crisis.
  2. 01:21 From me to we - He presents Doppelt’s framework and connects the shift from separateness to interdependence with Buddhist not-self.
  3. 04:14 Seeing the systems - Baraz develops the first commitment, showing how each person is nested in many overlapping systems.
  4. 06:44 Karma and accountability - He turns to cause and effect, arguing that actions matter even when consequences are delayed or hard to see.
  5. 13:16 The Buddha’s example - Using the story of the Buddha’s hesitation to teach, he shows how compassion responds to people’s conditioning.
  6. 16:40 Climate as consequence - He brings in John Seed and a series of climate examples to show how collective habits shape the Earth over time.
  7. 26:11 Hidden impacts - A Buddha discourse on impurities becomes a way to question older assumptions about how harm disappears into large systems.
  8. 32:01 Mindfulness over guilt - Baraz asks how to stay awake to ecological responsibility without becoming immobilized or self-blaming.
  9. 36:45 Three commitments - He closes by reading the first three commitments aloud and reframing them as a joyful practice of interdependence, accountability, and justice.
