Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Am I Free to Inquire?

Dharma Seed 1:15:48 2011-08-05

Rob Burbea explores inquiry as both intimacy and questioning: how attention draws close to experience, and how Dharma practice can keep asking what suffering is, how it is constructed, and what blocks freedom. He contrasts living inquiry with rote certainty, using the Buddha, Einstein, Jung, and others to show that real practice needs curiosity, passion, and a willingness to question assumptions. The talk widens from personal practice into ideas, culture, and frameworks, asking what we trust, what we ignore, and whether liberation itself may be more open-ended than we usually suppose.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Inquiry as intimacy - He begins by describing inquiry as a kind of close, kind attention that notices what is actually present and separates out what has become knotted together.
  2. 03:43 Questions that open - He distinguishes questions meant to dissolve rigid concepts from questions meant to reach insight, using the Buddha’s awakening as a model of tracing suffering back to its causes.
  3. 08:05 Experiment and possibility - He turns to the practical conditions for inquiry: permission to experiment, the growth of interest, and the way curiosity strengthens concentration and energy.
  4. 17:01 What blocks questioning - He explores how shoulds, self-view, and social pressure narrow the field of inquiry, and how widening the view can bring fresh energy and freedom.
  5. 24:53 Ideas shape experience - He argues that ideas are not abstract decorations but active forces that shape feeling, perception, and even what seems possible in practice.
  6. 32:48 Passion for truth - Through figures like Ramana Maharshi, Dogen, Picasso, Einstein, and Heisenberg, he shows inquiry as a sustained, sometimes restless devotion rather than a comfortable habit.
  7. 41:05 Comfort, certainty, and dissonance - He examines prejudice, simplicity, and cognitive dissonance, showing how the mind protects its views even when evidence challenges them.
  8. 55:28 Frameworks and observation - He compares science and Dharma as traditions shaped by paradigms, social pressures, and theories that determine what can be seen at all.
  9. 01:02:53 Cracking open the vessel - He closes by asking whether liberation is singular or open-ended, using Yamantaka, Kabbalah, and Einstein to suggest that inquiry may keep widening rather than arriving at a final endpoint.
