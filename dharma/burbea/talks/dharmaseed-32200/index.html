Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

An Ecology of Love (Part 3)

Dharma Seed 35:30 2015-12-21

What happens when love for the world opens into depth instead of narrowing into wanting more? The talk traces eros as a force that can reveal richer, more dimensional perception of nature and the Earth, while also showing how a limited view can distort that movement into greed and endless accumulation. It moves from philosophical clarification to concrete examples like travel, consumption, and planetary limits, then closes by distinguishing eros from metta: one preserves particularity, mystery, and expansion, while the other opens a more universal sense of love.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Eros Opens Nature - Burbea introduces the idea that eros can deepen perception, making nature and its objects feel more complex, vivid, and sacred.
  2. 01:55 Discovery and Creation - He explains that perception involves both discovering and fabricating, and links that “both and” understanding to emptiness.
  3. 03:34 Against Naive Realism - The talk distinguishes this view from both new-age and modernist assumptions about objective reality and settles on a more nuanced epistemology.
  4. 05:52 Eros Or Greed - Burbea takes up the Dharma concern that eros can turn into greed, showing how limitation in view or psyche distorts it.
  5. 08:32 Limits to Growth - He uses the Club of Rome’s Limits to Growth to illustrate how a one-dimensional worldview fuels endless material expansion.
  6. 11:20 Infinite Inner Expansion - When eros, psyche, and logos are not constrained, he says, they can keep opening without demanding more on the material level.
  7. 12:35 Travel, Fantasy, and Images - Using examples like travel and experience-collecting, he shows how a poverty of the imaginal can make eros slide into accumulation and literalism.
  8. 19:51 The Middle Sections - A long middle section contains extended Dharma discussion with several pauses and appears to move through less clearly segmented material in the source.
  9. 33:34 Metta, Eros, Mystery - He ends by contrasting metta’s universal opening with eros’s particularity, and emphasizes that love needs mystery and space to keep deepening.
