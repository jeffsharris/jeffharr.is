Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Gift and the Artifice of Self (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 53:38 2016-08-01

What if the modern sense of self is not simply mistaken, but thinly imagined? The talk moves through cultural critiques of secular humanism, consumerism, and reductionist views of mind, then turns toward Buddhist, Christian, Islamic, and other frames that treat the person as deeper, more sacred, and more open than a fixed identity. From there it asks what kind of view can actually hold both freedom and suffering, and why flexibility of self-view may be central to re-enchanting life without flattening it.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Cultural confusion - Burbea opens by tracing the mixed cultural inheritance that shapes modern ideas of self, virtue, individualism, and humility.
  2. 03:11 Empty self, wary culture - He turns to Buddhist notions of no-self and explains how they can be misheard in ways that leave people suspicious of selfhood itself.
  3. 06:43 Fantasy of origins - He challenges narratives about the pure origins of Buddhism and argues that modern realism and reification are also part of the problem.
  4. 10:25 Re-enchanting self - Burbea distinguishes different kinds of enchantment and begins to sketch what a re-enchantment of self and other would require.
  5. 14:16 Secular humanism - Using Charles Taylor and Freud, he describes secular culture as oriented toward purely human flourishing and asks what that leaves out.
  6. 20:15 What cultures make room for - He contrasts modern Western norms with a Burkina Faso story and then with social critique from Foucault and the Frankfurt School.
  7. 26:41 Sacred depth of person - Burbea gathers Islamic, Buddhist, and Christian references to suggest that the human being can be seen as far deeper and more sacred than modern flatness allows.
  8. 37:13 Process and flexibility - He qualifies the view of self as process and argues that dependent origination supports a flexible, non-final self-view.
  9. 42:04 Dukkha and soul-making - The talk broadens into a soulful frame in which personal events, suffering, and time can become part of soul-making rather than mere problems to escape.
