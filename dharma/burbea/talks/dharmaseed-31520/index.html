Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

A Sacred Universe: Insight, Theophany, Cosmopoesis (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 54:27 2015-08-12

Rob Burbea explores how cultural assumptions shape the way we understand personhood, reality, and the imagination. He contrasts modern materialist views with Buddhist emptiness, imaginal, and theophanic ways of seeing, asking not which philosophy is true but what each way of looking opens or closes in practice. The talk moves toward a non-realist account of Dharma as insight into the fabrication of experience, ending with the possibility of cosmopoiesis: inhabiting a more magical, soul-making cosmos.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the inquiry - Burbea frames the talk as a drawing-together of the retreat's themes and warns that the questions may open more loose ends before they resolve anything.
  2. 02:19 Modern selfhood - He turns to the cultural background of personhood, using Clifford Geertz to show how the bounded, integrated self is a historical assumption rather than a universal fact.
  3. 05:40 Self as a view - He sets the modern self beside popular Dharma views of process or interconnectedness, then emphasizes the Buddha's teaching that all self-views are empty and can be held as lenses, not truths.
  4. 10:02 Ways of looking - Burbea develops the idea that perspectives are ways of looking that open or close possibilities, and he questions the modern assumption that reality is a fixed object waiting to be seen.
  5. 17:01 Person and expression - He considers self-expression, meaningfulness, and the possibility of seeing a person as a theophany or portal for the divine, widening the sense of what a self might be.
  6. 23:26 The imaginal real - The talk shifts to imagination and the imaginal, with Henri Corbin, William Blake, Walter Wink, Longchenpa, Mipham, and Swedenborg used to show traditions that treat imaginal reality as deeply real.
  7. 28:20 Reality and quantum questions - Burbea compares cultural realism with more postmodern approaches, uses a map analogy for partial perspectives, and brings in David Bohm and quantum physics to question objective reality.
  8. 38:47 Non-realist Dharma - He argues for a Dharma grounded not in reified truth claims but in the exploration of fabrication, insisting that insight into how perception is made is direct practice, not abstraction.
  9. 48:56 Cosmopoiesis - The talk closes by describing how assumptions about reality constrain experience, and how deeper emptiness can open into new beauty, wonder, and a made-with meaning in a different cosmos.
