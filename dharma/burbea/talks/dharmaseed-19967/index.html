Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Brahma Viharas and the Climate Challenge

Dharma Seed 52:39 2013-07-11

How do you meet climate fear without collapsing into denial, panic, or resignation? The talk begins from that tension and moves through the brahma-viharas as a lived response: calming the heart with loving-kindness, opening to suffering with compassion, finding uplift in the beauty and life of the world, and widening into equanimity. Along the way, the speaker pairs Buddhist teaching with concrete images—a pelican’s broad view, a sick monk cared for by the Buddha, the reality of children and grandchildren living in a changing world—to show how wisdom and care have to stay together.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening Homage - The talk begins with traditional homage to the Buddha and a quiet opening gesture of respect.
  2. 01:01 Practice in Turbulent Times - She introduces the setting, names the uncertainty of the age, and describes meditation as learning how the mind is pulled by desire, aversion, and confusion.
  3. 04:50 Awakening Is Human - She reflects on the Buddha as a human being and on awakening as a real possibility rather than something remote or exceptional.
  4. 06:35 Training Awareness - The emphasis shifts to recognizing agitation, seeing states clearly, and returning again and again to presence and choice.
  5. 09:55 Facing Climate Anxiety - She turns directly to climate change, naming denial, overwhelm, and the need to let the reality in without collapsing into fear.
  6. 15:00 Metta and Compassion - A personal meditation shows how loving-kindness can steady the heart enough to open compassionately to suffering.
  7. 17:09 Mudita and Equanimity - She moves through appreciative joy and equanimity, using the beauty of Earth and the image of pelicans to evoke spacious, enduring love.
  8. 20:09 Ultimate and Conventional - She contrasts the broad, changing view with the close, frightened view, then tells the story of the Buddha caring for a sick monk to show that wisdom must include care.
  9. 26:26 Ethics and Renunciation - The talk becomes more explicit about pollution, consumption, and responsibility, and she frames the Brahma-viharas and right intention as tools for skillful action.
