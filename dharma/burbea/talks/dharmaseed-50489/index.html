Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Dukkha and Soulmaking (Part 5)

Dharma Seed 56:56 2018-01-01

In this talk, Rob Burbea continues developing the relationship between dukkha and imaginal practice, showing how being intimate with suffering can sometimes allow an image to arise that changes the whole feel of a situation. He distinguishes this from avoidance or forced visualization, and he keeps returning to the idea that the imaginal can bring harmony, clarity, and a sense of duty without erasing difficulty. The talk moves through examples from work, relationships, and teaching encounters, then closes by suggesting that some forms of pathology or fixation may be healed by uncovering an underlying imaginal image.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 From Dukkha to Image - He introduces the talk by turning from the dangers of soulmaking practice toward how imaginal perception can relieve suffering.
  2. 04:03 Intimacy With Suffering - Rob explains how staying close to dukkha in the emotional and energy body can let an imaginal image arise without contrivance.
  3. 09:28 Alive in the Moment - He emphasizes that an image must be alive now, not merely remembered, and that it can change over time.
  4. 12:28 Foucault as Spark - A personal example shows how a book by Michel Foucault became an imaginal spark that reorganized a difficult work situation.
  5. 16:53 Situation, Duty, and Soul - He develops how imaginal transformation can reduce pressure, clarify stance, and leave a challenge that is still meaningful.
  6. 25:57 Mutual Arising - Rob situates nervous system, history, current relations, and image as mutually dependent rather than any one being primary.
  7. 29:52 Imaginal Communication - He gives examples of how another person can communicate an image into the field, including conversations with Catherine and an unnamed woman.
  8. 33:41 Eros and the Beloved - The talk turns to erotic longing, showing how dwelling with a person as image can open more intimacy and reduce fixation.
  9. 44:56 Image at the Core - He closes by arguing that even apparently hopeless patterns may conceal an imaginal image whose uncovering brings relief and liberation.
