Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Fourth Jhana

Dharma Seed 1:31:35 2019-12-31

Rob Burbea explores the fourth jhana as a state of refined stillness, equanimity, and bright whole-body awareness, moving slowly through the Buddha’s canonical description and then into lived experience. He spends time on what makes this state distinct from the third jhana, how it can feel luminous or even like another world, and why the language of purity, brightness, and imperturbability matters. Later he widens the talk toward perception, dependent origination, emotional life, and how jhana practice can support insight without being reduced to a rigid formula.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 From Third to Fourth - He begins by distinguishing the third jhana’s tender peacefulness from the deeper refinement of the fourth.
  2. 04:17 Canonical Description - He reads the Buddha’s description of the fourth jhana and translates it into experiential language of pure, bright awareness.
  3. 07:04 Stillness and Brightness - He unpacks stillness, white light, and the sense that this state can feel like another realm or be called 'the beautiful.'
  4. 13:35 Perception and Emptiness - He broadens the teaching into how jhanas reveal the malleability of perception and point toward dependent origination and emptiness.
  5. 26:33 Equanimity Deepens - He contrasts equanimity with multiplicity against the deeper singleness of the fourth jhana, where almost nothing remains to react to.
  6. 44:04 Emotion and Intention - He discusses subtle metta and compassion within the fourth jhana and connects it to right resolve on the path.
  7. 47:21 Wrapped in Stillness - He develops the image of being wrapped in a white cloth and then in 360-degree stillness, including the common experience of leaning forward.
  8. 01:05:44 Purity, Malleability, Insight - He unpacks the post-jhana list as qualities of mind—pure, bright, workable, steady, imperturbable—and treats it as preparation for vipassana rather than a rigid sequence.
  9. 01:20:58 Base Practice and Maturing - He closes by discussing Ajahn Lee’s advice about using the fourth jhana as a base, his own preference for including the third, and the slow maturing of mastery.
