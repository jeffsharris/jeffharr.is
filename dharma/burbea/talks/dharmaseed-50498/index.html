Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Sensing with Soul (Part 6)

Dharma Seed 65:12 2017-12-26

What if sensing with soul is also a way of knowing values like beauty, love, goodness, and duty, when modern culture tends to treat those as merely subjective? The talk traces how ideas from Galileo through Descartes, Bacon, Locke, and later scientism helped install a hierarchy of what counts as real, and how that hierarchy can quietly limit imagination, practice, and even the way the body is perceived. It also warns that any method of knowing can become a prison when it is treated as absolute, whether in science, religion, or Dharma. The speaker argues that imaginal perception includes a real sensing of values, and shows how modern and religious ideas shape what we think we can know.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the Questions - Rob introduces the talk as an opening into ontology and epistemology in relation to soulmaking and imaginal perception.
  2. 03:02 Values in Imaginal Perception - He develops the claim that sensing with soul includes sensing values such as beauty, love, nobility, and humility.
  3. 12:15 Galileo to Descartes - Using Galileo, Locke, and Descartes, he sketches the rise of primary qualities, secondary qualities, and the modern split between objective fact and subjective value.
  4. 17:30 How Cultures Rank Knowing - He broadens the argument to show how cultures and subcultures create hierarchies of reality, respect, and legitimate knowledge.
  5. 25:20 Modes of Being - He distinguishes different modes of being, such as science, art, politics, and soulmaking, each with its own value-order and way of knowing.
  6. 36:22 Jung and the Prison of View - He uses Jung to describe how even liberating ideas can harden into a prison when treated as absolute.
  7. 40:46 Literalism and Interpretation - He shifts to biblical literalism as an example of interpretive rigidity and the way a narrow reading can shape religion and culture.
  8. 50:53 Body, Fantasy, and Perception - He closes with a retreat story and a Theravada-influenced body view to show how conceptual frameworks and imaginal backgrounds color perception.
