Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Dukkha and Soulmaking (Part 7)

Dharma Seed 1:34:46 2018-01-03

Rob Burbea explores how sensing with soul can relieve and even reframe dukkha, not by explaining suffering away, but by opening imaginal depth, reverence, and relationship. He moves from the pain of being seen too narrowly into a broader claim: that soulmaking can re-enchant what feels flat, truncated, or depressed. In the second half he compares this view with Hegel, Kabbalistic language about the divine need, and several ways of reading the Four Noble Truths.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Imaginal relief - He revisits the earlier claim that sensing with soul can drain suffering out of a situation and make that effect feel necessary rather than incidental.
  2. 02:33 Being seen fully - He turns to relationship, showing how being met only as a flat human self can feel like being missed, while imaginal seeing can heal in a deeper way.
  3. 07:57 Flatness and depression - He links narrowed perception, reified compassion, and the loss of eros to a kind of depression in which life loses its juiciness and dimensionality.
  4. 12:52 Re-enchanting dukkha - He expands the teaching into the idea that particular suffering can be taken up by soulmaking and sensed, in a Kabbalistic idiom, as somehow necessary to God or the Buddha nature.
  5. 19:45 Imaginal and divine - He develops the mutual relation between imaginal perception and divine necessity, describing them as co-arising facets of one shift rather than separate doctrines.
  6. 30:07 Hegel and the negative - He uses Hegel, and then Cyril O'Regan's reading of Hegel, to argue that personhood and even divinity may emerge through struggle, suffering, and the labor of the negative.
  7. 42:20 Questions of why - He pauses over theodicy, arguing that some answers to suffering only become meaningful if they give practice a task, a direction, or a way of looking that supports soulmaking.
  8. 57:37 Four Noble Truths revisited - He compares several readings of the Four Noble Truths, including ending rebirth, simplifying mental entanglement, and an existential reading of tragic impermanence, asking what each leaves out.
  9. 01:13:02 Soulmaking as the worthwhile - He closes by suggesting that for modern listeners, beauty, soulfulness, and soulmaking may become the most worthwhile response where older cosmologies no longer organize the path.
