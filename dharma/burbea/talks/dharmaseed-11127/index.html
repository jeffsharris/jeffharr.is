Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Love, Karma, and Healing

Dharma Seed 1:14:10 2009-02-08

Rob Burbea explores how emptiness, love, karma, and healing are not separate themes but different ways of seeing the same process. He moves from the fear that emptiness leads to nihilism into a quieter argument that less clinging, less selfing, and clearer insight naturally open the heart. The talk then turns to practice: working with compassion, anger, difficult emotions, and even the idea of healing itself without turning the self into the center of the story.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Why emptiness opens love - He begins by addressing the common worry that emptiness means nothing matters, then argues from practice and Khyentse Rinpoche that deeper insight tends to deepen love.
  2. 03:17 Less clinging, less separation - He returns to basic practice, showing how reduced clinging softens self-sense and separateness, while also noting that too much effort can make practice feel flat or disconnected.
  3. 08:13 Mindfulness and metta together - He looks at how insight practice and simple mindfulness can help when things feel difficult, then turns to how emptiness lenses can be brought into metta and compassion.
  4. 12:19 Heroic practice and release - Using the Buddha as a heroic image, he encourages imagination, release of self-concern, and the practice of giving back the aggregates, especially when health, death, or difficulty are close.
  5. 17:16 Compassion without burden - He challenges the idea that compassion is a sacrifice, and instead shows how self-centeredness and possessiveness create suffering, while practical views can loosen blame and judgment.
  6. 24:36 Seeing the world as malleable - With Shantideva, he deconstructs anger and then shows how our perception of others and the world depends on the state of heart and mind, making karma feel immediate and lived.
  7. 32:26 Karma, reasoning, and fading - He says karma is subtle and complex, contrasts logical Madhyamaka reasoning with direct insight through fading, and uses dependent arising to show how the world of experience is conditioned.
  8. 40:16 Healing, storms, and clinging - He moves into emotional healing, questioning the idea of a hidden store of pain that must be released, and instead emphasizes how clinging and belief help construct what arises.
  9. 54:01 Awakening and fabrication - Citing Ajahn Chah and the Buddha, he links awakening to understanding dependent origination, then introduces the three kinds of dukkha and the suffering built into fabrication.
