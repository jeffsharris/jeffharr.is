Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Image of Ethics (Part 2)

Dharma Seed 2:05:41 2020-02-15

What makes a life worthwhile, before we even ask what to do? The talk starts by widening ethics beyond rules and prescriptions, then moves into the sense that modern culture has split action from value and left ethics thin, mechanical, or directionless. From there it turns to shame and guilt, the place of hiri and ottappa in Buddhist teaching, and finally to the need for dimensionality: a higher or deeper order in which value, meaning, and ethical weight can actually be felt. It ends by sketching several historical ways ethics has been grounded, from rationality and cosmic order to voluntarism and concern with suffering.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Recap and ethical expansion - He recaps the earlier talk and restates the central move from 'what ought I do?' toward 'what is of value?' and 'what should I love?'.
  2. 04:04 Worthwhile life questions - Using Hartman and Charles Taylor, he argues that ethics becomes more fertile when it includes questions of the good life, the good to be, and what is worth aspiring to.
  3. 09:11 Freedom without grounding - He describes the modern freedom from imposed moral ends, but also the loss of a shared basis for deciding what is worthwhile and why.
  4. 18:56 Against mechanical ethics - He critiques a reduced, Abhidhamma-style picture of ethics as maximizing positive mind moments and minimizing negative ones, which can feel precise but hollow.
  5. 26:05 Needs of an ethics - He lists several needs for ethics: more than law, room for aspiration, emotion, eros, dimensionality, and a deeper ontology than empty abstraction.
  6. 34:07 Soulmaking and imaginal practice - He turns to the soulmaking Dharma approach and sketches two ethical applications: working with imaginal figures and working with values or virtues as imaginal ideas.
  7. 50:56 Shame, guilt, and conscience - He distinguishes shallow shame from soul-level shame, then brings in the Buddha's hiri and ottappa and Buddhaghosa's iron-rod analogy as ethical guardians.
  8. 01:01:15 Why ethics needs depth - He argues that ethics needs a higher or deeper dimension to avoid flattening into legalism, reductionism, or purely utilitarian calculation.
  9. 01:23:57 Historical sources of dimensionality - He traces ways ethics has been given depth historically, including the Protestant Reformation, angels and intermediaries, rationality, cosmic order, voluntarism, and suffering-based ethics.
