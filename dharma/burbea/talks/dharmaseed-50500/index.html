What is Awakening? (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 1:12:17 2018-01-05

What if the first question is not whether awakening exists, but why you want it? The talk begins by opening up the many meanings of awakening and then keeps turning back to motive: measuring up, pleasing authority, or longing for something more genuine. From there it moves through jhana practice, the pressure of spiritual labels like stream entry, and the way tradition, culture, and inner critic can quietly shape what we think liberation should be. The talk then shifts toward a more open-ended possibility, asking whether awakening might be less a final badge and more a living inquiry into freedom, beauty, and soulfulness. Along the way it uses Buddhist references, everyday examples, and a few pointed stories to keep testing what is actually being sought.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the Question - Burbea introduces the talk as an exploration of awakening rather than a fixed definition, and frames it as something to question from several angles.
  2. 05:17 Beyond the Usual Definition - He uses the Four Noble Truths and common Buddhist language to show how awakening is often reduced to the end of suffering, then asks what that really means.
  3. 16:24 Authority and Achievement - The talk turns to the way traditions and teachers shape our sense of awakening, and how that can become an unexamined authority.
  4. 20:28 Kindness and the Inner Critic - Burbea describes the courage of asking why we ask about awakening, and how that question can expose the inner critic and the wish to measure up.
  5. 28:00 Jhana and Motivation - Using jhana practice, he distinguishes egoic proving from a healthier motivation rooted in curiosity, love of depth, and sensitivity to conditions.
  6. 38:06 What Do You Want? - He shifts from descriptions of awakening to the deeper question of desire, showing how culture, media, and spiritual communities shape what seems desirable.
  7. 51:46 Training and Rebirth - Burbea examines the tension between wanting to be a certain kind of practitioner and resisting discipline, then reviews the classical Buddhist frame of awakening as the ending of rebirth.
  8. 59:56 Happiness and Finality - He questions what people mean by happiness and why awakening is so often imagined as a final endpoint, linking that finality to self-view.
  9. 01:07:25 Open-Ended Awakening - The talk closes by returning to beauty, sacredness, and soulmaking, suggesting that awakening may be better approached as an open-ended exploration than a fixed goal.
