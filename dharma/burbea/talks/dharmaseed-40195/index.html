Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Wisdom, Art, Balance (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 66:54 2017-01-28

How do we work with eros without either flattening it into craving or being carried away by it? The talk begins with two alchemical maxims—about the strength of the vessel and mastering the fire—and then uses them to explore how practice needs both elasticity and care when desire, image, and soul-making intensify the path. It moves through the dangers of over-simplified views of practice, the role of ethics and emotional skill as prerequisites, and a rethinking of equanimity as steadiness, spaciousness, and balance rather than detachment. By the end, the talk opens a middle way between dismissing eros and concretizing it, while also asking what duty to an image may mean and how it can coexist with freedom.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Two alchemical maxims - Burbea introduces two alchemical sayings and frames them as broadly relevant to practice, psychotherapy, and healing.
  2. 01:54 Eros needs a vessel - He explains why opening to eros requires care, boundaries, and a vessel strong enough to hold the work without acting it out.
  3. 04:41 Elasticity and shattering - The vessel must be able to stretch, and sometimes break, as development outgrows old forms and structures.
  4. 08:56 Fire and its dangers - He turns to fire as effort, intensity, and eros itself, stressing both the need for mastery and the inevitability of risk and change.
  5. 15:59 Dionysus and dismemberment - Drawing on James Hillman and Euripides, he uses Dionysian imagery to show eros as disruptive, archetypal, and not reducible to personal blame.
  6. 20:44 Preliminaries for practice - Burbea asks what must be in place before deeper eros work: ethics, preliminary disciplines, conceptual framing, sangha, and a willingness to question assumptions.
  7. 37:34 Ethics and rootedness - He argues that eros practice needs a heartfelt, embodied commitment to ethics, not just obedience to rules or fear of consequences.
  8. 42:34 Energetics and trust - He adds skill with emotion and energy, then a modest trust that allows experimentation and reveals eros as something potentially wise and meaningful.
  9. 48:28 Equanimity and duty - The talk closes by rethinking equanimity as balance and spaciousness, then linking freedom, imaginal duty, and a disciplined middle way in relation to what matters.
