Bonnie Duran, Chas DiCapua, Thanissara, Vinny Ferraro: Mindfulness and Climate Action : 4

Dharma Seed 65:35 2014-10-26

This conversation moves from climate action as a practical response to climate harm into a deeper inquiry about interdependence, colonization, and embodiment. Bonnie Duran frames climate change through Indigenous knowledge and the Buddhist roots poisons, Chas DiCapua turns to patriarchy, technology, and the need to come back into the body, and Vinny Ferraro brings the discussion toward belonging, grief, and action that arises from listening inwardly. The talk closes with breakout reflections and a merit dedication that widens the circle to animals, human communities, and the earth itself.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening and Introductions - Thanissara welcomes the teachers and outlines the conversation on mindfulness, Dharma, and climate action.
  2. 02:12 Plant-Based Ethics - The opening teaching argues for reducing animal products by linking factory farming to emissions, land use, water use, and compassion.
  3. 06:42 Colonization and Root Poisons - Bonnie Duran relates climate change to colonization, greed, hatred, and delusion, and introduces Indigenous ecological knowledge as part of the response.
  4. 13:28 Rights of Nature - The teaching moves through Pachamama, Bolivia and Ecuador, and a quote from John Mohawk to emphasize humility and the intelligence of the larger whole.
  5. 18:43 Technology and Patriarchy - Chas DiCapua expands the critique into patriarchy, overvaluing intellect and science, and the devaluing of body, feminine energy, and nature.
  6. 27:10 Returning to the Body - He brings the talk back to embodied practice, the Satipatthana Sutta, and the idea that connection with the body is the starting point for wise action.
  7. 32:24 Belonging and Grief - Vinny Ferraro speaks about interdependence, the pain of the world, and how despair can become connection rather than paralysis.
  8. 40:30 Listening and Action - The teaching becomes more explicitly guided as Vinny leads inward listening, quotes John Seed, Buddhadasa, and Rumi, and frames action as participation in a larger life.
  9. 51:19 Questions and Dedication - The group responds with questions about discouragement, integrity, and livelihood, then closes with a broad merit dedication and final housekeeping.
