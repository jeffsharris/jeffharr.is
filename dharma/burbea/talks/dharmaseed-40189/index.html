Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

'The Holy Life' (Part 3)

Dharma Seed 1:20:00 2017-02-09

What if practice is not mainly a way to reduce suffering, but a living relationship with eros, image, and soul? The talk contrasts several ways of understanding the path: as medicine, as scientific inquiry, as religious replication of the past, and as an open-ended artistic unfolding. It then asks whether the images of awakening, the self on the path, and even the Buddha and Dharma need to be seen as images so they can remain beautiful, fertile, and not harden into idols.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Eros and the path - The talk opens by linking eros, awakening, and the images that shape how practice is lived.
  2. 04:07 The medical model - He frames the Four Noble Truths as a reduction-of-suffering model and names its strengths and limits.
  3. 08:31 Tradition and replication - He examines a religious fantasy of practice that tries to reproduce the Buddha and place authority in the past.
  4. 12:00 The artistic possibility - An artistic model is introduced as more open-ended, allowing originality and even a kind of healthy obsession.
  5. 13:38 Open-ended practice - He contrasts attainment-oriented ideas with a practice understood as exploratory, authentic, and not confined to measurement.
  6. 22:30 What the soul wants - The talk shifts toward beauty, desire, and the question of whether practice is truly fertile for the soul.
  7. 34:45 Outgrowing fixed images - Through examples, he shows how even authentic visions of practice can become too small and need to expand.
  8. 45:04 Immanence and transcendence - He asks what allows practice to become soulmaking, and how transcendence can return to the world as depth and holiness.
  9. 58:17 Icons, not idols - The closing movement argues for seeing path-images as icons—open, inexhaustible, and capable of keeping eros alive without collapsing into craving.
