Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Q & A

Dharma Seed 20:13 2019-12-22

How do you tell whether a quiet, velvety state belongs to jhāna, imaginal practice, or something in between? The talk moves through a practical Q&A on pīti, sukha, ekaggatā, and the shifting emphasis of different jhānic factors, with the speaker repeatedly stressing that the right map depends on what is actually happening in experience. It also returns to the question of how to work with images, energy, and sleepiness without forcing a formula, and why one-pointedness and equanimity need to be read in context rather than as fixed definitions.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Pīti and sukha - He begins by addressing whether pīti belongs to the first jhāna and sukha to the second, and offers a practical reading of their meanings and felt qualities.
  2. 01:40 One-pointedness in context - He explains ekaggatā as something present in all consciousness, while becoming more developed and absorbed in jhāna.
  3. 03:12 Absorption and direction - He describes how jhānic absorption strengthens one thing’s prominence and why the felt direction of a state matters for practice.
  4. 04:02 Jhāna or imaginal - Using the image of a white velvet, breath-of-God-like space, he distinguishes between a jhāna reading and an imaginal or soulmaking reading of the same experience.
  5. 05:54 Choosing the emphasis - He talks about deciding whether to work with jhāna, imaginal practice, or both in sequence or parallel, and why the primary factor can shift over time.
  6. 08:59 Why maps stay partial - He notes that many states can resemble each other and uses the Buddha’s different kinds of equanimity to show why individualized guidance is often needed.
  7. 12:33 Energy and fatigue - He answers a question about how much energy the practice takes and suggests that tiredness may call for rest, base practice, or a different jhānic emphasis.
  8. 14:17 Images with pīti - He discusses whether accompanying images can be kept, and says they can remain in the background if pīti is still the main thing.
  9. 16:55 Soft pīti and waking up - He finishes by explaining how softer, calmer pīti asks for subtler attention, and how opening, sustaining, or maximizing enjoyment may each be the right move depending on the state.
