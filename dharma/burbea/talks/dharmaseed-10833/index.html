Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Sense Desire

Dharma Seed 59:34 2008-11-14

Rob Burbea explores sense desire as a practical and spiritual question, not a moral problem to be judged. He contrasts the Buddha’s hard warnings with a more careful inquiry: what actually happens to the heart, to freedom, and to love when we chase pleasure or practice restraint? The talk moves from everyday indulgence to renunciation, inner resource, and the possibility of a deeper happiness that makes sense desire look smaller by comparison.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the question - Rob frames sense desire as a difficult topic and sets up the need to examine it without quick reactions or self-judgment.
  2. 04:10 Danger and renunciation - He quotes strong Buddha imagery about the danger of sense pleasure and tells Ajahn Chah stories to show renunciation as serious but not joyless.
  3. 06:41 Where the loop lives - The talk turns to addiction, shame, self-view, and the way judgment can keep the cycle of indulgence and remorse going.
  4. 12:08 Inner resource first - Rob emphasizes that self-judgment is usually the more immediate problem, and that letting go needs enough inner well-being to make it possible.
  5. 18:27 Joy and connection - He brings in verses on non-attachment and meditation pleasure, then notes that mindfulness often makes the senses more alive while also changing the basis of practice.
  6. 24:06 Exploring pleasure directly - Rob invites full attention to indulgence and pleasure to see how brief and constructed they are, and how different kinds of happiness can be compared.
  7. 31:54 Mind shapes experience - He argues that pleasure and displeasure depend on the state of mind, and uses food, music, and sex to show how the heart conditions experience.
  8. 41:24 Love, contentment, restraint - The talk links contentment, frugality, and renunciation with an open heart, then asks what sex and connection are actually serving.
  9. 49:53 Beyond the senses - Rob closes by widening the frame to include neutral and unpleasant experience, and by pointing to a freedom that comes when practice leads beyond grasping.
