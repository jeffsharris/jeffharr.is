Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Dependent Origination, Awareness, and Time

Dharma Seed 68:01 2009-02-14

What if time is not the container everything happens in, but another dependently arisen pattern, held together by clinging, selfing, and the way consciousness meets objects? The talk moves from a careful analysis of the present moment—asking whether it is one or many, and whether it arises from itself, other, both, or neither—into a practice-oriented look at how time, attention, and the sense of self support one another. It then widens into dependent origination more generally, with the Buddha’s awakening used as a model for how mutual dependency can be seen directly rather than only thought about. The emphasis stays practical throughout: use the concepts, test them in meditation, and see what loosens when the sense of time is no longer taken to be solid.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 A Map of Practice - Burbea frames the talk as a continuation of the previous night and says he is drawing a map of what may be possible in practice.
  2. 02:25 Time Feels Solid - He describes how time is ordinarily felt as an independent vessel and how clinging, resistance, and dread make time feel thicker and more substantial.
  3. 05:37 Past and Future - He examines the way past, present, and future depend on one another and argues that the present is not exempt from the same emptiness.
  4. 10:05 Awareness and Time - Returning to Gampopa, he extends the inquiry from awareness to time and sets up the question of whether the present moment is one or many.
  5. 13:26 One or Many - Through a step-by-step analysis, he shows that the present moment cannot be found as inherently one or many, and points to this as a practical insight rather than a clever argument.
  6. 18:49 Arising From None - Using Nagarjuna's fourfold analysis, he asks whether the present arises from itself, other, both, or neither, and uses the impossibility of each option to conclude that the moment lacks inherent existence.
  7. 23:16 The Tripod of Self - He shifts from analysis to the lived sense of self, things, and time, describing them as a tripod that supports one another and gives solidity to experience.
  8. 36:00 Dependent Arising Deepens - He retells the Buddha's awakening sequence and then explains how consciousness, attention, intention, and the present moment arise mutually and cannot be cleanly separated.
  9. 44:22 Subtle Mind Factors - The talk becomes more technical as he traces how delusion, intention, attention, and perception interlock, and how time, objects, and consciousness each depend on the others.
