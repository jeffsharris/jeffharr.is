Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Pain, as Void and as Sacrament

Dharma Seed 2:34:37 2020-02-13

Rob Burbea offers a practical and increasingly imaginal set of ways of working with physical pain in meditation. He begins with simple mindfulness and the easing of reactivity, then moves through insight practices, emptiness-based ways of looking, jhana-based re-perception, and finally soulmaking and ritual. The talk gradually widens from reducing suffering to asking what is most sacred, and how pain can sometimes be met as gift, offering, or divine appearance rather than only as a problem to solve.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Opening the terrain - He sets out the scope of the talk: practical ways of working with physical pain, with some applications to mental dukkha as well.
  2. 05:01 A progressive list - He explains that he will move from easier, familiar methods toward more difficult but more powerful ones, and notes the relevance of earlier writing in Seeing the Freeze.
  3. 08:47 Mindfulness and bare attention - He begins with relaxing the body, dropping the story, reducing temporal expansion, and learning to rest with direct sensation.
  4. 15:13 How pain is fabricated - He deepens the first approach by showing how clinging, labels, and reactivity help fabricate both suffering and, in a stronger sense, the felt intensity of pain itself.
  5. 27:11 Insight ways of looking - He surveys three characteristics, emptiness, welcoming, metta to phenomena, and related practices that include aversion, attention, and the awareness of awareness itself.
  6. 42:25 Jhana and malleability - He describes re-seeing pain through jhanic qualities such as piti, happiness, stillness, consciousness, or nothingness, using this to argue for the malleability of vedana.
  7. 53:02 Why not fully fade? - He asks why one would only partially fade pain, and answers in terms of sacredness, hierarchy, and the relationship between the fabricated and the unfabricated.
  8. 01:09:38 Mahayana and Vajrayana frames - He turns to Buddhology, the bodhisattva ideal, and Vajrayana-style appearance as divinity, then links these to the practice of lightly holding emptiness without letting appearances disappear.
  9. 01:31:40 Sacrifice and soulmaking - He develops a soulmaking response to pain in which suffering, self, and image are woven into sacrifice, Christ imagery, and a wider imagination of sacredness.
