Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Nature of Enchantment

Dharma Seed 52:55 2016-07-27

What if re-enchantment is not a return to nature, but a shift in how perception itself is made? The talk begins by using a Guardian article about trees, stress, and city life as a foil, then turns to what is hidden in modernist and scientistic ways of framing value, reality, and nature. From there it opens into a practice-based account of subtlety, sensitivity, imaginal perception, and the need for depth, dimensionality, and a more poetic way of looking.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Retreat framing - Rob welcomes participants and outlines the four areas he will introduce: what re-enchantment means, why it matters, the retreat's context and bases, and how to approach the practices.
  2. 03:42 Emptiness as basis - He explains that the retreat assumes familiarity with emptiness, ways of looking, fabrication, imaginal practice, emotions, samatha, metta, and the energy body, and he emphasizes emptiness as a basis rather than a distant goal.
  3. 08:23 Trees and critique - Using a Guardian article about trees in cities, he highlights what is useful in the piece and then critiques its modernist assumptions, its flat view of nature, and its reliance on measurable value.
  4. 14:54 Depth and scientism - He develops the idea that re-enchantment must include depth and dimensionality, while also naming scientism as a cultural habit that treats measurement and physical explanation as the final standard of reality.
  5. 20:03 Subtlety in perception - Rob turns the article's concern with being affected by places into a meditation instruction, showing how refined attention can notice the energetic, emotional, and psychic effects of materials, landscapes, and environments.
  6. 25:11 Kinds of enchantment - He distinguishes immature, cultural, spiritual, and imaginal forms of enchantment, describing how each narrows or opens the self in different ways.
  7. 29:45 Imaginal and soul - The talk deepens into mature enchantment: non-flat perception, divinity, image as image, and the difference between a universal spiritual opening and a more particular soul-making orientation.
  8. 36:51 Poetic language - He returns to the basic qualities of the path he is describing—non-flattening, divinity, and poetic use of language—while stressing that these are not beliefs or dogmas but ways of encouraging transformation.
  9. 44:29 Open frameworks - Rob closes by describing concepts as elastic, experience-linked, and not truth-claims in a rigid sense, then uses Kabbalah's messianic eon to suggest that spiritual life opens into many possible perceptions of God.
