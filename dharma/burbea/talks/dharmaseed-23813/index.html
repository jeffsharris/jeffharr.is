Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Buddhist Perspective On Climate Change

Dharma Seed 67:11 2014-06-23

Climate change is first laid out as a concrete, evidence-based problem: warming atmosphere and oceans, shrinking ice, rising seas, and the role of greenhouse gases and human activity. From there, the talk moves into how a Buddhist lens changes the question from abstract blame to ethics, responsibility, and the way greed, consumption, and indifference shape collective harm. It brings in examples from the Northeast, Nepal, and a range of Buddhist texts and stories to widen the moral circle from human beings to animals, ecosystems, and future generations.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening frame - The talk introduces the subject and outlines a path from climate science to Buddhist responsibility.
  2. 01:17 What climate science shows - He reviews key IPCC findings on warming, oceans, ice loss, sea level rise, and greenhouse gases.
  3. 12:28 Human influence and local impacts - The talk turns to attribution, the warming since the industrial era, and examples from the northeastern United States.
  4. 20:14 Nepal and the Himalayas - He describes Nepal’s vulnerability, glacier retreat, black carbon, and downstream risks to water and food security.
  5. 28:34 Causes of the crisis - Natural causes are mentioned, but the focus settles on industrialization and human-caused greenhouse gas emissions.
  6. 32:19 Harmony with nature - Buddhist teaching is presented as cooperation rather than domination, using Peter Harvey and D.T. Suzuki as supporting voices.
  7. 40:05 Non-harming and metta - He expands the first precept, ahimsa, and the brahmaviharas to include animals, ecosystems, and all sentient beings.
  8. 48:05 Greed and responsibility - The talk connects climate change to consumer greed, the economic system, and practical ways of reducing harm and emissions.
  9. 59:59 Trees and future generations - A tree-spirit story, texts on planting groves, and a Dalai Lama quotation are used to close on gratitude and universal responsibility.
