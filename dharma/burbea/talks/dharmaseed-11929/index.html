Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Time and the Emptiness of Time

Dharma Seed 56:55 2007-11-24

What if the time we feel ourselves living inside is not as solid as it seems? The talk begins with everyday struggles around time—wanting things over, fearing what’s ahead, measuring ourselves across past, present, and future—and then carefully turns that whole assumption inside out. It moves from conventional clock time and Newton’s picture of absolute time, through Einstein, into meditation, where the speaker explores how craving, selfing, and perception help fabricate the sense of time and how that sense can dissolve into timelessness.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Ordinary time - Burbea begins with the everyday ways time feels real: wanting things over, fearing the future, and trying to preserve what we value.
  2. 04:33 Questioning the obvious - He turns toward the Dharma question behind that struggle, and briefly distinguishes conventional clock time from the intuitive sense of time.
  3. 08:25 Newton and Einstein - Newton’s absolute time and Einstein’s relativity are used as a bridge into the deeper claim that even our basic sense of time is worth examining.
  4. 14:35 Time and seeking - Using Meister Eckhart’s reading of the temple story, he warns that trying to get something from practice can reintroduce time and distance.
  5. 20:26 Now is not enough - He questions the refuge in the present moment alone, and shows how past, future, self, and thing-making feed one another.
  6. 28:24 Reasoning in meditation - Burbea explains a Tibetan-style use of logic in practice, then applies Nagarjuna’s analysis of arising to the present moment.
  7. 36:40 Self, things, and time - He shows how measurement, assessment, craving, and perception support the sense of self, objects, and time together.
  8. 48:10 Timeless unbinding - The talk moves into consciousness, intention, and objecthood, suggesting that when these are seen as dependent, time can begin to loosen.
  9. 54:06 Living from insight - He closes by contrasting glib claims that time is an illusion with the practical possibility of living from an insight that transforms life, death, and service to others.
