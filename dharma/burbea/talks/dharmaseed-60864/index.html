Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Developing Piti, Developing Focus, Developing Wellbeing

Dharma Seed 1:28:44 2019-12-21

How do you work with pleasure in meditation without grasping at it, dismissing it, or turning it into a project? The talk moves between two broad approaches: steady concentration on one object, and a more receptive way of coaxing well-being through the energy body, imagination, and a gentler quality of attention. From there it widens into samadhi as collectedness and integrity, practical ways to meet constriction, and the reminder that jhana is a matter of perception—something to play with, not snatch at.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 What Pīti Is - Burbea introduces pīti, gives his working definition, and sketches the two broad ways it tends to arise.
  2. 02:16 Forms of Pleasure - He names several manifestations of pīti, then explains how it can become steadier and how deeper jhānas change its texture.
  3. 06:02 Pleasure and Practice - He distinguishes pīti from sukha, warns against shame around meditative pleasure, and describes concentration as a way of gathering energy.
  4. 14:16 Breath and Subtlety - He turns to the subtle breath, access concentration, and the contrast between deliberate focus and a more receptive attunement to the whole energy body.
  5. 25:05 Collectedness and Integrity - Samādhi is reframed as harmony, coherence, and power of being, with implications that extend beyond formal meditation.
  6. 33:15 Working with Constriction - Burbea offers several ways to meet discomfort: widen awareness, imagine flow through blockage, connect areas imaginatively, and use insight to soften contraction.
  7. 40:03 Perception Attainments - He stresses that mindfulness is never neutral, that willingness needs skill, and that jhāna is fundamentally a training in perception rather than a race for attainments.
  8. 50:29 Spreading and Settling Pīti - The talk moves into practical ways to spread pīti through the whole body, especially when it feels too strong, including opening, surrendering, and letting the river calm.
  9. 01:07:10 Equanimity and Awakening - He distinguishes helpful energetic movement from habitual shaking, then closes by contrasting equanimity with awakening and returning to the malleability of perception as central to the path.
