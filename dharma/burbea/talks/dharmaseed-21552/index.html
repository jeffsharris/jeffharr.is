Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Climate Change, Capitalism, and Buddhadharma

Dharma Seed 57:26 2013-11-21

How do private property, comparison, and climate collapse feed one another? The talk begins with the urgency of the 2013 Warsaw climate talks and then turns to an early Buddhist origin story, the Aggañña Sutta, to show how craving, hoarding, and fencing off land lead to hierarchy, theft, and violence. From there it moves into practical questions about what can be done now: media silence, divestment, grassroots organizing, and small groups working together rather than facing the crisis alone. It closes by returning to Sangha as a model for collective response and shared responsibility.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the crisis - She situates the talk in the climate emergency and the Warsaw COP meeting, with a focus on the urgency, injustice, and corporate power involved.
  2. 09:12 Buddhist lens - She grounds her reading in the early Pali Canon and turns toward Buddhist ideas of anatta, mine-ness, and the suffering that comes from possession.
  3. 15:52 Introducing the sutta - She introduces the Aggañña Sutta as a satirical origin story and sets up its challenge to caste and fixed social hierarchy.
  4. 25:52 Desire and comparison - She retells the story of luminous beings, tasting, differentiation, comparison, lust, and discrimination as conditions shift.
  5. 32:05 Property and violence - The story moves to rice hoarding, fences, theft, lying, and violence, which she reads as the arising of private property and social conflict.
  6. 35:59 Climate and capitalism - She returns from the sutta to the present, arguing that climate breakdown and fossil fuel power reveal the same patterns of extraction and denial.
  7. 40:02 How to speak and organize - In the discussion, she offers ways to talk about climate without forcing resistance, and points to groups like 350.org and One Earth Sangha.
  8. 46:36 Divestment and resistance - She speaks about socially responsible investing, divestment, and the limits of international agreements in the face of corporate power.
  9. 52:08 Local action together - The closing comments return to grassroots response, peer support, and small groups, ending with the Buddha’s teaching that we go forth together for the welfare of all beings.
