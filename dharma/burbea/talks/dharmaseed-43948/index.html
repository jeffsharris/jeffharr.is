Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Problem of Desire

Dharma Seed 58:16 2017-03-26

Rob Burbea opens this retreat talk by questioning the usual Buddhist habit of treating all desire as a problem. He distinguishes craving, clinging, and eros, then shows how a narrower view of practice can miss beauty, mystery, sexuality, and the deeper movements that support soulmaking. The talk moves from criticism of simplifications to a provisional definition of eros as a desire for deeper contact and opening, with emptiness and ways of looking as the background to that exploration.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Opening the question - Burbea introduces desire and soulmaking as central, inexhaustible themes and frames the talk as an entry into a complex topic.
  2. 04:04 Culture and confusion - He looks at modern culture, consumerism, and the self, then turns to the mixed messages practitioners often inherit about desire.
  3. 06:47 Wholesome and unwholesome - Using the Buddha's distinction between skillful and unskillful desire, he shows how teachings can be oversimplified or turned back to front.
  4. 09:41 The path is not thin - He questions the idea that awakening is simply having no desires, especially around romance, beauty, senses, and the wish to live with integrity.
  5. 16:30 Beyond relief - Burbea challenges a reduced view of awakening as mere relief from unpleasantness and contrasts it with truth, beauty, mystery, and the transcendent.
  6. 20:06 Three kinds of desire - He proposes a working distinction between craving, clinging, and eros, then begins to show how each unfolds differently in practice.
  7. 25:49 Clinging and emptiness - This section develops clinging as subtle push-pull, traces it through ways of looking, and connects letting go with dependent origination and emptiness rather than with a fantasy of no clinging.
  8. 36:55 Defining eros - He offers a provisional definition of eros as wanting more contact, intimacy, and opening, distinguishes it from pleasure-seeking and other theories, and places it in the territory of the imaginal.
  9. 50:59 Eros, path, and sacredness - The talk closes by linking eros to soulmaking, sexuality, sacredness, and the way our concepts themselves shape what practice becomes for us.
