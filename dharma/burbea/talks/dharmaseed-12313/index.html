Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Second Samatha Instructions and Guided Meditation

Dharma Seed 42:04 2007-04-01

Rob Burbea offers practical samatha instructions, moving from the long breath to finding the breath that is most comfortable, enjoyable, and supportive of collectedness. He emphasizes keeping the whole body in awareness, balancing energizing and calming, and working skillfully with hindrances, self-judgment, and the tendency to fixate on what is difficult. The talk then turns to discomfort in sitting, with several ways to work with pain before ending in a guided settling into posture and breath, followed later by a stretch of repeated literary and teacher references, especially Rainer Maria Rilke.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Gathering the instructions - He opens by collecting the retreat's samatha points into a single set of instructions and introduces the long breath as the starting place.
  2. 05:25 Balancing energy and calm - The talk explains how to adjust breath, attention, and bodily awareness so practice stays balanced between energizing and calming.
  3. 08:39 Working with hindrances - He names the hindrances, especially sloth and torpor or restlessness, and encourages less judgment and less story around them.
  4. 10:53 Turning toward pleasantness - Rob points to the broader emotional field of practice and suggests leaning toward what is okay, pleasant, or nourishing instead of only what feels wrong.
  5. 13:12 Meeting sitting pain - He offers several ways to work with discomfort, including whole-body awareness, resting attention elsewhere, and imagining the breath into the painful area.
  6. 14:43 Posture and settling - The guided section begins with finding an upright, easeful posture, softening tension, and connecting with the felt sense of the body.
  7. 17:03 Guided breath practice - He leads the meditation through lengthening the breath, then settling into a breath that is most comfortable and enjoyable while keeping awareness spacious.
  8. 27:27 Influences and recitations - The later section contains a long string of names, with recurring references to teachers and writers, especially Rainer Maria Rilke, though the surrounding context is incomplete.
