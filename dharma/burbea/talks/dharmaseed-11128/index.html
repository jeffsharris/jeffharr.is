Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

On the Nature of Mind

Dharma Seed 67:30 2009-02-13

Rob Burbea maps a progression of practice around the nature of mind, beginning with why awareness matters at the level of suffering and compassion, and then moving through mirror-like awareness, luminous mind, and the limits of those images. He draws on the Buddha, Zen, Mahamudra, and Dzogchen sources to show how each helpful view can still be provisional. The talk gradually turns toward the subtler question of how knowing and known arise together, and why even consciousness itself is said to be empty.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Framing the inquiry - Rob introduces the talk as a practice-oriented exploration of mind, and sets out why the question matters at different levels of suffering and freedom.
  2. 05:35 Why emptiness matters - He explains why saying awareness is empty is not meant as abstract philosophy, but as something meant to deepen compassion and real release.
  3. 12:57 From self to center - Using Ajahn Maha Bua’s insight about a center to knowing, he distinguishes between releasing identification with consciousness and seeing awareness itself as empty.
  4. 20:55 The mirror image - He turns to the Zen mirror poem and Hui Neng’s reply to show both the usefulness and the limits of the mirror metaphor for awareness.
  5. 23:59 Provisional luminous states - A long Pali Canon passage on luminous realms and not conceiving things from them becomes a warning against reifying even refined meditative experiences.
  6. 28:36 Luminous mind revisited - Rob reads contrasting translations on the luminous mind, then shows how language about purity, space, and clarity can still point to helpful but incomplete views.
  7. 38:52 What cannot be found - He gathers Buddha, Third Karmapa, and Shantideva passages to argue that mind cannot be located as a thing, and that even self-aware mind remains problematic.
  8. 49:18 Knowing and known - Drawing on the Dalai Lama and Gampopa, he examines the mutual dependence of consciousness and objects, and shows how awareness is fabricated through clinging and identification.
  9. 59:45 Practice beyond the view - He closes by describing how contemplation of emptiness can become a lived practice that deepens freedom, while stressing that ordinary clinging to inherent existence is the default unless it is deliberately seen through.
