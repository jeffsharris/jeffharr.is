Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

No Mind

Dharma Seed 1:16:51 2010-02-12

What if the thing that seems most basic — awareness itself — is also empty, dependently arisen, and not a final ground at all? The talk moves from a broad map of practice and the pull of the inner critic into a slow, careful dismantling of the tendency to make mind, luminosity, and vastness into something solid. Along the way, the speaker uses the poisonous tree image, a Zen mirror poem, early suttas, and later Mahayana and Tibetan sources to show how meditation can first seem to polish awareness and then, more deeply, reveal that there was never a thing there to grasp.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Setting the map - Rob explains that the retreat will unfold as a spiral and that he is offering a practical map of how practice can deepen, not a final or only view.
  2. 07:38 Levels of practice - He uses the image of a poisonous tree to show different depths of practice, from trimming symptoms to uprooting roots, and frames the night’s topic as awareness itself.
  3. 11:46 Awareness and suffering - He argues that awareness, like all phenomena, lacks inherent existence and that the real problem is the gut-level habit of reifying things, not a philosophical mistake.
  4. 17:51 The center of knowing - Using Ajahn Maha Bua and the Hui Neng mirror poem, he explores the shift from identifying with awareness to seeing even the mirror-like model as provisional.
  5. 27:31 Luminous but not final - He turns to passages on luminous mind and the Buddha’s teaching on perception, showing how beautiful meditative states still need to be seen without making them 'mine'.
  6. 35:31 Translations and suttas - Rob compares translations of AN 1.49 and then moves through Prajnaparamita and MN 1, using them to question whether luminosity points to something real or to a perception shaped by language and habit.
  7. 48:16 Looking for mind - He gathers several Mahayana citations, including Shantideva and an unnamed sutta, to show that mind or awareness cannot be found as an entity inside, outside, or between.
  8. 01:01:04 Mutual dependence - He introduces Gampopa’s three avenues, then focuses on the second: knowing and the known arise together and lean on each other, which makes inherent existence untenable.
  9. 01:09:48 Letting consciousness fade - In the closing, he connects insight into emptiness with dispassion, the fading of consciousness, and the Buddha’s line about not relishing consciousness as the house-builder is dismantled.
