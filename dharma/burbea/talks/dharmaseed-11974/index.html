Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Nirvana and Transcendence

Dharma Seed 65:01 2008-06-24

What if the Buddha’s teaching points not only to the end of suffering, but to something beyond the six senses, beyond space and time, and beyond the ways we usually make a life feel complete? The talk moves from early Pali passages to Zen and Tibetan references, then turns to the listener’s own reactions: boredom, fear, relief, or a quiet sense that something deeper is being named. From there it asks how mindfulness, intimacy with life, and the inner critic fit alongside the harder question of fabrication, unmaking, and the deathless.

Chapters

  1. 00:58 The deathless question - Burbea frames the talk around the Buddha’s aim of ending suffering and asks what the teachings on the unborn and deathless are really pointing to.
  2. 04:32 Sutta passages - He reads a series of early Buddhist passages describing a dimension beyond the six senses, name-and-form, and ordinary notions of coming and going.
  3. 09:39 Huang Po and the void - A Zen passage from Huang Po reinforces the theme that the real Dharma is beyond birth, destruction, and conceptual grasping.
  4. 11:18 Reactions and assumptions - Burbea pauses to notice the range of responses to transcendence, including disinterest, fear, yearning, and inner criticism.
  5. 21:21 Mindfulness and life - He turns to mindfulness and a fuller participation in life, using Thich Nhat Hanh and another teacher’s remarks to show why this dimension is valuable but not complete.
  6. 29:33 Life or transcendence - The talk examines how modern Dharma can make 'Life' into the main object of practice, and whether that replaces a more radical transcendence.
  7. 36:41 Awareness and language - Burbea critiques the idea of vast cosmic awareness as the deathless, bringing in Buddha passages and Huang Po to show the limits of reifying awareness and language.
  8. 47:50 Fabrication and unmaking - He explains the Buddha’s instruction to know the destruction of fabrications, describing practice as a gradual dismantling of the made-up structures of experience.
  9. 58:48 Beyond existence - The closing section uses a Buddha passage on truth and a Rumi poem to suggest how emptiness can change our relation to world, self, fear, and hope without denying lived experience.
