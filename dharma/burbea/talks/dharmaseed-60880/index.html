The Second Jhana

Dharma Seed 1:31:05 2019-12-25

What changes when the second jhana is not just a quieter mind, but a shift from pīti to sukha, from the excitement of entry into a more refined and bodily happiness? The talk reads the Buddha’s descriptions closely, then keeps circling back to practice: how to tell pīti from sukha, how to let the base practice and happiness integrate, and why forcing progress is usually the wrong move. Along the way, the speaker uses images of a ripening mango, a lake filling from below, and even a street interview in Newton Abbot to make the territory feel concrete. The talk ends by widening the frame, asking whether jhana is really about getting rid of thought, or about learning how perception is fabricated and what that means for letting go.

Chapters

  1. 01:15 Reading the सूत्र - He begins by reading the canonical description of second jhāna and noting how translation choices shape practice.
  2. 03:18 What samadhi means - He unpacks samadhi as unification and harmonization, not just concentration, and points to internal assurance.
  3. 06:31 Lake and spring simile - A detailed reading of the lake simile shows how pīti and sukha suffuse the whole body from within.
  4. 12:29 Refinement, not force - He argues that jhāna is a spectrum of increasing refinement and less fabrication, not mainly stronger concentration.
  5. 21:19 Pīti and sukha - He distinguishes first and second jhāna by the shift from pīti to sukha, while noting that both are present in both states.
  6. 25:23 How it matures - Using mango ripening and practical examples, he describes how second jhāna arises organically rather than by pressure.
  7. 28:42 Working with pīti - He offers several ways to cultivate the transition, from staying with a base object to gently introducing happiness or a memory of it.
  8. 38:39 Thought and subtlety - The talk turns to vitakka and vicāra, and to the finer question of what counts as thought and what has actually ceased.
  9. 46:55 What jhāna gives - He explains why sukha, confidence, and reduced self-other fabrication are more significant than the mere quieting of thought.
