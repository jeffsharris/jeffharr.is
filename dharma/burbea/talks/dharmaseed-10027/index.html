Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Mindfulness, Insight, Liberation

Dharma Seed 57:03 2009-10-01

Rob Burbea examines what mindfulness is for, and argues that it is not simply bare awareness, calm presence, or accepting whatever arises. He contrasts “bare attention” with the Buddha’s emphasis on appropriate attention, then shows how mindfulness works inside the larger frame of the path, the Four Noble Truths, and liberation. The talk gradually moves from practical distinctions to a subtler claim: the way we look helps fabricate experience, so insight practice is learning ways of looking that release clinging and reduce suffering.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 What mindfulness means - Rob opens by naming the talk as a conceptual framework and noticing how many different meanings mindfulness can have, even among experienced meditators and Buddhist traditions.
  2. 03:23 Bare attention and its limits - He describes bare attention and acceptance as useful strands of practice, while warning that they can romanticize simple presence and hide deeper assumptions.
  3. 08:13 Appropriate attention - Turning to yoniso manasikara, he contrasts the Buddha’s language with bare attention and suggests that mindfulness and appropriate attention support one another in awakening.
  4. 12:40 Not the same as awakening - He uses the road analogy and the story of the tightrope walker to show that concentration, calm, or being “in the zone” are not the same as liberation.
  5. 18:30 The Satipatthana Sutta - Rob turns to the Four Foundations of Mindfulness to show that the Buddha teaches categories and ways of looking, not just open-ended observation, including body contemplation and stationing attention.
  6. 24:41 Mindfulness with an agenda - He explains that mindfulness is active within the Eightfold Path: it helps gather well-being, support practice, and bring understanding that leads to release.
  7. 31:52 Four Noble Truths in practice - Rob argues that every moment of mindfulness has a context and an aim, and that right mindfulness means noticing suffering, prioritizing its decrease, and releasing craving and clinging.
  8. 39:51 How perception is built - He goes deeper into how views, assumptions, and identification shape experience, making the case that perception itself is fabricated rather than passively received.
  9. 49:41 What real insight requires - Rob closes by drawing on dependent arising, Nagarjuna, and the limits of passive watching to suggest that liberation comes through seeing how experience is made and learning to let go at subtler and subtler levels.
