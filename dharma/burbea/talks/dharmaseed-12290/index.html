Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Expressions of Metta

Dharma Seed 66:46 2007-01-25

Rob Burbea explores metta as more than a single feeling, showing it as a changing constellation that can include tenderness, warmth, clarity, compassion, silence, and even boundaries. He moves from self-kindness and the social habit of self-neglect into friendship, neutrality, and the difficult terrain of anger, while keeping the talk grounded in ordinary relationships and practice. In the last third, he opens metta into the body, silence, and a sense of love as something already holding experience, then returns to practice instructions and the possibility of oneness and questioning separation.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Metta Has Many Flavors - Burbea introduces the talk by widening metta from one feeling into a range of colors, qualities, and expressions.
  2. 02:43 Self-Kindness and Boundlessness - He explains why self-metta matters, how repeated phrases slowly reshape habit, and why the categories are only tools on the way to unlimited love.
  3. 07:52 Attachment and Friendship - The talk turns to the near enemy of attached love, then to friendship as mutual seeing, appreciation, and being seen.
  4. 13:24 Uniqueness in Context - Burbea clarifies that metta and non-self do not erase individuality, instead placing uniqueness within a larger web of conditions.
  5. 18:52 Neutrality and Selfing - He looks at the neutral category and uses it to explore how clinging builds a narrow self-sense that makes others fade into the background.
  6. 25:22 Anger with Love - Burbea treats anger as a complicated practice area, warning against both repression and unhelpful venting while emphasizing boundaries and true speech.
  7. 36:12 Hearing Hurt Well - He frames difficult conversation as a practice of speaking what is true and helpful, and of hearing another's hurt without absorbing their anger.
  8. 45:40 The Body and Everyday Life - Metta is extended into bodily movement, touch, walking, attentiveness, slowing down, and the way ordinary busyness can shrink awareness.
  9. 55:05 Silence and Held Experience - A Neruda poem opens a discussion of silence as receptive space, followed by a retreat note about pain, remorse, and being surrounded by love.
