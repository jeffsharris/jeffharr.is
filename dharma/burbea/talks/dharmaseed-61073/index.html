Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Image of Ethics (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 2:12:55 2020-02-14

How do we keep ethics from shrinking into law, rights, and a thin sense of what we are allowed to do? The talk argues that ethical life needs a wider frame: a sense of what is worthwhile, beautiful, and sacred, plus room for omission, aspiration, emotion, and love of the virtues. Using examples from climate protest, Nazi Germany, and the limits of standard moral systems, the speaker builds toward an ethics grounded in dimensionality, disciplined eros, and a more expansive way of sensing what matters.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Setting the frame - Burbea situates the talk as an extension of Sīla and Soul and says he is assuming that background throughout.
  2. 07:07 Why ethics matters now - He argues that ethics needs reformulation because the current cultural conversation has become too narrow and urgent problems are being missed.
  3. 14:02 Limits of law and rights - He critiques ethical systems that reduce morality to legality, rights, and competing claims, and he notes their historical and social limits.
  4. 20:23 What ethics needs - He lays out a wish list for ethics: not just guidance, but beauty, sacredness, a compass-like orientation, and a way of navigating life.
  5. 28:35 Beyond duty alone - He expands the list to include omission, emotion, love, eros, and a hierarchy of values, while insisting ethics must have a deeper structure.
  6. 37:11 XR and public rhetoric - Using Extinction Rebellion and airport responses, he shows how law-and-order language can hide the ethical stakes of climate action.
  7. 58:03 Omission and aspiration - He uses Nazi Germany, the five precepts, and the idea of sins of omission to argue that ethics must include what we fail to do and what we can aspire to.
  8. 01:16:38 Virtue, image, and ritual - He revisits earlier Soulmaking material, linking virtue to imaginal form, and reflects on how emotion and ritual can support or obscure ethical depth.
  9. 01:33:32 Eros as ethical guidance - He ends by exploring how sensing the soul, discipline, and eros might offer a new epistemology for ethics, one that is neither flatly objective nor merely subjective.
