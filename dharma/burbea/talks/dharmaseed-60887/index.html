Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Q & A, and Short Talk

Dharma Seed 1:44:54 2019-12-29

Can emotion, pain, and even bodily tightness become part of jhana practice without losing the thread? The talk moves through that question by stressing context and intention first, then widening into the distinction between sadness and being touched, the use of perception in working with pīti and sukha, and the danger of turning practice into a narrow project of comfort or control. Later, it shifts into a more philosophical register: what it means for jhana to depend on causes and conditions, what “radical” points to, and why the deeper gift is not just relief but a changing view of self, experience, and emptiness.

Chapters

  1. 04:12 Context and intention - Rob begins by framing emotional work inside the larger context of retreat, personal practice, and ordinary life, and says the retreat’s central intention must stay steady.
  2. 10:14 Tears and openness - He distinguishes sadness from being touched, moved, or open-hearted, and suggests that tears and sensitivity can be part of the path rather than signs of collapse.
  3. 15:26 What radical means - Rob explains his use of “radical” as root-level practice and root-level emptiness, then links that to wholehearted practice rather than half measures.
  4. 20:43 Steady practice and warning signs - The discussion turns to the need for a stable intention, the role of anger and joy, and how subtle aversion can quietly shape perception and self-view.
  5. 41:58 Jhana mastery and views - Rob addresses the apparent tension between causes and conditions and entering at will, using it to talk about mastery, self-view, and keeping a broad range of practice available.
  6. 01:05:30 Momentum and hidden locks - He describes how jhana practice can create momentum, then turns to three kinds of lock—view, emotional territory, and body—and how each needs a different kind of response.
  7. 01:17:10 Pain and perception - A question about working with pain opens into a deeper teaching on the dependent arising of perception, with Rob stressing that the real gift is insight into emptiness rather than relief alone.
  8. 01:21:14 Limits of bliss-making - Rob cautions against reducing practice to making everything pleasant, then revisits bodily holding, subtle aversion, and the possibility that locks reveal deeper patterns of mind and life.
  9. 01:34:14 Happiness beyond the cushion - In the closing stretch he widens the frame again, speaking about desire, liberation, and how jhana happiness can appear outside formal sitting without becoming the whole point of practice.
