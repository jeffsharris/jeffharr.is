Logos in the Garden of Souls (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 61:10 2017-02-02

What if practice needs a logos — a living conceptual framework — to hold it together, guide its navigation, and keep it responsive as experience changes? The talk moves from that basic claim into a series of analogies, then widens the frame to include fabrication, dependent origination, emptiness, and the many ways of looking that can shape what is seen and lived. From there it opens into eros, soulmaking, body, relationships, death, earth, beauty, and divinity, asking what kind of framework can include all of that without reducing it.

Chapters

  1. 00:34 Logos as support - He argues that every path needs a conceptual framework to hold practices together, guide navigation, and provide stability.
  2. 05:19 Trellis and tree - Using the images of a trellis and then a fruit tree, he shows how logos can be organic, integrated, responsive, and breakable.
  3. 12:00 Concept beyond thought - He clarifies that conceptuality is not just discursive thinking and is still operating even in refined meditative absorption.
  4. 19:12 Fabrication and emptiness - The talk turns to the spectrum of fabrication, dependent origination, emptiness, and the legitimacy of multiple ways of looking.
  5. 23:51 What the frame must hold - He broadens the needed framework to include imaginal life, eros, body, relationships, death, the earth, beauty, psychology, sacredness, and divinity.
  6. 36:33 History and conditioning - Concept and experience are described as mutually conditioning, and the framework is placed within history and society rather than treated as ahistorical truth.
  7. 39:14 Modern self and soulmaking - A retreat story, the construction of the modernist self, and the claim that fabrication can still be genuinely soulmaking are brought together.
  8. 44:44 Holy bodies and Christ - He uses the image of holding a loved one's feet, then turns to Christ and Paul to show how devotion can generate theology and a larger cosmology.
  9. 51:19 Plural knowing - The final movement gathers the need for a fertile, non-dogmatic framework that avoids epistemicide, includes dependent origination and emptiness, and leaves room for a broad range of embodied ways of knowing.
