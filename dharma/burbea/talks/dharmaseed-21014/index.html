Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Letting Go of Judgement

Dharma Seed 55:19 2006-10-17

Judgment is traced back to a basic habit of the mind: the way it turns experience into self-criticism, comparison, and pain. The talk moves from distinguishing judgment from discernment, to practical ways of challenging judgment through attention, appreciation, kindness, and seeing thoughts as passing events rather than identities. It closes by widening the frame further, toward conditions, the shared human roots of greed, aversion, and delusion, and finally a Zen pointer to letting go of preferences altogether.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the question - He introduces the topic of letting go of judgment and shows how common and painful the judging mind can be.
  2. 02:19 Practice and freedom - He connects self-judgment with blocked creativity and practice, then reframes dharma practice as freedom rather than self-improvement.
  3. 05:26 Judgment versus discernment - He defines discernment as a neutral clarity about what is helpful, and contrasts it with judgment, where the self gets involved.
  4. 10:20 Working with judgment - He offers ways to challenge judging in daily life, including remembering how little we know about others and deliberately turning toward their positive qualities.
  5. 14:53 Imagination and perspective - He presents imaginative techniques, including a Shantideva-inspired exercise, to reveal the futility of judging and the mystery of life as it is.
  6. 18:57 Metta and kindness - He emphasizes metta as a slow but powerful way to recondition the mind and encourages meeting the suffering behind judgment with compassion.
  7. 25:28 Views and self-making - He shows how interpretations harden into self-stories, and how practice can help us notice thoughts as views rather than identities.
  8. 31:20 Conditions over self - Through retreat stories and personal examples, he points to seeing facts, conditions, and qualities directly instead of turning them into fixed identities.
  9. 44:40 Beyond preferences - He closes with the Buddha’s three roots of unskillfulness and a passage from Faith in Mind, pointing toward a freedom that includes letting go of all preferences and polarity.
