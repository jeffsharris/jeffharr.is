Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

First Instructions and Guided Metta Meditation

Dharma Seed 56:10 2011-02-06

Beginning with posture, breath, and a light sense of the heart, the talk guides a metta practice that moves from settling into the whole body to offering simple phrases of well-wishing. It then opens outward to an easy person, with suggestions for using words, images, or body-sense in whatever way is most natural. The speaker keeps returning to patience, flexibility, and the reminder that kindness is planted gradually rather than forced.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Entering the practice - Burbea introduces the metta practice, settles listeners into posture, and asks for a comfortable, upright, relaxed seat.
  2. 04:32 Opening the field - Attention widens from the body to the whole sphere of experience, with an emphasis on warmth, receptivity, and being held.
  3. 10:11 Self-directed metta - He begins the phrases of self-well-wishing and stresses that the practice should be tender, patient, and without pressure.
  4. 19:10 Seeds and tenderness - A Buddha saying about the bucket being filled drop by drop supports the theme of gradual cultivation, and the guided self-practice continues.
  5. 26:23 The easiest person - The meditation shifts to someone it is easy to wish well for, with suggestions for using phrases, light, or simple imaginative contact.
  6. 34:04 Returning to openness - The image of the other person fades, and the practice returns to the whole body and a more open field of awareness.
  7. 36:29 What metta means - Burbea explains metta as deep friendliness and describes its unconditional and boundless ideal, while also introducing stepping stones and flexibility in practice.
  8. 40:10 Ways of practicing - He explores different modes of metta—phrases, visualization, body sense, devotion—and warns against turning the practice into a corrective agenda.
  9. 48:04 Patience and walking - The talk ends with an emphasis on not forcing results, trusting the gradual planting of seeds, and bringing metta into walking practice and everyday movement.
