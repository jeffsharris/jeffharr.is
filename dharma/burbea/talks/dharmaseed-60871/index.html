Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Metta Practice, and a few things about Piti (Instructions)

Dharma Seed 26:17 2019-12-20

Rob Burbea offers a practical talk on using metta as a springboard into jhana, while clarifying how that purpose differs from cultivating metta for its own sake. He then turns to piti: what it is, how it can arise in different ways, and how to work with it once it is present through spread, narrow probing, and open receiving. The talk stays close to practice, with repeated emphasis on sensitivity, attunement, enjoyment, and not forcing the process.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the frame - He introduces the talk in two parts and sets up the difference between practice instructions and a broader discussion of view and effort.
  2. 00:57 Metta as springboard - He distinguishes metta practiced for love from metta used to support jhana, emphasizing intention, sensitivity, and the shift toward piti or well-being.
  3. 04:56 Attunement and phrases - He expands on how to use phrases, volume, spacing, and bodily responsiveness so the practice stays alive and responsive rather than mechanical.
  4. 10:22 From metta to joy - He explains how metta can deepen into piti, and why the practitioner may need to discriminate between loving feeling and the pleasure of well-being.
  5. 11:51 What piti is - He gives a brief definition of piti as non-sensual pleasure or rapture, with examples ranging from tingling and warmth to lightness and vibration.
  6. 14:19 How piti arises - He surveys several conditions that can give rise to piti, from concentration and energy-body work to insight, open-heartedness, and even nature appreciation.
  7. 19:30 Let it pervade - Using the Buddha's language, he suggests that once piti appears, the practice is to see whether it can saturate the whole body.
  8. 21:04 Two modes of attention - He describes two complementary ways of attending to piti: narrow, penetrating attention and open, receptive surrender, moving back and forth between them.
  9. 25:07 Practice at the edge - He closes the first part by reassuring listeners that the work can be adapted to where they are, even if piti only appears locally or briefly.
