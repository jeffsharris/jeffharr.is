Approaching the Dharma: Part One - Unbinding the World

Dharma Seed 60:50 2012-11-18

The talk starts with a question about the “end of the world” and slowly turns that into a question about how perception itself is built, sustained, and sometimes undone. Moving through the Bahiya sutta, the speaker argues that “in the seen, just the seen” is not mainly a technique of bare attention, but a clue to how reactivity, selfing, measurement, and story keep experience in place. From there the talk widens into a careful inquiry into fabrication, emptiness, and the limits of any simple claim that either this world or a transcendent beyond is the final answer.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the arc - Rob sets up a two-part exploration and asks listeners to stay with a longer line of thought rather than trying to pin the teaching down quickly.
  2. 03:02 The world of perception - He begins with the Buddha's saying about the end of the world and uses it to point toward the world as it is actually perceived through the senses and mind.
  3. 08:54 Beyond elements and extremes - He reads passages about a realm where the elements have no footing and brings in Sariputta to show the teaching moving beyond binary positions.
  4. 13:34 Bahiya's urgency - The story of Bahiya searching for the Buddha leads into the famous instruction and the reminder that the teaching is not just a philosophy but something to be lived.
  5. 19:49 Just the seen - Rob argues that the Bahiya instruction is not simply bare attention, but a way of dropping labels, story, and reactivity so perception is less burdened by papanca.
  6. 28:18 Desire and fabrication - He gathers more Buddha passages on abandoning desire, ending ignorance, and ceasing fabrication to show how consciousness and perception quiet when grasping stops.
  7. 35:45 A spectrum of construction - Perception, self-sense, and dukkha are presented as mutually dependent and variable, not as all-or-nothing states.
  8. 41:38 Mirage and illusion - Rob turns to the Buddha's descriptions of phenomena as fabricated, mirage-like, and illusory, then adds the middle-way warning against reifying either existence or nonexistence.
  9. 49:09 What practice means - He returns to Bahiya and asks what it means to train this way in meditation and daily life, ending with a careful question about tendencies toward the 'transcendent' or the world of experience.
