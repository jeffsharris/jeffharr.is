Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Imaginal Practice: Doorways and Directions (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 59:28 2015-08-07

Rob Burbea introduces imaginal practice by mapping the many ways imagination already functions in Dharma, psychology, and culture: as planning, rehearsal, devotion, self-cultivation, and symbolic inquiry. He then turns to the historical suspicion of images in Western thought, contrasting that with a more open view in which images can help loosen fixed ideas of self, world, and practice. Toward the end, he names a deeper aim behind the retreat: not just using images skillfully, but allowing them to participate in a re-enchanting of experience.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the terrain - Rob sets out the purpose of the course and names imaginal practice as the topic, framing the talk as an introduction to its directions and doorways.
  2. 02:59 Traditional uses of image - He surveys practical uses of imagination, from planning and rehearsal to death contemplation in the Satipatthana Sutta and recollection of the Buddha.
  3. 06:22 Devotion and qualities - He gives examples of sacred imagery, tantric visualization, and mountain practice as ways imagination can evoke compassion, faith, courage, and groundedness.
  4. 09:42 Helpful and unhelpful fantasy - Rob distinguishes intentional imaginal work from ordinary mind-wandering and papancha, and points to upcoming work with samadhi and the energy body.
  5. 14:08 Images and frameworks - He argues that the image itself is less important than the relationship to it and the conceptual frame that gives it direction, emphasizing openness and trust.
  6. 20:42 Cultural suspicion of images - Rob traces a history of Western denigration of imagination through psychotherapy, doctrine, and philosophy, using figures like Socrates, Winnicott, Nicaea, and Corbin to show how image has been narrowed or dismissed.
  7. 36:03 Why people resist - He names common objections to imaginal practice, including fears of madness, conflict with accepted reality, tension with suffering-reduction as the only aim, and discomfort with stranger or darker images.
  8. 44:59 Plural selfhood - Rob describes what draws others in: a sense of multiple selves, the legitimacy of eros and wrath, and a looser self-view that can make emptiness easier to sense.
  9. 55:19 Re-enchanting the cosmos - He closes by linking imaginal practice to broader shifts in philosophy and tradition, including tantra and the midrashic condition, and names re-enchanting the cosmos as a deep reason for the retreat.
