Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Refractions: Of Body, Sensuality, and Sexuality (Part 2)

Dharma Seed 69:52 2017-02-14

How does sexuality change when it is no longer treated as a fixed fact, but as something shaped by view, culture, and imagination? The talk moves from reductionist ways of talking about sex and love, through stories of Dharma communities and inherited moral judgments, into a sustained reading of the Inanna and Dumuzi myth as a contrasting image of sacred, fertile lovemaking. From there it asks whether sex can be soulmaking rather than merely distracting, and whether a more open, less contracted way of perceiving can reveal holiness, dimensionality, and participation in the cosmic. The final movement turns to modern identity and Foucault, asking how sexuality became so tightly bound up with selfhood, and whether kindness and questioning can open a different relationship to it.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Sex as a view - The talk begins by framing sexuality as empty and dependently arisen, not a fixed thing independent of how it is seen.
  2. 04:17 Reduction and reaction - Rob gives examples of reductionist and dismissive views of love and sex, including scientific, moral, and Dharma contexts.
  3. 10:21 Inherited moral frames - He reflects on Christian and Buddhist moral inheritances and how they shape what people judge, hide, or condemn around sexuality.
  4. 14:07 Questioning the binaries - He challenges the idea that sex is simply a distraction or that one must choose between sensuality and the unfabricated.
  5. 22:46 Inanna and Dumuzi - A long mythic passage presents lovemaking as sacred, fertile, and participatory in something larger than the private self.
  6. 29:24 Cosmic sexuality - He interprets the Inanna material as a vision of sexuality tied to divinity, abundance, and the fertility of the world.
  7. 41:16 Sex and soulmaking - The talk turns to intentions, ego, pleasure, stress release, and the possibility that sex can become soulmaking through imaginal openness.
  8. 53:21 Foucault and identity - Rob uses Foucault to trace how modern culture made sexuality more central to identity, diagnosis, and self-scrutiny.
  9. 01:07:12 Kindness in inquiry - He ends by returning to the practical test of whether a way of relating is soulmaking and emphasizes kindness over rigidity.
