Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Contemplating The 3 Characteristics (Pt 1) - Impermanence and Dukkha

Dharma Seed 53:39 2005-11-12

Rob Burbea opens with the Buddha’s three characteristics and keeps returning to the practical question of how they change the way practice is done. He moves from impermanence into dukkha, showing how clinging, aversion, and the wish for satisfaction shape suffering, and then broadens the frame with poems, a Diamond Sutra image, and warnings about turning insight into rejection. In the later part of the talk he points toward a quieter, wider way of seeing: steady awareness, less grasping, a sense of space, and the possibility of a love that does not divide experience into favourites and enemies.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Opening the Three Characteristics - Burbea introduces impermanence, dukkha, and not-self as a practical lens for practice rather than a doctrine to memorize.
  2. 03:21 Why Practice Must Be Deliberate - He argues that mindfulness often needs an intentional theme, using Ajahn Chah to show how experience can be filed into the three characteristics.
  3. 06:56 Facing Impermanence - The talk turns to the difficulty of seeing change clearly, including the Diamond Sutra’s images of a fleeting world.
  4. 11:46 Grief, Loss, and Time - Burbea speaks openly about sadness, death, and everyday losses, and brings in Mary Oliver’s poem to deepen the felt sense of transience.
  5. 16:48 Working With Change - He offers practical ways to contemplate impermanence in meditation and daily life, from body sensations and mind states to the vast scale of time.
  6. 26:38 Dukkha and Letting Go - The emphasis shifts to unsatisfactoriness, showing how clinging and pushing away create subtle suffering and how release brings relief.
  7. 31:46 Avoiding Aversion - Burbea warns that contemplation can become rejection, and re-centers the practice as a way toward equanimity, spaciousness, and love.
  8. 39:44 Beyond the Conditioned - He brings in T.S. Eliot and St. John of the Cross to describe the deeper unsettledness that remains, then points to waiting, silence, and a freedom that does not depend on experience.
  9. 49:26 Closing Reflections - The talk ends by distinguishing relative freedom from the deeper freedom beyond self-story, with a brief closing silence.
