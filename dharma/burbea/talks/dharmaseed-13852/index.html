Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Working with the Emotional Body (Instructions and Guided Meditation: Day Four)

Dharma Seed 48:44 2011-08-02

How does a painful feeling change when it is held in a larger field? The talk moves from brief review of earlier emotional-body practices into a long guided meditation that starts with upright, soft posture, then opens from the center of the body to the whole body, to sound, silence, and spacious awareness. In the second half, the discussion returns to questions about fear, stillness, and why expanding the context can make contraction, pain, or thought feel less compelling; a Buddha salt-crystal metaphor makes that point concrete. The talk closes with practical suggestions for walking, nature, and community as ways to keep the field open.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Review and orientation - He briefly reviews the earlier exercises and emphasizes that the practices are non-linear and different for different people.
  2. 03:32 Posture and kindness - The guided meditation begins with uprightness, softness, and an explicit sense of practice as care and loving attention.
  3. 08:57 Centering the body - Attention returns to the center of the body with a light, curious holding of whatever is most prominent.
  4. 13:01 Opening to sound - Awareness expands from the body to listening, receiving sounds as a total field rather than one object after another.
  5. 19:35 Space and letting go - He invites a wide, 360-degree awareness in which sounds, sensations, and thoughts arise and dissolve into silence.
  6. 26:10 Including contraction - The teaching turns to allowing even contraction and discomfort to belong to the same open space, without making them personal.
  7. 35:05 Discussion and salt crystal - In discussion he responds to reports of fear and stillness, then uses the Buddha’s salt crystal image to explain how context changes the felt impact of pain.
  8. 43:45 Choosing the larger context - He answers a question about staying interested in spacious awareness and ends with practical encouragement for walking, nature, and experimenting in daily practice.
