What is an 'Image'? (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 1:18:04 2015-08-09

What is an image when it is not just a picture in the mind, but something that can carry feeling, direction, and depth? The talk moves through ordinary uses of imagination, then into examples from metta, deity practice, meditation sounds, and the body, before widening into poetic and imaginal images that are ambiguous, resonant, and not meant to be decoded. By the end, the speaker is making a careful distinction between a vivid image that is merely striking and one that feels soulful, generative, and worth returning to.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the topic - He frames the talk as an attempt to clarify what he means by image, fantasy, and mythos, and recalls the earlier retreat context.
  2. 01:46 Familiar uses of imagination - He surveys ordinary and Buddhist uses of imagination, from death contemplation and metta to deity imagery and recollection of the Buddha.
  3. 06:37 Relationship and healing - He notes psychotherapeutic uses of images and stresses that healing comes through relationship rather than turning away from inner figures.
  4. 09:33 Not just visual - He broadens the definition of image to include sound and body-sense, with examples of the nada sound and bodily or felt images.
  5. 16:27 Dreams, music, and dialogue - Through a dream of music under threat and examples of inner dialogue, he shows how images can orient devotion and need not be treated as literal objects.
  6. 24:04 Ibn Arabi and presence - He uses Ibn Arabi to suggest that imaginal figures may disclose themselves through presence rather than verbal exchange.
  7. 26:16 Poetic images - He develops the idea of soulful, ambiguous, inexhaustible images, contrasting them with decipherable symbols and illustrating this with Rumi and Mary Oliver.
  8. 37:31 Iconic and narrative - He distinguishes narrative images from more timeless iconic ones, showing how images may be repeated, revisited, and gradually reshape sensibility.
  9. 53:33 Archetypal value - He closes by discussing images that feel archetypal in Hillman’s sense, then returns to dependence, emptiness, and the importance of a relational way of seeing.
