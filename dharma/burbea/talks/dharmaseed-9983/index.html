Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Metta Instructions and Guided Meditation 1

Dharma Seed 53:22 2010-08-07

Rob Burbea introduces a metta practice centered on self-kindness and the benefactor, with a strong emphasis on flexibility rather than formula. He explains how to work with phrases, imagery, and body-sense, and why the practice is more about planting intentions than manufacturing a feeling. The talk then moves into a guided meditation, first for oneself and then for a benefactor, with guidance on walking practice at the end.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Metta and Boundlessness - Burbea reviews metta as unconditional and boundless, and explains the day’s structure of beginning with self and benefactor.
  2. 03:05 Finding the Benefactor - He defines the benefactor as simply the easiest person to wish well, rather than necessarily a heroic figure.
  3. 05:05 Playful Practice - The talk opens up the range of methods, encouraging creativity and introducing phrases, visualization, and body-sense.
  4. 08:27 Working with Phrases - He offers sample phrases, warns against turning them into a corrective agenda, and stresses finding words that genuinely fit.
  5. 11:46 Feeling and Intention - Burbea explains that metta feelings are impermanent and that the deeper work is to plant seeds of intention with patience and faith.
  6. 16:41 Self Metta Guided - The guided practice begins with settling the body, centering awareness, and offering phrases of well-wishing to oneself.
  7. 23:56 Self Kindness Continues - The meditation continues with repeated phrases, tenderness toward the body, and reassurance that the feeling itself may come and go.
  8. 33:23 Turning to the Benefactor - He shifts from self to benefactor, inviting an easy image of someone who naturally evokes gratitude and kindness.
  9. 46:55 Imagery and Walking - The talk closes with refinements on visual and kinesthetic approaches, then offers ways to continue metta in walking meditation and daily body practice.
