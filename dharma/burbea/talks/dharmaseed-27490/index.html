Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Image, Mythos, Dharma (Part One)

Dharma Seed 41:19 2014-12-07

What does it mean when an inner image is not just a passing picture, but a living field of love, fear, memory, and meaning? The talk moves through a series of examples — a thicket and blue bird, a black devil figure, a train ride, a phoenix, a monastery in Thailand, Gaia House itself — to show how image, fantasy, and mythos can shape both practice and perception. From there, it turns toward the danger of flattening these experiences into fixed symbols, and toward the question of how Dharma practice might meet this subtler, more open-ended territory.

Chapters

  1. 03:52 Opening the territory - He introduces image, fantasy, and mythos as a broad field of practice and explains that he will use examples rather than strict definitions.
  2. 05:46 Blue bird and thicket - A personal meditation image of a thicket and blue bird shows how imaginal material can be subtle, moving, and not reducible to interpretation.
  3. 09:08 Devil, train, and change - A frightening recurring figure becomes an ally over years, followed by an ordinary train image that reveals fatigue, restlessness, and underlying peace.
  4. 13:11 Embodied tradition - He uses music and the feel of the instrument to show how image can be tactile, kinetic, and infused by lineage.
  5. 15:38 Phoenix and healing - A phoenix image is linked to shame around an old relationship and shows how an imaginal figure can reframe memory with love.
  6. 18:14 Fantasy in life and place - He broadens the discussion to shared fantasy in relationships, then to Gaia House and a Thai monastery as places made vivid through love and narrative.
  7. 24:20 Visionary seeing - William Blake and tantric visualization are used to show imagination as a deliberate way of perceiving the world.
  8. 28:55 Condensed meaning - Drawing on Ezra Pound and John Keats, he describes image as compressed emotional-intellectual complexity and soul-making through relationship.
  9. 34:22 Literalism and reality - He warns against literalizing archetypal images, argues that images are not flat symbols, and distinguishes individual truth from public certainty.
