Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Sensitivity, Receptivity, and Respect in Soul Work (Q & A)

Dharma Seed 65:52 2018-06-26

In this Q&A, Rob Burbea explores how imaginal practice can be understood through the difference between narrative images and more timeless, iconic ones. He then turns to autonomy, receptivity, and respect: how not to force images, how to work with disappointment when intensity fades, and how eros and inspiration can deepen practice over time. The discussion widens into long-term devotion, the way life itself can become imaginal, and a reflective play with causality and participation.

Chapters

  1. 01:14 Narrative and iconic images - He defines the difference between images that unfold in time and images that feel timeless, and invites listeners to notice how each affects self-sense and practice.
  2. 06:58 The Dartmoor example - Using a brief image from a walk on Dartmoor, he shows how the mind can add a story that the image itself does not contain.
  3. 08:23 The concertina field - He describes a field of available images around a person or situation, like a concertina, and explains how one image may be selected without excluding the others.
  4. 12:26 Intensity and jhāna - He responds to disappointment after a powerful opening, warning against equating intensity with truth or transformation, and comparing imaginal work to the subtleties that emerge after initial jhānic breakthroughs.
  5. 20:42 Experiment without judgment - He encourages a playful, experimental stance toward images, learning by trying things and noticing what feels more soulmaking rather than treating the process as a matter of right and wrong.
  6. 25:43 Eros and inspiration - He connects eros with inspiration and artistic work, describing how desire spreads through self and world and can make even the practitioner become an image.
  7. 36:11 Devotion through difficulty - He reflects on long-term projects such as writing a book, showing how perseverance, dryness, and difficulty can also become imaginal objects.
  8. 40:43 Life seen from beyond death - He suggests looking back on a life from beyond death, so periods of aridity and devotion can be seen as part of a larger pilgrimage.
  9. 49:38 Autonomy and causality - He closes by revisiting the autonomy of images, the way they can be both me and not me, and a poetic reversal in which life may express images rather than only being caused by the past.
