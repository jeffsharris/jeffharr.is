Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Attitude, Effort, Achievement, and View

Dharma Seed 69:47 2019-12-20

What if the real issue in practice is not how hard you try, but the pressure, self-judgment, and narrow view shaping that effort? The talk moves from the meaning of samadhi, to the problem of achievement timetables, to a slower, subtler question of how attention, attitude, and view either tighten or open the field. From there it widens into a practical reframe: quality over quantity, balanced effort, and eventually jhana as a kind of playing with perception rather than a state to seize.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the Frame - Rob introduces the talk’s main questions and invites a wider, less cramped way of understanding effort, attitude, and view.
  2. 02:05 Pressure and Narrowness - He describes how practice can become over-pressured, using his own retreat experience and a teacher’s warning to show how narrowing the field can block samadhi.
  3. 05:17 Widening Practice - The talk turns to taking pressure off the body and broadening the view so that mindfulness, patience, resolve, and self-judgment are all seen as part of what is being developed.
  4. 09:49 Goals Without Tightening - He examines the tension between having aspirations and getting trapped by achievement timetables, arguing that goals can be held without making practice miserable.
  5. 16:50 Quality of Attention - Rob emphasizes wholeheartedness, delicacy, and adjustable intensity, and suggests that the real question is the quality of attention rather than sheer duration or force.
  6. 25:09 Imaginal Breath - He opens a more poetic register, citing a broader traditional use of imagery in breathing practice and noting that even subtle imaginal coloring can change the whole experience.
  7. 28:55 Dropping the Snatch - The talk critiques fretting over attainment and explains balanced effort through the Buddha’s similes of the quail and the lute, where too much or too little pressure both distort practice.
  8. 39:58 Jhana, Eros, and Self - Rob develops a subtler view of jhana, energy, and effort, then brings in soulmaking language to show how the self on the path can be nourishing rather than punitive.
  9. 54:49 Perception and Emptiness - The closing section reframes jhana as perception-attainment, describes meditation as working with fabrication, and connects that shift directly to emptiness and the wider Dharma path.
