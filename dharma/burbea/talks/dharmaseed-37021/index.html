Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Gift and the Artifice of Self (Part 3)

Dharma Seed 45:09 2016-08-01

What if meaningfulness is not something added to life, but something that lets a self come alive? The talk moves from Einstein and Jung on the necessity of meaning, into a delicate argument that images, duties, and ways of seeing must be embodied if they are to stay alive rather than remain fascinating ideas. From there it widens into a series of poetic and philosophical touchstones—Hammarskjöld, Chaim Vital, Nietzsche, Orthodox theosis, Kabbalah, and Buddhist practice—to show how the self can be re-seen as open-ended, multiple, and capable of loving its fate without flattening suffering or difference.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Meaning and the self - Burbea begins by linking enchantment of self to meaningfulness, using Einstein and Jung to frame meaning as central to human well-being.
  2. 02:49 Images need embodiment - He argues that beauty, sacredness, and imaginal experience only become real when they are integrated through embodiment, duty, and life.
  3. 07:30 Yes to the more-than-human - Dag Hammarskjöld’s diary and a Latin phrase are used to suggest that meaning can arise from answering something larger than the human.
  4. 11:37 Many roots, many worlds - The talk turns to Chaim Vital and the idea of multiple roots, then to the Torah as read differently in each world, widening the frame of interpretation.
  5. 16:53 Loving fate and divinity - Burbea develops Amor Fati, Nietzsche’s eternal recurrence, and the possibility of becoming divine as a way of loving life without flattening its pain.
  6. 23:50 Seeing truly - He brings in Christian and Buddhist language about fallen perception, authentic seeing, and theosis to show perception itself as transformable.
  7. 30:08 Creating and shaping God - Kabbalistic themes of prayer, Shekhinah, and tikkun ha-olam lead into a radical suggestion that practice can both manifest and shape the divine.
  8. 36:15 Levels of self - Burbea gathers Plotinus, Vajrayana, and related traditions to argue for layered dimensions of being and a trainable way of seeing self and others as Buddha or deity.
  9. 41:31 Infinite soulmaking - He ends by describing how even the smallest act can carry infinite resonance when the conceptual frame allows ongoing soulmaking, beauty, and sacredness.
