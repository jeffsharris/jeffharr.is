Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Unshakeable Heart (Part 2)

Dharma Seed 30:55 2008-10-05

How does equanimity grow when suffering is already here? The talk keeps returning to the way the mind gets pulled into pain, then shows how compassion, loving-kindness, and a softer relationship to unpleasant feeling can create a wider inner space. From there it turns to karma, praise and blame, and the self-sense itself, arguing that less aversion and less identification mean less is being built in experience, until the talk closes with the image of the house-builder being seen and ended.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Warmth Before Equanimity - Equanimity is framed as something that can begin with accepting non-equanimity and meeting suffering with compassion.
  2. 01:54 Mind Sucked Into Suffering - He describes how the mind gets absorbed by difficulty, and why small practices and attention to vedana still matter.
  3. 04:04 Not Underestimating Practice - The talk pauses over the value of revisiting instructions and the risk of turning Dharma into another reason for self-blame.
  4. 06:58 Karma Without Simplification - Burbea challenges crude karma explanations and presents a more conditional view in which past and present meet, especially around pain and aversion.
  5. 10:52 Generosity Changes the World - Generosity and loving-kindness are shown to soften suffering, widen experience, and loosen the sense of self.
  6. 15:57 Praise, Blame, and Identity - Using praise and blame, he examines how selfing contracts around the eight worldly conditions and how reflection can reopen that contraction.
  7. 18:56 Seeing Through Self - The talk turns to the self as the hinge of worldly conditions and to a deliberate practice of seeing experience as not-self.
  8. 25:06 Letting Dualities Unwind - He reflects on delusion, investment in opposites, and the need to let go of both ends of a polarity rather than only the painful side.
  9. 28:29 House-Building Ends - The Buddha’s awakening and the house-builder poem summarize the path as a gradual ending of craving, aversion, and selfing.
