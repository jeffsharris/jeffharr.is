Dilemmas and Delineations: How did we get <em>here</em>?! (Part 1)

Dharma Seed 1:17:20 2017-01-18

What if eros is not a problem to eliminate, but a living force that can be clarified, studied, and even sacralized? The talk begins by asking why this word needs to be distinguished at all, then moves through a teachers’ discussion, a long example of a practitioner wrestling with love and monastic life, and a wider argument that desire is already part of the Dharma’s terrain. By the end, the speaker is sketching a more elaborate practice map in which eros, soulmaking, and emptiness can be held together without collapsing into either craving or renunciation.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Setting the Inquiry - The talk opens by placing eros inside a larger retreat arc and explaining why it needs careful definition.
  2. 03:40 Why Context Matters - Rob frames the discussion within insight meditation, then recalls a broader teaching context that makes the topic harder and more interesting.
  3. 10:02 Many Meanings of Eros - He reviews how different teachers used eros to mean desire, life, or a counterpart to death, showing how little agreement there was.
  4. 13:56 Desire on the Path - He argues that desire is not simply a problem in Buddhism, citing the four bases of power and the Buddha’s own intensity of aspiration.
  5. 18:14 Previous Explorations - Rob points listeners to earlier talks on desire, effort, and the ecology of love, preparing the ground for a more developed account here.
  6. 30:17 A Monk’s Dilemma - A long email from a young monk in love becomes the central example for why eros cannot be handled by simple renunciation or suppression.
  7. 44:48 Eros Beyond Sex - He defines eros as broader than sexuality, linked with soulfulness, divinity, cosmopoiesis, and a field of inquiry that can sacralize desire.
  8. 52:50 Relationship and Practice - The talk turns to romantic love, long-term relationship, and the need to understand how eros lives, weakens, or deepens over time.
  9. 01:05:24 Toward a Fuller Psychology - Rob places eros alongside craving, emptiness, and non-fabrication, arguing for a more elaborate Dharma psychology that can open new dimensions of experience.
