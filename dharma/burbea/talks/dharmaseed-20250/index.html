Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

The Dharma Of Global Climate Change

Dharma Seed 60:01 2013-07-30

Global climate change is approached here as a question of how we meet what is already happening: with denial and business as usual, or by turning toward reality. The talk moves from the smoke-in-a-room experiment to Buddhist teachings on craving, contentment, interdependence, and wrong view, showing how a sense of separate self feeds both overconsumption and ecological harm. Along the way, it brings in the Buddha, Atisha, and later voices like David Korten and Joanna Macy to ask what kind of response can come from clearer seeing rather than fear. It ends by framing climate change not only as a crisis, but as a demanding opportunity for awakening and a change in how we live with one another and the earth.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Opening the Topic - The talk begins by framing climate change as something to turn toward carefully, rather than avoid, and by setting up the Dharma questions that will guide the rest of the evening.
  2. 04:15 Smoke and Social Freeze - A room-smoke experiment is used to show how people can ignore clear danger when others act as if nothing is wrong, much like the cultural freeze around climate change.
  3. 06:50 Why It Matters - He explains why climate change is not just a political matter, but a moral and practical one that affects everyone, though not equally, and invites a conscious response.
  4. 11:55 Craving and Contentment - The talk turns to the Second Noble Truth, linking craving for sense pleasure to both suffering and ecological strain, then introducing Atisha's teaching on contentment.
  5. 19:46 Possession and Stewardship - He questions the assumption that land and resources can really be owned, and suggests that a stewardship view would naturally lead to less harm and more responsibility.
  6. 25:05 Interdependence and Story - Dependent arising, social narratives, and David Korten's warning about stories out of tune with reality are used to show how collective habits shape collective outcomes.
  7. 29:12 Cause, Effect, and Avoidance - Everyday examples of consumption, travel, and waste illustrate cause and effect, followed by Bob Doppelt's account of blame and avoidance and a return to the inner forces Dharma can address.
  8. 40:32 Mind and Separate Self - The Dhammapada and Ajahn Chah are used to point back to mind as the place where suffering and clarity are made, and Dilgo Khyentse Rinpoche frames ignorance and separation as root causes.
  9. 48:01 From Fear to Awakening - The ending turns toward Joanna Macy's 'great turning,' Nyoshul Khenpo's nondual language, and Jennifer Welwood's poem, presenting climate change as a demanding but potent field for awakening and compassion.
