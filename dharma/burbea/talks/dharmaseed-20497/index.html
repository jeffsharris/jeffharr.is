Rob Burbea Dharma Talks

Follow Your Heartbreak

Dharma Seed 51:12 2013-08-22

What if the heartbreak of seeing the world clearly is not a detour from practice, but part of the path itself? Drawing on Andrew Harvey’s sacred activism, the talk moves from a sobering view of global crisis into the suggestion that collective upheaval and retreat life follow the same pattern: dissolution, fear, letting go, and eventually a more spacious balance. Along the way, the speaker brings in the Kali Yuga, the progress of insight, the hero’s journey, and images from Buddha, Jesus, and Star Wars to show how breaking down can become a passage to wakefulness and action.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Opening and retreat context - James begins with a brief check-in about his self-retreat and the value of both solitude and practice with others.
  2. 03:06 Andrew Harvey's framing - He introduces Andrew Harvey’s book on sacred activism and explains why it has been shaping his own thinking and reading during retreat.
  3. 07:11 Dark night of the species - James uses Kali Yuga and Harvey’s language of collective decline to describe a troubled age that may also be ripe for transformation.
  4. 13:13 Practice through hardship - He turns inward, describing the ups and downs of retreat and arguing that difficulty is part of how courage, compassion, and verified faith are formed.
  5. 20:52 Progress of insight - James sketches the classical Theravada map from dissolution through fear, misery, and disgust toward equanimity and release.
  6. 28:56 The hero's journey - He connects the progress of insight with Joseph Campbell’s hero myth and examples from the Buddha, Jesus, Star Wars, and The Hunger Games.
  7. 33:21 Heartbreak and sacred activism - Returning to Andrew Harvey, James says that both personal and global transformation require a willingness to let the heart break open.
  8. 37:19 Signs of awakening - He ends with encouraging examples of global movements for justice and the possibility that consciousness can outpace fear.
