Alan Watts

Mysticism and Morals

KPFA Archive 18:32 1960-09-09

Alan Watts begins by separating mystical experience from any manufactured belief in harmony, then builds a case for mysticism as a sudden sense that self and world are one. From there he turns to morals, arguing that rules are social agreements, but that real ethics depends on inward honesty rather than forced feelings or outward compliance. The talk widens through biblical, institutional, and domestic examples before settling on a final distinction: the mystic sees the divinity of what is, while moral life begins by admitting what one genuinely feels.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Defining Mysticism - Watts defines mysticism as a real state of consciousness rather than a philosophy, emphasizing its suddenness, its sense of unity with the world, and its conviction that everything fits.
  2. 04:54 False Harmony - He warns against pseudo-mysticism, especially attempts to manufacture harmony by belief alone, and insists that genuine mystical insight cannot be forced or willed into place.
  3. 11:12 Religion as Play - Watts shifts into a broader metaphysical register, using a Gospel saying and then the Hindu idea of the world as drama to show how human striving, anxiety, and play all belong to the same cosmic performance.
  4. 20:16 Morals as Rules - He defines morals as social conventions like grammar, then contrasts ordinary enforcement with the heavier authority of religion, law, heaven, and hell.
  5. 25:24 Mystics and Priests - Watts uses glossolalia, a church anecdote, and Bishop Pike's response to show how institutions tolerate openness in theory but resist spontaneous spiritual experience in practice.
  6. 29:08 Inward Law - He argues, with Jeremiah and Luther, that real morality is inward and that trying to command inner feeling only produces hypocrisy and brittle public virtue.
  7. 36:53 Honesty in Practice - Through examples of business talk and his recommendation of Jung, Watts shows that moral clarity comes from admitting motives and limitations without self-righteousness.
  8. 47:13 Feeling Without Pretence - Watts develops his core ethical claim: there are no wrong feelings, only wrong actions, and honesty about resentment, sadness, or fear is more humane than forced sweetness.
  9. 56:41 Final Synthesis - He closes by joining the mystical and moral arguments: the mystic sees the divinity of whatever is, while ethics begins with the truthful recognition of one's actual feeling.
