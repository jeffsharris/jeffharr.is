Alan Watts

Parallel Thoughts, East and West

KPFA Archive 57:10 1960-04-22

The talk opens by framing East and West as if they have been making parallel discoveries, using science, philosophy, psychology, and linguistics to show how similar ideas can arise independently. It then moves into a longer argument about bodily awareness and social conditioning, with a close look at Elsa Gindler’s work and Charlotte Selva’s accounts of learning to trust what the body actually senses. By the end, the speaker widens the point into a critique of rigid norms and a plea to work with nature rather than against it.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Parallel Beginnings - Watts introduces the idea that Eastern philosophy and modern Western thought have arrived at similar insights independently.
  2. 02:30 Science and Language - He uses simultaneous discovery, Wittgenstein, and Korzybski to show how Western thinking began distinguishing words from reality.
  3. 05:17 Psychology and Biology - Watts extends the parallels into psychotherapy and organismic biology, with Jung, Rogers, Needham, and Whitehead as key examples.
  4. 07:58 Toward Taoist Practice - He sets up the shift from abstract parallels to a practical Western form of Tao-like responsiveness to the body.
  5. 14:25 Elsa Gindler's Method - Watts tells Gindler's story of illness, forest retreat, and recovery through attentive non-interference with bodily process.
  6. 18:29 Labels and Conditioning - Through Charlotte Selva's account, he criticizes the habit of classifying people and shows how social correction can deform natural movement.
  7. 26:07 Listening Inward - Watts explains how the work asks for rest, quiet, and a direct trust in one's own inner sense rather than authority.
  8. 30:36 Sanity with Nature - He closes by arguing that sanity means working with the grain of the world, not against it, even when that requires intelligence and restraint.
