Alan Watts

The Constitution of Nature

KPFA Archive 28:03 1960-06-17

The talk sets out three historical ways of imagining nature: as a construct, as a drama, and as an organism. It starts with the Western habit of seeing the world as made from outside, moves through Indian and Chinese alternatives, and then weighs what each view makes possible or distorts. The final section turns practical, arguing that the mechanical outlook has helped science and technology but also left modern life fragmented, psychologically strained, and too dependent on specialization. It closes by looking toward a more organic, relational way of thinking that could better fit biology, ecology, and the world’s increasing complexity.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Three Views of Nature - Watts introduces the talk’s central triad and explains why hidden analogies shape common sense.
  2. 01:55 Nature as Construct - He develops the Western picture of the world as made, formed, and organized from outside, drawing on Genesis and the clay-and-pot image.
  3. 05:27 Machine and Parts - The construct view becomes the mechanical model, tied to calculus, reduction into parts, and the success of Western technology.
  4. 08:08 The World as Drama - Watts turns to India, where reality is framed as divine play, and uses Campbell, Genesis, and the Upanishads to contrast eastern and western mythic directions.
  5. 12:40 The Organic View - He introduces the Chinese sense of nature as self-moving and self-ordering, without a separate maker behind it.
  6. 16:00 Costs of Mechanism - Watts argues that the mechanical picture eventually alienates people from nature and even from themselves.
  7. 19:52 Specialization and Complexity - He shifts to the technical limits of reductionism, showing how specialization and growing complexity make control harder.
  8. 23:13 Toward a New Practical Philosophy - The talk closes by suggesting that ecology, biology, and overlooked forms of intelligence may point toward a more adequate future view.
