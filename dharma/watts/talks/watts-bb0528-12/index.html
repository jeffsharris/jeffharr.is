Alan Watts

Discussion of Buddhist Mysticism

KPFA Archive 30:39 1960-03-18

The talk begins with a mention of D. T. Suzuki’s newly published book on Christian and Buddhist mysticism, then quickly turns to a sustained account of Shin, or Pure Land, Buddhism and its contrast with self-powered spiritual effort. From there, the speaker develops the idea of tariki through the vow of Amitabha and argues that what looks like passivity can also be read as a deeper release from anxious self-improvement. The second half shifts into a string of Ryokan anecdotes—playful, comic, and oddly moving—that put the teaching into lived form, ending with a portrait of a person who is at ease without making an effort to seem so.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Suzuki as springboard - Watts mentions Suzuki’s new book and makes clear that he is using it to reach a larger theme rather than reviewing it.
  2. 00:35 The easy way - He introduces Shinshu, or Pure Land Buddhism, as a popular form that seems easier than other Buddhist paths.
  3. 02:10 Amitabha’s vow - Watts recounts the Pure Land promise of rebirth through faith in Amitabha’s name and the language of spiritual rescue by vow.
  4. 07:45 Against self-power - He sharpens the contrast between jiriki and tariki, treating self-improvement as a bootstrap problem and reframing reliance on another power as trust in something deeper.
  5. 11:28 The Myokonin character - Watts defines the Shin ideal as an honest, self-aware, humorous person whose acceptance of himself becomes the ground of compassion.
  6. 15:59 Zen and futility - He briefly brings in Zen, suggesting that even strenuous discipline can lead to the discovery that egoic transformation cannot be forced.
  7. 17:12 Ryokan in anecdotes - A long sequence of Ryokan stories—tiger play, letters, persimmons, money, and simple wit—shows the doctrine embodied in childlike freedom and unforced kindness.
  8. 24:48 Possessions and humor - Watts ends the Ryokan portrait with his list of belongings, his tobacco pouch, and the barber story, emphasizing plainness, reciprocity, and comic directness.
  9. 29:55 The final turn - He closes by saying that such a life is hardest to describe and that the only real imitation is not to imitate at all, but to be oneself.
