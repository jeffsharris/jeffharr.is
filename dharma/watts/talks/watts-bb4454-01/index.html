Alan Watts

The Symbolic and The Real

KPFA Archive 60:22 1960-09-16

Alan Watts opens by distinguishing the symbolic world from the real, using money, words, and counting as his first examples. From there he builds a wide argument about consciousness as focused attention, showing how the mind creates the feeling of a separate ego and a fearful, time-bound self. The talk then widens into a series of analogies—Rorschach blot, circle and hole, space and solid, tree and fruit—to argue that inside and outside, self and world, only make sense together.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the problem - Watts introduces the subject and frames the talk as a distinction between the symbolic and the real.
  2. 01:02 Money and words - He uses money, shopping, and a quarter to show how symbols stand in for wealth and reality.
  3. 02:51 What symbols do - He explains how language and naming create a shared symbolic world and make human culture possible.
  4. 07:20 Time, death, and anxiety - Using Eden and the tree of knowledge, he links symbolic knowledge to foresight, mortality, and the human feeling of melancholy.
  5. 14:33 Consciousness as spotlight - Watts turns to attention, showing how consciousness isolates what seems significant and leaves most of life outside the beam.
  6. 20:30 Rorschach and the circle - He argues that the world is partly shaped by projection and that every inside implies an outside.
  7. 28:45 Space is not nothing - Through painting and architecture, he shows that background, space, and context are active parts of form.
  8. 31:38 The cosmos in focus - Watts enlarges the view, suggesting each organism is a local expression of the whole universe rather than a sealed ego.
  9. 37:32 Leaves, not visitors - He closes by contrasting accidental exile with organic belonging, then says symbolic analysis must be balanced by lived identity with the whole.
