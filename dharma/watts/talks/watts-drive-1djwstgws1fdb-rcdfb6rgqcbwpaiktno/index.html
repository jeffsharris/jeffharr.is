Alan Watts

Seeing Security Everywhere

Google Drive Uploads 1:01:16 2026-05-22

This talk begins with a personal preamble about returning from retreat, grief, caretaking, and the difficulty of speaking from a place that still feels in process. From there, the speaker turns to a sutta on fear, strife, and the wish for a haven, using it to trace a path from ethical conduct to concentration, insight, and the steady release of grasping. The talk keeps circling back to the same point in different forms: practice is not a linear ascent, but an interwoven training in how the world conditions the mind and how the mind learns to respond without clinging. Near the end, the focus shifts to the ordinary sense doors, the problem of “me and mine,” and the possibility of seeing security without relying on abstraction.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening in Retreat - The talk begins with a quiet preamble about retreat, loss, and the difficulty of speaking clearly from an unfinished inner place.
  2. 06:50 What the Sutta Asks - He introduces the sutta and frames bhavana, or mind culture, as the central question linking inner life, conduct, and insight.
  3. 09:07 Ethics and Cultivation - The discussion moves through generosity, ethical conduct, and mind training as mutually supporting parts of the path rather than separate stages.
  4. 20:29 Fear and Competition - Watts reads the opening of the sutta where the Buddha sees terror in a world of strife and looks for a haven beyond possession and rivalry.
  5. 24:25 Training for Unbinding - He lingers on the injunction not to cling to what is tied down in the world, then shows how speech, conduct, and the hindrances are woven together.
  6. 31:26 Attention and Jhana - The talk turns to the practical mechanics of practice: aiming, sustaining, calming, and the way attention itself begins to break the cycle of reactivity.
  7. 38:32 Conceit and the Raft - Watts uses repeated Buddha passages to show that conceit, greed, and other bindings must be directly known, while the raft of practice carries one across the flood.
  8. 44:52 Truth Made Concrete - He argues that impermanence and suffering are not abstractions but lived facts seen through the sense doors, and that peace comes from relinquishment, not theory.
  9. 53:26 No Mine, No Grasping - The final movement returns to the six sense doors, the sense of me and mine, and the possibility of seeing security everywhere through steady, ordinary practice.
