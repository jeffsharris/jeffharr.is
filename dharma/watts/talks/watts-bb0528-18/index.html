Alan Watts

A Problem of Strategy

KPFA Archive 24:38 1960-04-15

The talk begins by rejecting the idea that military strategy is too technical for philosophy, then pivots quickly to the nuclear standoff between the United States and Russia. From there it argues, bluntly and at length, that atomic weapons are not only morally disastrous but strategically self-defeating, because they destroy the very control war is supposed to secure. The second half turns to alternatives: a non-lethal "peace gas" example, electronic disruption, and finally a judo-like strategy of yielding at the top level while organizing civil disobedience, sabotage, and guerrilla resistance below.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Philosophy Enters Strategy - Watts opens by defending the philosopher's right to intervene when technical problems become too large for technocrats alone.
  2. 01:37 The Nuclear Deadlock - He frames the U.S.-Russia confrontation as an immediate threat and argues that ordinary moral appeals do not offer a usable near-term answer.
  3. 03:08 War's Real Objective - Watts separates the aims of war and politics from the current habit of pure destruction, calling atomic weapons a military blunder as well as a moral one.
  4. 06:00 Deterrence Is Not Control - He explains why the H-bomb cannot actually secure control, and why appeals to moral transformation are too slow for the present crisis.
  5. 08:07 Toward Useful Weapons - Watts shifts to expediency and suggests that research should be redirected toward weapons that restrain, immobilize, or disable rather than annihilate.
  6. 11:44 Wells and New Tactics - He cites H. G. Wells and imagines peaceful or disabling technologies, then extends the argument to electronic systems and surprise attack without mass killing.
  7. 16:55 King Hall and Judo - Watts introduces Commander Stephen King Hall and develops judo as the model for letting an aggressor overreach instead of meeting force head-on.
  8. 20:13 Civil Resistance Strategy - He closes by outlining a layered defense of civil disobedience, sabotage, and guerrilla resistance as a more workable answer than atomic exchange.
