Alan Watts

Answering Questions of Requests from Listeners

KPFA Archive 32:52 1960-08-05

This episode moves through listener questions with a loose but focused arc: first correcting easy assumptions about Eastern religion and compassion, then widening into a critique of religious identity, labels, and the cost of belonging. From there Watts turns to Judaism, Buber, and Hasidic story traditions before pushing into a more psychological argument about authority, fear, motivation, and what it means to be a person at all. He ends by folding criticism itself back into a larger view, so the talk closes less with a verdict than with a reminder that every judgment is only one part of the field.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Questions and assumptions - Watts opens by explaining the question-and-answer format and why transcripts are not central to the live work of the programs.
  2. 02:53 Eastern compassion - He answers the common charge that Eastern religion is passive by describing ahimsa, Buddhist concern for others, and Ashoka’s practical care for suffering people.
  3. 04:44 Christian charity and poverty - Watts compares Asian and Western social conscience, arguing that older Christian culture often accepted hierarchy and treated the poor as permanent objects of charity.
  4. 07:35 Buber and religious labels - He takes up Martin Buber, then broadens the reply into a critique of being defined first by Jewish, Christian, Catholic, or Buddhist identity.
  5. 14:08 Hasidic stories - Watts pauses on Buber’s Hasidic translations and praises their short, humane anecdotes as spiritually vivid and close in spirit to Zen.
  6. 15:08 God as father - He turns to monotheism and the image of an authoritarian God, contrasting it with Lao Tzu’s non-dominating Tao and with the example of his own father.
  7. 17:49 Fear and morality - Watts argues that a childlike fear of divine authority keeps people from growing up, and that morality based on fear is less open than morality based on love.
  8. 22:00 Motivation and process - He asks what life is for beyond mere survival, then rejects the split between inner motive and outer action by treating person and environment as one process.
  9. 27:39 Criticism and perspective - The closing remarks return to cultural dullness, ugliness, and criticism itself, ending with the idea that even complaint has its place in the larger order.
