Alan Watts

The Bhagavad Gita: The Song of the Lord

KPFA Archive 22:24 1960-04-01

The talk opens with Gandhi’s praise of the Bhagavad-Gita and uses it to frame a careful reading of the text as both spiritual classic and practical guide. It moves from the battlefield scene of Arjuna’s collapse into Krishna’s teaching on the indestructible self, then into larger ideas about Atman and Brahman, rebirth, duty, caste, and vocation. In its final stretch, the speaker returns to Gandhi and argues that the Gita’s central ethic is detached action: doing one’s work without fear, sentimentality, or attachment to results.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Gandhi Opens the Door - Watts begins with Gandhi’s praise of the Gita and uses it to frame the talk’s central question: why this book steadies people in crisis.
  2. 00:45 What the Gita Is - He identifies the text, names Krishna and the Mahabharata, and positions the Gita as a compact statement of Vedanta.
  3. 02:01 Arjuna on the Battlefield - The opening scene is retold in detail, with Arjuna collapsing before battle and Krishna answering his grief and hesitation.
  4. 04:08 The Indestructible Self - Watts quotes Krishna’s teaching that the self is unborn, eternal, and untouched by weapons, fire, or death.
  5. 06:13 Atman and Brahman - He broadens the metaphysics by contrasting the Hindu view of selfhood with the idea of an individual soul and explaining why Brahman cannot really be pictured.
  6. 11:16 Before Birth, After Death - Watts interprets rebirth by treating pre-birth and post-death as symmetrical limits of thought, where the ego cannot truly imagine what lies beyond itself.
  7. 13:56 Duty and Vocation - He explains caste language, svadharma, and the idea that Arjuna’s duty is tied to vocation rather than private sentiment.
  8. 17:18 Gandhi and Nonviolence - Returning to Gandhi, Watts argues that pacifism too must be rooted in vocation and not in fear, squeamishness, or emotional recoil.
  9. 20:04 Action Without Motive - The talk closes on Krishna’s teaching of detached action, which Watts renders as acting without attachment to outcomes and, finally, without motive at all.
