Alan Watts

Tribute to C. G. Jung

KPFA Archive 21:42 1960-08-26

The talk begins as a personal tribute to Carl Jung, recalled from a shelf of books and from a meeting in 1958, then turns to why Jung mattered as a corrective to one-sided enthusiasms for Eastern thought. From there it develops into a sustained argument about polarity, projection, and the danger of treating evil as an absolute outside oneself, with Adolf Eichmann used as a vivid test case. The middle and late sections move into Jung’s own words on accepting the dark side, healing, egoism, and the uneasy way Christian language, Eastern philosophy, and psychology can converge around self-knowledge.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 A late-night tribute - Watts opens with a quiet, personal frame and explains that he wants to honor Jung’s lasting influence on his own life and work.
  2. 00:58 East and West - He recalls Jung’s commentary on The Secret of the Golden Flower as a corrective to his youthful pull toward Eastern ideas and a reminder of his Western inheritance.
  3. 02:45 Polarity of life - Watts introduces Jung’s central insight: life is marked by polarity, not an absolute metaphysical split between good and evil.
  4. 05:00 Projection and violence - Using Eichmann and Koestler as contemporary touchpoints, he argues that demonizing others often hides the darkness one cannot bear to see in oneself.
  5. 08:14 The accepted dark side - Watts describes Jung’s own ease and humor as signs of an integrated character who could admit fear, guilt, and weakness without self-contempt.
  6. 13:38 Reading Jung’s passage - He reads a long excerpt on the doctor, the patient, and unprejudiced objectivity, emphasizing that real help comes only through acceptance rather than condemnation.
  7. 20:47 Healing and egoism - Watts turns to Jung’s claim that healing is a religious problem and that egoism, paradoxically, may be part of the patient’s necessary path toward becoming himself.
  8. 24:16 Enantiodromia and unity - He brings Jung back to Eastern thought, explains enantiodromia as conversion into the opposite, and ends on a peace that contains conflict rather than denying it.
  9. 28:16 Archetypes in art - Watts briefly notes Jung’s view of archetypes and the mixed artistic results that can emerge from Jungian analysis.
