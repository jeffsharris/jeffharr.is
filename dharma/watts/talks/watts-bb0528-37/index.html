Alan Watts

Haiku

KPFA Archive 22:43 1960-08-12

The talk begins with several haiku read aloud, then unfolds into an argument for why the form feels both spare and exacting. From there, the speaker moves through Zen ideas of direct seeing, ordinary action, and the value of stopping before explanation takes over, using brief stories and many poems to make the point. Midway through, the discussion turns more concrete: translation, formal limits, seasonal subjects, and examples from Basho and other poets show how haiku works by suggestion rather than statement. It closes by widening the idea to painting and to Zen sayings, ending on the thought that ordinary perception itself can be the path.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 What Haiku Does - Watts opens with a few poems and argues that haiku is both the simplest and most sophisticated literature because it knows when to stop.
  2. 01:59 Zen as Source - He places haiku within Zen and contrasts its directness with the weight of doctrine, symbolism, and philosophical commentary.
  3. 06:10 Ordinary Answers - Through brief Zen anecdotes and examples from English poetry, Watts shows how the form turns everyday facts into complete answers.
  4. 10:39 Moments of Perception - He turns inward, describing vivid moments of seeing and hearing, and uses them to explain why life is clearest when not grasped.
  5. 13:24 Basho and No-Mind - Basho’s lightning and frog poems lead into the idea of mushin, or no-mind, where subject and object are no longer divided.
  6. 16:27 Form and Restraint - Watts examines haiku’s strict limits, seasonal rules, and compressed images as conditions that force precision rather than decoration.
  7. 19:25 The Reader's Share - He argues that haiku depends on the listener’s participation and extends the point to Chinese and Japanese ink painting, where suggestion matters more than completion.
  8. 24:12 Thusness and Closing Examples - Watts returns to the Zenrin Kushu, the idea of thusness, and a final set of water-and-season images before ending with bibliographic details for Blythe’s collection.
