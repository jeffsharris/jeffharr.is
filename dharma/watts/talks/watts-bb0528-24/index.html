Alan Watts

The Fourth Way

KPFA Archive 46:29 1960-05-20

The talk begins as a response to a listener’s request for further comments on Ouspensky’s The Fourth Way, then widens into a comparison of Gurdjieff, Zen, and Taoist teaching. From there it argues that real instruction often works by obstruction, misdirection, and the frustration of fixed ideas rather than by clear explanation. The closing movement ties that method to Buddhist upaya, or skillful means, and ends by recasting spiritual seeking as a problem of wanting to get something in the first place.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 The Listener’s Request - Watts introduces the question on Ouspensky’s The Fourth Way and frames the talk as a fresh look at Gurdjieff’s influence and methods.
  2. 02:33 Zen as Barrier - He uses the Wumen Guan and Zen koans to show how some teaching is designed to obstruct ordinary understanding rather than explain.
  3. 05:32 Words and Skill - A wheelwright story from Zhuangzi leads into a broader defense of tacit knowledge, patient apprenticeship, and suspicion of overly explicit instruction.
  4. 08:33 Gurdjieff’s School - Watts describes Gurdjieff’s secrecy, feasts, labor, and self-observation exercises as a deliberate pedagogy of confusion and pressure.
  5. 13:16 Seeing Through the Game - He questions Gurdjieff’s cosmology and explains how the self-watching exercise works by trapping the student in frustration until the method is seen.
  6. 19:20 The Sly Way - Watts reinterprets the fourth way as the way of the sly man, linking Gurdjieff’s tactics to Taoist indirectness and tactical gentleness.
  7. 22:06 Shocking the Student - He compares Gurdjieff’s theatrics to Zen masters who break a student’s fixed image of wisdom by acting contrary to expectations.
  8. 23:25 Skillful Means - Watts closes by naming the Buddhist idea of upaya and the yellow leaf story, ending on the claim that seekers must finally see through their own wish to get something.
