Alan Watts

Natural Law

KPFA Archive 29:12 1960-07-15

Alan Watts opens by showing how Western readers projected the idea of immutable natural law onto Buddhism and karma, then traces that same legal habit through Christianity, Roman law, and modern science. From there he argues that what we call laws of nature may be better understood as human-made ways of describing recurring patterns, not external statutes that the world obeys. The talk ends by turning that critique inward: human nature, ethics, and self-understanding are not separate from us, but part of the same living structure we are trying to describe.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Natural law and projection - Watts begins by questioning the Western habit of reading Eastern thought through the lens of immutable law and cause and effect.
  2. 02:19 Karma and dharma - He uses Sir Edwin Arnold and Joseph Needham to show how karma and dharma were translated into a juridical framework they do not really fit.
  3. 04:10 Law in Christianity - The argument widens into Roman law and Christianity, with Tertullian as a bridge for the legal style that shaped theology.
  4. 05:32 The universe as code - Watts describes the older picture of creation as a plan or code and then shows how that idea fed the search for laws behind nature.
  5. 07:17 From theism to deism - He sketches the shift from a God actively present in the world to a clockwork universe that runs on its own after being set in motion.
  6. 09:47 Why science prunes - Watts argues that modern science increasingly drops the language of law because it is often a clumsy and unnecessary way to describe regularity.
  7. 14:02 Patterns, clocks, and rulers - Using clocks, rulers, and probability, he explains how measurement systems help us talk about the world without proving that nature is governed by laws.
  8. 18:09 Observer and world - He shifts to the structure of the senses and the mind, suggesting that apparent uniformity partly reflects the observer's own structure.
  9. 21:00 Ethics without external rules - Watts engages Karl Popper's instrumentalism, then brings the critique to ethics and human nature, arguing that our patterns of life are not separate from us but are what we are.
