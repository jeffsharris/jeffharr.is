Alan Watts

A Memorial Tribute to Alan Watts

KPFA Archive 32:44 1960-09-30

A group of friends gathers for a memorial tribute that moves between recollection, playful anecdote, and ritual remembrance. The talk sketches the speaker’s life through stories of his early broadcasting, his books, his friendships, and the distinct blend of Zen, wit, and improvisation that shaped his public voice. It also turns to the funeral itself, described in Zen Buddhist terms, before closing with a reading on the unborn, indestructible self.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the memorial - The program frames itself as a shared observance of Watts's life, name, and absence before turning to a brief public biography.
  2. 02:38 Who Watts was - The speakers sketch Watts's path from England and Anglican ordination to Eastern thought, his books, and the strange fact of his autobiography arriving near the end.
  3. 06:14 Cremation and return - The memorial shifts into the account of his death, cremation, and Zen funeral, where ritual detail and personal grief begin to overlap.
  4. 12:19 Friends remember - Laura Huxley, John Lilly, and others recall Watts through dinners, first meetings, and the lively social worlds he made around him.
  5. 16:20 Play, logic, and voice - Stories about the surgeon's puzzle, the boat in Sausalito, and his radio style show Watts as a performer whose improvisation was disciplined as much as spontaneous.
  6. 21:42 Presence on air - The panel follows Watts through early workshops and broadcasts, using an excerpted account of KPFA to show how his speech could blend jokes, Taoism, and precision.
  7. 27:48 Showman and listener - The memories widen into his stage presence, his use of props and instruments, and the way he could turn sound, language, and laughter into a teaching form.
  8. 36:56 Generosity and art - The discussion turns to his reading habits, his speed with manuscripts, and his range as an appreciator of both older and experimental art.
  9. 44:23 Funeral, influence, legacy - The talk revisits the Zen funeral, then asks what Watts changed in culture, with speakers arguing that he helped make mystical experience thinkable again.
