Alan Watts

The Art of Psychoanalysis Part 3

KPFA Archive 34:52 1959-12-18

The talk opens by revisiting earlier remarks on psychoanalysis and a critique of J. Haley’s idea of therapy as a kind of one-upmanship, then quickly broadens into a defense of emotional openness. From there it moves through tenderness, embarrassment, and the limits of a cold scientific attitude toward sex, medicine, and psychotherapy, arguing that real healing depends on love as much as insight. In its later stretch, the speaker turns to trust, risk, Christianity, and Jung, before ending with a Taoist note that softness and suppleness belong to life, while hardness belongs to death.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 One-Upmanship in Analysis - Watts revisits the earlier argument that psychoanalysis can look like a contest for control and explains why some listeners found that too narrow.
  2. 02:45 Feeling Beyond Control - He responds by showing how fear of emotion drives the need to stay in charge and why therapy must include spontaneity and feeling.
  3. 05:32 Tenderness Repressed - Drawing on Ian Suttie, he argues that Western culture hides tenderness more deeply than sexuality and trains people into stiffness and reserve.
  4. 10:37 Against Clinical Distance - Watts uses everyday embarrassment and a radio discussion about sex education to criticize the cold, objective attitude when applied to intimacy.
  5. 13:58 The Patient Must Be Felt - He argues that doctors and analysts cannot help by remaining impersonal, because real care requires the capacity to feel with another person.
  6. 17:04 Trust as a Gamble - Watts widens the theme into a philosophy of risk, saying life can only be lived with a basic trust that makes action possible despite uncertainty.
  7. 21:49 Love, God, and Theology - He extends trust into love, then tests the claim against Christianity, criticizing theological love as bloodless, masculine, and lacking the feminine element.
  8. 26:03 Softness as Strength - Watts closes by tying science, tenderness, and Lao Tzu together, insisting that softness and suppleness belong to life while hardness belongs to death.
