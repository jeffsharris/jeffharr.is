Alan Watts

Astrology

KPFA Archive 25:34 1960-08-19

The talk begins with a listener’s enthusiastic defense of astrology and uses that as a way into a careful, skeptical exploration of why the subject can seem convincing. It weighs symbolic and psychological readings of astrology, especially through Jung, before shifting to the limits of prediction, the problem of self-fulfilling expectations, and a failed attempt to test charts against medical cases. In the final stretch, the speaker broadens the argument to Tao, critical openness, and the warning not to confuse mystical unity with control over events, ending on the image of the world as improvisation rather than a fixed score.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 The listener's challenge - Watts opens with a listener’s enthusiasm for astrology and frames the question as more than a matter of amusement or prejudice.
  2. 01:51 Why astrology seems plausible - He argues that science already points toward an interconnected world, and brings in Jung as a modern figure who found astrology suggestive.
  3. 07:11 Jung and symbolic rhythm - Watts develops Jung’s idea that astrology may work as a way of correlating human and terrestrial rhythms rather than causing events from afar.
  4. 11:39 Patterns, not proofs - He turns to the difficulty of testing vague correlations, warning that it is easy to fit rhythms together without proving causation.
  5. 16:23 Prediction and disappointment - Watts tests astrology against specific cases, including illness and personal forecasts, and notes how wish, fear, and anxiety can bend results.
  6. 19:29 War and postmortems - He cites the failure to predict the Second World War and questions the retrospective explanations used to preserve confidence in astrology.
  7. 21:52 A critical openness - Watts argues for studying astrologers in action while keeping genuine openness tied to criticism, not credulity.
  8. 23:39 Mysticism is not occult power - He distinguishes Eastern mystical sensibility from astrology’s specific claims, warning against confusing unity with control over events.
  9. 27:31 Improvisation - Watts closes by saying the world seems to make itself up as it goes along, more like improvisation than a forewritten score.
