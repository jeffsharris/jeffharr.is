Alan Watts

Philosophy As Music

KPFA Archive 28:30 1960-07-29

The talk begins by taking Gustav Holst’s claim that music is a universal language and using it to ask what it means for anything to communicate without stating propositions. From there it moves into a broader argument: music, philosophy, and even mystical experience can be understood as intelligible patterns that do not point beyond themselves, but disclose a kind of order in their own form. In the second half, the speaker turns to Chinese and Hindu thought to defend difference, conflict, and variety as part of a larger harmony rather than evidence of contradiction or failure.

Chapters

  1. 00:30 Holst’s Opening Claim - Watts begins with Holst’s idea that music is a universal language and asks what kind of language music can be if it does not describe things.
  2. 01:00 Music Beyond Words - He distinguishes programmatic and emotional music from music that simply is itself, arguing that it can carry communication more deeply than ordinary speech.
  3. 03:55 Nonverbal Communion - A brief reference to Jakob Böhme leads into the idea of a lost sensuous language and the claim that music communicates without naming or directing.
  4. 07:07 Philosophy as Pattern - Watts turns to philosophy as intellectual music, contrasting it with propaganda and suggesting that both philosophy and music are intelligible as designs rather than messages.
  5. 10:41 Action Ends in Contemplation - Using Aristotle and Bach, he argues that serious thought and action culminate in enjoyment of a pattern that does not point beyond itself.
  6. 13:23 Mystical Vision - He names Vedanta and describes a consciousness in which the world is seen as wholly in place, trying to find language for that inclusive feeling.
  7. 15:39 Objections and Balance - Watts answers two objections: that the view says nothing and that it breeds passivity, insisting instead that reformers and dissenters also belong to the balance of the whole.
  8. 22:43 Difference Makes Harmony - He closes by drawing on Chinese thought and Hindu drama to show that unity is not sameness but a harmony of distinct parts, like a living symphony.
