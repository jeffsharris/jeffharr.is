Alan Watts

How to Get Out of Your Own Way

Google Drive Uploads 51:50 2026-05-22

The talk begins by setting up a stubborn paradox: if the mind is selfish, how can it improve itself without already having changed? From Christian grace to Buddhist letting-go, it moves through a series of familiar spiritual solutions only to show how each can slip back into the same self-assertion it meant to defeat. The middle broadens into social and practical examples—medicine, genetics, politics, education—where good intentions and schemes of control create their own distortions. It ends by backing away from self-improvement altogether and asking whether the real task is simply to stop interfering and watch what is happening.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 The mind-control problem - Watts introduces the talk as an inquiry into mind over mind and sets up the central paradox of self-control.
  2. 00:38 Grace and the vicious circle - He works through Christian grace and predestination to show how the self cannot reliably fix the self that needs fixing.
  3. 02:42 Buddha and letting go - Watts turns to the Buddha and nirvana, using the breath as a plain image of relinquishing the grip of craving.
  4. 04:31 Why improvement loops - He expands the argument into the modern desire to become better and shows why the wish to improve only deepens the contradiction.
  5. 09:13 The higher self disguise - From Atman and meditation to the story of the thieves and the police, he argues that the ego simply reappears in subtler forms.
  6. 12:08 One-upmanship everywhere - Watts mocks spiritual, social, and intellectual status-seeking as the same old game in refined clothing.
  7. 16:58 Good intentions go wrong - He uses the monkey-and-fish story, then moves through politics, ecology, genetics, and medicine to show how intervention often creates new damage.
  8. 26:09 Virtue without display - Lao Tzu, Confucius, war, schooling, scholarship, business, and money all become examples of self-conscious virtue and status replacing actuality.
  9. 38:34 Stop getting in the way - Watts closes by shifting from self-improvement to simple awareness, then ties Zen, authority, Paul, and grace into the final claim that you cannot lift yourself by your own bootstraps.
