Alan Watts

Myth of Myself

YouTube / Being in the Way 42:38 2023-08-04

Alan Watts begins with the question of who the self is, then shows how Western habits of thought turn that question into a cramped image of a “skin-encapsulated ego.” From there he moves through the myths that sustain that view—the world as an artifact, God as watcher, nature as dead mechanism—before reversing the picture and arguing that we grow out of the universe rather than arrive in it. The talk closes by widening consciousness itself: spotlight attention versus floodlight awareness, the self as a local focus of the whole, and a final warning that our habits of separation shape how we live on the land and with one another.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Who Am I? - Watts opens by treating identity as the most fascinating problem and by showing why the self cannot fully inspect itself.
  2. 01:17 The Skin-Ego - He sketches the ordinary Western picture of a controller inside the head and contrasts it with other cultural ways of locating the self.
  3. 05:24 Alienation From Nature - The talk turns to the learned feeling that we are strangers in the world, which feeds hostility toward everything outside us.
  4. 08:59 Myths of Making - Watts contrasts growth with manufacture, using Genesis and childhood questions to show how Western thought imagines reality as an artifact.
  5. 13:42 God and Mechanism - He moves from the oppressive image of a watching God to the modern substitute of a dumb mechanical universe, then criticizes both.
  6. 19:07 Intelligence in Nature - Watts argues that mind cannot simply be an accident in a dead world and uses trees, apples, and rocks to suggest continuity between cosmos and consciousness.
  7. 24:11 Science and the Whole - He redefines the self scientifically as inseparable from its environment and presents the individual as a wave of the cosmos.
  8. 30:04 Spotlight and Floodlight - Watts introduces his distinction between focused attention and the wider field of awareness, then links awakening to larger traditions.
  9. 35:48 Belonging to the Earth - The closing section connects fear of death, punitive religion, and social conditioning to the sense of exile, then ends with a critique of how we impose ourselves on landscapes.
