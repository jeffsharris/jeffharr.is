Alan Watts

Intro to Way Beyond the West

KPFA Archive 29:40 1959-11-06

This opening talk from the Way Beyond the West series explains what the speaker means by "way" or Tao, using Asian traditions to question the Western split between work, play, religion, philosophy, and science. The argument moves from a close reading of vocation and divine play to a sharper contrast between Western institutions and the more experiential, liberating aim he sees in Buddhism, Vedanta, and Taoism. It ends by shifting the emphasis from future plans to present awareness, and by saying the point is not conversion but a meeting of ideas between East and West.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Opening the title - Watts introduces the series title and says he will explain its double meaning for listeners in Southern California.
  2. 00:50 Way as Tao - He connects the English word “way” to Tao, describing it as the way of nature and a way of life.
  3. 02:59 Vocation and craft - Using svadharma and Japanese examples, he argues that work, craft, and art can be paths of initiation rather than mere technique.
  4. 05:11 Play instead of drudgery - He contrasts the Western split between work and play with the Hindu sense of creation as divine play or dance.
  5. 08:03 Beyond creed and code - Watts defines Buddhism, Vedanta, and Taoism as experience-centered ways that are not mainly creeds, moral codes, or institutional religions.
  6. 14:20 Community and convention - He explains religion as a community-binding system and shows how Taoist and Buddhist insight loosens the spell of social convention.
  7. 18:18 Theory and science - Watts distinguishes these ways from academic philosophy and from science, stressing that they are not primarily systems of ideas or prediction.
  8. 23:33 The living present - He turns to time, arguing that the present is real while past and future are abstractions, and that liberation means living now.
  9. 27:42 No conversion project - Watts closes by saying he is not trying to recruit listeners, but to explore how these ideas might mix with Western thought.
