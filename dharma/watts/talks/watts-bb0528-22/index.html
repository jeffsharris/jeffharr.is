Alan Watts

Summary Of Recent Programs

KPFA Archive 30:09 1960-05-13

The talk opens by taking the saying “by their fruits ye shall know them” as a test of philosophy, then quickly turns that standard around to question whether survival is really the highest moral value. From there, it builds a critique of childhood self-control, Freudian splits between mind and body, and the habit of treating discipline as something opposed to spontaneity. The second half broadens the argument through the Buddhist raft image, a discussion of Asian liberating traditions, and a contrast between utilitarian survival and a more musical, playful view of life. It closes by arguing that real liberation does not preach at the world but restores it through delight.

Chapters

  1. 00:30 Fruits and moral test - Watts begins with the phrase “by their fruits ye shall know them,” using it to question whether morality should be judged by social survival alone.
  2. 04:13 Childhood and control - He turns to child-rearing and Freud, arguing that training self-control often divides the person into controller and controlled.
  3. 07:08 Alienation from spontaneity - Watts describes the lasting split this creates: shame, frustration, and a hunger that can never be satisfied from the soul's abstract point of view.
  4. 10:53 The raft must be left - He develops the logic of a survival-fight against death, then uses the Buddhist raft simile to argue that disciplines are for crossing over, not clinging to forever.
  5. 15:15 Adult liberation - Watts says Asian paths of liberation are meant to help people become fully adult, while culture too often leaves them permanently stuck in childhood controls.
  6. 18:22 Utility versus delight - He criticizes Darwinian and bureaucratic habits of thought that treat everything as useful for survival, and contrasts that with a richer, less mechanical view of life.
  7. 21:38 Music as a model - Watts proposes a moral standard based on spontaneity and develops the difference between language that points beyond itself and music that exists as play in the present.
  8. 24:38 Rhythm, play, Orpheus - He closes with rhythm, a story of embodied joy, and the image of Orpheus: liberation is not preaching at the world but delighting it back to its senses.
