Alan Watts

Spiritual Odyssey of Aldous Huxley

KPFA Archive 28:51 1960-02-12

Alan Watts traces Aldous Huxley’s movement from acid wit and literary satire toward a more explicit spiritual program, beginning around 1938 and moving through the shift in his novels. The talk spends its middle on Island as a difficult utopia: part satire of modern life, part social essay, part attempt to reconcile spiritual insight with ordinary institutions like family, education, and sex. Watts closes by quoting Huxley at length on self-knowledge, not-two, and the need to hold spirit and everyday life together rather than flee from one into the other.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Huxley’s turn - Watts introduces Huxley’s spiritual odyssey and places the turning point around 1938, when the novelist begins to change direction.
  2. 01:21 The critical dispute - He summarizes the hostile response that accused Huxley of merely replacing one kind of hostility with another, then explains the charge in terms of Oriental symbols taken too literally.
  3. 03:41 From emptiness to inclusion - Watts describes Huxley’s gradual move away from a one-sided escape into spiritual negation toward a view that includes both the divine ground and the world of particulars.
  4. 05:30 Island as counterweight - Island is presented as the mature counterpart to Brave New World, a utopia that is deliberately hard to write and easy to attack.
  5. 07:59 The utopian system - Watts sketches the island’s Buddhist society, drug-assisted pedagogy, altered family structure, tantric sexuality, and experimental education.
  6. 10:39 Why the book provokes - He argues that the book will be snidely dismissed despite its intelligence, and then turns to what makes its satirical and imaginative strengths uneven.
  7. 14:13 Not-two - A long quotation from the island text centers on self-knowledge, the false self, and the reconciliation of opposites into the experience of not-two.
  8. 17:00 Words and sorrow - Watts reflects on the mixture of Zen, Krishnamurti, and other influences, then shifts into a warning about religious language and a quotation from the Buddha on sorrow and its ending.
  9. 19:01 Spirit and everyday life - He ends by calling Island a culmination of Huxley’s spiritual search and stressing the final reconciliation between nirvana and samsara, vision and ordinary life.
