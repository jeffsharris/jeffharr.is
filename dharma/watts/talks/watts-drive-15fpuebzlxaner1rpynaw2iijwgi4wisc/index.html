Alan Watts

Buddhism Explained

Google Drive Uploads 44:54 2026-05-22

This talk explains Buddhism as a method rather than a creed, starting with Sanskrit terms, Buddhist cosmology, and the figure of the Buddha as one who is awake. It then moves through the Four Noble Truths and the trap of trying to defeat desire, using repeated dialogue between teacher and student to show how spiritual effort can become another form of striving. Near the end, the talk widens into a broader argument about responsibility, spontaneity, and why both Buddhist and Christian sayings about non-anxiety are so hard to live by. The closing turn is stark: nirvana is presented not as a solution, but as giving up the search for one.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Word Origins - Watts begins with the Sanskrit root behind 'that' and 'tat tvam asi,' using language to open his argument about immediacy and non-separation.
  2. 01:55 Buddhism as Method - He sets Buddhism apart from creed or doctrine and defines dharma as a practical method, not a fixed belief.
  3. 03:24 The Wheel of Life - Watts explains Buddha as 'the awake one' and sketches the six realms of the wheel of life as states of consciousness and recurring striving.
  4. 09:12 The Still Center - He describes the Buddha as the unmoved center of the turning world and uses that image to show why waking means dropping the illusion of future salvation.
  5. 13:28 Solitude and Renunciation - The talk turns to shramanas, shamans, and forest dwelling, contrasting priestly order with the solitary search for what one is before social labels.
  6. 20:40 The Middle Way - Watts recounts the Buddha's rejection of ascetic excess and redefines Buddhism as dialogue, with the bodhisattva as the one who returns to society.
  7. 23:53 Four Noble Truths - He lays out suffering, craving, cessation, and the path, then shows how the effort to stop desire becomes another form of desire.
  8. 33:10 The Frustration Trap - Watts develops the paradox of impossible action, arguing that the student's impasse reveals the illusion of a separate controlling self.
  9. 41:28 Anxiety and Release - He compares Buddhist insight with Jesus' teaching on worry and the parable of the publican, then ends by defining nirvana as giving up the hunt for an answer.
