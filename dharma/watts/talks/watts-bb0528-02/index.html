Alan Watts

The Future of Christian Philosophy V

KPFA Archive 25:32 1960-01-15

Alan Watts returns to the Christian problem of freedom, but instead of treating it as a moral failure of will, he argues that the deeper issue is the way the self is imagined. He moves from psychology and Freud to Christian ideas of grace, baptism, and Christ indwelling the person, then challenges the ordinary church picture that leaves the ego intact and merely disciplined. The talk ends by pressing into mysticism, pantheism, and the claim that a unity beyond distinctions does not erase real moral differences.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Freedom and psychology - Watts resumes the question of human freedom, setting Christian ideas of fallen will against what psychology suggests about unconscious motives and limited control.
  2. 04:31 Liberation or license - He introduces Buddhism, Taoism, and Vedanta to ask whether total acceptance is liberation or merely indulgence, and why the distinction seems impossible under the usual terms.
  3. 06:02 The split self - He argues that the real assumption behind both free will and determinism is a divided person, one who stands apart from appetites, body, and impulses.
  4. 08:52 The lonely ego - Using Erich Fromm, Watts describes the isolated ego and the compensations people build around it, then says the problem of fate and freedom rests on that false center.
  5. 11:36 Christ lives in me - He turns to Paul, baptism, and Christian transformation as signs of a new kind of consciousness in which the world is no longer experienced as outside the self.
  6. 15:12 Church as one body - Watts uses the church-as-body image and an old hymn to ask whether Christianity has really pursued lived unity or mostly administrative piety and moral pressure.
  7. 18:36 Grace, guilt, confession - He traces how gratitude, penance, and repeated forgiveness can still leave the ego untouched, and sometimes deepen guilt instead of dissolving it.
  8. 23:18 Augustine and suspicion - Augustine becomes an example of how confession can become a rhapsody of guilt, while orthodox Christianity remains wary of mysticism and the idea of a transformed nature.
  9. 27:21 Beyond absolute opposites - Watts closes by defending mystical unity against the fear that it destroys morality or love, and argues for a future Christian philosophy rooted in a new sense of what man is.
