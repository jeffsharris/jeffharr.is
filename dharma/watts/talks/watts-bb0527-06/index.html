Alan Watts

The Art of Psychoanalysis Part 2

KPFA Archive 28:56 1959-12-11

Alan Watts returns to J. Haley’s essay and uses it as a frame for a wider argument about psychoanalysis, Zen training, and the strange usefulness of indirect guidance. He moves from the idea of therapy as a kind of one-upmanship to placebos, initiation rites, koans, and the way surrender often has to be produced by a deliberately frustrating process. In the final stretch, he shifts from the individual patient to the larger social context, arguing that what looks like private disorder is often rooted in shared assumptions and patterns of communication.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Haley’s frame - Watts recaps Haley’s article and restates the puzzle of whether it is satire or a serious defense of analysis.
  2. 02:13 One-upmanship and trickery - He develops the idea that analysis works through managed asymmetry, then widens it to the human tendency to rely on tricks, suggestion, and collaboration.
  3. 05:12 Placebo logic - Using daylight saving and the placebo as examples, Watts argues that people often need to be helped indirectly before change can happen.
  4. 07:36 Zen as inversion - He turns to Zen, where the master also refuses direct instruction and uses barriers, pressure, and apparent refusal to unsettle the student’s grasping.
  5. 09:50 Koans and surrender - Watts describes koans, the refusal to be taught, and the way striving to be natural or spontaneous only deepens self-consciousness.
  6. 13:13 The split self - He expands the analysis into a critique of self-consciousness, sincerity, and the mind’s split between observer and observed.
  7. 18:30 Doubt reaches its limit - Watts follows the logic of certainty inward until doubt turns on the doubter, leading him to argue that the unconscious cannot be fully inspected or controlled.
  8. 22:04 Acceptance, not control - He says therapy works by bringing enough material to force trust in one’s own center, so the impulse to improve or fix oneself can be dropped.
  9. 24:16 The social matrix - Watts closes by widening the lens again: psychotherapy must attend to context, shared assumptions, and the social matrix that shapes individual distress.
