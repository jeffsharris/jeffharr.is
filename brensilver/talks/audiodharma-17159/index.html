Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Ill-Will & Metta

AudioDharma 56:23 2022-10-25

Matthew Brensilver explores ill-will and metta as two very different ways the heart meets suffering. He moves from the mechanics of aversion—how craving, blame, and anger narrow experience—to the possibility of purification, radical accountability, and a softer, more spacious response. Near the end, he turns toward loving-kindness as a form of non-violence that can hold pain without collapse, and he closes by linking wisdom with love and non-contention.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Opening the problem - Brensilver introduces ill-will and frames it as a familiar way of suffering that the talk will examine closely.
  2. 00:58 The Dharma as a broad remedy - He uses pharmacology to argue that Dharma works through many mechanisms, not just the surface sign of a calm mind.
  3. 05:18 Purification and practice - He distinguishes concentration from purification and says the path includes peaks, valleys, and whatever is present now.
  4. 08:07 What and how - The talk turns to the 'what' and the deeper 'how' of practice, showing how motivations shape what we seem to know.
  5. 12:37 Craving shapes the view - He explores how craving and aversion thicken experience, using his own childhood sensitivity to show how ill-will can become a strategy for managing pain.
  6. 17:40 Mara and the turning inward - Brensilver describes the habitual impulses to approach, avoid, or ignore, then shows how clinging and aversion reinforce helplessness and suffering.
  7. 23:41 The heat of aversion - Using the boiling-pot simile, he explains how ill-will obscures judgment, while Agnes Callard, Ruth King, and the Dalai Lama help refine the view of anger as partially functional but never transformative.
  8. 31:53 Facing and owning anger - He turns to noticing ill-will directly, describing radical accountability as a training that stops externalizing blame without denying external causes.
  9. 38:30 Metta as antidote - The emphasis shifts to metta as warm friendliness, free of possessiveness and control, and as a way of being moved by goodness and finding a home in it.
