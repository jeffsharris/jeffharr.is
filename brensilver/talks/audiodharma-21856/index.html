Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Guided Meditation: What can you stop doing?

AudioDharma 15:08 2024-12-10

Matthew Brensilver explores loving-kindness as something different from ordinary wanting: not a stronger preference, but a letting go of clinging, fear, and motivational desperation. He moves from Buddhist teaching to the felt realities of attachment in family and romantic love, then toward a more honest, less possessive way of caring that can include grief and dukkha without becoming overwhelmed by them. The talk closes by linking metta to moral elevation, rest, and a heart made less available to hatred.

Chapters

  1. 00:16 Opening into metta - He begins by naming the week’s theme and setting up the central contrast between ordinary liking and love.
  2. 00:35 Love beyond wanting - Matthew argues that love is not just another form of wanting, but something that stands outside hedonic preference.
  3. 01:49 Wanting and fear - He uses the Buddha’s teaching on clinging to show how wanting and fear share a common motivational pressure that contracts the mind.
  4. 03:29 Metta as release - Loving-kindness is presented as a vast, receptive stance that appears when desperation and grasping fade.
  5. 04:26 Approaching what hurts - He describes love and mindfulness as forms of moving toward avoided experience so pain can unwind and lose its charge.
  6. 06:08 Kindness, not niceness - Matthew clarifies the kind of love he means by distinguishing kindness from mere social niceness and by naming the difference between generosity and possessiveness.
  7. 08:32 Attachment with grace - Using examples from parenting and romantic longing, he acknowledges that some attachment is irreducible and asks whether we can consent to its dukkha without utopian expectations.
  8. 10:20 Love without control - He turns toward a love that seeks no control, with metta arising through recognition of goodness, honesty, and moral beauty.
  9. 12:22 Abiding in goodness - The talk closes by linking loving-kindness to inner uplift, rest, and a heart that becomes less and less willing to live inside hatred and separation.
