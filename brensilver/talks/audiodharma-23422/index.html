Dharmette: Unquenchability and Questions of Meaning

AudioDharma 13:19 2025-07-16

Matthew Brensilver opens with a light, unexpected detour through golf and a recent quote from Scottie Scheffler, then uses it to explore a serious question: why accomplishment so often fails to satisfy. From there he turns to dukkha as not only suffering but also the heart’s unquenchability, asking what is actually worth wanting and why some questions cannot be answered in words. He closes by drawing on Rilke and on Dharma practice itself to suggest that the real answer is lived, trained, and gradually embodied.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Opening with golf - He begins with a humorous, personal route into the topic, using sports and golf as a way to approach meaning and restlessness.
  2. 02:08 Scheffler asks why - A quote from Scottie Scheffler becomes the center of the talk, revealing how success can still leave a person asking what the point is.
  3. 05:40 More than suffering - Matthew broadens dukkha beyond obvious pain to include the deeper sense that experience does not fully satisfy the heart.
  4. 07:27 What is worth wanting - He explores how craving is built into culture and why the question of what to want cannot be solved by a quick concept or answer.
  5. 09:04 Rilke and the questions - Rilke’s counsel to live the questions provides a literary frame for the idea that some understanding emerges only through time and practice.
  6. 09:39 Answered through training - He returns to Dharma language, saying that satisfaction and non-clinging are learned through training rather than through a single insight.
  7. 11:27 Doubt dissolves - The talk closes with the claim that practice can dissolve the question of meaning itself, leaving a freedom that can be trusted.
