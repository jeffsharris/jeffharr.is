Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Self, Self-Love, Selflessness

AudioDharma 46:05 2026-02-28

Matthew Brensilver explores how insight practice moves between simple clarity and complex psychological terrain, using self, self-love, and selflessness as a dialectic rather than a single answer. He argues that stability and repetition are not obstacles to awakening but the conditions that let disorientation, insight, and relinquishment be integrated safely. Along the way he draws on retreat life, neuroscience, and teachers such as Manindraji, Jack Engler, Ajahn Sucitto, and Thomas Metzinger to show why a workable sense of self can support deeper letting go.

Chapters

  1. 00:09 Simplicity and complexity - Brensilver opens with Manindraji’s line that if practice is not simple, it is not vipassana, then immediately complicates that simplicity with the real terrain of practice.
  2. 01:16 The dharma as dialectic - He argues that the path is not a single answer but a set of tensions, where teachings like letting go, love, emptiness, and not-self belong together.
  3. 04:00 Stability and disorientation - The talk turns to the need for stability as a base for meeting fear, uncertainty, and the disorienting teachings on anatta.
  4. 08:18 What self means - He defines self as identification, not as a thing, and explains how representation becomes transparent and feels like reality itself.
  5. 13:21 Learning readiness - Brensilver describes how Dharma practice creates the conditions for learning through predictability, precepts, routine, and tranquility, then links this to the three characteristics and retreat life.
  6. 20:47 Brain balance and retreat - He cites recent neuroscience on stability and adaptability to show a parallel with intensive retreat, where a stable container allows insight to disrupt rigid patterns.
  7. 25:21 A self that can hold - Using Jack Engler, he argues that deeper practice usually needs a solid enough sense of self to be integrated rather than overwhelmed.
  8. 30:13 Self-love and ownership - He distinguishes self-love from self-aggrandizement, draws on Ajahn Sucitto and the Dhammapada, and examines how ownership and territoriality feed suffering.
  9. 37:28 Unbinding and release - The closing movement traces how self-models become visible, how awareness brightens as identification loosens, and how self returns as something known with less fragility.
