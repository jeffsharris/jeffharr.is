Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Dharmette: attention, awareness, mindfulness

AudioDharma 15:31 2025-10-29

Matthew Brensilver distinguishes attention from awareness and mindfulness, arguing that practice is not just about focusing on an object but also noticing the background quality of the mind that knows it. He moves from a simple model of spotlighted attention to a broader account of receptive awareness, relaxation, and the way craving can quietly shape what seems like curiosity. The talk ends by reframing mindfulness as a subtle freedom: seeing clinging as an object rather than obeying it.

Chapters

  1. 00:09 Opening on mindfulness - He opens by admitting that mindfulness is still something he is trying to understand and begins to separate it from simple attention.
  2. 00:32 Attention and receptivity - Attention is described as a directed, selective power, but also as something that must make room for receptive awareness and surrender.
  3. 01:50 What attention does - He explains how attention shapes experience, using the image of a spotlight and the idea that concentration narrows and brightens the field.
  4. 03:40 Three kinds of attention - Drawing on scientific models, he describes alerting, orienting, and executive attention, then links them to samadhi and readiness.
  5. 05:26 Object focus and its limits - With a cognitive science example, he shows how attention can remain object-oriented even when the object is not vividly present.
  6. 07:01 Craving in the background - He warns that tight attention can become tense and grasping, and that control may disguise itself as curiosity.
  7. 09:11 Awareness makes mindfulness - Awareness is presented as the background context that lets us notice how attention is functioning, rather than only what it is fixed on.
  8. 12:04 Seeing craving clearly - He turns to how craving and aversion shape the mind and how mindfulness must stay open to those gravitational pulls without obeying them.
  9. 13:32 Freedom without strategy - The talk closes by saying awareness cannot cling, that clinging becomes an object when seen clearly, and that mindfulness is ordinary and free.
