Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Dharma Talk (Retreat at Spirit Rock)

Dharma Seed 47:22 2026-05-04

Matthew Brensilver gives a quiet, wide-ranging talk on silence as both a refuge and a revealing condition of practice. He starts with the everyday noise of conversation and broadens into samsara, craving, and the felt relief of silence, then turns to attention as the main way experience is shaped. Along the way he uses canonical lines, Ajahn Chah, Suzuki Roshi, and a passage from Jorie Graham to show how practice can soften reactivity and let insight emerge.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Opening with conversation - He begins with a social psychology study about when conversations end, using it as an entry into the talk's theme of silence and social coordination.
  2. 02:47 Noise and samsara - Silence is contrasted with the noise of craving, aversion, and the restless push of samsara, with the felt sense that the quieter we get, the more noise we can notice.
  3. 05:33 Silence as refuge - He quotes the Samyutta Nikaya's fire imagery, describes the heart's wish to be unstimulated, and emphasizes that practice is not against sound but toward a different relationship with it.
  4. 11:24 Not bothering the sound - An Ajahn Chah story and Shinzen Young's phrase 'love it to death' frame radical non-resistance: sound is met directly, without the extra layer of aversion.
  5. 13:45 What samadhi means - Samadhi is described as unification, attentional satiety, and a non-split heart, while also being protected from egoic capture and the fantasy that it is only something we do.
  6. 20:26 Attention shapes world - William James and the Buddha are invoked to show that volition and phenomena are rooted in attention, and that expanding attentional sensitivity expands the lived world.
  7. 26:43 Aiming and sustaining - He explains the two phases of attention—aiming and sustaining—using Ajahn Sucitto's image of pointing and opening the hand, then connects this to mindfulness practice and its shifts in stability.
  8. 31:42 Fear and meaning-making - The talk turns to how fear drives conceptualization, prediction, and salience, and how retreat can signal safety enough for the mind to relax its vigilance.
  9. 39:35 Hearing silence - He closes with perceptual research, then Jorie Graham's reflections on silence, grief, and time, ending by pointing toward insight and a clearer sense of how to live.
