Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Q&A

Dharma Seed 36:45 2015-12-31

In this question-and-answer session, Matthew Brensilver and others answer practical questions about how to relate to difficult experience without getting trapped in constant self-optimization. The talk moves from acceptance of the present moment to working directly with fear, anger, and low mood, emphasizing sensory experience, awareness, and choosing when to act or let things be. A playful Seinfeld reference helps illustrate the value of going against habitual patterns when practice has become rigid or over-effortful.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the questions - The teachers invite questions from the group and frame the discussion as open to whatever is close to heart.
  2. 02:10 Acceptance and change - Matthew distinguishes accepting this moment from passively accepting all of life, and explains how that clarity can reveal when action is needed.
  3. 04:50 What this moment means - The talk turns to what it actually means to accept the present, shifting from concepts like anxiety or fear to the body sensations underneath them.
  4. 10:03 Rerouting attention - Matthew describes how to stay with present experience without following the mind's story, including practical ways to redirect attention to bodily sensation or sound.
  5. 14:06 Wholesome attention - The discussion widens to neutral and pleasant experience, and to cultivating more attention to what is already okay rather than only noticing what is wrong.
  6. 18:14 Opposite of habit - Using Seinfeld's George Costanza as an example, Matthew suggests experimenting with the opposite of one's usual habit when stuck between forcing and avoidance.
  7. 23:57 Working with anger - A question about anger leads into the balance between suppression and acting out, with mindfulness described as a loving holding of the emotion.
  8. 29:34 Naming and relating - The teachers speak about naming emotions honestly with others and how that can create intimacy rather than shutdown or explosion.
  9. 32:30 Bad moods and closing - The final questions return to rerouting when there is no clear trigger, with Matthew encouraging simple attention to what is actually happening and acceptance of an ordinary bad day.
