Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Welcome - Abiding in Care

AudioDharma 48:21 2023-12-02

Matthew Brensilver opens with a quiet welcome and then reflects on what practice changes: less project-management of life, less fixation on past and future, and a more organic resting in the present. He uses an overheard line from Gil Fronsdal about ambition and vision to question his own relationship to purpose, then turns toward sangha, care, and the way a Dharma life may be lived into rather than engineered. The talk later becomes more explicitly embodied, drawing on Ilan Moffat’s phrase “becoming available to the Dharma,” and ends by bringing wisdom and love together as two wings of awakening.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Opening Welcome - Matthew greets the group and frames the day as one of giving oneself to the heart rather than forcing a plan.
  2. 00:47 Life Less Managed - He describes how practice can loosen preoccupation with managing life, making the future less puzzling and the past more settled.
  3. 03:03 Ambition and Vision - An overheard remark from Gil Fronsdal becomes a prompt for Matthew to question what guides a life without ambition.
  4. 04:44 A Record of Care - Looking at the registration list, he recognizes years of shared practice as a record of mutual care and Dharma companionship.
  5. 07:23 Living Into Practice - Matthew suggests that a Dharma life is not only a project of effort, but something we gradually live into together.
  6. 10:52 Entering Sangha - He invites people to share what is close to their hearts and then settles the group into sitting practice.
  7. 17:23 The Form of Love - During the sit, he notes that we do not choose the form love takes in a moment, and that some form of love is always beckoning.
  8. 22:07 Becoming Available - Using Ilan Moffat’s phrase, he links awake embodiment with receptivity, tenderness, and the softening of flatness and intensity.
  9. 31:44 Wisdom and Love - Matthew closes by teaching that wisdom and love are never far apart, and that wisdom never ends in hate.
