Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

TS Eliot and the Mystical Dimension of Buddhism

AudioDharma 16:57 2025-12-17

Matthew Brensilver explores the mystical dimension of Buddhism without romanticizing mystical experience. Using William James, T.S. Eliot, Joseph Goldstein, and a short canonical citation, he traces a path of ignorance, dispossession, non-ownership, and relinquishing views. The talk stays close to practice: ordinary life, egoic footing, and the quiet relief that can come from not knowing.

Chapters

  1. 00:11 William James and consciousness - Brensilver opens with William James to frame alternate forms of consciousness as real but not yet a path.
  2. 01:56 Mysticism without fetish - He distinguishes ordinary Dharma practice from the temptation to chase or idealize peak mystical states.
  3. 03:21 Renunciation and ego - The talk turns to renunciation, radical change, and the way mystical practice can undercut egoic grandiosity.
  4. 05:39 Eliot’s way of ignorance - He reads T.S. Eliot as a concise expression of the path through ignorance and dispossession rather than ecstasy.
  5. 07:22 The dry terrain - Brensilver reflects on how the mystical can sound alike across traditions and why the path may feel stripped of spiritual drama.
  6. 09:05 Keeping balance in not-knowing - He describes how clinging to knowing preserves egoic balance, and how Dharma asks for a willingness to lose that footing.
  7. 12:20 The practice of don't know - A personal anecdote about all-night meditation and a repeated 'don't know' note illustrates surrender to uncertainty.
  8. 14:09 Non-attachment and views - He cites the Atthaka Vagga and interprets not-self as non-ownership, emphasizing the release of doctrinal attachment.
  9. 15:53 Refrain and close - The Eliot lines return as a closing refrain, and Brensilver leaves listeners with the invitation to take what is useful and let the rest go.
