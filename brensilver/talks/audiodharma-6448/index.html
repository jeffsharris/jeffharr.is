Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Thinking

AudioDharma 36:04 2016-03-13

Matthew Brensilver explores thinking as both indispensable and deeply habit-forming, moving from ordinary self-talk to the way meditation reveals thought as a stream of words, images, and self-updating narratives. He brings in Douglas Harding, the Dhammapada, Shinzen Young, and Daniel Dennett to show how thought shapes identity, while also cautioning against making thought into an enemy. The talk ends by softening that tension: practice is not about ranking experience, but about seeing clearly, loosening identification, and learning to rest with what is here.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Thinking and its place - Brensilver opens by balancing the usefulness of thought with the common shame meditators feel about having it.
  2. 03:06 Stopping thought - Douglas Harding’s account of sudden quiet becomes a vivid counterpoint to the ordinary spell of discursive mind.
  3. 08:28 Mind shapes experience - The Dhammapada and a comparison with cognitive therapy frame thought as powerful, but not the whole path.
  4. 13:34 What thought is made of - He distinguishes inner words from mental images and describes how practice reveals the subtle mechanics of thinking.
  5. 22:09 Self as narrative - Neuroscience and philosophy are used to show how selfing works as a repeating update loop and a center of narrative gravity.
  6. 26:23 Seeing self as phenomena - The talk turns to the Buddhist move of treating self as just another experience, not a fixed core.
  7. 29:01 Settling without forcing - Brensilver describes samadhi as quieting and transparency, while warning that trying to manufacture calm creates more agitation.
  8. 31:04 Not making a hierarchy - Jason Siff’s perspective is introduced to caution against ranking states and to encourage learning from whatever arises.
