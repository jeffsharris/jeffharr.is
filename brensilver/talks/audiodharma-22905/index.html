Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Dharmette: 'A thing that masquerades as freedom of attention is on the rampage'

AudioDharma 13:46 2025-05-07

Matthew Brensilver reflects on attention as something deeply tied to love, desire, and ethical life, while arguing that modern technological frenzy distorts all three. He quotes a 2019 Friends of Attention manifesto, then moves through Rilke, Naomi Shihab Nye, and Simone Weil to show how slowing down and staying with experience can loosen craving and hatred. The talk settles into a quiet claim: wise attention is not a technique for control, but a form of prayer that lets the soul be drawn toward the good.

Chapters

  1. 00:19 Attention and love - Matthew begins by linking fragmented attention with compromised love and the effects of technological frenzy.
  2. 01:31 The manifesto's warning - He introduces the Friends of Attention manifesto and reads its critique of narrowed desire and compulsive satisfaction.
  3. 02:56 True freedom of attention - He contrasts false freedom with the burden and dignity of real attention, describing attention as a basic currency of practice.
  4. 04:29 Craving tightened - He turns to the second noble truth, suggesting that the craving-acquisition-satisfaction-heartbreak loop has become especially tight in modern life.
  5. 06:08 Rilke and slowing down - Rilke and Naomi Shihab Nye help him name helplessness, restraint, and the need to live more slowly and locally.
  6. 07:58 Depth over surface - He returns to the relation between attention and desire, showing how patient attention can cultivate fidelity and a deeper kind of engagement.
  7. 10:28 Attention as prayer - Simone Weil's words frame attention as prayer and as the means by which faults are healed without force.
  8. 12:11 Giving up hate - He closes by saying deep attention makes hatred harder to sustain and leaves us with a quiet invitation to consider this practice.
