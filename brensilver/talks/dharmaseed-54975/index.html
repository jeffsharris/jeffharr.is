Technology as an Existential Threat to Mindfulness - Matthew Brensilver and Randy Fernando

Dharma Seed 3:01:59 2019-02-28

Matthew Brensilver and Randy Fernando examine how smartphones, apps, and the attention economy shape mindfulness, restlessness, and even our sense of self. The first half builds the case that modern technology trains habit, urgency, and fragmentation; the second half turns practical, with suggestions for simpler defaults, gentler boundaries, and more humane ways of using devices. A short guided experiment and discussion sections help make the point concrete without turning the talk into a polemic.

Chapters

  1. 02:06 Arriving and settling - The meeting opens with brief welcome remarks, a few quiet minutes of sitting, and the transition into the afternoon.
  2. 09:34 Attention and mind wandering - Matthew begins by naming the question of technology, then frames mindfulness as a balancing factor while allowing for a middle way with mind wandering.
  3. 20:23 Time slipping away - He cites Jonathan Schooler and David Whyte to show how distraction and unconsciousness can let years pass almost unnoticed.
  4. 24:45 Phones as training - The talk turns to phones as pseudo-companions that soothe restlessness while quietly training attention, loneliness, and habits of response.
  5. 35:49 What tech rewards - Matthew describes technology as a system that rewards fragmentation, urgency, and egoic posting, and notes how this often shows up in ordinary social media use.
  6. 48:37 Division and deepfakes - The focus widens to political manipulation, the breakdown of shared facts, and the threat of fake media, before returning to the larger claim that some technology now undermines mindfulness itself.
  7. 58:58 Safer products and band-aids - In questions, he distinguishes humane tools from manipulative ones and treats screen-time apps and maps as partial aids rather than true solutions.
  8. 01:10:59 Mindlessness and guilt - He responds to questions about zoning out, shame, and the edge between wholesome rest and compulsive checking, emphasizing less guilt and more clear noticing.
  9. 01:25:31 Practical habits - The discussion moves toward resilience, family dynamics, and concrete changes like notification hygiene, grayscale, reduced texting, and not checking the phone first thing.
