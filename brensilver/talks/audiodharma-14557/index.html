Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Interpersonal Life as Dharma Practice (1 of 5)

AudioDharma 15:49 2021-11-15

Matthew Brensilver frames interpersonal life as a central field of Dharma practice, starting with the reminder that the self is not isolated and that relationship reveals our habits plainly. He moves from humor and vulnerability into a serious reflection on belonging, nervous system regulation, and the difference between knowing the Dharma and actually realizing it in lived experience. The talk ends by inviting a week of careful, forgiving inquiry into what gets activated in us with others, and how the Dharma feels in relationship compared with solitude.

Chapters

  1. 00:21 Relational Beginning - Tony Kushner’s line about two people frames the talk around the idea that human life is inherently relational.
  2. 00:52 Humility and Exposure - A humorous story of being introduced with praise while showing up with a zipper down introduces self-clinging, embarrassment, and the limits of appearances.
  3. 02:27 Interpersonal Richness - Matthew describes relationship as a place of beauty, vulnerability, shame, grace, and the full range of human feeling.
  4. 04:04 Nervous System in Contact - Using Lisa Feldman Barrett, he points to other people as both the best and worst thing for the nervous system and explores belonging, care, and contact.
  5. 05:46 Cushion and Life - He distinguishes practice that stays on the cushion from practice that needs to translate into the interpersonal realm.
  6. 07:00 What Becomes Real - The talk turns to how others reveal where insight has stabilized and where it has not, and how autobiographical assumptions get punctured.
  7. 09:58 Knowledge to Realization - A quotation about reality as what is realized leads into a fuller explanation of Buddhism as the work of making understanding real in the heart.
  8. 12:01 Practice this Week - He invites a serious but loving inquiry into all kinds of relationships, with respect, wonder, forgiveness, and less moralism.
  9. 14:20 Questions to Carry - The closing questions ask what gets activated in relationship, how one feels about oneself with others, and whether the Dharma feels more alive in company or alone.
