Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Strength and the Surrender to Goodness (4 of 5)

AudioDharma 13:43 2021-11-18

Matthew Brensilver reflects on reverence for another person’s goodness, arguing that healthy idealization can be part of love, relationship, and Dharma practice. He distinguishes reverence from projection or dependency, and suggests that surrender can be a form of strength rather than weakness. The talk closes by linking metta to the capacity to see goodness without turning it into comparison, envy, or self-judgment.

Chapters

  1. 01:00 From pain to goodness - He contrasts the previous day’s attention to another’s pain with today’s emphasis on goodness and power.
  2. 02:19 Why surrender matters - He argues that opening to another’s goodness asks for surrender, not just objectivity or caution.
  3. 03:36 Idealization and illusion - Using Stephen Mitchell and Stephen Wallace, he complicates the habit of treating idealization as mere fantasy and disillusionment as truth.
  4. 05:07 Reverence, not projection - He distinguishes reverence from using idealization to prop up the self, project needs, or take another’s power.
  5. 06:34 Surrender to Dharma - He turns from relationships to practice itself, describing how we fall in love with the path, grow disenchanted, and return again.
  6. 08:02 Awe for goodness - He broadens reverence into awe, suggesting that ordinary acts of goodness can be met with the same humility often reserved for famous teachers.
  7. 09:52 Envy and comparison - He warns against relating ego to ego, where another’s goodness becomes a measuring stick that leads to envy and self-judgment.
  8. 11:11 Metta and surrender - He frames metta as practice in seeing goodness directly, allowing brief moments of idealization, and taking in reverence without grasping.
  9. 12:19 Closing reflections - He closes by saying we are learning both self-sufficiency and dependency, and the strength of surrender.
