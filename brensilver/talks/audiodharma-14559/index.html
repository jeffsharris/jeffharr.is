Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Not-knowing is the Most Intimate (2 of 5)

AudioDharma 14:54 2021-11-16

Matthew Brensilver reflects on how self-understanding and intimacy change when we stop forcing fixed answers onto identity. Drawing on Simone Weil, a Zen story about “not knowing,” Philip Roth, and a brief father-daughter exchange, he argues that uncertainty can soften self-judgment and make relationships feel more alive. The talk moves from the question “Who am I?” to a quieter reverence for the mystery of other people.

Chapters

  1. 00:23 The question of self - He begins with the question of identity and shows how ordinary answers often feel inadequate or false.
  2. 02:04 When autobiography cracks - He points to moments when the familiar self-story opens through insight, beauty, or suffering.
  3. 03:31 We are more than imagined - Using Simone Weil, he links self-understanding with forgiveness and the release of certainty.
  4. 04:39 Not knowing is intimate - A Zen story becomes the central teaching that not knowing can be a doorway into intimacy.
  5. 05:15 Seeing loved ones anew - He turns to how habitual concepts can obscure the people closest to us and flatten relationship.
  6. 07:22 The hidden life of others - A passage from The Human Stain illustrates how every person contains a vast, unseen biography.
  7. 08:53 Roles and predictability - He examines how we freeze people into roles and how that predictability can reduce aliveness.
  8. 11:09 Beyond the role - A father-daughter exchange is used to show that our being is not reducible to relational roles.
  9. 13:00 Looking with new eyes - He closes by inviting listeners to honor not knowing and to perceive others with fresh attention.
