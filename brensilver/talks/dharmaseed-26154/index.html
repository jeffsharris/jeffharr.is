Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Feedback In The Context of Dharma Practice

Dharma Seed 39:54 2015-01-26

Matthew Brensilver reflects on feedback as a central Dharma practice: learning from experience, softening certainty, and noticing how self-view gets defended. He moves from a teaching of Shinzen Young, through Kathryn Schulz on being wrong, into the lived dynamics of criticism, praise, defensiveness, intimacy, and community safety. The talk ends by widening the lens from personal practice to Sangha culture, where honest feedback can help prevent harm.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Two guiding principles - Matthew opens with Shinzen Young’s two maxims and uses them to frame the talk: don’t fight with yourself and take feedback.
  2. 02:01 Everything is teaching - He broadens feedback into a contemplative stance, citing Ajahn Chah and the idea that the whole path is about learning from life.
  3. 03:53 The lure of being right - A long quotation from Kathryn Schulz helps him examine certainty, the pleasure of being right, and why wrongness can be a doorway to learning.
  4. 07:28 Greed, hate, delusion - He turns to the Buddha’s diagnosis of suffering and shows how feedback can reveal greed, aversion, and especially the harder-to-see patterns of delusion.
  5. 12:58 Seeing self-view clearly - Using the image of a cardiac stress test, he describes how feedback can expose the architecture of self-view and the sting of identity.
  6. 19:24 Praise and criticism - He explores defensiveness, the challenge of praise, and the vulnerability of hearing approval without immediately converting it into self-protection or shame.
  7. 25:16 Tolerating disorientation - The talk turns to the shakiness that arises when ego is challenged and the practice of staying present without rushing to reestablish ground.
  8. 28:39 Giving feedback well - Matthew summarizes practical guidance for offering feedback skillfully, then connects it to intimacy, dependence, and the importance of a caring context.
  9. 34:52 Feedback and Sangha safety - He closes by emphasizing that open feedback is not only personal practice but a safeguard for Dharma communities, followed by a short closing sit and dedication.
