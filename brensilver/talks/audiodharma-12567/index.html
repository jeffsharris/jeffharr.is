Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Saturday Dharma Talk: Freedom to Love

AudioDharma 50:43 2020-12-12

Matthew Brensilver explores freedom as something that is not only freedom from clinging, but freedom to love. He moves from the Dharma’s map of suffering into a quieter argument that real love is spacious, wise, grief-informed, and not confused with control, sentimentality, or avoidance. Along the way he draws on Tolstoy, Thich Nhat Hanh, the Dalai Lama, Gandhi, Parfit, and a teaching story about dāna to show how practice can soften defensiveness and widen the heart.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Suffering and freedom - Matthew opens with Tolstoy and turns toward the Dharma’s detailed understanding of dukkha and the many forms freedom can take.
  2. 02:39 Many forms of liberation - He reflects on how freedom shows up differently across practitioners, traditions, and personalities, and how the self can be expressed without becoming compulsive or rigid.
  3. 07:24 Freedom to love - The talk shifts from freedom from to freedom to: the liberation that most matters here is the capacity to love more deeply, with awareness understood as a kind of letting go.
  4. 11:19 Love after letting go - He explores non-hatred, emptiness, and the Brahma-viharas, arguing that what remains after relinquishment is ethically responsive and increasingly centered in loving-kindness, compassion, joy, and equanimity.
  5. 17:05 Compassion in public life - Matthew broadens the teaching to culture, ethics, and collective danger, bringing in the Dalai Lama and Gandhi to describe a strong love that can face injustice without collapsing into sentimentality.
  6. 22:52 Wisdom as understanding - He distinguishes real love from future-oriented fantasy, denial of grief, and clinging, then uses Thich Nhat Hanh’s salt-and-river image to show why understanding and compassion expand together.
  7. 32:00 Defensiveness and generosity - Returning to self-view, he argues that letting go reduces defensiveness and makes love more available, then tells a story of dāna that widens generosity to include body, breath, and ordinary life.
  8. 43:57 A surplus to spend - The closing section reflects on practice as a surplus of well-being, asks how that surplus should be spent, and ends with Parfit’s thought on future successors and a wider sense of what makes existence worthwhile.
