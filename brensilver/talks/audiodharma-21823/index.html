Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Dharmette: Believing that Special People Exist

AudioDharma 13:25 2024-12-04

Matthew Brensilver begins with a line from Rachel Kushner and uses it to explore why people imagine that special people exist. From there, he turns toward idealization, childhood dependence, ambivalence, and the difference between revering goodness and deifying a person. The talk ends by linking this to sympathetic joy and to practicing for the right reasons rather than for applause.

Chapters

  1. 00:17 The question of specialness - Brensilver opens with Rachel Kushner’s line about charisma and uses it to ask why the mind wants to believe in special people.
  2. 02:30 Projection and childhood - He looks at the pleasures and costs of idealization, then connects the pull toward special people to early dependence on powerful caretakers.
  3. 04:46 Growing up unresolved - He notes that adulthood does not simply resolve childhood, and suggests that the work is to notice our movement toward pedestal-making without needing to disparage anyone.
  4. 06:57 Reverence without ownership - The talk shifts toward maturity, ambivalence, and a form of reverence aimed at goodness itself rather than at specialness in a person.
  5. 09:49 Sympathetic joy - Brensilver uses mudita as a way of recognizing goodness without comparison, possession, or self-doubt.
  6. 11:06 Practice without applause - He closes with Norman Fischer on practicing because it is right and necessary, and reframes applause as praise for life and goodness more than for any individual.
