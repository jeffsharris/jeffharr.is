Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Emptiness of Self

Dharma Seed 53:12 2024-09-29

Matthew Brensilver explores emptiness of self through a simple practice of listening: to the body, to feeling, and to the impulses that want to fix or control experience. He then turns to questions about innocence, metta, and spiritual friendship, using the Buddha’s exchange with Ananda to suggest that friendship is a way of meeting what is present, not just a relationship between people. In the closing section, he offers a careful reflection on how equanimity can purify compassion, especially when love gets tangled with urgency, fear, or control.

Chapters

  1. 00:55 Listening as practice - He begins by framing meditation as a kind of self-directed empathy and asks what it means to listen to oneself expansively.
  2. 05:12 Empty of self - He develops the teaching that what we perceive as self is empty, inviting a more refined and less intimidated style of awareness.
  3. 09:47 Body and energy - The talk turns toward the body, emotion, and the impulse to solve, alongside practical anchors like full-body breathing and sound.
  4. 15:58 Evenly hovering attention - He emphasizes listening to the whole field of experience without forcing anything particular, while staying poised with pain.
  5. 19:42 Deep listening - Matthew extends the practice into a steady, sustained listening that includes autobiography without freezing the self into a thing.
  6. 28:38 Anchor everywhere - He broadens the instruction so that all phenomena can become the anchor, or even no anchor at all, as awareness runs through everything.
  7. 36:52 Innocence and not-self - In the Q&A, he explains why he uses innocence to point to the basic worthiness of beings and ties that directly to anatta.
  8. 40:30 Friendship with the moment - Drawing on the Buddha and Ananda, and then a Zen anecdote about a robe being happy, he presents spiritual friendship as a general attitude of befriending experience.
  9. 45:07 Equanimity and care - He responds to a question about reverence for others’ pain by describing how equanimity can keep compassion from becoming compulsive, and closes with practical retreat advice.
