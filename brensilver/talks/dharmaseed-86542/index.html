Plasticity, Call off the search, Unification and Love

Dharma Seed 43:15 2024-09-29

Matthew Brensilver reflects on freedom as something more varied than suffering, then organizes the talk around four “neighborhoods” of liberation: plasticity, calling off the search, unification, and love. He moves from psychological flexibility and dependent arising to the fading of compulsive becoming, the healing of inner division, and a more measureless, communal form of love. The talk closes by widening the moral imagination, drawing on Frederick Douglass and Abraham Heschel to suggest that love can be both tender and prophetic.

Chapters

  1. 01:05 Suffering and freedom - Matthew opens with Tolstoy and argues that suffering is patterned and repetitive, while freedom is more individualized and expressive.
  2. 05:18 Four neighborhoods - He introduces the talk’s frame: freedom can be approached through plasticity, calling off the search, unification, and love.
  3. 07:11 Plasticity and flexibility - He develops psychological flexibility as the capacity to respond adaptively, compare it with improvisation, and link it to the brain and retreat practice.
  4. 11:36 Loose self-story - The talk turns to rigidity in self-view, then to Foucault and Ajahn Sucitto as ways of seeing the self/world relation as fluid and dependently arisen.
  5. 16:23 Mind before the world - Matthew uses the Dhammapada to show how intention and wanting shape experience before events seem to happen to us.
  6. 20:23 Calling off the search - Drawing on Adam Phillips and bhava tanha, he describes the exhaustion of endless becoming and the relief of arriving in an imperfect life.
  7. 27:37 Unification and breadth - He explores how practice unifies divided roles, softens secrecy and fragmentation, and reduces shame by bringing more of life into the Dharma.
  8. 34:16 Love without measure - Love is presented as expansive and non-comparative, rooted in care, sangha, finitude, and a life shaped by goodwill.
  9. 39:42 Prophetic vastness - Matthew ends with Frederick Douglass and Abraham Heschel to suggest that love can become morally fearless, with a view vast enough for our shared responsibilities.
