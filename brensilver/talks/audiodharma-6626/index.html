Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Energy

AudioDharma 40:03 2016-04-06

Matthew Brensilver reflects on energy as a central Buddhist virtue, asking what it means to practice against habit without turning effort into strain. He moves from the inertia of ordinary conditioning to self-regulation, right effort, and the question of how to persist with less tension and more wisdom. The talk closes by widening motivation from personal awakening to service and compassion for others.

Chapters

  1. 00:30 What Energy Means - He introduces virya as energy, effort, persistence, and dedication, then links the topic to the ordinary pull of habit and comfort.
  2. 03:16 Against the Stream - Using the Buddha's teaching about going against the stream and the image of anger's honeyed tip, he shows why growth asks us to give up what is immediately sweet.
  3. 06:01 Knowing Isn’t Doing - A Sam Harris quote and a reflection on self-control highlight the gap between understanding the path and actually following it.
  4. 10:37 Self-Control and Fatigue - He brings in Roy Baumeister's model of finite self-regulation to explain why effort can feel depleted and why practice training matters.
  5. 14:09 Effort Without Strain - Matthew compares rigid, pushy practice with a more balanced approach, including Gil Fronsdal's example of Zen grounding amid intensive retreat work.
  6. 20:01 Relaxed Perseverance - He asks what can be stopped rather than added, then quotes Sayadaw Thajna on right effort as wisdom, interest, and relaxed continuity.
  7. 23:41 Why Practice - Nietzsche's 'why' leads into Buddhist motivation: samvega, the urgency born from seeing aging, illness, death, and the limits of ordinary life.
  8. 28:27 What Sustains Practice - Personal memories of early practice and the Buddha's severe language about striving become ways of naming what can keep someone going.
  9. 36:55 For the Sake of Others - The talk culminates in Shantideva's bodhisattva aspiration, reorienting the whole question of effort toward the welfare of others.
