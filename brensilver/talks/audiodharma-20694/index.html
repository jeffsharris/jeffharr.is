Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Dharmette: Crying

AudioDharma 15:08 2024-06-26

Matthew Brensilver reflects quietly and personally on crying in Dharma practice, beginning with his own tears while teaching and asking what they mean. He considers whether tears are disclosure, burden, or manipulation, then turns to research, memory, grief, joy, and the way practice can open both sorrow and tenderness. The talk ends by linking tears to embodied awareness and to the harder work of change, not just feeling.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 A talk on crying - He opens with a personal story that leads into the decision to speak about tears in practice.
  2. 01:32 What tears might mean - He asks whether crying is over-disclosure, burden, manipulation, or displacement, and begins setting aside those possibilities.
  3. 03:13 Words and research - A Beckett line and a brief look at crying research frame tears as both expressive and bodily.
  4. 05:02 Need and helplessness - He notes the interpersonal signal in infant crying and reflects on helplessness as a common source of adult tears.
  5. 06:15 Practice opens grief - He describes how Dharma practice, especially metta and compassion, brought tears tied to grief for the suffering of the past.
  6. 08:03 Teaching from the body - Ajahn Sucitto's remark about the path bringing more laughter and weepiness leads into teaching as embodied presence rather than performance.
  7. 09:56 Grief, solidarity, goodness - He turns to impermanence, social suffering, and the way tears can also express solidarity and public grieving.
  8. 12:00 Moral elevation - He explores the contrast between suffering and peace, and how perceiving goodness can lift the heart and support loving-kindness.
  9. 13:51 Tears are not enough - He closes with Baldwin's warning that crying is easier than change, bringing the talk back to body, practice, and action.
