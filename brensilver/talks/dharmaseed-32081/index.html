Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

The Intensity of Being a Teenager

Dharma Seed 44:39 2015-12-30

Matthew Brensilver begins with the pressure young people feel to fit a ready-made life, then shifts into a wider reflection on mindfulness as a way of hearing what the heart actually cares about. He returns again and again to the intensity of being human, especially the experience of adolescence, and shows how practice can help us stop personalizing that intensity as a mistake. In the second half, he turns to love, sexuality, non-harming, and the limits of what relationships can provide, ending with a quieter acceptance of uncertainty and the aliveness it can hold.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening and orientation - He opens with a personal story about being a bad salesman and uses it to clarify that he is not trying to sell Buddhism, only to support honest looking.
  2. 02:40 Missing our own life - He warns against taking on borrowed values and uses the Kabat-Zinn quote to show how easily people can wake up inside a life that no longer feels like theirs.
  3. 06:51 Listening inward - Mindfulness is framed as a steady, honest conversation with ourselves, one that can make experience feel more safe and less divided.
  4. 11:54 The intensity of being human - He turns to the basic intensity of sensory life, fear, and adolescence, emphasizing that this pressure is woven into being human rather than evidence that something is wrong with us.
  5. 19:22 Digesting experience - He describes practice as a way of not adding more intensity, but allowing the heart to digest pain, memory, and the past with wisdom and kindness.
  6. 22:44 Love and vulnerability - He moves into the importance of love, relationship, and sexuality, bringing in Adam Phillips to show how companionship and fear of aliveness can become entangled.
  7. 26:54 Eros and metta - He reflects on first love, then distinguishes attraction from kindness and suggests that mature love brings eros and metta together.
  8. 30:57 Clinging and harm - He explores how love becomes confused with clinging, why the precepts matter in intimate life, and how non-harming applies to sexuality and relationship.
  9. 34:55 Freedom, uncertainty, aliveness - He closes by stressing that love means honoring the other person's freedom, accepting that relationships cannot end all seeking, and making peace with uncertainty as part of a more alive life.
