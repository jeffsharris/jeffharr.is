Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Love as the Default Position of the Mind

Dharma Seed 50:36 2022-05-19

Matthew Brensilver explores love as the mind’s natural resting place, moving from the tension between acceptance and change to the way practice can free up tenderness rather than manufacture it. He draws on Bohr, Suzuki Roshi, Maslow, Levinas, Joseph Goldstein, Thich Nhat Hanh, and others to show how insight into impermanence, not-self, and suffering can deepen love. The talk settles into a quiet argument that practice is less about fixing the self than loosening clinging so attention, care, and equanimity become more available.

Chapters

  1. 00:07 Opposites can both be true - Brensilver opens with Bohr and Suzuki Roshi to frame the paradox that practice asks for both radical acceptance and real change.
  2. 01:31 Acceptance without bargaining - He warns against making practice into self-improvement or a hidden deal with the universe, and shifts toward accepting the totality of conditions that make up a life.
  3. 06:49 Growth points beyond self - Using Maslow, he argues that self-actualization and freedom are not the final point but a transition that erases itself and points toward love.
  4. 11:12 Seeing as love - Levinas and the language of deep attention support his claim that to truly see another person is already close to loving them.
  5. 15:50 From states to traits - He distinguishes momentary meditation states from the longer shaping of character, quoting Joseph Goldstein to stress that practice is about what becomes habitual.
  6. 20:12 Home base of the heart - The talk turns to the default position of attention and the Brahma-viharas as dwellings where the mind can rest in kindness, compassion, joy, and equanimity.
  7. 24:05 Insight frees love - Thich Nhat Hanh and the three characteristics are used to show that understanding dukkha, anicca, and anatta does not diminish love but loosens it.
  8. 29:12 Grief and non-clinging - A passage from Katherine Schultz and reflections on grief deepen the treatment of loss, while the talk argues that emptiness and self-love are not in conflict.
  9. 38:55 The cost of selfing - Brensilver examines defensiveness, shame, moralism, and autobiography as forms of clinging that harden the self and obstruct love.
