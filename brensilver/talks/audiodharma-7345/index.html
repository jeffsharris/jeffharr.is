Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Rhythms and Refinements of Practice

AudioDharma 43:12 2016-12-18

Matthew Brensilver reflects on the long, uneven unfolding of Dharma practice: the early sense that something meaningful is being touched, the surprise of being changed in ways that are not always visible, and the gradual refinement of motivation over time. He moves from personal story into a broader frame about unknowns, nonlinearity, and the way practice can deepen into love, service, and non-regret. The talk also draws on Leonard Cohen, Donald Rumsfeld, Rilke, Shinzen Young, and brief stories about other teachers to illuminate different facets of the path.

Chapters

  1. 00:43 A wish for peace - Matthew opens with Leonard Cohen’s "Treaty" and uses its longing for reconciliation as a frame for the talk.
  2. 02:13 First contact with practice - He рассказывает how an early friendship and a vague sense of recognition first drew him toward meditation without yet making him an earnest practitioner.
  3. 05:38 Early retreat and depth - He recalls early practice in the Shambhala world, a retreat with Galen Ferguson, and the emotional sense that the Dharma was far larger than he had imagined.
  4. 08:01 Unknowns on the path - Using Rumsfeld’s language, he describes practice as something that reveals what we did not know we did not know, and insists the path must be walked rather than imagined.
  5. 11:29 Subtle transformation - Matthew contrasts dramatic life changes with quieter inner shifts, including a quoted passage from Jonathan Franzen about insight changing everything without changing anything external.
  6. 14:37 Recalibrating intention - He questions the fantasy of arrival, compares practice to a spacecraft constantly correcting course, and describes how motivations for practice evolve and get outgrown.
  7. 18:31 Practice for others - As the circle of concern widens, practice becomes less self-referential and more explicitly oriented toward the welfare of others and toward seeing the uses and misuses of teachings.
  8. 24:59 Progress without arrival - He says there is real progress, but it grows out of letting go and not needing to get anywhere, and he names the non-linear, sometimes disorienting character of the path.
  9. 28:16 A loose Dharma map - Matthew summarizes Shinzen Young’s four-part model of growth, while warning that any map can become another ego project if held too tightly.
