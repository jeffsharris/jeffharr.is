Dharmette: Between Everything Means Everything and Nothing Means Anything

AudioDharma 13:36 2024-07-17

Matthew Brensilver reflects on how disappointment with institutions can turn into either cynicism or a deeper, more spacious disenchantment. He traces a path through heartbreak, nihilism, and the Buddhist language of samsara and dukkha toward a view that is wide enough to hold suffering without making everything absolute. The talk closes by linking that vastness to equanimity: a big, peaceful love that no longer tries to control what cannot be controlled.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Early disappointment - He begins with adolescent disappointment in institutions and the anger, hope, and heartbreak that came with it.
  2. 02:01 Dukkha at the group level - He connects those feelings to the first noble truth and describes how suffering shows up not only individually but within communities and systems.
  3. 03:59 Mesmerized by samsara - He explains how being caught in samsara makes appearances feel overly real, narrowing the mind into strategy, control, and the wish for a fixed solution.
  4. 05:57 Beyond nihilism - He names the pull toward 'nothing means anything' and warns that nihilism can hide inside what looks like disenchantment.
  5. 07:26 The universe's view - Using Sedgwick's phrase 'the point of view of the universe,' he turns toward a perspective grounded in vastness rather than reaction.
  6. 09:01 Emptiness and poignancy - He describes a view that knows change, suffering, and immeasurable time while still not denying the force of lived experience.
  7. 10:40 Love without control - He offers a gentle image of holding suffering like a wise elder and says love becomes heartbreaking when it is tied to control.
  8. 12:22 Equanimity as love - He closes by framing equanimity as love in the face of helplessness, where the heart becomes bigger, calmer, and less confined.
