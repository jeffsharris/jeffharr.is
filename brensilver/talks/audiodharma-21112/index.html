Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Moral Elevation, Moral Disgust and the Vastness Required to Open to Dukkha

AudioDharma 36:11 2024-08-17

Matthew Brensilver explores how Dharma practice can sensitize us to goodness without losing contact with suffering. He moves from moral elevation and the force of ordinary courage into moral disgust, asking how care can stay clear of hatred, cynicism, and political reactivity. The talk gradually widens into a reflection on vastness, mortality, and impermanence, ending with Ajahn Chah’s tea cup as a way to hold life with gratitude and less fear.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Opening the Heart - Matthew introduces kama muta and moral elevation as ways the heart is moved by goodness.
  2. 01:02 Wesley Autry's Courage - He tells the subway rescue story and uses it to show how ordinary bravery can evoke and transmit moral beauty.
  3. 04:05 Sensitivity to Goodness - The talk turns toward metta, Rumi, and the way devotion can make goodness feel ordinary, embodied, and shareable.
  4. 06:53 Moral Disgust - Matthew contrasts moral elevation with moral disgust and warns that ethical feeling can be pulled toward aversion and brutality.
  5. 14:13 Prophetic Speech - Using Frederick Douglass and Heschel, he considers moral language that burns past ordinary conscience without collapsing into moralizing.
  6. 19:22 Distance from Rumination - He brings in self-distancing research to distinguish helpful reflection from looping pain and to suggest a wider perspective on suffering.
  7. 24:25 Widening the Horizon - The talk expands into global care, mortality, and Kore-eda's After Life as a glimpse of being less trapped by becoming.
  8. 30:18 Already Broken - Matthew ties cessation of becoming to the broken tea cup teaching and shows how impermanence can open the heart to love and gratitude.
