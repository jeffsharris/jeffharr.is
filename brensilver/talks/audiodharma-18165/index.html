Dharmette: Reflections on Mortality (1/5)

AudioDharma 12:27 2023-04-24

Matthew Brensilver reflects on mortality as a form of practice: something to learn from without either exaggerating it or flattening it. He moves from Ajahn Chah’s reminder that everything teaches us, to the idea that finitude gives life meaning, and then into personal stories about a recent family death, a year-to-live practice, and a difficult conversation with a Holocaust survivor neighbor. The talk settles on humility before the mystery of death, and on how mortality can inform love, ethics, concentration, and the sense of self.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Learning from life - Matthew opens with Ajahn Chah’s teaching that everything can teach us, and frames the talk around learning wisely from experience.
  2. 00:45 Overlearning and underlearning - He explains the danger of making one experience mean too much, or not enough, and names the middle way the practice asks for.
  3. 02:36 Mortality as teacher - The theme turns explicitly to death, with a focus on how mortality can soften or harden us depending on how we relate to it.
  4. 04:53 Finitude and meaning - Using Kafka, he explores the claim that life’s ending is part of what gives it significance rather than merely taking meaning away.
  5. 06:13 A personal confrontation - He shares memories of doing Stephen Levine’s Year to Live practice and being challenged by a Holocaust survivor neighbor not to treat death abstractly.
  6. 07:47 Death’s ordinary nearness - The talk widens into family history and aging, showing how death can feel both everyday and sacred at once.
  7. 09:33 Mystery and humility - Matthew revisits the phrase 'a good death starts now,' then lets a listener’s question soften the idea into something more modest and open-ended.
  8. 11:43 How death informs practice - He closes by naming the week’s theme: letting mortality inform samadhi, sila, self-understanding, and love.
