Sīla, Samādhi, Pañña

Dharma Seed 53:57 2018-10-22

Matthew Brensilver frames the Buddhist path through sīla, samādhi, and paññā as three inseparable expressions of one training, grounded in love rather than moral performance. He moves from the ordinary hindrances and the Four Noble Truths into a patient account of ethics, collectedness, and wisdom, using images of refuge, the face, and ownership to show how practice softens fear and self-clinging. The talk steadily widens from personal suffering to the way a heart becomes safer, clearer, and more available to others.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Hindrances and response - He opens with the common retreat hindrances and gives a concise set of responses to each, setting a practical tone.
  2. 03:51 Four Noble Truths - He recounts a conference with the Dalai Lama and Ajahn Amaro's medical framing of diagnosis, cause, prognosis, and treatment for suffering.
  3. 08:12 One fruit, three trainings - He names the Eightfold Path in its three baskets and uses Ajahn Chah's mango image to show that sīla, samādhi, and paññā are one fruit.
  4. 11:49 Ethics as refuge - He develops sīla as a wish to be safe for others, drawing on the Buddha's language of refuge and the softening that comes from self-care and direct encounter with the face of another.
  5. 19:14 Self-judgment loosens - He turns to the suffering of self-hatred, reading Heather Havrilesky as a pointed invitation to leave the imagined better self behind and rest in what is already here.
  6. 29:31 Collecting the mind - He explains samādhi as gathered stability rather than mere concentration, and shows how settling the mind gives temporary refuge from the relentlessness of change.
  7. 35:52 Thought and narrative - He questions the urgency of thought, noting how the mind builds narratives for security and how precepts create enough safety to begin letting go.
  8. 42:08 Wisdom and non-ownership - He arrives at paññā through the surprise of practice, the loosening of delusion, and a critique of ownership as a root of suffering.
  9. 47:05 Gift, identity, release - He closes by describing life as generosity rather than possession, letting go of identity as refuge, and returning to the simple work of living, serving, and dying.
