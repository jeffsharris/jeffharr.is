Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Friday Dharma Talk

AudioDharma 49:38 2024-08-30

Matthew Brensilver explores the First and Second Noble Truths through the felt difference between craving and desire, arguing that suffering is often normalized before it is clearly seen. He moves from personal reflection and humor into neuroscience, psychoanalytic writing, and Buddhist sources to show how wanting, clinging, and a divided life keep us looped in frustration. The talk turns practical near the end, emphasizing patience, equanimity, dharma pleasures, and the possibility of loosening subtler forms of selfing.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Normalizing Dukkha - Matthew begins by questioning how people report being happy while living with a low-grade baseline of distress that often goes unnoticed.
  2. 02:16 Seeing Suffering Clearly - He frames practice as comprehending suffering rather than acclimatizing to it, and links that to the Buddha’s way of defining freedom by what it is free from.
  3. 05:05 Compulsivity And Cause - The talk shifts from pain to compulsivity, presenting the Second Noble Truth as a cause-and-conditions teaching that asks for a path, not just better advice.
  4. 09:43 Craving And Desire - Matthew distinguishes craving from aspiration, describing craving as a fever that never quite reaches satisfaction while desire can be oriented, sober, and real.
  5. 16:32 Wanting Versus Liking - He brings in Kent Berridge to show that wanting and enjoyment are separable, then uses Ajahn Sucitto to deepen the classic teaching on thirst for sense input, becoming, and non-becoming.
  6. 20:34 Unlived Lives - A long quotation from Adam Phillips expands the emotional texture of craving as division, grievance, and a life organized around what might have been.
  7. 27:04 Patience And Insight - Matthew turns toward practice: noticing the loop of craving and heartbreak, developing dharma pleasures, and letting tranquility make suffering more visible.
  8. 32:26 Equanimity And Subtle Clinging - He describes equanimity, radical permission to feel, and the way language and thought can orchestrate clinging before ordinary grasping is even noticed.
  9. 44:43 Relaxing The Self - The closing section points to the loosening of subtler selfing, with a Zen line about the disappearance of the subject and a final invitation to sit quietly.
