Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Grief, Love and Groundlessness

Dharma Seed 53:49 2016-04-18

Matthew Brensilver explores grief as something inseparable from love, while also asking how Dharma practice changes what grief does to the heart. He moves between Buddhist teaching, poetry, psychology, and stories from the room to describe loss as ordinary, disorienting, and sometimes deeply transformative. The talk is less about techniques than about learning to stay honest with impermanence, tenderness, and the limits of control.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Love and Loss - Matthew opens by linking love with the inevitability of grief and setting the emotional tone with Leonard Cohen.
  2. 01:45 Poetry of Grief - He uses Lorca and Malidoma Somé to describe grief as enclosing, socially real, and needing to be grieved rather than bypassed.
  3. 03:49 Impermanence and Honesty - The talk turns to Buddhist teachings on impermanence, with a caution against applying letting-go in a mechanical way.
  4. 07:01 What the Buddha Grieves - Matthew reflects on the tradition’s language beyond grief and complicates it by wondering whether awakened life can still include sorrow.
  5. 09:25 Belonging and Attachment - He brings in psychology and the human need to belong, arguing that avoiding love is not a satisfying answer to loss.
  6. 13:52 Loss That Stays - Mary Oliver, Naomi Shihab Nye, and personal reflections deepen the sense that grief is the enduring presence of absence.
  7. 18:10 Making Room for Heartbreak - Through Freud and a scene from Louie, he argues that heartbreak can be part of the good of having loved, and that Dharma changes pain more by preventing harm than by eliminating hurt.
  8. 27:30 Purifying the Grief - Matthew describes grief as something that can be distilled of confusion and resentment, bringing the heart close to simplicity and groundlessness.
  9. 34:24 Questions, Practice, and Closing - In dialogue with listeners, he addresses mourning, hospice, death, and reading suggestions, then closes with a short blessing about courage, kindness, and greater love.
