Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

The Role of Sila, Samadhi and Panna in Interpersonal Life

AudioDharma 58:05 2019-05-18

Matthew Brensilver explores how sila, samadhi, and panna show up in everyday relationship, not just on retreat. He begins with learning as a lifelong, whole-life path, then turns to vulnerability, speech, feedback, and the ways ego hardens around contact with others. The talk closes by showing how loosening self-view can make us more trustworthy, more open, and more able to love without fixing or clinging.

Chapters

  1. 00:31 Whole-life learning - Krishnamurti’s quote frames Dharma as an ongoing process of learning that includes the full range of life.
  2. 01:33 Insight that generalizes - Matthew reflects on how understanding often remains partial and how practice asks us to carry insight into ordinary behavior.
  3. 08:04 Vulnerability and dependence - Using Martha Nussbaum, he describes human incompleteness, emotion, and the pressure that leads to clinging and self-protection.
  4. 11:01 Relationship amplifies practice - He looks at groups, friendship, competition, and speech as places where both wholesome qualities and defilements become stronger.
  5. 19:36 Trust, speech, and feedback - The teaching turns toward becoming a refuge for others, speaking carefully, and staying open to correction without collapsing into defensiveness.
  6. 29:08 Samadhi as agility - Rebecca Solnit’s emphasis on equality and listening leads into a discussion of steadiness, subtle mind states, and staying with experience instead of story.
  7. 37:16 Less self-curation - Matthew explores how relaxing self-view makes relationships less tense and more nourishing, including the role of playful honesty and being seen.
  8. 49:59 Not creating others - Chuang Tzu’s empty boat and Ajahn Sumedho’s advice to 'not create them' deepen the point that love stays open when people are not frozen into fixed identities.
  9. 56:05 Closing blessing - He ends with a brief sit and a blessing that these practices lead to wisdom, love, and deeper enjoyment of others.
