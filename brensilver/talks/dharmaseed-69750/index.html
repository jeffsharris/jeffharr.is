Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Memory, prediction, Bharatanha, and the present

Dharma Seed 47:18 2022-03-04

Matthew Brensilver explores how the mind becomes caught in memory and prediction, and how that catch shapes our sense of self, time, and safety. He moves from the felt experience of boredom, pressure, and unfinished life into a quieter argument: practice is not about forcing attention into the present, but about becoming available to it through awareness, love, and a more purified view. Along the way he draws on Thich Nhat Hanh, Elie Wiesel, Buddhist teachings on the three characteristics, and a brief neuroscience reference to show how past and future are woven together.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the room - Matthew begins with gratitude for the retreat setting and frames the talk as an inquiry into what it means to be human and to see that clearly.
  2. 02:15 Caught in time - He describes boredom, pressure, and urgency as different ways of being trapped in time, then asks what it really means to be present.
  3. 05:50 Availability, not force - Using Thich Nhat Hanh as an example, he contrasts trying to fit the mind into the present with becoming available to it through embodiment and practice.
  4. 08:00 Memory and the past - He turns to memory, the story of self, and the sense of an undigested past, arguing that practice blesses and transforms what remains sharp or sticky.
  5. 13:11 Rewriting the story - With Elie Wiesel and then the Buddha, he shows how memory can be held differently, how autobiography is seen from a view, and how Dharma rewrites the story of me.
  6. 20:13 Digesting experience - Matthew develops the image of practice as cooking or digesting life, where staying with difficult moments smooths the sharp edges of the past and allows more integration.
  7. 26:32 Prediction and survival - He explains how the future pulls the mind forward through safety-seeking, vigilance, and constant simulation, and how that habit is rooted in past experience.
  8. 31:22 Becoming and fear - A neuroscience reference supports his point that remembering and imagining use overlapping machinery, and he connects this to bhava tanha, the craving to become.
  9. 37:22 Letting the future go - He closes by describing retreat as a safe place to release future-oriented grasping, rest in the three characteristics, and discover the present as open, porous, and bottomless.
