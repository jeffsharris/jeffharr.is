Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Delusion

AudioDharma 42:16 2018-06-10

Matthew Brensilver explores delusion as one of the three roots of suffering, distinguishing it from greed and aversion and showing why it is often harder to notice. He moves from personality tendencies and everyday self-deception into mindfulness, humility, and the willingness to be wrong, then closes by emphasizing feedback, ethical conduct, and the need to become safer for ourselves and others. The talk stays practical throughout, with examples from relationships, ambivalence, and the subtle ways the mind rationalizes its own patterns.

Chapters

  1. 00:21 Three Roots - Matthew introduces greed, aversion, and delusion as a simple Buddhist map of behavior and personality, while noting its limits.
  2. 02:06 Delusion in Daily Life - He reflects on his own aversive temperament and explains how all three strategies are understandable attempts to manage insecurity and pain.
  3. 06:38 What Delusion Feels Like - The talk turns to delusion as a subtler root, using the fish-and-water image to show how what surrounds us can be hardest to see.
  4. 11:41 Humility and Self-Deception - Matthew describes practice as a gradual thawing into clearer contact with experience, where self-deception loosens and humility deepens.
  5. 16:56 Willingness to Know - He focuses on shame, not-knowing, and the need for genuine curiosity, cautioning that inquiry can backfire if it is not sincere.
  6. 21:50 Wise View and Rationalizing - The talk moves into practical ways of approaching delusion, including wise view, a focus on suffering and its absence, and the ways delusion rationalizes greed and aversion.
  7. 27:11 Ambivalence and Confusion - Through a story of hurt and a quote about rationalizing animals, he shows how delusion builds narratives to avoid pain and keep ambivalence in place.
  8. 33:05 Signs and Antidotes - Matthew names cues such as numbness, ethical drift, and ruptures in life narratives, then offers impermanence, non-fighting, and taking feedback as supports for practice.
  9. 40:56 Lowering the Waterline - He closes by turning from spiritual heights to the depths of confusion, asking that the Dharma spread through ordinary life so we can be safe for ourselves and others.
