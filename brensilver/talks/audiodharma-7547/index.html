Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Clinging, letting go and the path of sila, samadhi and panna

AudioDharma 47:01 2017-02-25

Matthew Brensilver explores letting go as the thread running through meditation, ethics, relationship, and wisdom. Starting from a Joseph Goldstein anecdote, he asks what it means to live without clinging, then moves through ownership, self-concern, and the way love can be separated from possession. The talk closes with classical sutta imagery and a quiet case that when nothing is taken as "mine," experience can feel more open, spacious, and responsive.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 A Dharma in one sentence - Matthew opens with a story about Joseph Goldstein and uses it to frame the talk around his own short answer: the mind not clinging to anything.
  2. 04:12 Mindfulness as non-clinging - He turns to the Satipatthana Sutta and the basic instruction to attend to body, feelings, and mind without clinging to the world.
  3. 06:21 The habit of ownership - Using a quoted reflection on ownership, he argues that possessiveness is a deep mental habit underlying greed, hatred, and suffering.
  4. 10:48 Dana and the feel of giving - A story from Michelle McDonald about an elderly Burmese teacher and a pile of fruit becomes a vivid example of dana as complete giving rather than holding.
  5. 15:22 Love without control - He distinguishes love from clinging, especially in relationship to pleasure and self-image, and suggests that the wisest self-love may be releasing our fixation on who we think we are.
  6. 20:25 Security and intimacy - Quotations from Bhikkhu Bodhi, Stephen Mitchell, Rilke, and Kernberg deepen the point that clinging arises from insecurity, while love honors the freedom of the other.
  7. 27:15 Open air and mortality - Matthew reflects on living more openly when fear of death loosens, and uses Derek Parfit’s image of the glass tunnel disappearing into open air.
  8. 30:56 The threefold path - He briefly lays out sila, samadhi, and panna as a path animated by letting go, showing how practice softens grasping, anger, and the urge to engineer experience.
  9. 36:46 Nothing lands - A Majjhima Nikaya passage and the image of a sunbeam that does not land become the closing meditation on unhooked awareness, space, and a life that can keep releasing what is not needed.
