Matthew Brensilver, Tuere Sala: Tuere Sala and Matthew Brensilver in dialogue about wisdom

Dharma Seed 54:53 2020-10-08

This dialogue turns on a quiet question: what is wisdom, really, and how does it mature in practice? Tuere Sala and Matthew Brensilver move from an opening poem about trees as listeners into a grounded conversation about generosity, retreat practice, suffering, and the way insight becomes lived understanding rather than theory. They close by returning to poetry and to the need for clear signals, mutual recognition, and wakefulness in relationship.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening with listening - Tuere opens with a poem about trees as quiet listeners and uses it to frame the talk as an act of generous, nonjudgmental attention.
  2. 02:58 Dharma as generosity - She reflects on the unusual role of teachers in the West, the awkwardness of asking for support, and generosity as the basis of the retreat container.
  3. 07:59 From anecdote to inquiry - Matthew introduces the dialogue format, explains the missed ninth parami, and sets up wisdom as the shared theme for the conversation.
  4. 11:38 Morrison on wisdom - A Toni Morrison quote helps name the difference between data, knowledge, and wisdom, and the retreat is described as collecting lived data inwardly.
  5. 14:01 What wisdom means - Tuere and Matthew explore wisdom as a process of hearing, testing, and integrating what does not fit our usual view of life, including desire, happiness, and the limits of equanimity.
  6. 24:33 Learning through suffering - They turn toward suffering as the place where clarity and resilience are formed, drawing on Ajahn Chah, the Buddha, and a story about staying with anxiety instead of escaping it.
  7. 34:04 The redemptive path - The conversation deepens into the idea that wisdom and love converge when we can stay with difficulty, trust the path, and let suffering become compost rather than something to fix.
  8. 43:35 Three layers of suffering - Tuere offers a practical map of suffering, impulsive reactivity, and the grumbling mind, using the image of ripples on a pond to show where practice can actually intervene.
  9. 50:33 Closing with clear signals - Tuere reads William Stafford's poem about mutual recognition and wakefulness, and the talk ends with a brief silence and thanks.
