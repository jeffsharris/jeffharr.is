Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Brief Post-Election Reflections

Dharma Seed 16:37 2024-11-06

Matthew Brensilver offers a brief, quiet reflection on waking up after the 2024 election, beginning with the ground of practice and spiritual friendship before moving into grief, uncertainty, and the thin line between stability and collapse. He draws on Michelle McDonald, Mother Teresa, Leonard Cohen, the Buddha, and Shinzen Young to explore how heartbreak can deepen care without slipping into naivete or brittle equanimity. The talk gradually turns from personal processing to the question of what a more mature, nonviolent form of engagement might become.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Arriving Together - He opens by settling into practice and naming the room as a place of spiritual friendship rather than political agreement.
  2. 01:19 What This Moment Means - He names the election as a special moment marked by greed, hate, and delusion, and grounds the gathering in a shared orientation to suffering.
  3. 02:56 Thinness and Uncertainty - Using Michelle McDonald’s language, he reflects on the thin line between okay and very bad, and on how anicca meets shock in the body and mind.
  4. 05:15 Grief and Naivete - He moves through cynicism, collapse, and grief, then contrasts naive kindness with a love that knows how easily the heart can be broken.
  5. 07:09 Heartbreak That Opens - Mother Teresa and Leonard Cohen become touchstones for the idea that sorrow can widen compassion and let more light in.
  6. 09:16 Sorting Pain - He distinguishes pain rooted in love from pain colored by righteousness or aversion, and reflects on how practice loosens self-absorption.
  7. 11:39 Comprehending Dukkha - He asks what it means to truly comprehend suffering, including how to hold power, helplessness, and a heart that does not collapse.
  8. 13:52 Beyond Equanimity - He questions whether the dharma is the same on a day like this, then turns to the possibility that care must mature beyond complacency.
  9. 15:56 A Nonviolent Protest - He ends with the hope that his whole life can become a nonviolent protest, then invites listeners to keep practicing.
