Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Facets of Mindfulness

AudioDharma 15:16 2015-02-25

Matthew Brensilver explores mindfulness as more than present-time awareness, arguing that equanimity and non-judgment are essential to its benefits. He draws on a research study that found people high in awareness but low in equanimity did worse on measures like worry and depression, then turns to retreat practice and the slow ripening of an open heart. The talk becomes a quiet case for staying with discomfort as a path toward less fear, more confidence, and a deeper capacity for love.

Chapters

  1. 00:30 Mindfulness and research - Brensilver introduces the scientific interest in mindfulness and the common idea of it as a route to well-being and brain training.
  2. 01:51 Two facets of mindfulness - He distinguishes present-time awareness from the accepting, non-judging quality that completes mindfulness.
  3. 02:49 The cluster study - He describes a study of roughly 940 people that grouped mindfulness profiles and looked for differences in well-being.
  4. 04:59 Awareness without equanimity - The surprising result is that people high in observing but low in equanimity fared worse than the other groups.
  5. 06:51 Why equanimity matters - He reflects on how a tense, judgmental relationship to experience can coexist with attention, and why equanimity is its necessary complement.
  6. 08:43 Ripening through retreat - Using Michelle McDonald’s teaching, he frames retreat as a place where equanimity matures slowly through repeated cycling of experience.
  7. 10:39 Renunciation and courage - He names equanimity as a deep gesture of renunciation, one that requires seeing how strongly we resist discomfort.
  8. 12:54 From pain to love - The talk closes by linking equanimity with softening the heart, reducing fear, and increasing empathy, metta, and inner safety.
