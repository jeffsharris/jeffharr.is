Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Dukkha as Ambivalence in Relational Life

Dharma Seed 46:16 2025-04-18

Matthew Brensilver explores dukkha through the lens of ambivalence in love, friendship, family, and group life. He moves from comic and psychoanalytic references to a practical Buddhist account of equanimity: how to stay with mixed feeling, resist the urge to control, and distinguish care from clinging. The talk closes by returning to compassion as love in the face of helplessness, and to the difficulty of living with the imperfection of others without hardening or taking over.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 A Comic Opening - A satirical newspaper headline about a 'better soulmate' introduces the unstable wish for others to complete us.
  2. 02:31 Love and Lack - Lacan and the First Noble Truth frame intimacy as a place where projection, disappointment, and real beauty all meet.
  3. 05:54 Pain and Disconnection - The Buddha’s back pain and a bleak radio story point to the unavoidable suffering built into relationship and group life.
  4. 09:29 What Ambivalence Means - Matthew defines dukkha as ambivalence and uses examples from emotion and mood to show how mixed feeling is ordinary.
  5. 14:30 Freud and Mixed Feeling - Drawing on Adam Phillips and Freud, he argues that love and hate, trust and suspicion, self-criticism and self-love are intertwined.
  6. 17:51 Equanimity Widens - Equanimity is presented as a way of widening attention, softening tunnel vision, and making room for what is actually present.
  7. 24:57 Acceptance in Practice - He outlines recognition, allowing, non-judging, and non-attachment as a practical way to stay with sadness and grasping without acting them out.
  8. 29:01 Relationship and Dependence - Retreat, regression, and the wish for safety reveal how dependence brings control impulses, fear, and the need to revise old stories.
  9. 39:44 Compassion Without Control - A story of former monastics and a final distinction between compassion and control lead into a quiet close about caring, helplessness, and stillness.
