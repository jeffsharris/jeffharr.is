Working with Anger: Part 1

AudioDharma 49:11 2013-04-18

Matthew Brensilver opens with listener questions and uses them to frame anger as something to meet carefully rather than suppress or act out. He then moves through Buddhist perspectives on accountability, self-identity, and the way anger can feel persuasive while still obscuring the full picture. Later he brings in Thich Nhat Hanh, Ajahn Chah, the Dalai Lama, and Shantideva to show both the hazards of anger and the possibility of working with it skillfully and with care.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening questions - Listener questions set up the talk around acted-out anger, suppression, and the experience of being around other people's anger.
  2. 01:43 Not rehearsing anger - Matthew introduces Thich Nhat Hanh's warning against rehearsing anger while still avoiding suppression.
  3. 03:39 Impact and response - He explores the gap between what happens to us and our reaction, and the search for a middle path between bottling anger and acting it out.
  4. 09:00 Responsibility without self-blame - The talk turns toward self-anger, the pain of greater mindfulness, and the reminder that we are responsible for our problems without being identical with them.
  5. 14:36 Not an angry person - Using Ajahn Chah, Matthew questions the identity story of being an angry person and places anger within the wider context of suffering and not-self.
  6. 18:30 Natural, but costly - He acknowledges that anger can sometimes be useful, especially around injustice, while also describing its limits and its role in human and historical suffering.
  7. 27:28 Shantideva and patience - A Shantideva passage on patience deepens the case that anger erodes trust, safety, and virtue, and Pema Chodron's reading softens the tone into practical self-admonition.
  8. 34:53 Seeing anger directly - Matthew describes anger's 'honeyed tip and poisoned root,' then explains how to untangle thought, feeling, and self-image in practice.
  9. 45:53 Closing reflection - He ends by inviting direct mindfulness of anger, followed by a short period of quiet and a closing blessing toward non-harming and peace.
