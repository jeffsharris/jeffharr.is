Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Grace, Gravity, Surrender and Silence

Dharma Seed 43:37 2024-09-27

Matthew Brensilver builds this talk around Leonard Cohen’s image of living between gravity and grace, then turns it into a practical Dharma inquiry into suffering, helplessness, effort, and surrender. He moves from biography and literary quotation into meditation practice, asking how faith, receptivity, and silence change our relationship to control. The talk gradually settles into a quieter argument: that letting go is not passivity, but a way of meeting impermanence without losing the heart.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening with Cohen - Matthew opens with Leonard Cohen’s biography and an unexpected encounter that frames the evening’s central image.
  2. 01:33 Gravity and grace - He reads Cohen’s language about falling bodies and uses it to introduce the felt reality of dukkha and grace.
  3. 03:47 What we do not own - The talk widens into groundlessness, the Buddha’s teaching on non-ownership, and grace as something found within gravity.
  4. 06:54 Falling and surrender - Matthew explores why we resist falling, how practice reveals our hidden efforts, and why surrender can begin to feel like power.
  5. 10:37 Augustine and receptivity - He brings in Augustine as a parallel for surrender, then contrasts that devotional language with Buddhist receptivity and willing fallenness.
  6. 14:57 Effort, helplessness, faith - The talk turns to effort, willfulness, and the aversive experience of helplessness, which he suggests is only bearable through love and grace.
  7. 21:10 Letting go gradually - Using Ajahn Sucitto’s rope image, Matthew describes awakening as a paced descent into freedom, trust, and the relaxation of control.
  8. 28:10 Silence and safety - He connects surrender with silence, sangha, and safety, showing how fear drives meaning-making and the need to narrate experience.
  9. 37:17 Falling into freedom - The closing section reframes falling as a way to live in impermanence, ending with the claim that surrender can make us more effective rather than less.
