Dharmette: Self-Esteem Preoccupation as Symptom

AudioDharma 15:56 2025-04-02

Matthew Brensilver reflects on self-esteem as a symptom rather than a solution, drawing on social psychology and Dharma to question the habit of measuring worth. He moves from scientific conversation and familiar retreat anecdotes into a quieter argument that suffering, fear, and need deprivation generate self-preoccupation, and that practice can soften that grip. The talk closes by pointing toward ordinariness, ethical living, refuge, and the possibility that it is enough simply to be a person.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Opening the conversation - Matthew begins with a few light remarks about the Dharma path and its conversation with science.
  2. 01:26 Science and Dharma - He recalls a Mind and Life conference with Richard Davidson to show how scientific and contemplative approaches are learning from each other.
  3. 02:29 Self-esteem as symptom - Using Brown and Ryan, he frames self-esteem preoccupation as a sign of unmet needs rather than a healthy foundation.
  4. 04:37 Not-self without idealism - He turns to not-self as a way of seeing things as they are, not as a moral project or spiritual achievement.
  5. 06:23 Ordinary worthiness - A memory of Jack Kornfield leads into a gentler understanding of dignity rooted in ordinariness rather than specialness.
  6. 07:49 Selfing and pain - Matthew argues that selfing is an effect of pain, fear, and grasping, not a fixed cause standing apart from them.
  7. 10:06 Thawing the wound - He describes a gradual thaw in which heaviness, shame, and unmet need are met more tenderly.
  8. 12:07 Ethics and refuge - Ethical conduct and taking refuge are presented as ways life becomes less self-centered and more incidental.
  9. 14:16 Just being a person - He closes with Garfield's distinction between self and person, suggesting it is enough to play our roles without proving who we are.
