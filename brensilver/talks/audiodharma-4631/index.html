Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Practice Note

AudioDharma 08:04 2014-02-20

In this short practice note, Matthew Brensilver responds to a question about the relative roles of mindfulness and concentration. He argues against treating them as separate capacities, suggesting instead that mindfulness is richer when attention is gathered, steady, and able to contact experience more fully. The talk moves from a simple question to a practical reframe: even broad awareness involves a kind of concentration, and deeper practice can make each moment feel more saturated and alive.

Chapters

  1. 00:13 Opening Question - Matthew invites questions and briefly acknowledges the session’s format before turning to the practice issue at hand.
  2. 01:00 Mindfulness or Concentration - A listener asks how to understand the relative importance of concentration and mindfulness in practice.
  3. 01:53 A Continuum Model - Matthew describes one common way of seeing the two as broad and narrow attention, then says he prefers a different framing.
  4. 03:00 Gathered Attention - He explains that mindfulness without concentration can feel scattered, while rich mindfulness involves awareness being poured into the object.
  5. 04:25 Definitions And Insight - Matthew cites Thanissaro Bhikkhu and a Shinzen Young lineage teacher to show how mindfulness is often defined with concentration already included.
  6. 05:48 Concentration And Wisdom - He addresses the traditional idea that deep absorption lacks insight, while suggesting concentration still propels the path and is not fully separate from wisdom.
  7. 06:53 Saturated Awareness - In the closing exchange, Matthew describes a practice where attention moves among sensations and thoughts, yet each contact feels deeply tasted.
