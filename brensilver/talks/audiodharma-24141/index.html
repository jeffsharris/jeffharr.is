Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Dharmette: Empathic Resonance

AudioDharma 15:39 2025-11-05

Matthew Brensilver explores empathy as something that begins with close attention to one’s own mind and then extends outward without collapsing self and other. He distinguishes emotional resonance from perspective-taking, emphasizes humility and nonprojective listening, and returns several times to the need for the “as-if” condition in compassionate contact. The talk moves from mindfulness as self-directed empathy to a more careful account of calibrated, boundaried presence in healing relationships.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening and context - After a brief introduction, Matthew sets up the talk as an expansion on a previous discussion of protecting empathy.
  2. 00:40 Knowing our own mind - He argues that mindfulness is a kind of self-directed empathy, a deep listening that reveals the longing and suffering moving through experience.
  3. 02:34 Three parts of empathy - Matthew draws on neuroscience and Tanya Singer to distinguish emotional resonance, mentalizing, and regulation, and says both love and understanding must be cultivated.
  4. 04:53 Knowing and not knowing - He describes empathy as a dance between hypothesis and humility, where openness protects against projection and confirmation bias.
  5. 06:40 Reverence for difference - The talk turns toward not filling in the other with our own stories, and toward the self-knowledge and humility needed for real contact.
  6. 09:17 Calibration and boundaries - Matthew explains that empathy can become intrusive if it is not calibrated, and uses Krishnamurti to frame the tension between subject and object.
  7. 12:18 The as-if condition - He returns to Carl Rogers’ phrasing about entering another’s pain while keeping enough distance to track where self and other feelings can blur.
  8. 14:23 Love and closing - The talk closes by naming the kinds of collapse that can make responses counter-therapeutic, and by placing empathy in the realm of love, art, literature, and healing.
