Sila and Our Debt to Others

AudioDharma 49:41 2023-11-04

Matthew Brensilver reflects on sila as the place where Dharma practice meets other people: speech, action, livelihood, and the practical shape of our debt to one another. He moves from precepts and non-harm into questions of helplessness, grief, and how compassion can become sustainable when balanced by equanimity. The talk widens into civilizational responsibility, then closes with impermanence and the urgency of spending love wisely.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Ancient Path - Matthew begins with the Buddha’s ancient path and frames the eightfold path as our interface with the world.
  2. 02:04 Sila and Obligation - He turns to ethical training, the precepts, and the question of whether abstaining from harm is the full extent of our debt to others.
  3. 05:35 Continuity and Wakefulness - Parfit and Maya Angelou help him consider how much continuity with the future self matters and how clearer understanding should change action.
  4. 08:26 Backward Step - A Dōgen passage becomes a cue to step back from habit, notice ethical disorientation, and ask how practice remains sustainable.
  5. 12:18 Too Much To Hold - He describes overwhelm, moral vertigo, and the sense that suffering and information flow can exceed what the heart can bear.
  6. 18:51 World at Risk - Drawing on Toby Ord, he widens the lens to existential risk, then explores how helplessness can collapse into nihilism or frenzy.
  7. 24:57 Love and Rest - Tom Waits, T. S. Eliot, and Thomas Merton support a meditation on holding on, resting, and suffering more efficiently rather than more wastefully.
  8. 32:50 Being of Use - He shifts to kindness research and effective giving, showing how small acts and careful generosity can become realistic forms of service.
  9. 40:43 Already Broken - The talk closes with equanimity as a balance to compassion, Ajahn Chah’s broken cup, and the reminder that impermanence makes the urgency of love clearer.
