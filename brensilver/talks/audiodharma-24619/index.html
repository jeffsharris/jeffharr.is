Love, Grief and Inconsolability

AudioDharma 37:25 2026-01-17

Matthew Brensilver reflects on grief, love, and mortality by moving between Buddhist impermanence teachings, a deathbed instruction from Ajahn Chah, and his own questions about fear and control. He considers what Dharma can and cannot heal, especially when suffering remains irreducible or inconsolable. The talk turns toward a quieter freedom: love that is less entangled with outcome, more willing to simply illuminate.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Fear and death - He opens with the claim that freedom is tied to less fear of death, and begins to unpack what that might mean.
  2. 01:33 Ajahn Chah's teaching - Brensilver reads an Ajahn Chah deathbed instruction, centering aging, letting go, and resting in peace.
  3. 07:04 Freedom and theology - He reflects on how doctrines of rebirth and karma can change the felt meaning of suffering and practice.
  4. 09:07 The Dharma as medicine - The talk shifts to Dharma as deep but incomplete medicine, with some suffering remaining even after practice.
  5. 13:25 What grief meets - He distinguishes grief from despair, nihilism, and hate, and suggests some grief is not something to get over.
  6. 17:04 Emotion and history - Drawing on Martha Nussbaum, he treats emotions as intelligent responses to value and asks about the narrative history of grief.
  7. 20:24 Love, fear, mortality - He brings in Thich Nhat Hanh and turns to the question of whether love could be less bound to suffering if it were less afraid.
  8. 24:26 Limits of care - He describes the strain of caring in a world of large-scale suffering and the limits of what any one person can change.
  9. 27:07 Light that does not land - Using a sutta image of light with no surface, he explores awareness, self, and a love that may not need to land as an outcome.
