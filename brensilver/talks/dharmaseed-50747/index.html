Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Ethical Implications of Mindfulness

Dharma Seed 47:00 2018-06-16

Matthew Brensilver speaks about mindfulness as cultivation rather than quick self-assessment, returning again and again to the idea that practice changes us gradually and often invisibly. From there he broadens the talk into ethics: how insight shows up in speech, relationships, generosity, and our sense of responsibility to other beings. The talk moves between personal practice, Buddhist references, and secular moral arguments, ending with a quiet reminder to practice without needing applause.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Measuring Progress - Matthew begins by challenging the habit of checking after every breath to see whether meditation is going well.
  2. 03:21 Little by Little - Using Suzuki Roshi’s fog-and-shower metaphor, he describes practice as gradual, often unnoticed change.
  3. 05:44 Conscience and Responsibility - A quoted passage from Václav Havel shifts the talk toward humility, civic responsibility, and respect for life.
  4. 08:49 Compassion Needs Action - He uses a Bhikkhu Bodhi story to argue that compassion must become concrete conduct, not only intention.
  5. 10:33 Insight Must Be Lived - Matthew develops the idea that insight is only the beginning and asks how wisdom can be consolidated in daily life.
  6. 17:15 Practice in Relationship - Through examples from Sylvia Boorstein, Stephen Levine, and a personal eye-contact story, he shows how interpersonal life reveals self-view and tests practice.
  7. 28:56 Compassion and Giving - He turns to Shantideva and Peter Unger to explore radical compassion, ethical obligation, and what generosity means in a wealthy society.
  8. 36:23 Doing the Most Good - A long reference to Will MacAskill and effective altruism frames the question of how to use a life well without drifting into complacency.
  9. 42:36 Without Expecting Applause - The talk closes with Norman Fischer’s lojong slogan on practicing without praise, followed by a brief period of silence.
