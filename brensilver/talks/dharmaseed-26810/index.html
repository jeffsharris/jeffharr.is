Psychotherapy and Dharma

Dharma Seed 59:55 2015-04-06

Matthew Brensilver explores the overlap between psychotherapy and Dharma as a question of balance: awakening, maturity, ethics, and psychological development all have a place, but none should be collapsed into the others. He describes how mindfulness has entered mental health, why exposure and non-avoidance matter, and where Dharma goes beyond symptom relief toward deeper freedom. In the second half, he takes questions on wisdom, relationships, boundaries, and charisma, returning again to the themes of discernment and purified power.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the question - Matthew introduces the talk as an inquiry into the overlaps, tensions, and mutual relevance of psychotherapy, mental health, and Dharma.
  2. 01:25 Insight and maturity - He contrasts enlightenment-centered and psychologically integrated approaches, arguing that insight and growth need one another.
  3. 04:30 Mindfulness enters therapy - He traces the spread of mindfulness into psychology, noting both its promise and the risk of overstatement or dilution.
  4. 12:11 Thoughts and suffering - Using Steve Hayes and related examples, he explains the shift from judging thought content to relating differently to experience.
  5. 17:19 Exposure as practice - Matthew develops the idea that both psychotherapy and Dharma work by helping us meet avoided experience in a gradual, embodied way.
  6. 21:21 Different aims of each path - He compares the spectrum of well-being in therapy and Dharma, bringing in Freud, Maslow, William James, ethics, and the three characteristics.
  7. 29:36 Secular Dharma questions - The talk turns to samadhi, the role of view and wisdom, and the challenge of teaching mindfulness as secular without flattening the tradition.
  8. 40:03 Questions and lived practice - In questions and answers, he addresses wisdom, reading, relationships, discernment with fear, and the responsible use of charisma and power.
  9. 55:05 Compassionate closing - Matthew closes by clarifying that desensitization should not mean hardening, and ends with a brief dedication of practice.
