Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Dharmette: Moral outrage and love

AudioDharma 13:25 2025-03-05

Matthew Brensilver reflects on how deep care can become entangled with outrage, disgust, and the urge to punish. He asks how moral commitment can stay clear and useful without hardening into hatred, drawing on Ruth King and Hannah Chapman along the way. The talk moves toward grief, fear, and the idea that love—not anger—has to have the last word.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Love before practice - He begins with the depth of care already present in ordinary life and suggests that practice can widen and free that love.
  2. 01:20 Outrage and entanglement - The talk turns to moral outrage, the question of whether it can be trusted, and Ruth King’s reminder that anger can begin action but not complete it.
  3. 02:49 When care turns fierce - He explores how love can become yoked with rage, punishment, and revulsion, and asks how to use moral intensity without corroding the heart.
  4. 04:18 Purifying moral emotion - Drawing on Ruth King and his own uncertainty, he distinguishes useful engagement from the distortions of clinging, hatred, and the wish to force change.
  5. 05:56 Disgust and hate - A cited passage from Hannah Chapman helps frame moral disgust as a borrowed, ancient withdrawal response that can blur into hatred.
  6. 07:38 Fear and self-view - He emphasizes learning to surf strong moral emotions, take care of fear, and notice when moral commitment becomes egoic tribalism.
  7. 09:26 Love without a self - The talk deepens into self-view, empty processes, and the tendency to attach hatred to identity markers rather than seeing clearly.
  8. 11:25 Grief as the burden - He closes by saying moral sanity is kept through love, even if that means more grief, and that tears can become a source of motivation.
