Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Talk: Insight into Dukkha

AudioDharma 45:40 2018-03-22

Matthew Brensilver uses the film After Life, Leonard Cohen, and a series of Dharma and contemporary references to ask whether any moment of experience can be enough. From there he turns to dukkha as a plain feature of being human, not a personal failure, and shows how careful attention to suffering can become a basis for insight rather than despair. The talk moves gradually toward equanimity, softness, and a happiness less dependent on conditions.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 After Life as image - Brensilver opens with the film After Life to frame the question of whether any moment of life might be one we could willingly remain in forever.
  2. 02:47 Stability and confidence - He names the practice capacities that make insight possible: steadiness, well-being, and the confidence that any moment can become a gateway to freedom.
  3. 07:01 What dukkha means - The talk turns to the First Noble Truth, clarifying dukkha as both ordinary frustration and the deeper pattern of reaching for completion through experience.
  4. 11:09 Study suffering carefully - He brings in Tejaniya and other sources to distinguish suffering from insight into suffering, and argues that careful attention to dukkha can soften confusion about life and success.
  5. 18:13 Retreat and realism - Using Thomas Merton and Adam Phillips, Brensilver reframes retreat, success, and failure as ways of meeting the human condition more honestly.
  6. 22:33 Not your fault - He emphasizes that dukkha is not a verdict on the self, and uses personal stories to show how seeing suffering clearly can loosen perfectionism and blame.
  7. 28:24 Grief as doorway - Drawing on Pema Chödrön, he describes grief as a path into bodhicitta and explains how tenderness with suffering can become purification rather than defeat.
  8. 34:12 Equanimity and love - Brensilver distinguishes liking from loving, then develops equanimity as radical permission to feel and a stable way of not bracing against experience.
  9. 40:35 Condition-free happiness - He closes by returning to Shinzen Young and Nisargadatta, pointing toward a happiness that can coexist with pain and a knowing that is larger than suffering.
