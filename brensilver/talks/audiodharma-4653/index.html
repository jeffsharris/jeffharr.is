Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Failing Well

AudioDharma 15:34 2014-03-12

Matthew Brensilver reflects on perfectionism as a subtle form of suffering: a habit of self-harshness, comparison, and constant evaluation that can also enter spiritual practice. He argues that meditation is not about getting everything right, but about learning to begin again, soften around failure, and let metta reframe mistakes as part of the path. The talk closes with stories and phrases from Norman Fischer, Samuel Beckett, and a retreat anecdote about “a good mistake” to show how failure can open connection rather than separation.

Chapters

  1. 00:20 Perfectionism and the path - Brensilver introduces the tension between lofty spiritual aspirations and the perfectionistic mind that wants to avoid disappointment.
  2. 01:18 How it shows up - He names the familiar forms perfectionism takes in ordinary life and meditation: harshness, striving, and relentless self-checking.
  3. 02:45 Striving and comparison - The talk turns to how spiritual practice can become a site of benchmarking, comparison, and spiritual materialism.
  4. 05:21 Connection instead of separation - He reframes the path as one of deeper connection and suggests that perfectionism tends to isolate us from ourselves and others.
  5. 06:14 Failing as practice - Drawing on Norman Fischer and Beckett, he argues that ongoing practice depends on the willingness to fail, begin again, and resist the demand for success.
  6. 09:10 The good mistake - A retreat story about a misplaced gift becomes an example of how generosity can hold error without self-condemnation.
  7. 12:23 Metta and innocence - Brensilver closes by linking failure with metta, tenderness toward the First Noble Truth, and a more innocent way of meeting life.
