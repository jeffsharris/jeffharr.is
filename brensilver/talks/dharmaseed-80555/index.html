Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Reflections and Guided Meditation on Mudita

Dharma Seed 35:36 2023-10-13

Matthew Brensilver reflects on mudita, or appreciative joy, by first grounding it in tenderness, grief, and the ways the heart can stay open without pretending. He then turns to comparison and envy as forms of suffering that support a false self, and shows how practice can loosen that grip through compassion, softness, and rejoicing in another’s welfare. The talk closes with a guided meditation that begins with an easy person, then moves toward someone who stirs comparison, and ends in self-care and blessing.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening reflection - Matthew begins with a short talk about practice and tells a Zen story that frames tears, tenderness, and Dharma as part of heart practice.
  2. 04:00 Tears and tenderness - He explores how grief and compassion can feel close to the heart, while joy may feel remote or even disloyal to suffering.
  3. 06:19 Joy without pretending - Matthew describes how practice can make the heart more fluid and argues that Dharma does not ask anyone to manufacture joy.
  4. 08:54 What mudita is - He defines mudita as delight in another’s welfare and names comparison and envy as the main obstacles, bringing in a brief quotation about envy and the burden of measurement.
  5. 12:15 Selfing through comparison - The talk turns toward the ways envy reveals self-view, and Matthew repeatedly offers the line that anatta is the only true form of self-esteem.
  6. 15:40 Sharing another’s good - He explains that joy is not about ignoring suffering, but about easing self-construction so we can genuinely share in another person’s happiness.
  7. 20:31 Guided practice begins - The meditation starts with images and phrases of joy, then invites a person for whom mudita comes easily and the felt sense of partaking in their happiness.
  8. 24:53 Working with envy - Matthew shifts toward someone who is harder to rejoice with, encouraging close attention to contraction, comparison, and the felt signposts of self.
  9. 29:07 Softening and closing - The practice ends in forgiveness, release of self-importance, rejoicing in the other, and a final turn toward one’s own happiness and well-being.
