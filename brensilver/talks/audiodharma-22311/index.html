Equanimity 5

AudioDharma 12:00 2025-02-16

Matthew Brensilver reflects on why spiritual practice rarely fits a neat personal story, then turns that same humility toward equanimity: how it is noticed, strengthened, and limited in ordinary life. He connects equanimity with not-self, the draining of egoic charge, and the practical need to respect when a mind is too depleted to do much more than stop and regroup. The talk closes by widening equanimity into a care for others, where holding pain becomes part of love, solidarity, and remorse.

Chapters

  1. 00:30 Unreliable spiritual stories - Brensilver questions the neat stories people tell about their own practice and why they cannot fully explain a life of Dharma practice.
  2. 01:58 Sincerity over certainty - He briefly invokes Suzuki Roshi to suggest that sincerity matters more than a clean yes-or-no idea of enlightenment.
  3. 02:51 Noticing lack of equanimity - He shifts to equanimity as something you can notice when you are caught, much as you notice a thought or trance.
  4. 04:33 Equanimity and not-self - The talk develops the mutual relationship between equanimity and not-self, especially through loosening the emotional charge around self-stories.
  5. 05:54 Draining self-charges - He gives everyday examples of letting identity stories rattle until their affect drains out, making the heart less brittle and reactive.
  6. 07:52 Respecting limits - Brensilver emphasizes practical limits: when the mind is gripped or depleted, the skillful move may simply be to stop, rest, or say no.
  7. 09:38 Expanding pain tolerance - He describes practice as gradually increasing tolerance for pain and reducing its unpleasantness, rather than eliminating sensation itself.
  8. 11:05 Holding pain for others - The talk ends by linking equanimity to compassion, solidarity, and the wish to be safe for others when our own regulation falters.
