Matthew Brensilver, Sylvia Boorstein: Day 2: Evening Practice Session (Retreat at Spirit Rock)

Dharma Seed 1:35:05 2018-01-14

This evening practice session opens with Sylvia Boorstein gently gathering the retreat together at the end of a few days of silence, then moves into a close reading of the Metta Sutta as both poetry and instruction. The talk lingers on how lovingkindness is not just a feeling but a disciplined way of seeing suffering clearly, withholding ill will, and training the mind through repetition. It ends by widening the circle to difficult people and to Martin Luther King Jr.’s language of loving enemies, linking compassion with freedom and steadiness.

Chapters

  1. 01:30 Opening the evening - Sylvia marks the retreat’s nearing end, names the strange feeling of time on retreat, and invites the group to settle into the evening together.
  2. 02:58 Reading the text together - The group is asked to read the Metta Sutta aloud and sit with it as a shared practice rather than receive a lecture about it.
  3. 15:44 Metta as instruction - Sylvia explains that every line of the sutta is a practical instruction, not just a beautiful poem, and points toward the phrase that feels most central.
  4. 20:00 Pair work and daily use - The retreatants discuss the text with partners, and Sylvia encourages them to take the sutta home as something to revisit every day.
  5. 28:57 Repetition and reflection - After the small-group exercise, the talk moves through questions of how the sutta functions as a path of morality, mind training, and wisdom, and how its phrasing shapes conduct.
  6. 58:16 Suffering and consequence - Sylvia returns to the line 'may all beings be at ease,' links it to the truth of suffering, and discusses conscientiousness, karmic consequences, and the need for careful action.
  7. 01:13:48 Benefits of metta - She recalls Sharon Salzberg’s teaching on memorizing the traditional benefits of metta and describes how repetition steadies the mind, softens opinion-making, and warms perception.
  8. 01:19:48 Enemies and freedom - The talk closes with the hardest edge of the practice: not wishing harm, even toward feared or opposed people, and Martin Luther King Jr.’s language of loving enemies as an expression of liberation.
  9. 01:23:34 Closing sit and walk - The session ends with a brief period of sitting and walking meditation, along with final thanks and instructions for the rest of the evening.
