Dharmette: On not understanding dharma talks

AudioDharma 13:50 2025-02-19

Matthew Brensilver responds to a listener who sometimes understands Dharma talks immediately and sometimes feels left out of them. He reflects on how teaching always involves judgment calls, why recognition can feel like remembering, and why not understanding can itself be part of wisdom. The talk gradually shifts toward patience, humility, and a practice of learning without needing to be right.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 The listener's question - Matthew reads the anonymous question about talks that feel clear versus talks that seem out of reach.
  2. 01:37 Teaching is imperfect - He reflects on the difficulty of teaching Dharma at all and the many editorial choices teachers make.
  3. 03:00 Recognition and intimacy - He turns to sincerity, the feeling of remembering, and the intimacy that can arise when a teacher speaks plainly to one's mind.
  4. 05:21 Wisdom in not knowing - Matthew says that recognizing what one does not understand is itself wise, and uses Agnes Callard to frame the challenge of questioning our own assumptions.
  5. 07:34 Learning to be wrong - He develops the theme of doubt, overconfidence, and the willingness to be surprised, drawing on Socrates as an example.
  6. 10:06 Practice matures - Matthew looks back on years of practice, describing a shift from dramatic discovery to a steadier but still vulnerable relation to Dharma.
  7. 12:09 Patience and belief - He ends by encouraging patience, saying there are no makeup classes for wisdom and that understanding often comes later than trust.
