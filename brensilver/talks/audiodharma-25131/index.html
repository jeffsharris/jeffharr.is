Working with Self-Hatred

AudioDharma 15:03 2026-03-25

Matthew Brensilver responds to a question about self-hatred and self-compassion by gently questioning the assumptions underneath both. He suggests looking for the functions self-hate serves, staying with suffering long enough to see it clearly, and trusting that deeper change may come through love, not force. The talk moves from practical inquiry into a broader reflection on faith, conditioning, retreat, and the ways deep feeling can open a different relationship to the self.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 The question of self-hate - Matthew introduces a question about why standard self-compassion practices can feel unnatural and what to do when self-forgiveness does not land.
  2. 01:23 Finding what fits - He warns that heartfelt practice can drift into sentimentality and suggests improvising until the approach feels natural and true.
  3. 02:23 What self-hatred protects - Rather than judging self-hatred, he asks what it soothes or protects, and whether it may feel less painful than facing the alternative.
  4. 04:01 Ordinariness and suffering - He connects self-hatred with grandiosity, then encourages lingering with dukkha long enough to stop glancing off the basic fact of suffering.
  5. 05:53 Faith through suffering - Using a Carol Wilson quote and a teaching on faith, he argues that suffering can become the condition that deepens trust and softens the heart.
  6. 08:02 No spiritual battle - He brings in Megan Cowan to emphasize that deep conditioning is not defeated by force and that the work is more like inner communication and education.
  7. 10:12 Beyond the self game - Matthew says not to isolate oneself in the house of self, and uses Ajahn Sucitto to point toward a shift in direction and energy rather than more self-analysis.
  8. 12:15 Retreat, mortality, and love - He reflects on how retreat can stir emotional chaos, then names mortality as a gateway into deep feeling that can be stitched to care for oneself.
