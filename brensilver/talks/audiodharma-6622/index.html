Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Mindfulness

AudioDharma 39:34 2016-04-04

Matthew Brensilver treats mindfulness not as a fixed definition, but as something understood more fully through practice. He moves from the limits of ordinary knowing to a two-part framing of mindfulness: present-time awareness and equanimity, then shows how those qualities change the way suffering is seen and met. The talk closes by loosening the whole project, urging listeners to hold mindfulness lightly and keep the deeper concern on suffering and freedom.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Opening the question - Brensilver begins by saying he is not fully sure what mindfulness is, and suggests that understanding it may be the fruit of practice rather than its starting point.
  2. 02:23 False promises of relief - He uses a passage from Sam Harris to argue that many goals are really attempts to secure peace in the future instead of meeting the present.
  3. 03:57 Mindfulness in context - He places mindfulness within the wider Dharma and introduces consciousness as a mystery that Buddhist practice helps approach without pretending to solve it.
  4. 07:32 An umbrella term - Using Jon Kabat-Zinn as a reference point, he explains how mindfulness came to function as a broad container in the West while still pointing back to the Dharma.
  5. 10:06 Knowing that we know - He defines mindfulness as more than awareness itself: it is knowing phenomena as phenomena, with present-time awareness and equanimity as two key facets.
  6. 14:07 Seeing causality clearly - Through Sayadaw Tejaniya's image of missing chapters in a book, he shows how steady attention reveals the causal flow behind suffering and ease.
  7. 17:42 Equanimity and softness - Brensilver deepens equanimity with Sharon Salzberg and Shinzen Young, presenting it as radical permission to feel and a way to soften the heart rather than control experience.
  8. 21:37 Practice as orientation - He moves through a study of mindfulness styles, then into a longer reflection on how practice changes our relation to time, identity, safety, and the felt groundlessness of life.
  9. 34:18 Hold it lightly - Drawing on Joseph Goldstein's free-fall image, he ends by saying he cares less about mindfulness as a concept than about suffering, and invites listeners to keep the question alive in practice.
