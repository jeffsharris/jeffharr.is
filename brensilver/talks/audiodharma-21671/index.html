Dharmette: Post-Election Reflections

AudioDharma 16:48 2024-11-06

Matthew Brensilver reflects on the morning after an election, using the shared shock as a starting point for spiritual friendship, grief, and clearer seeing. He moves through impermanence, the thin line between safety and danger, and the way heartbreak can deepen love without becoming naivete. The talk turns toward what it means to comprehend dukkha, how to care without collapsing, and how practice may ask for a larger form of nonviolent engagement.

Chapters

  1. 00:17 Gathering in practice - He opens by naming the value of being together and framing the moment as one for spiritual friendship rather than ideology.
  2. 01:23 Holding the shock - Matthew names the political context and describes the mind's first responses: uncertainty, disappointment, and the need for honesty about what happened.
  3. 03:09 Anicca and the thin line - Using Michelle McDonald's language, he reflects on how quickly life can tip from all right to very bad, and how impermanence explains the mind's lag.
  4. 05:22 Grief that teaches - He traces cynicism, collapse, and grief, then turns to the image of a broken heart and the warning that loving-kindness can be mistaken for naivete.
  5. 08:09 Love after heartbreak - With Leonard Cohen's words, he describes grief that sweetens rather than disables, and distinguishes love's pain from the pain of righteousness or aversion.
  6. 12:07 Comprehending dukkha - He asks what the Buddha meant by comprehending suffering and explores questions of power, helplessness, and keeping the heart open without false equanimity.
  7. 14:45 No complacent resting - Matthew considers how a victory might have fed complacency, cites Shinzen Young on not getting stuck in a good place, and names a deeper care that wants to emerge.
  8. 16:09 Closing offer - He ends by offering the talk for consideration, expressing gratitude, and returning to practice together.
