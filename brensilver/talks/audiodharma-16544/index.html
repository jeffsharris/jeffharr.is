Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Dharma and Concern for the World

AudioDharma 41:47 2022-07-03

Matthew Brensilver reflects on what it means to care about the world without collapsing into despair, numbness, or constant reaction. He moves from the strain of news and social grief into a Dharma-centered inquiry about suffering, attention, action, and rest, with brief references to Justin Rosenstein, Tom Waits, Toby Ord, and others. The talk keeps returning to a practical question: how to engage the world honestly, without wasting our limited energy or losing our heart.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Dharma as inquiry - Matthew opens by framing Dharma practice as an open question rather than a set of answers, then names the widespread weariness and strain people are carrying.
  2. 02:54 Extraction and attention - He uses Justin Rosenstein’s critique of profit-at-all-costs economics to show how the planet, and now our attention, are being mined.
  3. 05:18 Collapse and helplessness - The talk turns toward the felt sense of historical and personal decline, including social crisis, collective pain, and the helplessness that can follow.
  4. 10:09 Holding on - Matthew briefly recalls Tom Waits’s 'Hold On' as a tone of endurance, then names the pull toward nihilism and the hard edge of uncertainty.
  5. 12:01 What to do - He asks what response is possible when practice can feel like multitasking between saving ourselves and saving the world, and begins to move toward a clearer approach.
  6. 16:00 Seeing what improves - Matthew cautions against flattening real suffering, but also notes that many conditions are improving, and cites research showing how the concept of badness can expand as harm becomes less common.
  7. 21:01 A risky century - He widens the frame with Toby Ord’s concern about existential risk, suggesting humanity may be in a powerful, adolescent phase that requires care without catastrophizing.
  8. 25:40 Dharma and other medicines - Matthew distinguishes the limits of Dharma from the larger field of wisdom, then turns to efficient suffering, thoughtful media consumption, and action bound to anxiety.
  9. 31:11 Give, rest, and let go - The closing movement emphasizes giving until it hurts, resting fully, widening empathy, grieving well, and coming to terms with death so that service is less distorted by fear.
