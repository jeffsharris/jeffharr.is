Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Intentions for Practice and the Jewel of Curiosity (Retreat at Spirit Rock)

Dharma Seed 45:55 2017-05-24

Matthew Brensilver offers a reflective talk on what actually sustains practice over time: first urgency, then trust, and finally a quieter curiosity that keeps the heart engaged. He moves from a small mouse-in-a-trap story into questions of renunciation, mindfulness, equanimity, and the subtle ways practice can still be clouded by aversion. Toward the end, he turns to story itself—asking whether we can live less as a character in a script and more as awareness knowing experience directly.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Mouse as mirror - A retreat-morning mouse story becomes a small parable about fear, identification, and shared vulnerability.
  2. 02:55 Practice and trust - Matthew reflects on the depth of Dharma practice, his own long arc in practice, and the sense that life itself can be trusted as part of the path.
  3. 09:36 Urgency and renunciation - Using David Whyte and Bhante Gunaratana, he explores turning away from deferred life, the pull of renunciation, and the need for skillful use of hard-edged teachings.
  4. 15:38 Love and freedom - He considers different phases of practice, the need for wisdom to stay joined with love, and the ordinary appearance of freedom in the Zen ox-herding pictures.
  5. 20:16 Curiosity becomes mindfulness - Curiosity emerges as the deepest intention, then broadens into mindfulness as a living wish to know what is happening right now.
  6. 25:06 Fresh attention - Drawing on Gil Fronsdal and Ñāṇaponika Thera, he describes practice as being relearned each time and mindfulness as present-time awareness with stability, clarity, and alertness.
  7. 30:59 Equanimity and knots - He develops equanimity as radical permission to feel, then uses Michelle McDonald's phrase 'karmic knots' to show how subtle aversion can hide inside apparent acceptance.
  8. 38:36 Beyond story - With Yuval Harari as a foil, Matthew questions our addiction to narrative and returns to the possibility of knowing experience directly without collapsing into a fixed story of self.
