Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Intensity, Clinging and Anatta in the teen years (Retreat at Spirit Rock)

Dharma Seed 41:09 2016-12-30

Matthew Brensilver speaks to teenagers about intensity, self-consciousness, and the habits of clinging that make life feel tighter than it needs to be. He moves from grief and listening, to the pressure of performing a self, and then into a gentle argument for letting go as a trainable part of practice. The talk ends by widening that teaching into love, compassion, and a less defended relationship to who we are.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Grief Softens - He opens with a Leonard Cohen lyric and frames pain, grief, and letting go as experiences that can soften the heart rather than harden it.
  2. 02:01 Trusting Young People - Using Norman Fisher’s words, he says older people do not really know what the future will require and encourages listeners to discover what they care about.
  3. 05:28 Being Heard - A holiday-gathering story leads into the difference between small talk and the relief of being really listened to, which he connects to mindfulness practice.
  4. 08:38 Intensity of Being Human - He names the basic intensity of adolescence and ordinary life, including the worry that something is wrong if things feel so vivid.
  5. 10:51 Performing a Self - Through a high-school story, he shows how adolescence can feel like constant performance and how that pressure becomes tiring and lonely.
  6. 15:54 Clinging and Letting Go - He lays out the central teaching: clinging hurts, control is ultimately impossible, and letting go often begins as grief before it becomes relief.
  7. 20:00 Love Without Possession - Drawing on Martin Luther King Jr., Jesus, and a song about love and hate, he argues that love is patient, redemptive, and not the same as possessiveness.
  8. 25:04 Freedom and Compassion - He extends the teaching inward, suggesting that love honors freedom, compassion needs space to move through us, and self-love means not turning the self into a fixed object.
  9. 31:52 The Me Flag Relaxes - Using the image of planting flags on identity, he shows how we defend skills, tastes, and self-image, then closes by inviting listeners to step out of the debate and rest.
