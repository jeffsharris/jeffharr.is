Dharmette: Pleasant and Unpleasant Dissolution

AudioDharma 14:31 2025-04-23

Matthew Brensilver explores why dissolution can feel peaceful in one setting and terrifying in another, using emptiness, derealization, and death anxiety as a comparison. He brings in Nagarjuna, a recent study by Aviva Berkovich-Ohana and Stephen Fulder, and Stephen Levine to show how the Dharma changes the meaning of self-loss. The talk closes by returning to impermanence, grief, and the courage that practice can build around mortality.

Chapters

  1. 00:17 A peaceful dissolution - Matthew begins with a personal memory of self-boundaries melting away in retreat and how the experience felt exquisitely peaceful.
  2. 01:17 When emptiness unsettles - He contrasts contemplative emptiness with derealization and depersonalization, noting the overlap in language but not in meaning.
  3. 02:34 Nagarjuna on emptiness - A passage attributed to Nagarjuna is used to show emptiness as a way of reducing grasping, conflict, and affliction rather than creating numbness.
  4. 04:05 Why experiences diverge - Matthew cites recent research on dissolution experiences and turns to predictive models of self and world to explain why some states feel nourishing while others feel destabilizing.
  5. 06:08 Self as fear's effect - He argues that the sense of self is conditioned by fear, especially the deeper fear of death, which makes mortality feel like a threat to the center of subjectivity.
  6. 08:20 Craving to become - Using Stephen Levine and the Buddha's teaching on bhava tanha, he describes how dissolution can feel like an attack when the self is being protected against non-being.
  7. 10:20 Impermanence at the center - Matthew returns to practice, suggesting that placing anicca at the center of understanding softens the fear response and changes the tone of letting go.
  8. 12:14 Grief and courage - He closes with Levine's line that death is safe, then qualifies it with his own view that grief remains irreducible while the Dharma still strengthens courage.
