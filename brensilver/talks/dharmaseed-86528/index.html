Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Healing the Self

Dharma Seed 50:20 2024-07-11

Matthew Brensilver explores healing as a flexible relationship among self-love, self-acceptance, and self-forgetting rather than a fixed sequence. He moves from shame and fear into the role of faith, sangha, and mindfulness as conditions that make change possible, then turns toward how the past is carried, digested, and gradually rewritten as a Dharma story. The talk stays grounded in ordinary psychological life while keeping liberation in view: a stable, fluid self that can live with both suffering and ease.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Three modes of healing - Matthew frames the talk around the question of whether we heal, love, or forget the self first, and argues that the answer is flexibility rather than sequence.
  2. 04:21 Why letting go feels risky - He describes how unhealed pain makes surrender feel dangerous, and how faith and self-acceptance create enough safety to release.
  3. 10:46 Insight and backlash - The talk turns to the way insight into not-self can open healing, but can also stir fear, egoic defensiveness, and old material.
  4. 17:14 Healing as acceptance - Matthew broadens healing into emotional well-being, loving work, and transformed habits, drawing on Suzuki Roshi and Marsha Linehan to show that change grows inside deep acceptance.
  5. 24:37 What we really want - Mindfulness is presented as de-shaming and normalizing, while desire is distinguished from craving so that practice can clarify what is actually worth wanting.
  6. 32:22 Story, presence, and the past - He brings in Martha Nussbaum and Thich Nhat Hanh to show that changing the heart requires story, and that being present means meeting the momentum of the past rather than forcing attention.
  7. 39:48 Too much to digest - Using dukkha as 'too much,' Matthew describes practice as blessing undigested experience with awareness and love until the past begins to release its charge.
  8. 43:25 A stable, flexible self - The close gathers the talk into self-familiarity, a coherent life story, and finally a good enough self that can support deeper liberation beyond any fixed territory.
