Dharmette: Courage and Fear

AudioDharma 16:42 2025-04-04

Matthew Brensilver reflects on courage as something distinct from general effort, and as a practice that is often shadowed by fear. He traces how fear narrows attention, distorts cognition, and makes letting go feel unsafe, then turns toward the way spiritual practice asks for honesty, surrender, and the willingness to meet uncertainty. The talk closes by connecting courage to virtue, the Buddhist perfections, and a quiet question about what love looks like when it is less afraid.

Chapters

  1. 00:13 Opening to courage - Matthew introduces courage as the day’s theme and speaks candidly about his own temperament and limited confidence teaching on it.
  2. 01:40 Fear at the threshold - He describes courage as disorienting because it asks us to step toward what we cannot fully predict and to accept possible pain or change.
  3. 03:58 Faith and the path - The talk turns to the spiritual path as a series of small acts of courage, where aspiration requires leaving old habits without knowing exactly what will replace them.
  4. 06:08 How fear works - Matthew explores fear as a deeply wired force that narrows cognition, makes clinging feel protective, and disrupts attention and samadhi.
  5. 08:25 Groundless moments - He names the courage needed to face suffering, change, and the moments when the ground feels unstable or entirely groundless.
  6. 10:03 Terror and waiting - Using the example of being lost in the woods, he suggests that sometimes the wisest response is not to force a solution but to stay still and let help arrive.
  7. 11:34 Fear of suffering - Matthew distinguishes sensitivity to suffering from fear of suffering, and notices how the wish to control pain can quietly shape practice.
  8. 13:56 Shared courage - He reflects on the courage he sees in others, invokes Aquinas on courage as foundational to virtue, and gathers the talk toward moral bravery.
  9. 15:30 Love less afraid - The closing question asks what love looks like when fear loosens, ending with a brief exhortation to keep going.
