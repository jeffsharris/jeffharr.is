Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Self-Love and Selflessness - Part 2

Dharma Seed 52:51 2015-03-02

Matthew Brensilver explores how self-love and selflessness are not opposites so much as two ways of loosening clinging. He moves from ordinary self-judgment and the pressure of success and failure into Buddhist not-self teachings, then into experiential examples that point toward a shift in how self is felt and seen. The talk ends with a quiet meditation on letting go of the need to make anything of oneself, and with a blessing for less suffering and more safety in the world.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Self-Love and Selflessness - Matthew sets up the tension between self-love and selflessness and reframes both as part of a continuum of acceptance.
  2. 05:52 Worth and Success - He uses psychologists and Adam Phillips to question self-evaluation, success, and the habit of making a life out of winning and failing.
  3. 11:11 The Buddha on Not-Self - A sutta from the Majjhima Nikaya shows not-self as a practical teaching for letting go, tied closely to impermanence and dukkha.
  4. 18:39 Grasping Makes the Self - Matthew explains how selfhood is manufactured in the act of grasping, and how mindfulness reveals that process.
  5. 22:42 Seeing Through the Ground - He describes subtle selfing, the narrative center, and the felt shift from alienation toward a sense of being at home in nature.
  6. 31:52 No Head, Gained World - The talk turns to experiential accounts of selflessness, including Douglas Harding's 'On Having No Head' and a broader sense of perspective dropping away.
  7. 40:54 Face, Heart, and Compassion - Matthew connects not-self with tenderness, noting that seeing through the face as self can open the heart rather than flatten it.
  8. 46:18 Practice Becomes Life - He closes by describing commitment to practice as a shift in identity itself, where the imagined life falls away.
  9. 48:18 Closing Meditation - A brief silence and closing blessing invite listeners to let go of needing to become anything and to wish well for the world.
