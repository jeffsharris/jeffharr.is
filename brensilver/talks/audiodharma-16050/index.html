Reflections on Thich Nhat Hanh's Teachings

AudioDharma 37:57 2022-04-10

Matthew Brensilver reflects on Thich Nhat Hanh as a teacher whose deepest transmission was not authority but love. He moves from personal remembrance and early encounters with Thich Nhat Hanh’s community into themes of suffering, compassion, anger, interbeing, and fearlessness, using memorable images like “no mud, no lotus” and the cloud in the paper. The talk closes with a tender story of renunciation and asks what it means to receive and carry forward Thich Nhat Hanh’s love.

Chapters

  1. 00:31 Remembering Thay - Matthew begins with Thich Nhat Hanh’s death, his own grief, and the first ways Thay’s presence entered his life through sangha and practice.
  2. 04:10 Love as credibility - He reflects on why Thich Nhat Hanh’s teaching about love carries moral weight, especially in light of war, exile, and the bell hooks conversation.
  3. 08:14 Available to the kingdom - Matthew turns to Thich Nhat Hanh’s use of Christian language to point to present-moment practice and to evoke curiosity rather than fixed belief.
  4. 12:38 Mud and lotus - He lingers on “no mud, no lotus” as a gentle image of purification, showing suffering as the material of freedom.
  5. 14:28 Wisdom and love - The talk develops Thich Nhat Hanh’s non-duality of understanding and compassion, including the line that understanding is love’s other name.
  6. 19:29 Caring for anger - Matthew summarizes Thich Nhat Hanh’s teaching on anger, including the refusal to suppress or vent it and the vulnerability of speaking honestly in relationship.
  7. 24:53 Interbeing and paper - He explains interbeing through dependent origination and the cloud-and-paper contemplation, widening a single object into a whole world of conditions.
  8. 27:02 No birth, no death - The teaching turns toward death, with the body seen through lineage and interdependence, and fearlessness named as a deeper joy.
  9. 30:36 A love story - Matthew ends with Thich Nhat Hanh’s story of loving a nun, the pain of separation, and the transformation of personal longing into wider love and service.
