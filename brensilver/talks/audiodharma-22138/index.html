Dharmette: To Care and Not to Care Simultaneously

AudioDharma 12:44 2025-01-22

Matthew Brensilver explores T.S. Eliot’s phrase “teach us to care and not to care” as a lived Dharma question, not a slogan. He moves from the alternating rhythms of compassion and rest to a more radical possibility: caring and not caring at the same time, as an expression of emptiness and release. The talk then turns to sutta imagery of perception thinning out and consciousness no longer landing anywhere, ending with a quiet suggestion that this freedom may still feel benevolent and warm.

Chapters

  1. 00:09 Eliot and the question - Matthew opens with T.S. Eliot’s Ash Wednesday and uses its line about caring and not caring to frame the talk’s central tension.
  2. 02:02 Love and rest - He describes care as costly and argues for a rhythm between compassion and rest, rather than a heart that is either closed or overextended.
  3. 04:57 Beyond sequence - The talk shifts from alternating states to the possibility of caring and not caring simultaneously, which he presents as one way emptiness can be understood.
  4. 06:59 Refining perception - Using the Lesser Discourse on Emptiness, he traces perception becoming less agitated, from village to wilderness to earth, with fewer coarse forms appearing.
  5. 08:47 Less and less - He extends the emptiness teaching into subtler states of perception, including the sense of infinite space and a mind less dependent on ordinary sensory life.
  6. 09:58 Where mind does not land - Matthew turns to the sunlight simile from another sutta to describe consciousness that does not land anywhere, connecting that freedom with the end of sorrow.
  7. 12:01 Quiet closing - He offers the teaching for reflection, then closes with a brief farewell and well-wishing.
