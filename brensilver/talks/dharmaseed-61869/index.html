Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Your Face: The Gaze, Self-view, Intimacy and Letting Go (Online Retreat at Spirit Rock)

Dharma Seed 46:11 2020-05-17

Matthew Brensilver explores how face-to-face contact, even through Zoom, reveals self-view, intimacy, and the ways we project onto one another. Moving from ethics and gaze to meditation, he shows how clinging contracts connection, while loosening the self can make relationships safer, clearer, and more loving. The talk closes with Levinas, a brief reflection on not-self, and a long film example from Her to illustrate unpossessible love.

Chapters

  1. 00:46 A small thought - He opens with Wittgenstein and turns immediately to how little moments of perception can shape a life.
  2. 01:25 Seeing and being seen - He introduces the talk’s central concern: the charged experience of meeting another face, even through digital mediation.
  3. 03:36 Ethics in the gaze - He connects gaze with sila, boundaries, objectification, and the care needed in how we look at others.
  4. 08:57 Presence and mirror - Using Marina Abramović’s The Artist Is Present, he explores sustained eye contact as a mirror that can reveal both intimacy and neurosis.
  5. 11:49 Self-view in contact - He describes how being seen can evoke projection, shame, comparison, and the sudden coagulation of self in ordinary relationships.
  6. 19:44 Relational practice - He draws on Ajahn Sucitto and everyday examples to show that meditation practice can leave relational patterns untouched unless it reaches the way we meet others.
  7. 26:55 The face as command - Through Levinas and Batchelor, he presents the face as an ethical appeal that calls forth empathy, restraint, and responsiveness.
  8. 35:10 Less self, more refuge - He moves through anatta, self-acceptance, and the way a less defensive self reduces pressure on others and frees connection.
  9. 39:37 Love without possession - He closes with Her and the image of the ocean to show that love can be real without ownership, and that letting go opens deeper tenderness.
