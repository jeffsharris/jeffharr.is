Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Thinking

Dharma Seed 58:00 2015-06-29

Matthew Brensilver explores thinking as both a powerful human capacity and a source of suffering when it is taken too personally. He moves from Descartes and the Buddhist critique of an enduring self, through the felt experience of mind wandering, to neuroscience and contemporary meditation teaching, showing how practice changes our relationship to inner speech, self-story, and silence. The talk ends by softening the usual opposition between thought and meditation, inviting a steadier and more loving relationship with the mind.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 What thinking is - Matthew opens by framing thinking as central to life and meditation, then sets up the talk with Descartes and the Buddhist challenge to taking the thinking self as solid.
  2. 03:26 Silence and value - He contrasts thought’s usefulness with the appeal of stopping thought, using Douglas Harding’s description of quiet as a way to show the draw of non-discursive awareness.
  3. 08:42 Meditation reveals the mind - The talk turns to what practice exposes: how much thinking is happening, how easily shame attaches to it, and how mindfulness often means noticing distraction more clearly.
  4. 16:16 Knowing thought directly - Matthew asks what a thought actually is, distinguishes mental images from inner talk, and emphasizes learning to hear thought as a phenomenon rather than obey it.
  5. 31:58 Mind wandering by default - He draws on Malia Mason and Dan Gilbert to show that wandering thought is a baseline condition, then links this to self-referential processing and chronic self-updating.
  6. 39:07 Self, story, and urgency - Using Mark Leary, Dan Dennett, and related references, he describes self-awareness as both a blessing and a curse, and suggests practice loosens the urgency and grip of the self-story.
  7. 49:56 Unlearning aversion - Matthew brings in Jason Siff to question the hidden hierarchy that treats thinking as a problem, arguing that practice need not make thought the enemy.
  8. 54:28 Loving relationship - After a brief sit, he closes with a blessing that points toward an easier, more affectionate relationship with thought and the mind.
