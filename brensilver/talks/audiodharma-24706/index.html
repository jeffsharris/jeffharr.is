Dharmette: Orienting to Goodness

AudioDharma 15:39 2026-01-28

Matthew Brensilver reflects on what it means to orient toward goodness when suffering, fear, and political division can make love seem inadequate or naive. He argues that goodness is not a luxury but something the mind and the world both need, and he connects that claim to a recent peace walk, to the Dalai Lama’s emphasis on compassion as necessity, and to Samuel Bagg’s writing on epistemic trust and democracy. The talk moves from a personal and moral case for goodness into a practical account of how love softens identity, supports solidarity, and makes genuine listening possible.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the Theme - Matthew introduces the question of orienting to goodness and begins to examine why that might feel insufficient in the face of suffering.
  2. 00:30 Goodness and Forgetting - He names the inner voice that dismisses goodness as not enough and describes suffering as a kind of forgetting that narrows the heart and mind.
  3. 01:58 Why Hatred Matters - He turns to the danger of hatred, arguing that goodness feels like a luxury only when dukkha and the consequences of hatred are not fully appreciated.
  4. 03:47 Protecting the Mind - Using the image of goodness as glue and a story from a peace walk, he shows how proximity to goodness can steady the mind and why both the world and our own minds need protection.
  5. 06:31 Compassion as Necessity - He invokes the Dalai Lama to underline that love and compassion are necessities, not optional ideals, even when they do not answer every problem.
  6. 07:38 Democracy and Trust - Matthew brings in Samuel Bagg’s argument that democratic breakdown is tied to social identity and epistemic trust, and that repair depends on relationships and solidarity.
  7. 10:33 Love Softens Identity - He explains how hatred and judgment harden identity while love creates openness, loosens motivated reasoning, and gives others room to step out of self-protective stories.
  8. 13:33 Return to Goodness - He closes by urging listeners to stay oriented to goodness, connect across difference, and let care make hatred harder to sustain.
