Dharmette: Measurement and the Immeasurable

AudioDharma 15:28 2024-08-14

Matthew Brensilver reflects on how the comparing mind turns people into objects, and how that habit feeds pride, shame, envy, and a sense of lack. He then widens the frame to measurement itself, drawing on sutta language to show how greed, aversion, and delusion depend on making themes and distinctions. The talk closes with the Brahma-viharas and the “immeasurable” quality of loving-kindness, suggesting a relief that comes when measurement falls silent.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Comparing mind - Matthew introduces a question from a young adult retreat and explores how comparison shapes identity.
  2. 01:18 Pride and shame - He describes the coarse pain of comparing oneself to others and the way it produces pride, shame, envy, and self-objectification.
  3. 02:50 Envy and illusion - Using Joseph Epstein and Mathieu Ricard, he frames envy as painful, unreal deprivation rooted in wounded self-importance.
  4. 04:58 Beyond comparison - He turns from social comparison to the deeper habit of measurement itself, linking it to a fluid, less fixed sense of self.
  5. 06:23 Defilements as measurement - A cited sutta passage is used to show greed, aversion, and delusion as ways the mind makes 'something-ness' and themes.
  6. 08:28 The immeasurables - He contrasts all that measuring with the Brahma-viharas, especially loving-kindness as vast, boundaryless, and not based on evaluation.
  7. 10:05 Pervading the cosmos - A sutta-style description of goodwill pervading all directions leads into a sense of immeasurable awareness and spaciousness.
  8. 12:04 Relief in letting go - The talk closes by pointing toward the relief of not measuring, the fading of phenomena, and the tenderness of living with the immeasurable in view.
