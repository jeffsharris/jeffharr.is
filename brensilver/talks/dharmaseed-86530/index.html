Not-self

Dharma Seed 50:02 2024-07-12

Matthew Brensilver explores not-self as a practical loosening of the burdens built into selfing, using comparisons from psychotherapy, narrative identity, shame, and meditation practice. He traces how the sense of self is repeatedly maintained through effort, comparison, and fear of being seen, then points toward a quieter mode of awareness that is less owned and less defended. The talk closes by suggesting that as clinging drops away, personality can appear more fully and more naturally, with more ease and less strain.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the inquiry - Matthew begins with a light opening and frames the talk around the question of what suffering remains even when the self seems well adjusted.
  2. 01:17 Healing has limits - He uses Freud to contrast ordinary psychotherapy with the deeper dissatisfaction Buddhism addresses, suggesting that self-healing eventually reaches a limit.
  3. 04:03 What experience is like - Drawing on Nagel and Ajahn Sumedho, he distinguishes raw immediate experience from the way selfing flavors that experience.
  4. 08:35 Narrative gravity - He develops the image of self as a center of narrative gravity and compares egoic balance to proprioception, something mostly operating beneath awareness.
  5. 13:28 Why self is tiring - The talk turns to the strain of trying to complete or stabilize the self, and to the wider impact of egoic fixation on other people.
  6. 17:26 Shame and comparison - Matthew links not-self to safety, defensiveness, comparison, and the social pressures that keep self-image in constant renegotiation.
  7. 21:53 Humor and fluidity - As identification loosens, he notes that the mind can become more playful and less serious about its own foibles, though shame still appears at the edge.
  8. 25:20 Truthfulness and shame - He brings in Pamela Meyer and Sartre to show how shame drives self-curation, deception, and the pain of being seen as an object.
  9. 30:20 Practice without a doer - The middle of the talk shifts into practice, emphasizing relaxation, not-ownership, and the way some meditation instructions can subtly strengthen a doer-self.
