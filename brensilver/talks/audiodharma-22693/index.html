Dharmette: Power and Safety

AudioDharma 15:21 2025-04-09

Matthew Brensilver reflects on the strange mix of power, responsibility, and humility that comes with Dharma life. Drawing on a story about Ajahn Chah and the Thai forest tradition, he warns against ego inflation in teaching, then turns to sila, feedback, and communal safety as practical protections. The talk moves toward a quiet but forceful conclusion: practice can deepen our power, but it should also make us safer, more transparent, and more able to serve as a refuge for others.

Chapters

  1. 00:36 Why Power Matters - Matthew introduces the talk by naming a context of harm and unethical leadership, then frames the question of ethical conduct.
  2. 01:35 Ajahn Chah's Warning - He relays Kittisaro's account of Ajahn Chah advising monks not to identify as teachers, but to remain practitioners first.
  3. 04:57 Ripples of Goodness - Matthew reflects on how practice and teaching spread across time, and how one moment of goodness can reverberate for decades.
  4. 06:45 Dharma as Response - He turns to the Thai forest custom of requesting a Dhamma talk, using it to show that teaching should answer a real need rather than become performance.
  5. 08:22 Unpossessable Dharma - The talk develops the theme that Dharma, goodness, and even our roles cannot be owned, and that surrender and softness are necessary.
  6. 10:52 Feedback and Safety - Matthew emphasizes that we cannot fully see ourselves, so honest feedback and shared calibration are essential for communal safety.
  7. 12:07 Power and Prestige - He returns to the language of power and prestige, arguing that one cannot perform Dharma but must become it in a way that remains safe and ethical.
  8. 13:58 A Quiet Trace - The talk closes by linking insight into emptiness with safety, then asking listeners how they will use their power and make their hearts a refuge.
