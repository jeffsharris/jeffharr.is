Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Reflections on Sila

AudioDharma 43:02 2019-03-03

Matthew Brensilver reflects on sila as more than a list of rules: a lived path of non-harming, self-knowledge, humility, and the willingness to be changed by suffering. He moves from the image of becoming a refuge for all beings into questions of grief, forgiveness, emptiness, and how ethical life deepens as the mind becomes more unified. The talk then turns toward modern responsibility, including the pull between self-protection and widening concern for others, animals, and future generations.

Chapters

  1. 00:30 Sila as refuge - Matthew begins with his written reflection on becoming a refuge for all beings and develops it into a view of ethics, safety, and compassion as the heart of the path.
  2. 06:07 Safety and self-knowledge - He explains how being safe for others depends on seeing the roots of harm, including greed, hatred, delusion, and the need for humility and sensitivity.
  3. 09:58 Letting go and grief - The talk turns to the way letting go can feel like grief, including the pain of recognizing harm done by us and to us, and the importance of not squandering suffering.
  4. 13:06 Universal and personal - Matthew holds together the shared human condition and the idiosyncratic ways each person meets the path, emphasizing that ethical growth is improvisational and autobiographical.
  5. 17:10 Heart, karma, and love - He describes how mindfulness of goodness and pain both bring love, how sila and samadhi deepen one another, and how a steadier mind can take on the coloration of care.
  6. 24:19 Emptiness as nonviolence - With a Nagarjuna passage, he links emptiness to radical nonviolence and argues that harm loosens when grasping and the sense of solidity in experience begin to fade.
  7. 27:25 What goodness requires - The talk widens into social and moral questions about what we owe, using Peter Singer to question self-justifying ethics and to challenge easy limits on responsibility.
  8. 34:11 Shelter and enough - Matthew acknowledges exhaustion, quotes Dylan on needing shelter from the storm, and returns to the question of when we have enough stability to serve more fully.
  9. 41:02 Completeness and offering - He closes with a quiet reflection on death, completeness, and the wish that the goodness of practice overflow into the lives of others.
