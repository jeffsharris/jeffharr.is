Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Dharmette: Death at the Center of Life

AudioDharma 14:40 2025-07-23

Matthew Brensilver reflects on mortality as something woven into the present rather than a distant ending. Moving from William James and Kafka to Hegel, Thich Nhat Hanh, and the five remembrances, he asks how finitude shapes meaning, practice, and love. The talk settles into a practical question: if death is already at the center of life, what becomes possible when we are a little less afraid?

Chapters

  1. 00:18 From golf to mortality - He opens with a light pivot from last week’s golf talk into the topic of death and why it belongs at the center of reflection.
  2. 00:54 Death at the core - William James and Kafka frame mortality as intrinsic to human life, and Matthew argues that finitude is woven into the present rather than waiting at the end.
  3. 02:23 Living with finitude - He describes an early, personal sense of time slipping away and asks how Buddhism helps make sense of a life that cannot go on forever.
  4. 03:59 Eternity and meaning - Using Hegel, he questions the appeal of eternity and argues that mortality gives actions, commitments, and love their stakes.
  5. 05:58 Inter-being and loss - Matthew briefly turns to Thich Nhat Hanh’s language of no birth and no death, then notes that inter-being still does not fully answer the reality of loss.
  6. 06:56 The deathless and refuge - He names Nibbana as the deathless and the refuge from Mara and samsara, while also saying he wants to speak about liberation without leaning too hard on metaphysics.
  7. 08:33 Five remembrances - The five remembrances are presented as a practical daily practice that clarifies priorities by keeping aging, sickness, death, and change in view.
  8. 09:42 Uncertainty at the center - Anicca is expanded from impermanence to uncertainty, and Matthew connects that insight to the human hunger for orientation and security.
  9. 11:19 Selfing and freedom - Drawing on Marty Hasselton, he suggests self-view may be an adaptive bias and closes by asking what life and love would look like with less fear.
