Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Mindfulness, Mind-wandering, and Equanimity

AudioDharma 48:46 2024-01-23

Matthew Brensilver frames mindfulness as a practical training in learning what the mind does, rather than a quest for silence or special states. He moves from the pull of mind-wandering and self-referential thought to the role of sustained attention, de-reification, and non-reactivity, and then broadens into equanimity as the quality that lets suffering crest and soften. The talk closes by showing how this wider, steadier relationship to experience reduces identification with story and helps pain become something metabolized rather than spilled onto others.

Chapters

  1. 00:27 Mindfulness as learning - He begins with Nyanaponika Thera and argues that practice starts with attention, learning, and a new way of understanding human life.
  2. 01:53 What we actually want - Brensilver explores how mindfulness reveals our confused habits, the degraded experiences we settle for, and what the nervous system is really asking for.
  3. 04:50 The allure of unconsciousness - He uses William James and everyday examples to show why mind-wandering and going slack can feel soothing, even when they keep us stuck.
  4. 08:18 Mind wandering as baseline - The talk turns to the biology of distraction, the usefulness of mind-wandering, and why retreat practice has to work with this tendency rather than condemn it.
  5. 14:11 Continuity without strain - Using Malia Mason and Ajahn Tejaniya, he describes how retreat trains a sustainable continuity of attention without turning practice into over-effort.
  6. 22:27 Anchoring and noticing - A research-based definition of mindfulness leads into how present-centered awareness, monitoring for distraction, and de-reification work in practice.
  7. 30:16 Thought, space, and reactivity - He explains how thoughts become confining when treated as reality, and how mindfulness creates space through metacognitive monitoring and non-reactivity.
  8. 38:20 Equanimity and release - With Thich Nhat Hanh and Shinzen Young, he shows how equanimity lets suffering crest and break, softening the heart and making pain usable.
  9. 44:29 Less story, more life - He closes by connecting this practice to less self-identification and less autobiographical narration, so life is lived more directly and with less leakage of suffering.
