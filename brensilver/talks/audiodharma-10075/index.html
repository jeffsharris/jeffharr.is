Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Seven Factors of Awakening

AudioDharma 59:20 2019-05-14

Matthew Brensilver lays out the seven factors of awakening as a living sequence rather than a checklist, beginning with mindfulness and investigation and moving through energy, delight, tranquility, concentration, and equanimity. He keeps returning to the feel of practice in the body and mind: how curiosity opens inquiry, how effort becomes relaxed perseverance, and how equanimity makes room for the messiness of human life. Several brief references — including Sīdō Tejaniya, Gil Fronsdal, Sharon Salzberg, Leonard Cohen, Heather Havrilesky, Michelle McDonald, and Shinzen Young — help ground the teaching in concrete examples and personal stories.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening into release - He begins with a small personal example to frame the path as one of release rather than acquisition and to set up the talk’s focus on suffering and opening.
  2. 06:01 Seven factors overview - Matthew introduces the seven factors, explains the movement from hindrances to awakening, and cites the Buddha story of asking for the factors during illness.
  3. 08:29 Learning mindfulness - He describes mindfulness as something learned through practice, not a fixed technique, and develops it as awareness of awareness.
  4. 11:32 Equanimity and balance - The talk turns to equanimity as the balance that keeps mindfulness from becoming brittle or reactive, including the idea of the mind’s relative balance among factors.
  5. 18:07 Softening into experience - He explores how equanimity and mindfulness reduce craving and aversion in the act of noticing, and uses a Leonard Cohen line as a brief image of radical openness.
  6. 24:06 Tolerating ambivalence - A long Heather Havrilesky quotation illustrates the ordinary mess of love, irritation, and judgment, which Matthew uses to show equanimity as tolerance for human ambivalence.
  7. 28:00 Curiosity and investigation - Matthew develops dhammavicaya as curiosity without agenda, cites the Buddha’s four responses to pain, and tells a retreat story with Michelle McDonald about hidden defensiveness.
  8. 34:43 Energy, delight, calm - He distinguishes investigation from rumination, then moves through energy as persistence, delight as a sense of homecoming, and tranquility as the heart’s relief from stimulation.
  9. 43:03 Collected mind, not thought - The final movement shows how samadhi steadies listening, shifts practice from philosophy into lived path, and ends by contrasting compulsive thinking with knowing life as experience.
