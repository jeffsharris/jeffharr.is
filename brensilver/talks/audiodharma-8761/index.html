Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Grief

AudioDharma 42:13 2018-04-01

Matthew Brensilver explores grief as a central part of letting go, rather than something separate from practice. He moves from personal reflections and poetic quotations into a wider view: grief as a response to love, attachment, impermanence, and the limits of control. The talk also widens into collective grief, mortality, and the way Dharma practice can make room for vulnerability without rushing past it.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening the Topic - Matthew introduces grief as a serious subject and asks listeners to notice whether this is the right talk to hear right now.
  2. 01:48 Non-clinging and Grief - He frames suffering through Buddhist teaching, then uses Leonard Cohen and Joseph Goldstein to suggest that letting go often involves grieving.
  3. 04:25 What Grief Feels Like - Grief is described as universal, sometimes clear and sometimes vague, and as the painful movement from clenched holding to release.
  4. 07:26 Practice and Collective Loss - He reflects on tears in loving-kindness practice and broadens the conversation to national and historical grief that has not been fully faced.
  5. 10:03 Risk, Darkness, Wound - Rilke’s language of darkness and the slow healing of wounds help him explore grief as vulnerability that cannot be forced or simplified.
  6. 14:00 Buddha, Attachment, Impermanence - He revisits Buddhist ideas, the Buddha’s own losses, attachment theory, and Mary Oliver’s poem to show how loving and letting go belong together.
  7. 21:06 Fog and Titration - Matthew describes how grief disrupts our assumptions of permanence and how we move through it in small, bearable doses, one stretch of road at a time.
  8. 25:44 Heartbreak and Groundlessness - Using a classroom story, he contrasts a quick theory of heartbreak with a slower honoring of its beauty, then links grief to groundlessness and practice.
  9. 33:15 Mortality and Gratitude - He closes by connecting practice to dying, the softening of fear, gratitude for what is given, and the ordinary losses that reveal how much we value what we love.
