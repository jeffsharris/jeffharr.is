Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Removing the Thorns of Memory and Becoming

AudioDharma 56:09 2019-05-16

Matthew Brensilver explores memory as something practice does not erase but gently transforms. He moves from present-moment awareness into the way memory, habit, and autobiography shape suffering, then widens to mortality and the craving to become something else. The talk settles into a quiet argument that safety, relinquishment, and openness can let the past be digested and the future feel less fearsome.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening and context - He greets the group, speaks about tailoring instruction to different practitioners, and frames the talk as something listeners will need to take in for themselves.
  2. 03:30 Presence and memory - He questions what it means to be present, then shifts from stepping outside time to a practice of letting memory and planning arise within awareness.
  3. 09:20 Memory in the brain - Using Elie Wiesel and a neuroscience study, he argues that memory is central to both personal healing and the way thought begins before it becomes explicit.
  4. 14:05 Rewriting the past - He brings in Yalom and Philip Roth to show how the past is constructed, how autobiography can harden, and how practice loosens the grip of self-story.
  5. 19:50 What we become - With Thich Nhat Hanh, Freud, and Ajahn Sucitto, he shows how the present carries the past and how what is not fully heard can become identity and repetition.
  6. 24:16 Undigested harm - He tells a childhood story of sibling rivalry to illustrate jealousy, cruelty, and the way practice brings buried harm back into view so it can be felt and unwound.
  7. 30:07 Blessing the past - He describes practice as blessing former selves and relates this to Kore-eda’s film After Life, where one precious memory is chosen before disappearing into it.
  8. 36:29 Fear and becoming - He turns to mortality, existence bias, and the Buddha’s teaching on bhava tanha to question the deep drive to secure a future and become someone.
  9. 44:30 Freedom as release - Citing Ajahn Sumedho, Sariputra, and Ajahn Sucitto, he presents nibbana as the cessation of becoming and practice as a gradual settling into safety, relinquishment, and a freer relation to life.
