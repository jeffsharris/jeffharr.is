Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Dharmette 2 - Love, Helplessness, Equanimity, and Affect

AudioDharma 37:09 2023-12-02

Matthew Brensilver uses early Buddhist passages, modern philosophy, and recent research to look at love as something real, costly, and often inseparable from helplessness. He moves from the Buddha's fear and compassion toward a wider question: how to care without turning care into ego, despair, or a demand that life keep improving. The talk gradually settles into equanimity as a way of staying open at the edge of what we can control, and ends with a vivid reading of Philip Roth on finitude and aliveness.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Opening Setup - Matthew introduces the talk, mentions posted references, and begins with a critical look at idealized images of the Buddha.
  2. 01:11 Fear and the Thorn - He turns to an early sutta about fear, violence, and the thorn in the heart as a way to understand suffering and release.
  3. 04:27 Love and Helplessness - The Buddha's reaction to suffering is read as deep love mixed with helplessness, leading into samvega, urgency, and renunciation.
  4. 08:20 Widening Empathy - Matthew brings in Parfit and Susan Langer to describe empathy as a widening circle that becomes compassion and connects naturally with equanimity.
  5. 11:36 Compassion Costs - He warns that even practice can become spiritual materialism, then uses recent research to show that compassion can feel effortful and needs care.
  6. 14:25 Progress and Rest - The talk asks how to balance love and peace, then examines our habit of seeking progress and why equanimity means giving up the demand to always get somewhere.
  7. 18:58 Grief and Finitude - He reframes grief as part of maturity, then reflects on emotions as tools shaped for a local world that now meets global power and mortality.
  8. 25:29 Honest Ethical Seeing - Using Martha Nussbaum, he argues for a sober investigation of emotion and warns that ethics can become a channel for greed, hate, and false consolation.
  9. 31:45 Spending Love Wisely - He closes by asking where care is actually useful, then ends with Philip Roth on living at the edge of life and the strange ecstasy of finitude.
