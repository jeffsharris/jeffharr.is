Dharmette: Grief as a Facet of Dharma

AudioDharma 14:59 2025-04-01

Matthew Brensilver offers grief as something deeper than a passing emotion: a companion to wakefulness, love, and ethical growth. He moves through the Buddha’s life, the First Noble Truth, and contemporary psychology to suggest that Dharma practice does not erase grief so much as transmute it. The talk stays close to the lived experience of change, loss, and the widening of the heart.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Dharma and tension - He begins by framing Dharma as a set of teachings held in dynamic tension, introducing grief as one indispensable side of practice.
  2. 01:32 Grief as irreducible - He names grief as the space between freedom and suffering, and argues that even deep practice does not eliminate it.
  3. 03:10 The Buddha and loss - Using Leonard Cohen and the Buddha’s own life story, he shows how wakefulness and loss belong together rather than cancel each other out.
  4. 05:51 Not a pathology - He turns to anthropodenial and explains that grief is not simply an afflictive emotion to be removed, though it can become stuck or misleading.
  5. 07:12 Practice and growing pains - He links grief to grief-informed ethics, the pain of letting go, and the growing pains that come with deeper compassion and change.
  6. 10:18 Facing dukkha - Drawing on the First Noble Truth and Shelley Taylor’s work on positive illusions, he contrasts truthfulness with self-deception and the agitation it creates.
  7. 12:46 Grieving to change - James Baldwin helps him distinguish real transformation from merely expressive sorrow, emphasizing that grief can accompany genuine change.
  8. 14:02 Grief into love - He closes by describing grief as pregnant with bodhicitta, a process of purification in which pain is not far from love.
