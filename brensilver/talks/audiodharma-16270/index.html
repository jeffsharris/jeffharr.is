Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Reflections on Equanimity

AudioDharma 16:16 2022-05-13

Matthew Brensilver reflects on equanimity as a form of rest for the heart: not indifference, but the capacity to meet experience without constant strain. He moves from a brief Ajahn Buddhadasa line through the tension between caring and not caring, then into the balance of equanimity and compassion in the face of loss and grief. The talk stays grounded in ordinary exhaustion, impermanence, and the way a steadier heart can serve more wisely.

Chapters

  1. 00:12 Opening the theme - Matthew begins with a line attributed to Ajahn Buddhadasa and uses it to frame ease, rest, and letting things settle.
  2. 00:46 The need for rest - He turns to the pressure of caring, the limits of the nervous system, and the sense that the heart cannot stay in bloom all the time.
  3. 02:04 Heart and sleep - A flower, sleep, and ordinary exhaustion become analogies for how the heart opens, closes, and needs periods of being unstimulated.
  4. 04:05 False ways of resting - Matthew contrasts true rest with stimulation and unconsciousness, suggesting that equanimity may be the heart's deepest rest.
  5. 05:16 Care and not care - He uses T. S. Eliot's line about caring and not caring to explore the danger of nihilism, the pull of over-significance, and the middle path between them.
  6. 08:14 Equanimity with compassion - The talk shifts to how equanimity and compassion support one another, introducing two quotations about death and loss.
  7. 10:11 Loss made visible - Quotations from Katherine Schultz deepen the sense that loss is everywhere, and that grief can reveal the normal fragility of life without turning into despair.
  8. 13:44 A steadier response - Matthew closes by describing how equanimity purifies and strengthens compassion, allowing a rhythm of resting and serving.
