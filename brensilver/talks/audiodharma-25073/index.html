Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

A Meaningful Dharma Life

AudioDharma 17:10 2026-03-18

Matthew Brensilver reflects on a quieter promise of Dharma practice: not only greater happiness, but a life that becomes more meaningful. He traces how the mind keeps making meaning, how practice loosens that habit, and how ethical conduct, vow, and lineage can build a more grounded sense of purpose. The talk moves from a research definition of meaning to personal examples, then toward compassion, non-regret, and the consolation of being part of a long stream of wisdom and love.

Chapters

  1. 00:22 The promise of practice - Matthew opens by contrasting the familiar promise of happiness with a less discussed promise of meaning.
  2. 01:18 Meaning and its making - He explains meaning as something the mind continually constructs from experience and how practice invites release from that compulsion.
  3. 03:31 What meaning links - Drawing on Zhou and Baumeister, he describes meaning as non-physical connection across time, purpose, value, and self-worth.
  4. 05:04 A personal turn - Matthew briefly shares how he came to the path through a sense that his life had reached a dead end and might be searching for meaning.
  5. 05:59 Drama, culture, and emptiness - He uses an overheard workplace story and reflections on celebrity fascination to show how often people inflate significance and chase outside sources of meaning.
  6. 08:58 Meaning from within - A line from Philip Appleman and a return to the research support the claim that meaning has to be built inside lived experience, not imported from outside.
  7. 10:29 Practice and purpose - He grounds Buddhist practice in sentience, suffering, and the wish for peace, then names clarity, compassion, and personal Dharma as expressions of purpose.
  8. 12:23 Vow and non-regret - Matthew turns to Aristotle, love, ethical conduct, and the bodhisattva vow as ways of living meaningfully even when our power is limited.
  9. 15:04 Lineage and consolation - He closes by placing practice in a long lineage and naming the consolation of having lived in accordance with goodness.
