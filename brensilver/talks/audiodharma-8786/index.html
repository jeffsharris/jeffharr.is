Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Samadhi

AudioDharma 46:28 2018-04-14

Matthew Brensilver explores samadhi as a quiet gathering of the mind that comes less from forcing concentration than from relaxing friction with experience. He links attention to restlessness, technology, narrative thinking, and the habit of waiting for a better future, then turns toward the ways practice can feel safe without words. The talk closes with teachings on letting go, including brief references to Shinzen Young, Daniel Kahneman, Joseph Goldstein, Suzuki Roshi, and Ajahn Brahm.

Chapters

  1. 00:08 Opening reflections - He begins with a light preface and moves into a reconsideration of enlightenment as the easing of friction rather than the solving of every problem.
  2. 02:06 Less friction, more gathering - Matthew explains how samadhi can arise when the mind relates to experience with less resistance, and how restlessness keeps leaning toward the next thing.
  3. 05:55 Attention under pressure - He connects modern technology and engagement-based attention systems to compulsive stimulation, then contrasts that with the restorative quality of nature.
  4. 10:40 Waiting without seeking - The talk turns to the subtle habit of waiting for the next moment, and to meditation as staying present without trying to find refuge elsewhere.
  5. 15:03 Samadhi as refuge - Matthew describes samadhi as a way of turning the dial down, reducing urgency, and making the Dharma more than an idea.
  6. 20:13 Narration and fear - Using Kahneman and his own observations, he examines the compulsion to narrate experience, the fear of silence, and the need to feel safe without words.
  7. 26:19 Mindfulness and concentration - He broadens samadhi beyond narrow focus, brings in Shinzen Young's view of relevance, and argues that concentration is part of mindfulness rather than separate from it.
  8. 31:36 Not chasing peak states - Matthew warns against attaching to blissful experiences, including a Joseph Goldstein story and a second cautionary example about a practitioner chasing an old peak moment.
  9. 36:25 Letting go of will - He closes with Suzuki Roshi and Ajahn Brahm on sufficiency, patience, and the way stillness comes when control is released rather than intensified.
