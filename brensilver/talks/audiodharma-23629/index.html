Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

The Circuitous Journey to Presence through Past and Future

AudioDharma 39:08 2025-08-16

Matthew Brensilver reflects on why “being present” can feel less like a choice than the result of long training. He explores how memory, regret, fear, and future-orientation keep pulling the mind away from refuge, then argues that practice works gradually by making the past less personal and the future less threatening. Along the way he brings in Thich Nhat Hanh, Ajahn Sucitto, Leonard Cohen, Zadie Smith, and Bob Dylan to show how Dharma moves through time rather than bypassing it.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 What presence means - Matthew begins by questioning the usual instruction to “be present” and suggests it often means not being identified with discursive thought.
  2. 01:50 Thich Nhat Hanh's influence - He recalls first encountering practice through Thich Nhat Hanh and shows how early teachings on peace in the here and now both inspired and unsettled him.
  3. 04:03 Presence as fruit - The talk turns toward the idea that presence is not forced but arises when memory, desire, regret, and fear have been worked with deeply enough.
  4. 07:10 Dharma and conditionality - Matthew frames practice as deconstructive and emphasizes conditionality: the present is shaped by a long causal history, including wounds, habits, and evolution.
  5. 10:06 Backlog of the past - He describes the mind's backlog of undigested experience, shame, and unresolved loss, using Leonard Cohen to point toward patient listening rather than premature fixing.
  6. 13:34 Self and opacity - The talk moves into how memory can feel like identity itself until mindfulness makes these beliefs opaque and knowable rather than simply lived as self.
  7. 18:04 Grief loosens shame - Matthew describes mindfulness as a gradual investigation that lets the past breathe, turning grief into relief and loosening the shame bound up with self-view.
  8. 25:22 The urge to fill time - Using Zadie Smith, he reflects on the craving to become and to keep time filled, linking this restlessness with fear, ego, and the body's threat system.
  9. 31:49 Futurelessness and refuge - He closes by describing refuge, mortality, and anicca as ways to rest without false alarms, then uses Bob Dylan to suggest that the journey to the present runs through both past and future.
