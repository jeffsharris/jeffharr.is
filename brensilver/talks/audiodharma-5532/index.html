Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

"History is Ending Today"

AudioDharma 11:30 2015-03-25

Matthew Brensilver reflects on anicca, or impermanence, as more than the simple fact that things end. He moves from the Buddha’s three marks of existence to a research study on how poorly we predict our own future change, then turns to the emotional shift this teaching can bring: first heartbreak, then refuge. The talk closes by widening impermanence into an everyday practice of seeing each moment, each story, and each identity as born and passing.

Chapters

  1. 00:09 Three Marks of Existence - Brensilver introduces dukkha, anicca, and anatta as a way of seeing that runs against habit.
  2. 01:36 Impermanence Deeper Than Ending - He uses Bhikkhu Bodhi’s formulation to show that anicca means constant breaking up, not just eventual ending.
  3. 02:53 The Self As Stable - The talk turns to the way we view change from a sense of self that feels fixed, and why that matters.
  4. 05:28 Research On Blind Spots - A Science study and a New York Times anecdote about Dr. McAdams illustrate how little change people expect in themselves.
  5. 06:57 The Heartbreak Of Anicca - Brensilver names the painful side of impermanence and the need to let go of what cannot be held.
  6. 08:05 Openness As Refuge - He begins to reframe impermanence as a source of openness, rather than only loss.
  7. 09:20 Born And Dying - The talk closes by bringing impermanence down to the moment-by-moment level, where each story and identity is seen as arising and passing.
