Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Developing our Dharma Stories

AudioDharma 46:13 2022-06-22

Matthew Brensilver reflects on how Dharma practice works through both direct experience and the stories we tell ourselves. He moves from the use of thought as a temporary support, to the importance of personal, well-fitted “Dharma stories,” and then into a sustained meditation on mortality, helplessness, and the way death-awareness can loosen fear and self-clinging. The talk closes by shifting from getting to learning, and from self-improvement toward a life that can meet whatever arises as practice.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Thought and silence - Matthew opens by contrasting samadhi and silence with the use of thought, suggesting that both have their place in practice.
  2. 03:59 The ladder of thought - He uses Wittgenstein to show how teachings and instructions can function as temporary supports that are eventually set aside.
  3. 06:06 Stories that shape change - The talk turns to Dharma narratives and the idea, through Martha Nussbaum, that the heart changes through story as much as through analysis.
  4. 09:21 Mother tongue Dharma - Matthew emphasizes that practice language must be personal and resonant, using the image of hearing the Dharma in one’s mother tongue.
  5. 14:35 Pep talks and awakening - He reads Patrul Rinpoche and a teaching from Ziegler Rinpoche as sharp reminders to wake up rather than keep rearranging a dream.
  6. 23:10 Sitting with mortality - From the instruction to sit like a dying person, Matthew explores helplessness, grief, and the shaping force of death awareness in his own life.
  7. 32:41 Fear, being, and release - He connects the Buddha’s teaching on becoming with the fear that builds defensive identity, then quotes Rumi to show a more joyful relationship to death.
  8. 38:56 Learning instead of getting - The closing section gathers Ajahn Chah and Joko Beck into a practical reorientation: life is for learning, and whatever arises can be used as practice.
