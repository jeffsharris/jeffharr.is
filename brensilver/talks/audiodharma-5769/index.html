Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Limits of Technique

AudioDharma 13:25 2015-06-24

Matthew Brensilver explores the limits of treating meditation as a fixed technique, while still affirming the value of having reliable methods to lean on. He argues that Dharma practice also requires improvisation, curiosity, and an ability to meet the particular conditions of one’s own mind. Near the end, he turns to the role of right view, drawing on Sayadaw Tejaniya to show that wisdom is not just added to technique but shapes how practice is understood from the start.

Chapters

  1. 00:29 Why teach at all - He begins by reflecting on teaching as a response to experience and to what is useful for the community.
  2. 01:30 The appeal of systems - He names the modern pull toward integrated systems and briefly invokes Nietzsche’s suspicion of systematizers.
  3. 02:46 Technique has value - He emphasizes that a few dependable meditation methods can be deeply helpful, especially in difficulty.
  4. 03:59 Improvise with the moment - He shifts to Dharma as improvisation and tells a Zen story about the teaching of an entire life being an appropriate response.
  5. 05:20 No fixed template - He says practice is shaped by the specific conditions of a person’s life and mind, so response must be tailored and flexible.
  6. 06:54 Curiosity and surprise - He describes practice conversations as requiring precision and notes that discovering delusion depends on curiosity and a willingness to be surprised.
  7. 09:08 Technique and view - He contrasts technique with view, suggests that secular mindfulness often favors method, and argues that right view cannot be left out.
  8. 11:14 What technique misses - He closes by showing how blind application of method can obscure the texture of the mind itself, and invites honoring both the usefulness and the limits of technique.
