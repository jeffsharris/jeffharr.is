Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Love & Rest Daylong - Tranquility, Shinzen Young's Do-Nothing Practice, Intro and Guided

AudioDharma 57:11 2024-10-27

Matthew Brensilver explores tranquility as a necessary form of protection, not avoidance, and frames the path as a balance between seclusion and opening. He then moves through concrete ways of settling the body and senses—breath, gravity, silence, visual blankness—before introducing Shinzen Young’s do-nothing practice as a rest from controlling attention. The talk gradually turns from skillful stabilization into a guided period of letting experience be, including the release of self-correction and the recognition that there is often nothing to improve in the moment.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Tranquility and insight - Matthew opens with the Buddha’s teaching that tranquility and insight arise together, then explains tranquility as a protective settling of the heart rather than avoidance.
  2. 02:25 Protection and sensitivity - He describes the dialectic between opening and seclusion, and how practice must honor individual sensitivity, brittleness, and overwhelm.
  3. 05:24 Why settling matters - The talk turns to the need for tranquilizing the mind before meeting grief, fear, and sensory intensity, using attention as something that shapes experience deeply.
  4. 09:52 Breath and body - Drawing on Shinzen Young and other teachers, Matthew offers ways to settle through the breath, gravity, elemental heaviness, and the body’s sense of relaxation.
  5. 12:31 Listening to silence - He guides attention toward silence inside and outside, and toward the visual field as blankness rather than as a set of objects.
  6. 15:28 Do-nothing practice - Matthew introduces Shinzen Young’s do-nothing instructions, emphasizing the dropping of any intention to control attention and the possibility of deep rest.
  7. 22:58 Guided release - A long guided practice follows, beginning with fuller breathing and grounding, then moving into silence, visual rest, and surrender of effort and preference.
  8. 40:25 No good or bad meditation - The practice deepens into letting whatever happens happen, with repeated reminders to drop self-correction, release the urge to engineer experience, and leave oneself alone.
