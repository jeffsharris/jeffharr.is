Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Guided meditation: feeling as a prediction of risk

Dharma Seed 57:29 2022-03-05

This guided meditation begins with a long settling into the body, using the breath, posture, and soundscape as a stable anchor while gently releasing bracing. It then turns toward Matthew Brensilver’s central theme: feeling as a prediction of risk, including urgency, aversion, and bodily pain, and how equanimity can keep those signals from turning into extra struggle. The closing section opens into brief questions about walking meditation, then returns to the practical tone of staying close, sensitive, and steady in retreat.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Settling the body - The talk opens by easing into natural breathing, posture, and a softer relationship to the body.
  2. 04:09 Finding an anchor - Matthew invites a scan of body and mind, then offers several possible anchors, including the whole body, the nostrils, groundedness, and sound.
  3. 10:00 Object and background - Attention is framed as a balance between what is held in awareness and what is allowed to remain in the background.
  4. 17:05 Feeling as threat - He names the future’s pull, vigilance, and the felt sense of threat or opportunity as part of the affective system.
  5. 24:13 False alarm - Urgency is treated as something to meet openly rather than obey, with no need to fix or protect anything in the moment.
  6. 27:42 Opening to feeling - The teaching turns toward radical openness, freedom without a signpost, and the way feeling shapes our sense of meaning and urgency.
  7. 35:10 Pain and aversion - Physical discomfort is explored as a prediction of harm, and the practice of meeting it as transient and workable is emphasized.
  8. 40:49 Walking and equanimity - After brief references to figures such as Shinzen Young and Avalokiteshvara, Matthew answers a question about walking meditation and then deepens the discussion of aversion, surrender, and love.
  9. 55:14 Closing retreat instructions - He ends with practical retreat reminders to stay gentle, slow down, and remain aware of the whole field and its effects on others.
