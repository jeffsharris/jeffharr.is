Matthew Brensilver Dharma Talks

Reflections on Ajahn Sucitto Talk

AudioDharma 38:41 2023-03-05

Matthew Brensilver reflects on an Ajahn Sucitto story about visiting a brother with advanced illness, using it to explore what remains meaningful when words, memory, and control fail. The talk moves from embodied care and mettā to mortality, family, and the limits of empathy, then toward the quieter relief of equanimity. Along the way he brings in Nick Cave, Cormac McCarthy, Carl Rogers, and Ajahn Chah to show how practice can stay close to the body and open to suffering without being overwhelmed by it.

Chapters

  1. 00:59 Ajahn Sucitto's visit - Matthew introduces Ajahn Sucitto's visit to his brother and describes how touch, chanting, and presence become the real language when communication breaks down.
  2. 04:01 What remains after loss - He extends the story into a meditation on surrendering the wish to make things different, and on family as a karmic bond carrying both pain and inherited potential.
  3. 08:09 Mortality as messenger - The talk turns toward death as a Dhamma messenger, including a student's fear of dying and a reading of Bertrand Russell that reframes finitude as a prompt toward goodness.
  4. 11:49 Body over abstraction - Matthew contrasts embodied practice with getting lost in concepts, using illness, touch, and Cormac McCarthy to point back to breathing, feeling, and the heart.
  5. 18:12 Kindness without words - He dwells on the simple potency of touch and then brings in Nick Cave's grief to show how small gestures can matter more than explanations in moments of sorrow.
  6. 23:11 Love made vast - The chanting of mettā opens into a reflection on love that does not depend on conditions, and on the relief of being met without needing anything fixed or perfect.
  7. 28:08 Limits of compassion - Matthew examines the pain of wanting to rescue loved ones, the pull toward overidentification, and Carl Rogers' distinction between empathy and emotional contagion.
  8. 33:12 Equanimity and release - The closing section returns to the relief of not having to force change, presenting equanimity as another species of love that can stay open in the face of suffering.
