Alan Watts

Bang or Whimper Sequel

KPFA Archive 24:27 1960-01-01

Alan Watts begins with a listener’s objection to his remarks on survival, technology, and the threat of a world ending by “bang” or “whimper.” From there he turns the challenge into a larger argument against human exceptionalism, using Taoist and comic fables to question whether people are really the center of nature. The talk moves steadily from apocalypse and anxiety toward relativity, interdependence, and the relief of giving up the need for absolute certainty.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 The Survival Question - Watts opens by framing the talk around population, technology, and the narrow choice between a bang and a whimper.
  2. 01:00 A Listener Objects - He reads a long letter challenging his remarks on survival, mortality, and the fate of the earth, and begins answering its concerns.
  3. 07:48 Taoist Counterpoint - A Lieh Tzu story is used to argue that the ten thousand creatures belong to the same category and none exists merely for human use.
  4. 10:04 Head and Stomach - Watts develops the idea that seeming hierarchy can hide interdependence, using the body fable to show that the head is not the whole of the organism.
  5. 13:06 The Human Weed - A comic farmer story punctures the idea that human cultivation perfects nature and reframes our interventions as temporary and partial.
  6. 14:08 Fish Against Humanity - Watts toys with the idea that fish, not humans, might be judged superior, reversing the usual standards of culture, grace, and spiritual life.
  7. 20:00 No Top Creature - He broadens the argument across species, insisting that every creature can be made to look central, so no single form of life can claim the top.
  8. 24:29 Relativity and Release - The talk closes by turning from cosmic relativity to the human hunger for certainty, ending with the claim that freedom lies in letting go of the need to be right.
