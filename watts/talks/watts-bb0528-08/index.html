Alan Watts

Interview with Dr. Chang Chung-yuan

KPFA Archive 30:49 1960-02-19

Alan Watts opens with a Chinese poem about searching for a hermit, using it to set up Dr. Chang Chung-yuan as a rare Taoist voice worth hearing. The conversation then moves from wu-wei as non-interference, through practical examples of craft and science, into a tighter argument about li, shi, and the refusal to split reality from appearance. In the final stretch, the talk turns toward cooperation rather than opposition, ending on Tao as an invisible, self-balancing harmony known more by feeling than by theory.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 The Hidden Sage - Watts opens with a Chinese poem about an elusive hermit and introduces Chang as a living Taoist figure unusually hard to find in civilized life.
  2. 02:23 Artificiality and Nature - The interview turns to Zhuangzi, technical artifice, and the question of whether human behavior can really run against the Tao.
  3. 05:00 Wu-wei in Practice - Chang explains wu-wei as non-interference, extending the idea from shelter and craft to social order and the handling of materials.
  4. 09:50 The Bottle Story - Watts brings in a Zen riddle about a ghost in a bottle to show how simple action can cut through conceptual confusion.
  5. 12:47 Science and Principle - The exchange widens to modern technology, then to the Chinese term li, with chairness and material forms used to test what counts as nature.
  6. 16:15 Reality and Appearance - Chang and Watts develop li as both principle and reality, then reject any sharp split between the spiritual and the concrete world.
  7. 20:18 River, Judo, Cooperation - Using li and shi, Watts argues that one cannot truly swim against the current of reality, so wisdom lies in reading the direction of things and cooperating with it.
  8. 24:46 Insight Beyond Ego - The talk shifts toward transformed selfhood, intuitive prajna insight, and the claim that Taoist effectiveness depends more on the right person than on the right method.
  9. 26:15 The Self-Balancing Whole - The closing section uses a table, organs, and homeostasis to describe the universe as an interrelated organism whose balance is Tao itself.
