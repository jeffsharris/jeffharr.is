Alan Watts

The Libido For Ugliness

KPFA Archive 26:26 1960-06-24

Alan Watts opens by reading Mencken’s corrosive portrait of industrial Pennsylvania, then turns that complaint toward California, where scenic Marin is being carved up by tract homes and a proposed highway. From there the talk widens into a critique of progress as automatic, machine-led, and finally as a symptom of self-hatred: not just damage to the land, but to the human relation with it. He closes by moving from roads to food to the everyday habits that make ugliness feel normal.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Mencken’s indictment - Watts introduces the issue by reading Mencken’s portrait of Westmoreland County as a vivid case study in industrial ugliness.
  2. 10:21 Marin under pressure - He shifts to Marin County, where tract homes and a planned highway threaten a landscape he describes as still unusually beautiful.
  3. 15:12 When beauty is consumed - Watts argues that the desire to reach scenic places by effortless mass travel tends to spoil them, like passive entertainment.
  4. 18:11 Progress and machinery - He connects road-building to bureaucracy and automatic expansion, then broadens the point into a Chinese-inflected warning about fighting nature.
  5. 20:51 Self-hatred in culture - Watts suggests that the deeper source of environmental damage may be an inward hostility that shows up in how people build and live.
  6. 23:02 Food and final warning - He extends the argument to food and everyday taste, then ends with a direct call for citizens to resist destructive development.
