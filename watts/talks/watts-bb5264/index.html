Alan Watts

Man is a Hoax

KPFA Archive 30:06 1960-09-23

Alan Watts starts with a critique of childhood and schooling as systems of postponement, then widens the argument into work, money, retirement, and the culture of permanent future-tense living. From there he turns to identity itself: the person as mask, the split between self and other, and the idea that we are taught to feel like strangers in the world. The talk moves through breathing, blame, and cosmic imagery before ending on a sharp contrast between modern mechanistic common sense and older, more immediate ways of understanding responsibility and being.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Children on Probation - Watts opens by attacking the cultural habit of treating children as not yet fully human, using family, school, and parental authority as examples.
  2. 01:07 The Preparation Machine - He extends the critique through schooling, careers, and retirement, arguing that every stage promises arrival but keeps moving the finish line.
  3. 03:51 Work, Money, Pleasure - Watts turns to the everyday hoax of work and consumption, mocking the idea that money purchases pleasure and that possessions substitute for lived skill.
  4. 06:19 Living in Tomorrow - He explains how the culture trains people to expect happiness later, then uses Confucius to contrast that habit with a fully lived moment.
  5. 09:24 Person as Mask - Watts shifts to language and identity, arguing that 'person' means mask and that social definitions of self create chronic frustration.
  6. 13:08 Split Self, Split World - He develops the idea of a divided ego, using Burrow's terms and the notion of a skin-encapsulated self to show how tension becomes identity.
  7. 17:31 Breath and Inseparability - Breathing becomes his practical example of the voluntary and involuntary blending together, leading into a broader claim that self and other are mutually necessary.
  8. 21:24 Blame and Cosmic Action - Watts retells the Adam-and-Eve story as a chain of blame and then recasts birth and the Big Bang as expressions of one ongoing process rather than separate causes.
  9. 26:23 Who Are You, Really? - He closes with Zen-style questioning, a contrast between Christian and Hindu images of divinity, and a final attack on mechanistic modern common sense as the myth behind alienation.
