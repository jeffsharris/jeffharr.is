Alan Watts

Consciousness and Concentration

KPFA Archive 33:08 1959-11-27

Alan Watts begins by reading Freud and Gardner Murphy to suggest that modern psychology already brushes against a wider, less self-bound sense of consciousness. From there he moves into a practical question: whether concentration is really a struggle of will, or whether forcing attention only deepens the ego’s strain; a Japanese Zen reply becomes the pivot for his argument. The talk then widens into a critique of effort, a defense of natural attention, and a closing turn toward human limits, with a final tribute to Lewis Hill and an appeal for KPFA.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Psychology and the larger self - Watts opens with Freud and Gardner Murphy to show that psychology already contains hints of a self beyond ordinary ego-bound awareness.
  2. 02:41 The twist of perception - He argues that the real problem is a basic distortion in how we feel ourselves and the world, a problem Indian and Chinese philosophy try to correct.
  3. 03:56 Why shortcuts matter - Watts insists the change of awareness is not a trance but a different standpoint, then jokes about spiritual shortcuts before defending simplification over effort.
  4. 07:11 Laziness and simplification - He compares spiritual discipline to finding a better technique, arguing that insight and naturalness matter more than heroic exertion.
  5. 10:44 Objects of concentration - Watts turns to repetitive practices like OM, MU, and Jesus’s name, then quotes a Zen master to argue that concentration cannot be forced.
  6. 15:32 The snake with legs - He explains that trying to concentrate intentionally is self-defeating, using the Zen image of putting legs on a snake to show the absurdity of forcing what is already happening.
  7. 18:03 Against egoic strain - Watts broadens the point: our habitual effort to dominate thought is itself the ego, and letting go brings a more natural ease that can feel like voidness or oneness.
  8. 22:21 Effort, sensitivity, and character - He pushes back against the cult of struggle, arguing that human character is measured by sensitivity and quality of experience rather than sheer endurance.
  9. 25:05 Lewis Hill and KPFA - The talk ends as a memorial and a plea: Watts reflects on Lewis Hill’s death, praises his sensitivity and genius, and asks listeners to support KPFA.
