Alan Watts

Bang or Whimper

KPFA Archive 27:28 1959-12-25

Alan Watts begins with population alarm and the modern habit of treating survival as the only goal, then widens the question to the whole logic of technological culture. He brings in Taoist restraint, Norman O. Brown’s critique of repression, and a Big Sur parable of aphids and bare stalks to test whether progress is actually human-friendly. The talk settles into a hard choice between stability and intensity, only to argue that both sides can become anxious, future-obsessed forms of the same problem: not knowing how to live now.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Population Alarm - Watts opens from the current panic over world population and uses it to frame the larger problem of whether humanity’s usual assumptions about survival make sense.
  2. 01:57 The Pace of Change - He traces the explosive acceleration of technical and social change and asks whether the logic of progress may be moving against human interest.
  3. 05:56 Taoist Restraint - Turning to Taoist thought, Watts presents a criticism of over-management, efficiency, and the urge to improve things without limit.
  4. 08:57 Civilization and Repression - He brings in Norman O. Brown to connect civilization, restraint, and repression, suggesting that the same habits that organize society may also make it anti-human.
  5. 12:30 Bang or Whimper - A story from Big Sur and a Freud quotation sharpen the question into a stark choice between a brief intense life and a long dull one.
  6. 15:00 Temperament and Survival - Watts expands the argument through ant life and different temperaments, showing how the wish for stability or excitement can mask the same fear of ending.
  7. 19:01 The Future as Excuse - He criticizes both utopian reform and hands-off caution for sacrificing real people in the name of a future survival plan.
  8. 22:22 Living Now - Watts shifts from abstract survival to the richness of immediate experience, using children, play, and Norman Brown’s theme of bodily openness to argue for presence over anxious planning.
  9. 26:01 Handling Time - He closes by saying that apocalyptic worry is a symptom of not knowing how to handle time, and that fixation on the end can destroy enjoyment before anything ends.
