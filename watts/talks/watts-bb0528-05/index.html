Alan Watts

Return to the Forest

KPFA Archive 29:06 1960-02-05

Alan Watts uses Joseph Campbell’s paper as a frame for a talk about how shared worldviews form, collapse, and are replaced by more solitary ways of finding meaning. He moves from hunting and agrarian cultures to the mandala, caste, and the loss of a stable cosmos in modernity, then turns to forest-dwelling, Taoism, psychology, and the need for firsthand discovery. The talk ends by arguing that when old authorities fail, people are pushed back into direct contact with their own experience.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Campbell's Frame - Watts opens with Joseph Campbell, the Eranos setting, and the paper that gives him a way to talk about symbols and collapsing worldviews.
  2. 02:40 Hunting and Shamanism - He contrasts hunting cultures with agrarian ones, describing shamanism as solitary, individual, and won through ordeal rather than tradition.
  3. 05:19 Agrarian Order - Watts explains how settled societies require division of labor, priestly authority, and shared symbols that hold the community together.
  4. 09:52 The Mandala Breaks - He develops the mandala as a figure of integrated communal life and then shows how the old cosmos breaks apart in the modern West.
  5. 12:04 Return to the Forest - Watts turns to India and China, where renunciation, vanaprastha, and Taoist solitude all point toward a symbolic return to the forest.
  6. 15:00 Beyond Social Patterns - He stresses that this return is not regression but a way of going beyond socially conditioned ways of seeing and belonging.
  7. 17:56 Description and Anxiety - Watts brings in Korzybski to argue that language and symbolic thought make memory and prediction possible, but also produce chronic worry.
  8. 21:15 Finding It For Oneself - He contrasts freedom from social pressure with doctrinaire therapy and orthodox spirituality, insisting that liberation cannot be handed down like doctrine.
  9. 25:33 Alone Together - Watts closes by saying that the loss of authoritative masters leaves us on our own, yet this very condition can lead to direct, authentic selfhood.
