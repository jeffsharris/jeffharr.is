Alan Watts

The Study of Asia

KPFA Archive 22:10 1960-05-27

Alan Watts opens with a brief note of thanks, then turns to a larger argument about why the study of Asia matters to Western education. He dismisses both utilitarian and overly specialist approaches, arguing instead that Asia is valuable as a way to expose Western assumptions about progress, individuality, time, and space. The talk widens into examples from Joseph Needham, Dogen, Dignaga, and Taoism, before ending on a defense of imagination, play, and non-striving as the real sources of intellectual usefulness.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Opening thanks - Watts briefly thanks listeners for helping Sabro Hasegawa before moving into the lecture proper.
  2. 00:31 Why Asian studies began - He describes six years of work on introducing Asia into higher education and the practical frustrations that came with it.
  3. 01:50 Against narrow definitions - Watts sketches the two dead ends he wants to avoid: crude practicality on one side and dry specialist scholarship on the other.
  4. 04:04 The Western crisis - He argues that Western science and technology have enlarged human power while exposing a deeper lack of wisdom and control.
  5. 06:52 Not a borrowed solution - Watts rejects the idea that the answer lies in simply importing Eastern wisdom and instead asks for a closer examination of Western premises.
  6. 10:15 Asia as contrast - He explains that foreign cultures are useful because they define our boundaries and make the hidden assumptions of the West visible.
  7. 12:46 Needham as model - Joseph Needham becomes the chief example of serious cross-disciplinary Asian study, especially in showing Chinese science by contrast with Western science.
  8. 15:37 Modern Asia, old questions - Watts narrows the case for Asian study, admitting the limitations of contemporary Asian culture while pointing to older ideas and disciplines that still provoke thought.
  9. 19:45 Play over utility - He develops his central image of scholarship as imaginative play, drawing on university research, engineering fantasy, and the refusal to force results.
