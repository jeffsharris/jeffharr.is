Alan Watts

Art, Philosophy and Self-Improvement

KPFA Archive 26:20 1960-07-22

Alan Watts begins by questioning the fantasy of the wholly admirable teacher, then widens the point into a defense of art and philosophy as things that are not chiefly about moral improvement. From there he contrasts preacherly exhortation with the Taoist and Jungian sense of life as a whole, where light and dark, gain and loss, and virtue and defect belong together. The talk settles into a practical paradox: real adjustment is possible, but not by attacking the self as an enemy or treating life as a mere means to a future better world.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Idols and ordinary lives - Watts opens by deflating idealized images of teachers and authorities, arguing that moral instruction often depends on a false expectation of perfection.
  2. 02:01 What improvement can mean - He distinguishes genuine discipline from moral posturing and suggests that any attempt to improve one part of life often reveals hidden compensations elsewhere.
  3. 05:38 Jung and the divided self - Using Jung, Watts turns toward the need to acknowledge the dark side rather than deny it, insisting that people are made of contraries.
  4. 10:53 Art versus exhortation - He contrasts philosophy as moral advice with art as a way of presenting life without trying to use it as a corrective or a lesson.
  5. 14:56 Life as a whole - Watts develops the Taoist view of the universe as an integrated, purposeless totality, like a work of art rather than a project.
  6. 18:49 Chuang Tzu's comic example - He introduces Chuang Tzu's monkey-ration story to show that relations matter more than rigid piecemeal improvement.
  7. 20:56 Against cynicism - Watts separates his view from cynical exposure, arguing that the dark side should be seen as complementary rather than discrediting.
  8. 24:00 Beyond defects and neuroses - He ends by saying the point is not to perfect oneself but to move beyond self-division into a quieter center of things.
