Alan Watts

Bernard Beinson's War Diaries

KPFA Archive 28:32 1960-07-08

Alan Watts begins with Bernard Berenson’s defense of the first person and uses it to unsettle the usual suspicion that saying “I” is egoistic. From there he widens into a critique of Western confusion about humility, morality, and spiritual practice, arguing that Eastern paths are often misread as moral systems rather than disciplines of contemplation and direct participation in life. The talk gradually turns from social manners and false self-effacement to a sharper distinction between the ego as a defensive stance and the self as vivid, responsive presence.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Berenson and the word I - Watts opens with Berenson’s war diaries and uses the defense of first-person writing to question the idea that saying “I” is automatically self-centered.
  2. 00:48 False humility - He expands the point through Berenson’s own passage and an Austen anecdote, showing how reluctance to speak plainly can come from convention, class feeling, or self-consciousness rather than modesty.
  3. 03:22 The hidden ego - A Chesterton example turns the argument upside down: avoiding self-reference can become another way of showing off, with ego expressed indirectly instead of openly.
  4. 04:22 Ego and spiritual pose - Watts uses a remark about Buddhists and Hindus to separate spiritual doctrine from personality, suggesting that opposition to ego can itself be ego-driven.
  5. 06:15 West meets East - He argues that Western listeners often mistake Vedanta, Zen, and Taoism for moral systems, when their center of gravity is contemplative life rather than ethical reform.
  6. 08:23 Not a superhuman ideal - Watts insists that spiritual realization does not cancel illness or ordinary frailty, using Krishnamurti to reject the fantasy that wisdom should look like invulnerability.
  7. 10:15 Sensitivity versus self-deception - The talk moves toward a more practical criterion: true growth is not becoming insensible, but becoming honest about one’s feelings instead of repressing them in the name of goodness.
  8. 13:18 The defensive ego - Watts defines the ego as a refusal to participate fully in life, a stance of standing back from experience that is rooted in fear and self-protection.
  9. 16:55 Trusting life - Using Charlotte Selver and then Zen and Hindu examples, he describes a stronger self as one that can relax into experience, feel intensely, and remain fully engaged rather than aloof.
