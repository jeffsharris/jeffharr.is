Alan Watts

Karma, Law of Retribution

KPFA Archive 60:51 1960-07-01

Alan Watts begins by recasting karma as a mistaken picture of cause and effect, then moves toward a cleaner reading of Indian philosophy as a way of seeing through inherited cosmologies. He argues that what we call necessity, causality, and even natural law may be projections of language and pattern-making rather than features of reality itself. The talk closes by expanding the point through examples from food, gravity, and Wittgenstein’s image of a grid laid over the world.

Chapters

  1. 00:30 Opening the problem - Watts frames the talk as a correction to ordinary ways of posing life’s problems before introducing karma as the central issue.
  2. 02:26 Karma and retribution - He defines karma, contrasts action with popular causality, and describes the common belief that present conditions are payback for past acts.
  3. 05:30 Seeing through cosmology - Watts compares liberation from karma to the way science made older worldviews like Dante’s cosmos seem implausible without needing a direct refutation.
  4. 10:07 Action versus necessity - He returns to karma’s root meaning and begins separating chronological cause and effect from the idea of things arising together.
  5. 13:43 Food, hunger, death - Using living organisms, appetite, and death, he argues that causal language often just redescribes an event more completely rather than explaining it.
  6. 18:30 Rocks, sunflowers, and must - Watts tests the idea of necessity with stones and sunflowers, showing how much of what sounds like natural law is really built into our definitions.
  7. 24:15 Grids over nature - He develops Wittgenstein’s grid metaphor to suggest that laws, coordinates, and regularities are patterns we impose in order to describe the world.
