Alan Watts

Varieties of Order

KPFA Archive 30:49 1960-05-06

Alan Watts begins with the old question of whether societies should be treated like organisms, then weighs nominalism against realism to show why both views are partly persuasive and partly misleading. From there he shifts toward the difference between living order and mechanical organization, arguing that institutions can be described and managed, but cannot match the complexity of a human being. He closes by turning to Confucian, Chinese, Buddhist, Taoist, and Christian references to distinguish social conformity from a deeper order of nature that transcends both the individual and the collective.

Chapters

  1. 00:21 Society as Organism? - Watts introduces the talk through Spengler and the recurring debate over whether cultures resemble biological organisms.
  2. 01:21 Individuals and Collectives - He sets nominalism against realism, testing the claim that only individuals are real against the idea of the collective as a real pattern.
  3. 04:33 Communication Creates Groups - Watts argues that language, institutions, and shared habits bind people together and make social life more than a simple sum of parts.
  4. 06:47 The Limits of Social Machinery - He stresses that organizations are knowable and rule-bound, which makes them more mechanical and less complex than the human organism.
  5. 10:13 Against Rule Worship - Watts criticizes the growing faith in organizations, then uses intelligence as a way to name deeper integration than any procedure can capture.
  6. 12:48 Confucius and the Wheelwright - He turns to Confucius and the wheelwright story to show that virtue and skill depend on subtle judgment, not rigid instructions.
  7. 16:49 Small Teams, Then God - Watts allows that intimate groups can act like living systems before moving into the Christian idea of God as personal rather than merely collective.
  8. 21:12 Li and Hidden Pattern - He introduces Neo-Confucian Li as a deeper, ineffable order whose complexity exceeds both social explanation and scientific model-making.
  9. 25:08 Beyond Individuality - Watts ends by rejecting the notion that transcending individuality means social conformity, placing it instead in the larger order of nature and the universe.
