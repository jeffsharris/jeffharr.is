Alan Watts

The Work of Sokei-an Sasaki

KPFA Archive 30:56 1960-02-26

Alan Watts introduces Sokei-an Sasaki as an early and influential Zen figure in America, then uses a series of Sasaki excerpts to show how Zen resists becoming a fashionable system of self-improvement. The talk moves from comic anecdote and the idea of spontaneity, through the paradox of “gaining nothing” in Buddhism, into personal testimony, meditation, and finally Bodhidharma’s silent, uncompromising teaching. It ends by returning to the core Zen refusal to turn awakening into a measurable benefit or a verbal explanation.

Chapters

  1. 00:01 Zen Boomlet - Watts opens with the growing American interest in Zen and warns against turning it into a fad or organization.
  2. 01:35 Meeting Sokei-an - He introduces Sokei-an Sasaki as a friend, influence, and pioneering Zen teacher who lived and worked in New York.
  3. 03:52 Character and Color - Watts sketches Sasaki’s dignity, humor, and incongruity through vivid memories and a temple lecture scene.
  4. 06:03 Spontaneity and Nature - A comic anecdote about spontaneity becomes a way to show the shared Zen-Taoist sense of what happens of itself.
  5. 08:00 Studying Buddhism - Watts reads Sasaki on the question of whether Buddhism is beneficial and develops the paradox that practice yields nothing in the ordinary sense.
  6. 15:17 No Benefit, No Profit - The Bodhidharma and Ikkyu material sharpens the anti-utilitarian point and recasts spiritual seeking as a way of losing false ideas.
  7. 19:03 Satori and Buddha Mind - Sasaki’s account of awakening and his definition of Buddha shift the talk toward direct experience, meditation, and inner awareness.
  8. 24:32 The Quiet Life - Watts turns to monastic contemplation, defending quietude against Western assumptions that usefulness must be public and active.
  9. 27:14 Bodhidharma's Silence - The closing stories of Bodhidharma bring the talk to its stark end: the seeker cannot force the Dharma, and enlightenment is not a split between mind and matter.
