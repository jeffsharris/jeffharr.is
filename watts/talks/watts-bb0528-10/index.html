Alan Watts

Fundamentals of Eastern Philosophy

KPFA Archive 26:09 1960-03-04

Alan Watts returns to Hindu philosophy by using Vedanta to reopen the question of self, role, and liberation. He argues that moksha is not escape from life but release from convention, then turns to the Bhagavad Gita to show why detachment in Hindu thought still leads back into the world. The talk closes by redefining maya as a mental distortion, not the senses themselves, and by presenting Hinduism as a strong affirmation of earthly participation.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Returning to Vedanta - Watts reopens the series by returning to Hindu philosophy and framing Vedanta as the ground for the talk.
  2. 00:54 God, self, and masks - He develops the dramatic Hindu view of the universe, contrasting it with Western theism and arguing that the self is more than the social person.
  3. 03:47 Liberation from convention - Moksha is described as leaving role, name, and position behind in order to discover who one really is.
  4. 05:49 The limits of social identity - Watts broadens the argument into a critique of oversimplified personhood, bodily boundaries, and the assumption that the self is only a skin-encased mind.
  5. 10:41 Maya reconsidered - He redefines maya as the mind's habit of categorizing experience and warns against reading Hinduism as a rejection of the natural world.
  6. 14:26 The Gita on action - The Bhagavad Gita becomes the central corrective: Arjuna's battlefield dilemma is used to show action without inner entanglement.
  7. 17:18 Into the world again - Watts argues that divine play, accepted suffering, and the idea of lokasangraha all point toward active participation rather than withdrawal.
  8. 22:02 Affirmation, not escape - He closes by folding the cycles of Brahma, the boundary of maya, and the images of Purana and temple art into a final defense of Hinduism as world-affirming.
