Alan Watts

Bus Or Tram

KPFA Archive 26:44 1960-04-29

Alan Watts begins with the bus-or-tram joke and turns it into a critique of moral systems built on fixed rails: divinely revealed law, nature, and the word "ought" itself. From there he argues for an experimental attitude to ethics and politics, moving through scientific method, Cold War absolutism, and the weakness of a culture that prefers being right to being alive. In the second half, he shifts toward desire, design, and social imagination, asking why modern life is so often thought about only superficially and why people seem ashamed of wanting anything at all.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Tramlines of Morality - Watts introduces the bus-or-tram image and uses it to challenge fixed ideas of right conduct.
  2. 02:25 The Dictionary Joke - A comic story about God, a lost dictionary, and the words "ought" and "should" becomes a critique of rigid ethics.
  3. 04:30 Experiment Over Certainty - He argues that human affairs need trial and revision, not the anxious need to be right from the start.
  4. 07:08 Politics Without Flexibility - Watts turns to Cold War rhetoric and treats hard-line political certainty as a self-destructive refusal of experiment.
  5. 10:31 A Missing Vision - He broadens the critique to a culture that has warnings about catastrophe but little positive imagination for human life.
  6. 13:30 What We Actually Want - Watts reframes desire as something that must be clarified, tested, and planned for in detail rather than vaguely invoked.
  7. 16:50 Surface Design, Surface Living - He attacks superficial planning in consumer goods and the discomfort people feel when wanting seems too easy.
  8. 19:45 Suicide and Judgment - The talk closes on the psychology of self-destructive certainty and a sharp rejection of heaven imagined as vindication and punishment.
