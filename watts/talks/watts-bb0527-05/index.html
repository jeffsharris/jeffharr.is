Alan Watts

The Art of Psychoanalysis Part 1

KPFA Archive 28:57 1959-12-04

Alan Watts spends this talk reading and riffing on J. Haley’s satirical article about psychoanalysis, using it to recast the analytic room as a game of status, reversal, and controlled helplessness. He moves from Freud’s setting — the couch, the fee, silence, and interpretation — into a brisk catalog of patient maneuvers, analyst counter-moves, and the professional language that keeps the contest going. The final stretch broadens out to psychoanalytic meetings and the social theater of analysts themselves, before Watts returns to the central question: whether the article is mocking analysis or admiring it by implication.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 Introducing Haley's Article - Watts frames the episode around J. Haley’s piece and situates it in a Stanford-linked communications context.
  2. 02:43 One-Upmanship Defined - He lays out the central joke of the essay: human relations as a constant struggle to put the other person one down.
  3. 06:25 Freud's Setting - The couch, the fee, silence, and the analyst’s placement behind the patient become the basic tools of control.
  4. 10:54 Analyst and Patient Ploys - Watts runs through the patient’s tactics and the analyst’s standard replies, from doubt and resistance to transference.
  5. 18:39 Limits and Variations - He turns to special cases, including the analyst’s vulnerabilities, suicide threats, and the contrast with Rogerian methods and psychosis.
  6. 24:39 Professional One-Upmanship - The talk widens to psychoanalytic meetings, where status is won through closeness to Freud and verbal obscurity.
  7. 26:57 Typical Course of Treatment - Watts summarizes the arc of analysis from the honeymoon phase through resistance, collapse, and eventual indifference to control.
  8. 30:02 Final Question - He closes by naming Haley as the source and leaves the listener with an unresolved question about satire versus praise.
