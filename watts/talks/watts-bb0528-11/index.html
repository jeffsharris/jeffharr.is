Alan Watts

Fundamentals of Buddhism

KPFA Archive 29:58 1960-03-11

Alan Watts begins by clearing up a point from his earlier remarks on Hinduism, using Shankara to argue against a bloodless, world-denying reading of Maya. He then turns to Buddhism as a practical system: the Buddha as an awakened one, the Wheel of Life as a map of states of mind, and the Four Noble Truths as a diagnostic sequence. The talk closes by tracing suffering, grasping, and unwisdom into nirvana and the Eightfold Path, stressing that the path is not obedience but a way of waking up.

Chapters

  1. 00:45 Correcting Shankara - Watts revisits his earlier comments on Hinduism and pushes back against interpretations of Shankara that make Maya mean sheer unreality.
  2. 05:26 Who the Buddha Is - He introduces Buddhism in historical terms, explains Gautama as the Buddha, and situates awakening within the wider Indian tradition.
  3. 07:54 Wheel of Life - The Wheel of Life becomes a visual summary of Buddhist cosmology and, more importantly, a psychological map of mind-states.
  4. 10:13 Rebirth as Process - Watts reframes reincarnation as continuous change in present experience, using T.S. Eliot to show identity as pattern rather than fixed soul.
  5. 14:05 The Four Truths - He presents the Four Noble Truths as a medical diagnosis: suffering, its cause, whether it can be cured, and the prescription.
  6. 17:52 The Middle Way - Using opposites like being and non-being, he explains Buddhism as a doctrine of relativity rather than a compromise position.
  7. 20:58 Grasping and Ignorance - Watts threads together trishna, avidya, and karma to show how clinging and mis-seeing produce the vicious circle of experience.
  8. 23:40 Nirvana and the Path - He corrects popular ideas of nirvana, then closes with the Eightfold Path as disciplined understanding, conduct, and meditation leading to integrated consciousness.
