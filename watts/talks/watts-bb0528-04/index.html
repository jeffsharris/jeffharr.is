Alan Watts

Eastern Orthodox Spirituality

KPFA Archive 26:46 1960-01-29

Alan Watts uses a little-known book on Eastern Orthodoxy as a springboard for a wide-ranging comparison between Orthodox Christianity and the West. He moves from body-and-spirit, doctrine-and-life contrasts into kenosis, theosis, and then into close parallels with Pure Land Buddhism and the language of self-power versus other-power. The talk ends with Orthodox stories about despair, holy fools, and a freedom that looks like simplicity, trust, and even absurdity from the outside.

Chapters

  1. 00:00 A Book on Orthodoxy - Watts opens by recommending John Gregerson's The Transfigured Cosmos and frames the talk as a postscript to earlier remarks on Christian philosophy.
  2. 02:01 East and West Contrasted - He reads a summary of the differences between Western and Eastern Christianity, stressing community, synthesis, and religion as lived mystery rather than analytic doctrine.
  3. 03:59 Theosis and Cosmic Salvation - Watts explains divinization, the iron-in-fire image, and the Orthodox sense that salvation is cosmic rather than merely individual.
  4. 06:41 Kenosis and Creation - He turns to kenosis, the self-emptying of God, and extends the idea to creation as concealment of divine glory and the world as a process of return.
  5. 09:50 Other-Power Buddhism - Watts brings in Pure Land Buddhism, contrasting tariki with jiriki and arguing that liberation comes through reliance on what is already true.
  6. 12:46 Prelest and the Unfree Will - He develops the Orthodox idea of prelest, likens it to maya, and uses a Jesus prayer passage to show why self-salvation by will is seen as impossible.
  7. 17:39 Despair Is the Danger - A story of two Egyptian hermits becomes a lesson that the real spiritual failure is despondency, not sin itself, because despair closes one to divine love.
  8. 21:58 Evil, Shadow, and Resistance - Watts briefly invokes Aquinas and then argues that evil is amplified by fighting it, whereas resistance can make it more rigid and self-justifying.
  9. 24:58 Providence and Holy Fools - The talk closes with Theophan on surrender to divine providence and a Russian holy-fool story that turns sanctity into playful, fearless absurdity.
