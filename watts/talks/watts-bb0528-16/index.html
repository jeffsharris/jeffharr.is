Alan Watts

The Useless in Art

KPFA Archive 33:03 1960-04-08

Alan Watts builds the talk from a simple irritation: modern culture keeps demanding that art justify itself in terms of usefulness. From Genesis and Ionesco to theater, design, and representational painting, he argues that art belongs with play, freedom, and the direct apprehension of things rather than propaganda, technique worship, or profit. The talk closes by turning the question outward: if we can look at a tree or a cloud without asking what it is for, why should art be judged differently?

Chapters

  1. 00:00 The Demand for Use - Watts opens with grant applications and the pressure to prove that contemplation and culture have practical value.
  2. 01:26 Genesis Recast - He turns to Genesis and argues that the Fall can be read as a shift toward judging things by usefulness and survival.
  3. 02:33 Ionesco on Art - Watts reads Eugene Ionesco at length to frame art as self-sufficient, gratuitous, and not answerable to utility.
  4. 05:14 Freedom and Humor - He expands Ionesco’s critique into a warning against realism, ideology, and the fear of imagination in modern life.
  5. 09:35 The Artist Emerges - Watts shifts from the useful to the arts of making, showing how skill, style, and beauty emerge once people begin to notice technique.
  6. 13:40 Anti-Art and Reaction - He follows the recoil from refined art into propaganda, caprice, and deliberate anti-art, including examples from Paris and film.
  7. 16:54 Nature as Measure - Watts argues that public love for landscape and representation shows art at its best when it copies nature’s purposeless fullness.
  8. 20:13 Seeing Without Purpose - Returning to Ionesco, he insists that a tree, the universe, and the works of art that matter most are not properly met by asking what they are for.
  9. 23:54 Play Against Profit - He closes by contrasting play with the art market, mock utility, and the habit of treating even art as an investment.
